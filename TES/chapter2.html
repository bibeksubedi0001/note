Q1. What is LEP? [1 mark] 2071 Magh

Answer:

LEP stands for Labour-based, Environment-friendly, and Participatory development approach. It is a sustainable development approach that combines local labour utilization, environmental protection, and active stakeholder involvement to achieve inclusive and sustainable infrastructure development, especially relevant for developing nations like Nepal.

Q2. Describe briefly about the LEP development approach. [4 marks] 2080 Chaitra

Answer:

LEP is a development approach consisting of three components:

  1. L – Labour Based: Uses local labours with light equipment. It is generally cost-effective and produces high-quality results. Appropriate for developing nations and mostly used to construct feeder roads, drainage systems, sewerage systems, irrigation systems, and houses. It provides local employment, skill development, and income generation.
  2. E – Environment Friendly: Obeys environmental policies, uses eco-friendly materials and technology, keeps pollutants within acceptable limits, and conducts IEE (Initial Environmental Examination) and EIA (Environmental Impact Assessment). Aims for minimum disturbance to the environment and sustainable development.
  3. P – Participatory: Involves active participation of stakeholders (end users) in the planning and decision-making process. It cultivates a feeling of ownership, is important for sustainable development, and makes project service delivery easier.

Q3. Discuss LEP approach in infrastructure development. [4 marks] 2071 Bhadra

Answer:

The LEP approach in infrastructure development ensures that projects are sustainable, inclusive, and environment-friendly:

  • Labour Based (L): Infrastructure projects like feeder roads, drainage, sewerage, irrigation, and housing are constructed using local labour with light equipment. This generates local employment, develops skills in the community, and is generally cost-effective while maintaining high quality. It is especially appropriate for developing nations like Nepal.
  • Environment Friendly (E): Development activities account for the real impact on the environment. Projects use eco-friendly materials and technology, maintain pollutants within acceptable limits, and conduct IEE and EIA before implementation. Simple mitigation measures are applied to face the challenges of sustainable development.
  • Participatory (P): Everyone participates in the decision-making part of the development process, either individually or as group representatives. This is the ideal approach since each participation is encouraged and there is no dominant figure. It ensures wider acceptance and sustainability of infrastructure projects.

Q4. Describe the LEP Approach for the development of Nepal. [4 marks] 2072 Ashwin

Answer:

The LEP approach is significant for Nepal's development because:

  • Labour Based: Nepal has abundant labour force but limited heavy machinery. The labour-based approach uses local labours with light equipment, providing employment, income generation, and livelihood improvement at the local level. It utilizes local-level technology and develops local skills.
  • Environment Friendly: Nepal's fragile mountain ecology demands minimum disturbance during development. This approach ensures environmental policies are obeyed, eco-friendly materials are used, and IEE/EIA are conducted before projects begin.
  • Participatory: Nepal's diverse communities benefit from active involvement in planning and decision-making. This creates a feeling of ownership, ensures the project serves actual needs, and makes service delivery easier. It is important for achieving sustainable development in Nepal's context.

Q2. Discuss about participatory approach for community management. [8 marks] 2075 Bhadra

Answer:

Community Management: It is the management of common pool resources along with the community as a whole by the community itself, with the help of volunteers and stakeholders within it.

Participatory Approach for Community Management:

Participation is a process through which people influence and share control over planning and decision-making. For effective community management, the participatory approach operates at multiple levels:

Importance of Participation:

  • Freedom of thought and expressing views
  • Opinions and decisions of people count
  • Identify weaknesses and strengths of the community
  • Build self-esteem and capacity

Strategic Approaches:

  1. Learning: Analyzing problems, finding solutions, shaping plans
  2. Partnership: Developing professional teams
  3. Empowerment: Involving stakeholders at all levels, shaping programmes and placements

Steps to Participation:

  1. Manipulation: Managing people in a clever or skilful way to participate
  2. Information: Informing citizens of their rights, responsibilities, and options through news, media, pamphlets, posters
  3. Consultation: Inviting citizens' opinions and formally consulting or discussing
  4. Partnership: Association of two or more people as partners like joint policy boards, planning committees
  5. Delegated Power: Authorizing people to act as representatives through negotiations between citizens and public officials

Community Development Approaches:

  • Top-down: Participation as a means (static, passive, controllable) — Low empowerment
  • Bottom-up: Participation as an end (active, dynamic, self-mobilization) — Moderate empowerment
  • Partnership (cooperative): Working together — High empowerment and sustainable

6 C Principles of Community Management:

  1. Content: Create content that supports your goals
  2. Cross Promotion: Use content of others relevant to your audience
  3. Creating Relationships: Communicate through social media to connect people
  4. Cultivating Relationships: Acknowledge people joining and send personal thanks
  5. Collaboration: Work closely with businesses in your industry or complementary parties
  6. Consistency: Make sure your audience knows what to expect

Infrastructure is the basic physical and organizational structures and facilities (e.g., buildings, roads, power supplies) which form the foundation for society and economics. It is broadly classified into:

  1. Economic Infrastructure: Basic facilities that directly benefit the process of production and distribution — roads, railways, telecommunications, waterways, airways, financial institutions, electricity, water supply, etc.

Infrastructure Gap: The difference between required infrastructure and current economic infrastructure. Nepal needs to invest 10-15% of national income to bridge the infrastructure gap. 1% increase in the stock of infrastructure is associated with a 1% increase in GDP.

2.4 Ethnographic Approach to Collect Information 2081 Chaitra, 2079 Jestha, 2071 Magh

Q1. What do you understand by 'ethnographic data collection approach'? [3 marks] 2079 Jestha

Answer:

Ethnography literally means "writing about folk" (ethno = folk; graphy = description). It is a qualitative research method that involves observing human interactions in social settings and activities, studying people in their cultural context — learning from the inside rather than studying from the outside.

Key characteristics:

  • Partial or full description of a group — the researcher becomes part of the group studied
  • Derived from the field of Anthropology (study of mankind)
  • No preset limitation of what will be observed; it is a continuous process
  • Data collected through observations and interviews in the group's natural habitat

Procedures: (1) Identify problem and make questionnaires, (2) Observe community — open access (without permission) or closed access (with permission), (3) Analyze data concurrently with collection, (4) Create ethnography report.

Common Methods: Field research, participant observation, case studies (exploratory, explanatory, descriptive), and focus groups.

Q2. Discuss about ethnographic approach for community management. [3 marks] 2071 Magh

Answer:

The ethnographic approach is valuable for community management because it involves studying people in their own cultural context to understand their needs, behaviors, and social structures.

Application in Community Management:

  • Understanding Culture: Searching for meaning of cultural norms, views, and reasons for certain behaviors and practices.
  • Social Interactions: Examining social trends, instances, interactions, and encounters within the community.

  • Data Collection: Using content, artifacts, and interactions to collect data. Methods include participant observation, case studies, and focus groups.

The ethnographic approach helps managers and engineers understand community needs from the inside, enabling more effective participatory development rather than imposing top-down solutions.

Detailed Reference:

Definition:

Ethnography literally means "writing about folk" (ethno = folk; graphy = description). It is a method of observing human interactions in social settings and activities, studying people in their cultural context. Rather than studying people from the outside, ethnography means learning people from the inside.

  • Partial or full description of a group
  • The researcher becomes part of the group he/she studies
  • Derived from the field of Anthropology (study of mankind)
  • No preset limitation of what will be observed
  • No end point — it is a continuous process

Qualities of Ethnography:

  1. Data is collected through observations and interviews
  2. Research is conducted in the group's own natural habitat
  3. Content, artifacts, and interactions between them are studied
  4. The researcher is also a learner, constantly studying and verifying new aspects of culture

When to Use Ethnography:

  • Searching for meaning of cultural norms and views
  • To find reasons for certain behaviours and practices
  • To examine social trends and instances
  • To examine social interactions and encounters
  • To understand the roles of families, relationships, and organizations

Procedures of Ethnographic Studies:

  1. Identifying problem and making questionnaires
  2. Observation of community and data collection through:
    • Open access: Without permission (e.g., in malls, parks, etc.)
    • Closed access: With permission (e.g., in hospitals, homes, institutions, etc.)
  3. Analysis and data collection are done concurrently
  4. An ethnography is created that gives pictures of the community and its data

Common Methods in Ethnography:

  • Field research
  • Participant observation
  • Case studies (exploratory, explanatory, and descriptive)
  • Focus groups

Q3. List out main data collection methods in ethnographic survey. Explain the importance of ethnographic survey to understand societal dynamics. [3+5 marks] 2081 Chaitra

Answer:

Main Data Collection Methods in Ethnographic Survey:

  1. Participant Observation: The researcher immerses in the community, observing daily activities, rituals, and social interactions firsthand while participating in them.
  2. In-depth Interviews: Structured, semi-structured, or unstructured interviews with community members to gather their perspectives, experiences, and knowledge.
  3. Focus Group Discussions: Group discussions with selected community members to explore shared views, values, and issues.
  4. Field Notes & Documentation: Detailed written records of observations, conversations, and events during fieldwork.
  5. Case Studies: Intensive study of specific individuals, families, or subgroups — can be exploratory, explanatory, or descriptive.
  6. Surveys & Questionnaires: Structured data collection tools used to gather quantifiable information.
  7. Artifact & Content Analysis: Studying cultural artifacts, documents, photos, and other materials produced by the community.

Importance of Ethnographic Survey to Understand Societal Dynamics:

  1. Cultural Understanding: Reveals the underlying cultural norms, values, beliefs, and practices that shape community behavior — going beyond surface-level data.
  2. Insider Perspective: By studying people from within their own context, ethnography captures authentic views rather than outsider assumptions.
  3. Social Interaction Patterns: Helps identify how individuals, families, and organizations interact, cooperate, and resolve conflicts.
  4. Power Dynamics & Inequality: Uncovers hidden social hierarchies, access to resources, and patterns of marginalization.
  5. Effective Development Planning: Engineers and planners can design infrastructure and programs that genuinely align with community needs rather than imposing top-down solutions.
  6. Conflict Identification: Identifies existing or potential resource conflicts, social tensions, and their root causes.
  7. Behavioral Change Insights: Understanding why people behave in certain ways helps design effective health education, environmental awareness, and community management programs.

2.6 Participatory Tools, Focus Group Discussions & Key Informant Interviews

Participatory Tools:

  1. Participatory Rural Appraisal (PRA): A joint learning and decision-making approach that aims to incorporate the knowledge and opinions of rural people in the planning and management of development projects. It includes methods to help local people understand through planning, monitoring, and evaluation. Facilitates information exchange and analysis among stakeholders.
  2. Beneficiary Feedback: A system to collect the views of stakeholders on the current ongoing status and quality of development work. Involves feedback about the development work to provide sustainability.
  3. Key Informant Interview (KII): A qualitative assessment technique to gain information from community leaders, residents, etc. A key informant is a person who can offer in-depth insight into a particular organization or community. Examples: community leaders, facility supervisors, administrators, business owners.
  4. Most Significant Change (MSC) Technique: Influential change stories of people directly or indirectly affected by development activities are collected at field level. The most significant stories are selected and subjected to discussion. Helps bring positive impact to the community.
  5. Outcome Mapping: A methodology that monitors and evaluates development activities so that these bring sustainable change in the community. Based on evaluation rather than theory.

Planning Key Informant Interviews:

  1. Gather and review existing data
  2. Determine what information is needed
  3. Choose key informant
  4. Choose type of interview
  5. Develop an interview tool
  6. Determine documentation method
  7. Conduct the interview
  8. Compile and organize the data

Focus Group Discussion (FGD):

A data collection method in which a certain number of people (usually 6-10) sharing common interest are gathered together to discuss a specific topic with the help of a facilitator. The moderator creates an environment where all participants can share views, information, perception, and ideas. Multiple sessions are conducted to identify trends and patterns.

How to Conduct a Focus Group:

  1. Conceptualization Phase:
    • Determining the purpose — what information is needed and why
    • Determining whom to study
    • Developing a plan and estimating required resources
    • Specifying procedure, time, and proposed budget
    • Allowing others to review the plan
  2. Interview Phase:
    • Developing carefully planned questions (around 5-6)
    • Arranging questions in logical sequence
    • Using open-ended questions, avoiding "yes/no" questions
    • Moderator keeps discussion flowing, avoids irrelevant topics
  3. Analyzing and Reporting Phase:
    • Immediately after session, moderator and assistant spot-check the data
    • Arrive at short, mutually agreeable summary
    • Data examined at three levels: raw data, descriptive statements, and interpretation
    • Present the meaning of data rather than simply summarizing
    • Data analysis must be systematic and verifiable

Advantages of Focus Groups:

  • Can obtain data from children or illiterate individuals
  • Provides data more quickly and at lower cost
  • Requires less preparation and comparatively easy to conduct
  • Researchers can directly interact with respondents (allow clarification, follow-up)
  • Very flexible — can be used with wide range of topics, individuals, and settings
  • Results are easy to understand and accessible for decision makers

Disadvantages of Focus Groups:

  • Less control over group; less control over what information is produced
  • Produces relatively disorganized data, making analysis difficult
  • Small sample size limits ability to generalize to larger population
  • Requires carefully trained interviewer
  • Results may be biased by dominant members; reserved members may hesitate

2.7 Participatory Observation & Structured Questionnaire

Participatory Observation:

A type of data collection method typically used in qualitative research. The evaluator gains information through observation and records from direct involvement with the surroundings.

  • It helps understand the different aspects of locality
  • Helps develop relationships between components within observation areas
  • Helps the researcher interact with groups and individuals to know them closely

Scope of Participatory Observation:

  1. Appearance: Clothing, age, physical appearance, social status, religion
  2. Verbal Behaviour & Interactions: Languages, tone of voice, how they interact with each other, ethnicity
  3. Physical Behaviour & Gestures: Body language for communication, how they express feelings, social ranks, profession
  4. Personal Space: Relationships between each other, how close people are
  5. Human Traffic: Movement of people at observation site, time spent, types of people (age, gender, ethnicity) entering or leaving
  6. People Who Stand Out: People receiving more attention, their characteristics, what makes them alike, kind of help people want from them

Advantages:

  • Used to observe actions across time
  • More in-depth look at society and societal phenomena
  • Supplies critical information helpful for project plan, data compilation, and understanding
  • Helps to be more familiar with contexts, relationships, and behaviours
  • Observe the situation and background when any action occurs

Disadvantages:

  • Time consuming
  • Needs good memory and attentiveness of researchers
  • Researchers can lose their objectives as they participate in groups
  • Need to focus on objective of research as it is a subjective method

Types of Participatory Observation:

  1. Non-Participatory: No contact with the population or group; doesn't allow relationship formation
  2. Passive Participation: Limited connections without making any relationships
  3. Moderate Participation: Researcher acts both as insider and outsider; keeps focused on research objectives
  4. Active Participation: Active member of group accepting traditions and norms; in-depth understanding and relationship; risk of losing objectives
  5. Complete Participation: Entirely integrated with traditions and norms; highest risk of losing research objectives

Phases of Participatory Observation:

  1. Establishing relations
  2. Fit with the surroundings
  3. Data recording and observations
  4. Data analysis

Structured Questionnaire:

Data collection on a particular issue in which participants are presented a list of questions requiring clear and concise answers. It should not be subjective and should make people realize the importance of their answers and the purpose of the study.

Types:

  • Self-completion questionnaire: Postal, electronic questionnaire
  • Interview schedules: Face-to-face, telephone questionnaire

Uses of Questionnaires:

  • Collecting realistic information to categorize people and circumstances
  • Measuring satisfaction of clients with services
  • Observing opinions and attitudes of groups of people
  • Should NOT be used to investigate complex issues or explore controversial topics

Advantages:

  • Touch a large number of people at low cost
  • Simply connect with geographically diversified people
  • Postal questionnaires can be completed at suitable time
  • Telephone questionnaires make it easy for disabled people
  • Face-to-face questionnaires help recognize suitable persons and gather additional information

Disadvantages:

  • Response rate is low (postal)
  • Rejection rate is high (telephone, face-to-face)
  • No control over who completes postal questionnaire
  • Postal not suitable for people with visual and reading difficulties
  • F2F and telephone questionnaires need skilled interviewers
  • F2F questionnaires are more time consuming and labour intensive

Q1. What do you mean by Resource Mapping? [8 marks] 2075 Bhadra

Answer:

Definition: Resource mapping is the process of identifying what is valuable in a community and developing strategies for mobilizing those resources. Resources include human resources, financial resources, institutional assets, material assets, networks, etc. It is used in education, project management, business, and more.

Community Resource Mapping: An approach in which information concerning topography, human settlements, cultures and activities, abundance and distribution of resources, access levels, and use of resources are shown at a glance. It is a participatory mapping tool under participatory tools (alongside social mapping, wealth ranking, and diagramming tools).

Basic Result: A resource map is a map showing a community's or group's living environment, natural resources, social facilities, and infrastructures.

Uses of Resource Mapping:

  • Identify new resources
  • Enable to picture resources and features
  • Avoid duplication of services and resources
  • Cultivate new partnerships and relationships
  • Identify problems, possibilities, and opportunities
  • Encourage collaboration

Limitations of Resource Mapping:

  • Bounded by spatial limitations on social, cultural, and economic domains
  • Not viable for large geographical areas
  • Limited by available data; high-quality data may not be in mappable format
  • Defines issues of access in terms of physical proximity to resources

Steps for Resource Mapping:

  1. Step 1 — Pre-Mapping: Establishing a task force, setting a vision and goals, communicating continuously
  2. Step 2 — Mapping: Identifying resources, developing mapping tools and strategies, gathering information, determining meaning, communicating and using results
  3. Step 3 — Taking Action: Developing an action plan, achieving consensus, implementing, sharing
  4. Step 4 — Evaluation & Maintenance: Evaluating progress, maintaining momentum, sustaining the effort

Wealth Ranking:

Wealth Ranking (PWR — Participatory Wealth Ranking) is a participatory poverty assessment method that uses the ratings of local reference groups concerning the relative poverty status of households in their community. It is also known as well-being ranking.

  • Identifies levels of well-being in households using local criteria defined by community members
  • Provides insight into socioeconomic differences in a locality
  • Highly significant to prioritize development actions fitting each specific group's needs

Results Provided by Wealth Ranking:

  • Local concept of wealth, economic and well-being states
  • Economic and well-being profile of community
  • Social stratification at community level
  • Ownership and user rights

Purpose of Wealth Ranking:

  • To understand socioeconomic differences within a community
  • To categorize households or social groups into different economic categories
  • To know how people access and use resources
  • To identify likely susceptible changes within a community from project interventions or climate change
  • To identify marginalized groups and develop equitable benefit distribution mechanisms

Limitations of Wealth Ranking:

  • Gives a stationary picture with no expression of poverty dynamics
  • Narrow focus on ranking doesn't deal with distribution of well-being within the household
  • May be a susceptible matter — information may not be completely true due to fear of data being used against people

Steps for Wealth Ranking:

  1. Select local analysts
  2. Separate groups (gender, ethnicity, age, caste, etc.)
  3. Provide instructions and explanations
  4. Check whether the group is comfortable for this process
  5. Take data
  6. Produce wealth ranking
  7. Analyze
  8. Conclude

Facilitation Methods of Wealth Ranking:

  • Mapping method
  • Card method
  • Percentage distribution method
  • Summary matrix method

Social Mapping:

A graphic representation of the community layout presenting information on: infrastructures, population, social stratifications, chronic health cases, disability, malnourished children, family planning, vaccination, widows, destitute, etc.

Uses: Develop comprehensive understanding of physical and social aspects; collect demographic information household-wise; provide a forum of discussion; serve as a guiding instrument for planning interventions; serve as a monitoring and evaluation tool.

Procedure: Prepare a base map → Code each household → Focus on the indicators → Monitor and evaluate.

Poverty Definitions:

Poverty is a multidimensional concept including social, economic, and political elements. It encompasses material well-being, food security, employment, physical capital, human capital, physical deprivation, psychological aspects (voice, power, independence), and cultural and social norms.

  • Absolute Poverty (Extreme Poverty/Destitution): The complete lack of means necessary to meet basic personal needs such as food, clothing, and shelter. The threshold is considered about the same, independent of a person's permanent location or era.
  • Relative Poverty: Occurs when a person in a given country does not enjoy a certain minimum level of "living standards" compared to the rest of the population. The threshold varies from one country or society to another.

Strategies for addressing poverty typically include welfare, economic freedoms, and providing financial services. Constraints include corruption, tax avoidance, debt, loan conditionality, and brain drain of health care and educational professionals.
