Q2. Define technology. Discuss about the characteristics of technology with suitable examples. [2+6 marks] 2080 Chaitra

Answer:

Definition: Technology is the application of scientific knowledge for practical purposes. It is the branch of knowledge that deals with the formation and use of technical resources and their interrelation with life, society, and the environment.

Characteristics of Technology: (as per the syllabus)

  1. Knowledge Based: Technology is rooted in scientific knowledge and research. Every technological product is the result of organized knowledge applied to solve a problem. Example: The design of earthquake-resistant buildings is based on structural engineering knowledge.
  2. Driven by Consumer Demand: Technology evolves in response to the needs and demands of society. Example: The demand for faster communication led to the invention of smartphones and 5G networks.
  3. Evident in All Cultures: Every civilization, from primitive to modern, has developed some form of technology suited to its environment. Example: Indigenous Nepali communities developed water-powered grain mills (Ghatta/Dhiki) centuries ago.
  4. Fundamental to Survival: Technology is essential for human survival — from clothing and shelter to food production and medicine. Example: Irrigation technology is fundamental to feeding the world's population.
  5. Accumulative (Cumulative): Technology builds upon itself — each invention forms the foundation for the next. Example: The invention of the transistor led to integrated circuits, then microprocessors, then modern computers.
  6. Extends Our Capabilities: Technology enables humans to do things beyond their natural physical or mental capacity. Example: Cranes extend our lifting ability; calculators extend our computational ability.
  7. Inevitable: Technological change cannot be stopped — it continuously evolves. What is advanced today becomes obsolete tomorrow. Example: Film cameras replaced by digital cameras, then by smartphone cameras.
  8. Irreversible: Once a technology is introduced and adopted, society cannot easily go back to the pre-technology state. Example: After the introduction of mobile phones, society is permanently dependent on them for communication.

Q3. Define technology is the foundation of civilization. [4 marks] 2072 Magh

Answer:

Technology is rightly called the foundation of civilization because the progress of human society has been directly linked to technological advancements at every stage:

  • Stone Age: The invention of stone tools and fire enabled early humans to hunt, cook, and survive harsh environments, forming the first communities.
  • Agricultural Revolution (~10,000 BC): The development of ploughs, irrigation systems, and crop cultivation techniques transformed nomadic tribes into settled civilizations (Indus Valley, Mesopotamia, etc.).
  • Industrial Revolution (18th–19th century): The steam engine, mechanized looms, and railways revolutionized production and transportation, creating the modern urban civilization.
  • Information Age (20th–21st century): Computers, the internet, and telecommunications have created a globally connected civilization.

Without technology, civilization as we know it — with organized agriculture, cities, infrastructure, governance, and communication — would not exist.

1.4 Conflict of Technology & Technology Creates Opportunity for Society to Change 2077 Chaitra

Q1. Describe how technology helps create opportunity while transforming a society. [6 marks] 2077 Chaitra

Answer:

Technology acts as a catalyst for societal transformation by creating new opportunities while simultaneously disrupting existing structures:

  • Economic Opportunity: The internet has created entirely new industries — e-commerce, app development, digital marketing, and freelancing platforms that provide employment to millions globally. In Nepal, platforms like Daraz, eSewa, and Khalti have created new economic ecosystems.
  • Social Mobility: Technology allows talented individuals from disadvantaged backgrounds to access education and opportunities previously reserved for the elite. Free online courses from MIT, Harvard, and Khan Academy democratize knowledge.
  • Agricultural Transformation: Improved seeds, mechanization, and weather forecasting technology help subsistence farmers increase yields, generating surplus for commercial sale.
  • Governance & Transparency: E-governance initiatives (online land registration, digital citizenship, online tender systems) reduce corruption and increase efficiency.
  • The Conflict: However, technology also causes disruption — traditional artisans lose markets to factory-made goods, small shops are displaced by online retailers, and privacy is compromised. This tension between opportunity and disruption is the fundamental "conflict of technology."

1.5 Appropriate Technology 2077 Chaitra, 2073 Bhadra, 2079 Shrawan, 2071 Bhadra, 2075 Bhadra, 2074 Bhadra, 2073 Magh, 2071 Magh

Q1. Define appropriate technology with suitable examples. [2–4 marks] 2077 Chaitra, 2073 Bhadra, 2071 Bhadra, 2071 Magh

Answer:

Definition: Appropriate technology is a small and local level technology whose primary goal is to make use of local level skills and manpower to provide basic human needs and services. It is an approach that promotes small-scale, decentralized, energy-efficient, locally controlled and environmentally sound technology that attempts to solve basic human problems.

Dr. Ernst Friedrich described it as the technology which should be able to promote health, beauty, and permanence. Ideologies of Mahatma Gandhi and Mao Zedong advocated small, local and predominantly village-based technology to help local villages become self-reliant.

Properties of Appropriate Technology:

  1. Small scale and simple
  2. Decentralization of goods, products and services
  3. Labour intensive
  4. Energy efficient
  5. Self-sustaining
  6. Emphasizes use of renewable resources
  7. Environmentally sound
  8. Locally controlled

Examples:

  • Improved Cooking Stoves (ICS): Use less firewood and produce less indoor smoke compared to traditional open fires — suitable for rural Nepal.
  • Rope Pumps for Water: Simple, locally manufactured hand pumps for groundwater extraction in villages where electric pumps are unaffordable.
  • Biogas Plants: Convert animal dung into cooking gas and organic fertilizer — widely used in Nepal's rural areas.
  • Gabion Walls: Wire-mesh boxes filled with locally available stones for slope stabilization, cheaper than concrete retaining walls.
  • Micro-Hydropower: Small-scale hydroelectric systems (5–100 kW) providing electricity to remote hill communities not connected to the national grid.

Q2. What are appropriate technologies? How are they useful in least developed countries like Nepal? [8 marks] 2075 Bhadra, 2079 Shrawan

Answer:

Definition: Appropriate technologies are those designed with consideration to local environmental, cultural, economic, and social aspects. They are affordable, simple to operate and maintain, and use locally available resources and skills.

Why Appropriate Technology is Relevant to LDCs like Nepal:

  1. Affordability: Nepal has limited capital for expensive, high-tech solutions. Appropriate technologies like biogas plants, solar home systems, and ICS require low investment.
  2. Local Employment: These technologies are labor-intensive rather than capital-intensive, generating local jobs (e.g., construction of suspension bridges using local labor).
  3. Self-Reliance: They use locally available materials and skills, reducing dependence on imports and foreign expertise.
  4. Environmental Sustainability: Technologies like micro-hydro and biogas use renewable resources and produce minimal pollution.
  5. Social Acceptability: Because they are designed for local conditions, communities adopt them more readily than foreign "high-tech" alternatives.
  6. Terrain Suitability: Nepal's rugged Himalayan terrain makes large-scale infrastructure difficult. Small-scale appropriate technologies (rope-way cargo transport, trail bridges) are far more practical.

Q3. What are the elements for sustainable development? How can you say indigenous technology is sustainable in case of Nepal? [8 marks] 2074 Bhadra, 2073 Magh

Answer:

Elements of Sustainable Development:

  1. Economic Viability: Development must generate income and improve livelihoods without excessive cost.
  2. Environmental Protection: Natural resources must be used at a rate that allows regeneration; pollution must be minimized.
  3. Social Equity: Benefits must be distributed fairly across all sections of society, including marginalized communities.
  4. Institutional Framework: Good governance, transparent policies, and community participation are essential.

Indigenous Technology as Sustainable in Nepal:

  • Traditional Irrigation (Rajkulo): Gravity-fed canal systems built with local stone and labor — no energy cost, minimal maintenance, and centuries of proven effectiveness in hill farming.
  • Mud-and-Stone Construction: Traditional Newari houses use locally quarried brick, mud mortar, and timber — thermally comfortable, low-carbon, and earthquake-adaptable through flexible timber frames.
  • Dhiki & Janto (grain grinding): Traditional water-powered grain mills require zero fossil fuel, use local craftsmanship, and are still operational in many villages.
  • Terraced Farming: An indigenous land management technology that prevents soil erosion on steep slopes while maximizing cultivable area in the hills.

These technologies satisfy all three pillars of sustainability — they are economically viable (low cost), environmentally sound (zero carbon, local materials), and socially equitable (accessible to all).

1.6 Intermediate Technology, Labor Based & Labor Intensive Technology 2078 Kartik, 2079 Chaitra

Q1. Define labor-based and labor-intensive technology with suitable examples. [4 marks] 2078 Kartik

Answer:

  • Intermediate Technology: Technology suitable for use in developing countries, typically making use of locally available resources. Designed to focus on people rather than machines, it is more harmonious with the environment and traditional ways of life. Examples: Solar powered bulbs, hand weaving machines, pedal power maize sheller.
  • Labor-Based Technology: It is the technology that combines labour with light equipment and tools as necessary, rather than sophisticated machinery, to create cost-effective products. Local labour is preferred wherever applicable, and the quality must meet required standards.
    Example: Road construction using manual labor for earthwork but using a small roller for final surface compaction.
  • Labor-Intensive Technology: It involves a large number of workers to produce products or services. There is high labour cost relative to capital and involves physical and manual jobs. In simple words, it uses comparatively larger amount of labour and small doses of capital.
    Example: Hand-excavation of irrigation canals in rural Nepal where heavy excavators are inaccessible.

1.7 Shifts in Employment Due to Technological Advancement 2073 Bhadra, 2069 Bhadra

Q1. Explain how technology provides shift in employment. [8 marks] 2073 Bhadra, 2069 Bhadra

Answer:

Technological advancement fundamentally restructures the employment landscape by eliminating some jobs, transforming others, and creating entirely new categories of work:

  1. Agricultural to Industrial Shift: The mechanization of agriculture (tractors, harvesters, irrigation pumps) dramatically reduced the need for farm labor. Displaced agricultural workers migrated to cities and were absorbed by factories during the Industrial Revolution.
  2. Industrial to Service Shift: Automation in manufacturing (robots, CNC machines, assembly lines) reduced factory employment. Workers shifted to the service sector — finance, healthcare, education, IT, and retail.
  3. Service to Knowledge/Information Shift: Computer technology and the internet created a new category of "knowledge workers" — software engineers, data analysts, digital marketers, and IT professionals. Manual clerical and administrative jobs were automated by software.
  4. Emerging Trends:
    • Gig Economy: Platforms like Uber, Upwork, and Fiverr enable freelance and contract work, replacing traditional full-time employment.
    • AI Displacement: Artificial intelligence is now automating even intellectual tasks — legal research, medical diagnostics, financial analysis — threatening white-collar employment.
    • New Roles Created: Every technology wave creates new job categories — drone operators, cybersecurity experts, renewable energy technicians, data scientists.
  5. Nepal Context: Nepal has seen rural-to-urban migration as agricultural technology reduces farm labor needs. IT and BPO sectors are emerging as new employers. Foreign employment (Gulf countries, Malaysia) is partly driven by mechanization of local agriculture reducing rural livelihood options.

1.8 Role of Technology to Unmask Old Social Problems & Society's Control of Technology 2076 Bhadra, 2075 Bhadra, 2069 Bhadra

Q1. How does technology unmask old social problems? Justify with suitable examples. [8 marks] 2076 Bhadra

Answer:

Technology is the systematic application of organized knowledge and tools so that a human or a society can adjust to their environment. Different technologies have been invented to meet society's demands. The use of these technologies has helped in revealing and reducing different social problems:

  1. Communication & Information: The development of internet and mobile phones over the post office system has made information flow among the general public quick and easy. Huge amounts of data can be stored in a small chip. This has unmasked problems of misinformation and lack of awareness.
  2. Transportation: The evolution of cars, trains, and planes over wheeled carts has solved accessibility problems. Digital technology in highway traffic management and ropeways in tough geography (like Nepal's hills) have revealed how isolation was a root cause of underdevelopment.
  3. Civil Engineering Problems: Software like CAD, SAP has provided accurate and reliable design over old methods. Excavators, cranes, and dozers have replaced manual labor in road engineering. GPS, GIS, Total Station have replaced plane table and compass surveys — revealing inaccuracies in old surveys.
  4. Health & Education: Treatment machines have solved public health problems. Projectors and digital tools have replaced blackboards and chalks. Infant mortality rates have been drastically reduced, unmasking the scale of preventable deaths that existed before.
  5. Social Crimes: Theft, robbery, and murders have been greatly reduced with advanced surveillance and security technologies. Media like television, newspapers, and internet share social problems that were previously hidden.
  6. Banking & Finance: Computers keep records of transactions instead of papers. ATMs, internet banking, and remittance services have solved financial access problems, especially for rural and migrant populations.

Society's Control of Technology: Technology and its evolution is controlled by society's needs and demands. Society controls technology due to: (1) Economy, (2) Education, (3) Resources/Infrastructures, and (4) Environment.

Q2. How technology can control the price of basic commodities? [8 marks] 2075 Bhadra, 2069 Bhadra

Answer:

Technology helps control prices through multiple mechanisms across the supply chain:

  • Increased Production Efficiency: Mechanized farming, improved seed varieties, and precision agriculture increase crop yields, boosting supply and reducing per-unit costs.
  • Reduced Transportation Costs: Better roads, fuel-efficient vehicles, refrigerated transport, and GPS-optimized logistics reduce the cost of moving goods from producer to consumer.
  • Elimination of Middlemen: E-commerce platforms and mobile apps connect farmers directly to consumers, removing layers of intermediaries who inflate prices.
  • Market Information Systems: Mobile phones and internet-based market price information systems allow farmers and consumers to compare prices, preventing exploitation by traders.
  • Cold Storage & Preservation: Refrigeration and food processing technologies reduce post-harvest losses (which can be 30–40% in Nepal), maintaining supply levels during off-seasons.
  • Renewable Energy: Solar and hydropower reduce dependency on imported fossil fuels, stabilizing energy costs which directly affect the price of manufactured goods.

1.10 Technology Is Irreversible 2078 Kartik

Q1. Justify technology is irreversible. [4 marks] 2078 Kartik

Answer:

Once a technology is introduced and adopted by society, it becomes practically impossible to return to the pre-technology state. As stated in the syllabus: "Technological advancement does not occur in isolation. It modifies society in irreversible and irreparable way."

  • Technology Breeds Technology: Every invention leads to further inventions. The wheel led to carts, then to automobiles, then to self-driving cars. Each advancement triggers the next — there is no going back.
  • Technology Always Upgrades Itself: Newer technology replaces old technology. Computing power of phones has grown exponentially while prices remain similar, making technology more affordable and widespread.
  • Improvement in Productivity: Technology improves productivity and output. Industries, banking systems, communication methods, and purchasing methods have all changed for the better — no one wants to return to the old, slower ways.
  • Life Enhancement: Technology unlocks life that was unimaginable. We now have more than 12 hours of visible light (unlike life before electric lights). Modern medicine, faster travel, and digital banking have permanently changed daily life.
  • New Technology for New Age Problems: Technology is needed because it saves time, is more efficient, is economical, and enhances life. Problems created by old technology require newer technology to solve — not a return to primitive methods.

"Technology has helped humankind reach unremarkable heights. Once underway, its progress is inevitable, unavoidable, and irreversible."

Q2. What do you mean by an Information Society? Differentiate it with agricultural and industrial society. [8 marks] 2076 Bhadra, 2069 Bhadra

Answer:

Information Society is a society where the creation, distribution, and manipulation of information is the most significant economic, political, and cultural activity. The primary wealth-generating resource is knowledge and data rather than land (agricultural) or machinery (industrial).

Key Differences:

  • In an agricultural society, wealth is measured by land ownership, and power is held by landlords and monarchs.
  • In an industrial society, wealth is measured by capital and factory ownership, and power shifts to industrialists and corporations.
  • In an information society, wealth is measured by knowledge, data, and intellectual property, and power is held by those who control information flow (tech companies, media, governments with surveillance capability).

The transition from agricultural → industrial → information society represents a fundamental shift in what is the most valued resource at each stage of civilization.

1.12 Characteristics of Information Society 2072 Magh, 2071 Magh, 2073 Bhadra, 2070 Magh

Q1. What do you understand by information society? Explain with characteristics. [7–8 marks] 2072 Magh, 2071 Magh, 2073 Bhadra

Answer:

Definition: Information society means social and economic structures where productive usage of resources such as information and knowledge-intensive production perform a prominent role, and where individuals (consumers, workers) use information extensively (OECD, 1994). The aim of information society is to gain competitive advantage internationally through using Information Technology (IT) in a creative and productive way.

Characteristics of Information Society:

  1. Information as an Economic Resource: Information is used as an economic resource. Organizations make greater use of information to increase their efficiency and competitiveness.
  2. Greater Use Among General Public: It is possible to identify greater use of information among the general public for daily activities.
  3. Development of Information Sector: An information sector develops within the economy — including manufacturing, telecommunications, printing, mass media, advertising, accounting, education, finance, and insurance.
  4. Focus on Better Education: Society places emphasis on education and continuous learning as the foundation for participating in the information economy.
  5. Information as Wealth: Information is wealth — like capital was in the industrial society. The new source of power is not money in the hands of a few, but information in the hands of many.
  6. Remote Communication: Telecommunication and internet allow instant communication across the globe.
  7. Distribution of Global Information Networks: The internet and networks enable worldwide information sharing and collaboration.
  8. Easy Access to Vast Information: Internet provides easy access to enormous amounts of information, though information cannot always be reliable.
  9. Robots Doing Skilled and Unskilled Jobs: Automation replaces human labor in both skilled and unskilled tasks.
  10. Knowledge Theory of Value: Marx's theory of labor value has been replaced by knowledge theory of value — intellectual contribution matters more than physical labor.
  11. New Possibilities for Interaction: Technology creates new possibilities for public interaction and opinion expression (social media, forums, online platforms).

Challenges of Information Society:

  • Information age is not absolute — not all parts of the world have equal access.
  • Erosion in employment opportunities due to computers and robots.
  • Challenge to make people computer literate.
  • Challenge to control computer-related theft and fraud.
  • Challenge to implement rules and regulations for information society.

Q2. Discuss the impacts of computer and cybernetics in creating information society that makes the whole world as a global village. [8 marks] 2070 Magh

Answer:

  • Computers made it possible to store, process, and transmit vast amounts of data at unprecedented speeds. This enabled electronic banking, e-commerce, digital communication, and online governance.
  • Cybernetics (the science of control and communication in complex systems) enabled automated feedback loops — thermostats, autopilot systems, automated manufacturing, and algorithmic decision-making.
  • The Internet (a product of both computer science and cybernetics) created a single global communication platform, enabling:
    • Instant messaging and video calls across continents
    • Real-time global news coverage
    • Global supply chain management
    • International collaboration in research (e.g., the CERN Large Hadron Collider project)
  • Global Village Concept: Marshall McLuhan proposed that electronic communication would contract the world into a "village" where everyone is aware of and affected by events everywhere. Social media, 24/7 news channels, and instant messaging have made this a reality — a political event in the Middle East affects oil prices in Nepal within hours.
