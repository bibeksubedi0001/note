3.1 Early Civilization 2070

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

  1. State the different ages of human development. [3] (2070 Magh, Q2)

The history of early human civilization can be divided into the following ages:

A. Stone Age (2.5 million years ago – 3500 BC)

  • Age prior to the beginning of civilized society, began in Africa.
  • Began with use of stones as weapons along with wood, animal bone, and horn.
  • Nomadic culture: Humans moved from place to place searching for food.
  • Cereal cultivation and animal domestication began.
  • At the end, land gradually evolved to towns and finally into cities.

B. Bronze Age (3500 BC – 1100 BC)

  • First civilization in Mesopotamia.
  • Discovery of copper and alloyed with tin to get bronze.
  • Many civilizations flourished: Egyptian, Sumerian, Maya, Assyrian.
  • Civilizations in China and India were developed.
  • Spreading of Aryans from central Europe to Asia.
  • Navigation technology was developed.
  • Invention of first writing system by Sumerians and picture writing system by Egyptians.
  • Old Babylonian and Akkadian empires were developed.
  • Constructions of temples, tombs, war chariots.

C. Iron Age (1100 BC – 500 AD)

  • Main ingredient of weapons was iron.
  • Historical figures: Gautama Buddha (550 BC), Confucius and Lao Tzu in China (6th century BC), Emperor Ashoka, Jesus Christ.
  • Victory of Aryans over the whole ancient world.
  • Development of iron technology.
  • Beginning of Science: gathering information, analyzing, and solving problems.
  • Greek and Roman civilizations were developed.
  • Greek philosophers: Plato, Aristotle, Socrates.

D. Middle Age (500 AD – 1450 AD)

  • Beginning of Islamic religion; Prophet Muhammad dictated the Koran.
  • Islamic invasion took place.
  • Vikings invasion (~700 AD).
  • Mongolian conquest: Genghis Khan (1200 AD) conquered China, Turkmenistan, Persia, Armenia, parts of India.
  • Revival of European intelligence.
  • Commercial and industrial activities developed; growth of cities and trading.
  • Silk Road linked Asia and Europe for trading.
  • Universities at Paris, Oxford, Bologna were established.
  • Exploration: Marco Polo travelled throughout Asia.
  • Inventions: windmills, spectacles, compass, spinning wheel.

E. Towards the Modern World (1450 AD – 1660 AD)

  • Birth of scientists: Leonardo da Vinci, Galileo, Newton, Robert Hooke.
  • Columbus discovered America in 1492 AD.
  • Age of mechanical revolution and technological development.
  • Advances in education throughout the westernized world.
  • Inventions: sawmill, microscope, telescope, clocks.

3.2 Great Renaissance of Europe (1300s–1600s) 20772071

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

  1. Explain the role played by Renaissance period in the development of science and technology. [3] (2077 Chaitra, Q3a)
  2. Describe important features of Renaissance Period. [4] (2071 Magh, Q4)

Renaissance means "re-birth" in French. It was the time period following the Middle Ages that lasted from the 1300s to 1600s. As the renowned Rome fell and the feudal structure came and went, a new period emerged known as the renewal of arts — "The Renaissance." There was a renewed interest in learning about ancient Greeks and Romans. The Renaissance had its beginnings in Italy.

Key Features:

  1. Geography: Italy's Venice and Genoa were major trade centres of both physical products and ideas. Money earned from trade financed Italy's research and exposure to science and arts. Adequate food production gave citizens more time for science and art.
  2. Science: Technology developed significantly — telescope and printing press (developed by Johann Gutenberg from Germany). People started to divide science and religion. Nicolas Copernicus proved Earth was not the centre of the solar system. Galileo Galilei was sentenced to house arrest for confirming Earth revolves around the Sun.
  3. Music: Distinguished by graceful flowing melodies written solely for voice. It was the golden period of Acapella style.
  4. Arts: Many artists preferred nude style of painting and sculpting. Famous works: David by Michelangelo (1501–1504), Sistine Chapel, Leonardo da Vinci's Mona Lisa. Leonardo was also an inventor, designer, engineer, and scientist — first to design helicopter, tank, parachute, and flying machine.
  5. Politics: Run by ideology of despotism. City-states (Venice, Genoa, Milan, Florence, Lucca, Pisa) were self-governing and self-sufficient. They became wealthy through trade, commerce, and capitalism. Italy later declined when Ottomans took over the Byzantine Empire, cutting trade with Asia.
  6. Philosophy: Humanism originated during Renaissance. Humanists were attentive to goals about being human rather than religious matters. Francesco Petrarch was one of the first Humanists. Great value was placed on education — artists, architects, leaders, writers, and scientists were greatly admired.

Role of Renaissance in Development of Science & Technology: The Renaissance directly paved the way for modern science and technology by: (a) reviving the scientific method of observation and experimentation (Galileo, Copernicus), replacing medieval reliance on religious authority; (b) inventing transformative technologies — Gutenberg’s printing press (c. 1440) democratized knowledge, the telescope enabled astronomical discovery, and Leonardo da Vinci’s engineering designs (helicopter, tank, parachute) anticipated modern machines by centuries; (c) creating an intellectual culture that valued inquiry, education, and innovation — the foundation on which the later Scientific Revolution and Industrial Revolution were built.

3.3 Early Part of Industrial Revolution (1660–1815) 2080207920732070

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

  1. Define industrial revolution. [2] (2079 Shrawan, Q3)
  2. Describe briefly the impact of early part of industrial revolution. [3] (2070 Bhadra, Q1)
  3. Define industrial revolution. Describe the environmental consequences industrial revolution. [1+3] (2080 Chaitra, Q4a)
  4. Describe the major changes the industrial Revolution brought to human civilization. [8] (2073 Magh, Q4)

The Industrial Revolution was the transition to new manufacturing processes in the period from about 1660 to 1815. The transition included going from hand production methods to machines, new chemical manufacturing and iron production processes, the increasing use of steam power, the development of machine tools, and the rise of the factory system. It began in towns across UK and USA where a series of astonishing inventions transformed the method people lived and worked.

3.3.1 Impacts of the Industrial Revolution 2070

  1. Population Increase: There was a simultaneous increase in both population and per capita income. Europe's population increased from about 100 million in 1700 to 400 million by 1900.
  2. Urbanization: Shift of population from rural to urban residency. Growth of modern industry since late 18th century led to massive urbanization and the rise of new great cities.
  3. Impact on Women & Family Life: Capitalism lowered the status of women. Middle and upper-class women were confined to domestic life; lower-class women were forced to take poorly-paid jobs.
  4. Child Labour: Limited opportunity for education; children were expected to work. Employers paid children less than adults despite comparable productivity. In 1788 England and Scotland, two-thirds of workers in cotton mills were children.

3.3.2 Environmental Consequences 2080

  1. Air Pollution: Factories and combustion of fossil fuels (coal for steam engines) released gases and toxins into the atmosphere.
  2. Water Pollution: Industrial waste, oil spills, acid rain, and urban runoff contaminated water bodies.
  3. Soil Pollution: Industrial waste deprived soil of essential nutrients.
  4. Deforestation: Demand for fuel (charcoal, wood) and land for factories led to clearing of forests, disrupting ecosystems and natural carbon sinks.
  5. Resource Depletion: More mines were discovered for coal and iron for steam engines, causing significant environmental damage.
  6. Global Warming: Increased emission of greenhouse gases (CO2) from burning fossil fuels began the long-term trend of rising global temperatures.

3.3.3 Important Technological Developments 2073

  1. Textiles: Mechanized cotton spinning powered by steam or water increased worker output. James Hargreaves invented the 'spinning jenny' (~1764) enabling production of multiple spools simultaneously. The power loom was developed in the 1780s. One machine could do the work of 50 people.
  2. Steam Power: Switch from water to steam power sped up manufacturing, transportation, and farming. Steam engines were used in railroads and steamboats. More mines were discovered for coal and iron.
  3. Iron Making: Substitution of coke for charcoal lowered the fuel cost of pig iron and wrought iron production. Cast iron blowing cylinder first used in 1760.
  4. Machine Tools: First machine tools included screw-cutting lathe, cylinder boring machine, and milling machine. Made economical manufacture of precision metal parts possible.
  5. Other Areas: Chemicals, agriculture, paper machines, mining, railways and roads, canals, cement.

3.3.4 Industrial Revolution in America

  • Samuel Slater memorized English machinery designs, moved to USA, and opened a spinning mill in 1790.
  • In 1814, copies of industrial power looms were produced.
  • Eli Whitney invented the cotton gin in 1793, making cotton farming the backbone of Southern US wealth.

3.3.5 Impacts in Nepal

  • Nepal had a rich tradition of handmade textiles (rugs, bags, blankets, pashmina, embroidery).
  • Industrialization led to mass-produced goods at lower cost, reducing demand for handmade items.
  • Nepal was largely isolated but still affected — handmade textiles faced competition from power looms.
  • The country was late to begin its own industrialization, making competition with nations like China difficult.

3.4 Transformation of Industrial Society into Information Society 2081207820712069

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

  1. Discuss in brief about the transition phase of industrial to information society in terms of challenges and opportunities. [4] (2081 Chaitra, Q3a)
  2. Discuss in brief on transformation of industrial society to information society. [4] (2078 Chaitra, Q3a)
  3. Describe in brief an how industrial society transformed to information society. [4] (2078 Kartik, Q3)
  4. Discuss about the transformation from industrial to information society. [Part of 8] (2069 Bhadra, Q1)
  5. How industrial societies enter into information society? [5] (2071 Magh, Q3)

Industrial Society is characterized by the maximum use of mechanical machines. Information Society is the society after the discovery of the computer, where the creation, distribution, and manipulation of information has become the most significant economic and cultural activity.

The transformation occurred through the Digital Revolution (also known as the Third Industrial Revolution) — the shift from mechanical and analogue electronic technology to digital electronics. It began in the late 1950s–1970s with the adoption of digital computers and digital record-keeping.

3.4.1 Key Developments in the Transformation 20782069

  • Telephone, computer, television, and internet merged to form an information and communication system.
  • Digital technology made it possible to create identical copies and distribute information remotely.
  • Computers achieved semi-ubiquity in schools, homes, businesses, and industry.
  • Technologies introduced: ATMs, industrial robots, CGI, electronic music, bulletin boards.
  • Motorola created the first mobile phone (DynaTac) in 1983.
  • The first true digital camera was created in 1988.
  • Tim Berners-Lee invented the World Wide Web in 1989.

3.4.2 Advantages of Information Society 2071

  • Industrial Efficiency: Programmable machines, automation, early warning systems.
  • Medicine: 3D surgical modelling, preplanning, X-ray, MRI, CT-Scan.
  • Quick Response: Ambulance, fire brigade; disaster communication; crime reporting.
  • Remote Meeting: Group calls over internet, screen sharing.
  • Scientific Research: Easier information sharing for research.
  • Long Range Communication: Satellite and orbiter for inter-planetary information sharing.

3.4.3 Challenges of the Transition 2081

  • Job Displacement: Automation and computerization replaced many manual and manufacturing jobs, causing unemployment in traditional industries.
  • Digital Divide: Unequal access to technology between developed and developing nations, and between urban and rural communities.
  • Skill Gap: Workers trained for industrial-era tasks needed retraining for information-era jobs requiring digital literacy and specialized knowledge.
  • Privacy & Security Concerns: Digital information is vulnerable to hacking, cyber crimes, and misuse. Data security became a major challenge.
  • Cultural Disruption: Rapid technological change disrupted traditional social structures, family dynamics, and cultural practices.

3.4.4 Opportunities of the Transition 2081

  • Industrial Efficiency: Programmable machines, automation, and early warning systems greatly improved productivity and safety.
  • Medicine & Healthcare: 3D surgical modelling, X-ray, MRI, CT-Scan, and telemedicine revolutionized healthcare delivery.
  • Global Communication: Internet, mobile phones, and the World Wide Web enabled instant information sharing across the globe.
  • Quick Response Systems: Emergency services (ambulance, fire brigade), disaster communication, and crime reporting became faster and more effective.
  • Scientific Research: Easier information sharing, digital libraries, and computational tools accelerated the pace of research and innovation.
  • Remote Work & Education: Video conferencing, screen sharing, and digital platforms enabled remote meetings, online education, and global collaboration.

3.5 Impact of World War I and II, Population Explosion 20742070

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

  1. Discuss about world war I and world war II along with the positive and negative impacts. [8] (2074 Magh, Q3)
  2. Describe the impact of World War I and II in our society. [5] (2070 Bhadra, Q1)
  3. Write the key developments on technology during first and Second World War and their positive and negative impact on population. [5] (2070 Magh, Q2)

3.5.1 World War I (1914–1919) 20742070

Causes: Alliances, Imperialism, Militarism, Nationalism (AMIN)

Key Events: Assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand (June 28, 1914) → Austria declares war on Serbia → Russia mobilizes → Germany declares war on France → Britain enters war → Conflict spreads worldwide → Treaty of Versailles (June 28, 1919).

3.5.2 World War II (1939–1945) 20742070

Causes: Treaty of Versailles, Hitler's imperialism, Mussolini's fascism, Japan's conquest of China, Militarism, Racism.

Key Events: Germany invades Poland (Sep 1, 1939) → Britain/France declare war → Japan attacks Pearl Harbor (Dec 7, 1941) → Hitler suicide (Apr 30, 1945) → Hiroshima (Aug 6, 1945) → Nagasaki (Aug 9, 1945).

3.5.3 Positive and Negative Impacts 20742070

Positive Impacts:

  • End of dictatorship, colonialism, and imperialism
  • Strengthening of nationalist movements in Africa and Asia
  • UNO established (Oct 24, 1945) for world peace
  • Technological advancements: aircraft, submarines, computers
  • Greater access to education; women given opportunity to work and vote
  • African Americans granted jobs in cavalry, infantry, medicine, engineering
  • IMF established to prevent economic depression
  • Growth of trade unions

Negative Impacts:

  • WWI: 18 million deaths, 23 million injured; 60% world economy devastated
  • WWII: 50 million deaths (22M soldiers, 28M civilians)
  • 70% of industry collapsed; widespread unemployment
  • 4 major empires disappeared (German, Austro-Hungarian, Ottoman, Russian)
  • Germany divided; Cold War tensions between USA and USSR
  • Nuclear weapons introduced — lasting threat to humanity

3.5.4 Key Technological Developments 2070

  • WWI: Machine guns, tanks, poison gas, aircraft for warfare, submarines (U-boats)
  • WWII: Advanced aircraft and bombers, radar, jet engines, nuclear weapons (atomic bombs), advanced computers for code-breaking, rockets (V-2), improved submarines

Positive Impacts on Population:

  • Medical advances (penicillin mass production, blood transfusion techniques)
  • Technological progress later applied to civilian uses (jet aviation, computing, nuclear energy)
  • Increased women's participation in workforce and economic activities
  • Greater access to education after wars ended

Negative Impacts on Population:

  • Massive casualties: 18M (WWI) + 50M (WWII) deaths
  • Nuclear weapons — Hiroshima (Aug 6, 1945) and Nagasaki (Aug 9, 1945) killed hundreds of thousands
  • Chemical weapons caused lasting health effects
  • Displacement and refugee crises across Europe and Asia
  • Psychological trauma on surviving populations

3.5.5 Population Explosion

The industrial revolution and world wars led to significant population growth. Europe's population grew from 100 million (1700) to 400 million (1900). Post-WWII advances in medicine, agriculture, and sanitation caused rapid global population growth, leading to increased demand for resources, urbanization, food security challenges, and environmental strain.

3.6 Rise of Environmental Issues

The Industrial Revolution and subsequent rapid industrialization led to a wide range of environmental issues. Major environmental issues include:

A. Pollution:

  • Industry, factories, and motor vehicles are major culprits.
  • Toxins: heavy metals, nitrates, and plastics.
  • Air Pollution: Gases and toxins from industries, factories, and fossil fuel combustion.
  • Water Pollution: Oil spills, acid rain, and urban runoff.
  • Soil Pollution: Industrial waste depriving soil of essential nutrients.

B. Global Warming:

  • Result of emission of greenhouse gases: CO2, CH4, N2O.
  • Rise in global temperature causing melting of polar ice caps.
  • Rise in sea level.
  • Unnatural precipitation patterns: flash floods, excessive snow, desertification.
  • Increase in frequency of category 4 & 5 storms (every 1°C increase in sea surface temperature = 31% increase in strong storm frequency).
  • Retreat of glaciers, permafrost, and sea ice.
  • Ocean acidification and species extinction.
  • Migration of flora and fauna.

C. Overpopulation:

  • Shortage of resources: water, fuel, and food.
  • Intensive agriculture damages the environment through chemical fertilizers, pesticides, insecticides.

D. Natural Resource Depletion:

  • Fossil fuel consumption results in greenhouse gas emissions.

E. Waste Disposal:

  • Over-consumption and plastics creating a global waste disposal crisis.
  • Developed countries dump waste in oceans and less developed countries.
  • Nuclear waste disposal has tremendous health hazards.

F. Loss of Biodiversity:

  • Ecosystems perfected over millions of years are endangered.
  • Human activity threatens crucial processes like pollination.
  • Destruction of coral reefs supporting marine life.

G. Deforestation:

  • Forests are natural carbon dioxide sinks, produce oxygen, regulate temperature and rainfall.
  • Deforestation leads to: destruction of animal shelters, untimely rainfall, drought, ecosystem imbalance.

H. Ocean Acidification:

  • Ongoing decrease in pH caused by uptake of CO2 from atmosphere.
  • Depresses metabolic rates and immune responses of marine organisms.
  • Causes coral bleaching.

I. Ozone Layer Depletion:

  • Attributed to chlorine and bromine found in CFCs.
  • Human impacts: skin ailments, increased risk of skin cancer, eye damage, immune system damage, respiratory diseases.
  • Affects all stages of life cycle of amphibians.
  • Affects physiological processes such as flowering time of plants.

J. Acid Rain:

  • Caused by mixing of SO2 and NO2 with water molecules in atmosphere.
  • Serious effects on human health, wildlife, and aquatic species.
  • Causes slow growth, injury, or death of forests.
  • Washes away soil nutrients and releases aluminium, making water absorption difficult for plants.

K. Water Pollution:

  • Industrial development polluting rivers, seas, and oceans with toxins.
  • Clean drinking water becoming a rare commodity.
  • Water becoming an economic and political issue.

L. Urban Sprawl:

  • Results in land degradation, increased traffic, environmental and health issues.
  • Growing demand for land displaces natural flora and fauna.

M. Genetic Engineering:

  • Genetic modification of food results in increased toxins and diseases.
  • Engineered genes may prove toxic to wildlife.
  • Increased use of toxins for insect-resistant plants can create antibiotic-resistant organisms.
  • Can produce unforeseen allergens in food.

3.7 Climate Change as a Threat to Human Civilization 2079

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

  1. Justify with suitable examples on how climate change becomes threat to human civilization. [4] (2079 Chaitra, Q3a)
  2. “Climate change is a Threat to Human Civilization!!”. Justify it. [5] (2079 Jestha, Q3)
  3. Justify, that climate change has become a great threat to human civilization. [6] (2079 Shrawan, Q3)

Climate change has become a serious threat to human civilization. Justification with examples:

  1. Widespread Drought: Greenhouse gas emissions increase air temperatures, increasing evaporation. Example: prolonged droughts in Sub-Saharan Africa affecting millions.
  2. Infectious Diseases: Rising temperatures create ideal conditions for virus replication. Example: Dengue fever spread increased 3–5.9% globally compared to 1990 levels.
  3. Food Insecurity: Higher CO2 affects vegetation growth; extreme weather disrupts food production and distribution. Example: crop failures from flooding and drought.
  4. Extreme Weather Events: Increased flooding, storms, heat waves. Example: every 1°C increase in sea temperature = 31% increase in strong storm frequency. Vulnerable populations (children, elderly, poor) are disproportionately affected.
  5. Rising Sea Levels: Melting polar ice caps threaten coastal cities. Example: White Chuck Glacier retreated 1.9 km in 30 years.
  6. Health Impacts: Ground-level ozone increase causes asthma and respiratory diseases; mental health impacts (PTSD, depression) from disasters.
