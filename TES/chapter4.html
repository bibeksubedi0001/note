4.1 Definition of Environment 2073 Magh, 2070 Magh

Q1. Define environment and ecosystem. [2 marks] 2073 Magh

Answer:

Environment: The surroundings of all living and non-living things constitute an environment. It is defined as "the surroundings in which an organization operates including air, water, land and natural resources, flora, fauna, humans and their inter-relations" (ISO Definition). The word is derived from the French word "environ" meaning "encompass" or "encircle." It was introduced by German biologist Jacob Von Uexkull in the early 1900s.

Ecosystem: An ecosystem is a grouping of organisms that interact with each other and their environment in such a way as to preserve the grouping. It includes both biotic (living) and abiotic (non-living) components functioning together as a unit.

Q2. Define environment and ecosystem. [2 marks] 2070 Magh

Answer:

Environment: Environment refers to all external conditions and influences affecting the life, development, and survival of organisms. It comprises the hydrosphere (water), atmosphere (air), lithosphere (land), biosphere (flora/fauna/microbes), and anthrosphere (man-made things).

Ecosystem: An ecosystem is a biological community of interacting organisms and their physical environment. It is characterized by nutrient cycling, energy flow, and structural inter-relationships between organisms. There is a great variety of ecosystems, all characterized by general structural and functional attributes.

Components of Environment:

  1. Hydrosphere — Water
  2. Atmosphere — Air
  3. Lithosphere — Land
  4. Biosphere — Flora, Fauna, Microbes
  5. Anthrosphere — Man-made things

Components can also be classified as:

  • Biotic Components (living): Producers (autotrophs — plants), Consumers (heterotrophs — animals), Decomposers (detritivores — fungi, bacteria)
  • Abiotic Components (non-living): Climatic (temperature, light, moisture, air currents) and Edaphic (soil-related factors)

4.2 Importance, Ecology & Ecosystem 2070 Bhadra, 2074 Bhadra, 2072 Ashwin, 2071 Magh, 2069 Bhadra

Q1. Define ecosystem and ecology. How does increasing population disturb the natural ecosystem and how to minimize the disturbance? [2+3 marks] 2070 Bhadra

Answer:

Ecology: Ecology is the branch of biology concerned with the relations between organisms and their environment. It is the scientific study of interactions between different organisms and between organisms and their surroundings.

Ecosystem: An ecosystem is a grouping of organisms that interact with each other and their environment in such a way as to preserve the grouping. It operates through three major principles: nutrient cycling, energy flow, and structure.

How increasing population disturbs the ecosystem:

  • Excessive use of fossil fuels leading to greenhouse gas emissions and global warming
  • Deforestation for agricultural land and settlements — destroying carbon sinks and habitats
  • Overfishing and overgrazing depleting natural resources
  • Industrial waste contaminating water, air, and soil
  • Loss of biodiversity — extinction of species due to habitat destruction
  • Excessive use of agrochemicals (fertilizers, pesticides) damaging soil and water

How to minimize the disturbance:

  • Conservation of natural resources through sustainable management
  • Use of renewable energy sources (solar, hydro, wind, geothermal)
  • Awareness programs about environmental conservation
  • Enacting and enforcing environmental protection policies
  • Reducing, reusing, and recycling (3 R's)
  • Forestation and protection of biodiversity

Q2. What are the primary reasons for human interventions in the ecosystem? [8 marks] 2074 Bhadra

Answer:

Human interventions in the ecosystem are driven by the following primary reasons:

  1. Population Growth: Increasing population demands more food, shelter, and resources, leading to exploitation of natural ecosystems — deforestation, overfishing, and land conversion for agriculture.
  2. Economic Development: Industrialization, urbanization, and infrastructure development require clearing of forests, mining of minerals, and damming of rivers, all of which disrupt ecosystems.
  3. Energy Needs: Dependence on fossil fuels (coal, petroleum, natural gas) for energy leads to resource depletion, air pollution, and greenhouse gas emissions causing global warming.
  4. Agricultural Expansion: Intensive agriculture uses chemical fertilizers, pesticides, and insecticides that degrade soil quality and contaminate water bodies, disrupting nutrient cycling.
  5. Resource Extraction: Mining of minerals and fossil fuels, logging, and sand extraction cause habitat destruction, soil erosion, and water pollution.
  6. Urbanization: Expansion of cities leads to land degradation, displacement of flora and fauna, increased waste generation, and air/water pollution.
  7. Technological Advancement: While technology improves living standards, industrial emissions, electronic waste, and chemical pollutants disrupt natural processes.
  8. Consumption Patterns: Unsustainable consumption and waste generation — particularly plastics — create pollution that harms marine and terrestrial ecosystems.

Impact on Ecosystem: These interventions disrupt nutrient cycling, energy flow, and structural relationships within ecosystems, leading to pollution, global warming, biodiversity loss, ocean acidification, ozone depletion, and climate change.

Q3. What are the primary reasons for human interventions in the ecosystem? How are natural resources of the ecosystem converted into wealth? [8 marks] 2072 Ashwin

Answer:

Primary reasons for human interventions (as above): population growth, economic development, energy needs, agricultural expansion, resource extraction, urbanization, technological advancement, and unsustainable consumption patterns.

How natural resources are converted into wealth:

  1. Agriculture: Soil, water, and sunlight used to grow crops and raise livestock — the backbone of national economies, especially in developing countries like Nepal.
  2. Energy Generation: Fossil fuels (coal, petroleum, natural gas) and renewable sources (hydropower, solar, wind) converted into electricity and fuel for industrial production and transportation.
  3. Mining & Extraction: Minerals, metals, and building materials (sand, stone) extracted and processed into construction materials, machinery, and consumer products.
  4. Forestry: Timber used for construction, furniture, and paper. Non-timber forest products (medicinal plants, herbs) collected and traded.
  5. Water Resources: Used for irrigation, hydropower generation, fisheries, and domestic/industrial consumption.
  6. Tourism: Natural landscapes, national parks, and biodiversity attract tourism revenue.

Key principle: Natural resources are the backbone of national economy. However, the current depletion rate risks the needs of future generations. The goal should be optimum utilization — satisfying human needs at minimum cost on a sustainable basis.

Q4. Write notes on: Ecology and Ecosystem. [4 marks] 2071 Magh

Answer:

Ecology: The branch of biology concerned with the relations between organisms and their environment. It spans micro-level (cells) to planetary scale (biosphere). Ecosystems are dynamic — they do not follow a linear path but are always changing.

Ecosystem: A grouping of organisms interacting with each other and their environment. Three major principles:

  1. Nutrient Cycling: Movement of chemical elements from the environment into living organisms and back — organisms live, grow, die, and decompose.
  2. Energy Flow: Energy transforms inorganic nutrients into organic tissues; the driving force of the ecosystem.
  3. Structure: The pattern of inter-relationships between organisms.

Components:

  • Abiotic: Sunlight, temperature, radiation, nutrients, rainfall, minerals
  • Biotic: Producers (autotrophs — plants using photosynthesis), Consumers (herbivores, carnivores, omnivores), Decomposers (bacteria, fungi — breaking compounds into simpler forms for reuse)

Linkages: Carbon-oxygen cycle (circulation of carbon and oxygen), Nitrogen cycle (absorption by plants, consumed by animals, returned to soil through decomposition). Nutrients are circulated and recycled continually.

Importance: Environmental conservation, resource allocation, eco-friendliness, organism ecology, community of organisms, well-balanced ecosystem.

Q5. Write short notes on: Ecology, ecosystem and its characteristics. [4 marks] 2069 Bhadra

Answer:

Ecology: Scientific study of interactions between organisms and their environment. Scope ranges from micro-level (cells) to planetary scale (biosphere).

Ecosystem: A biological community of interacting organisms and their physical environment functioning as a unit.

Characteristics of Ecosystem:

  • Dynamic nature: Ecosystems are always changing — sometimes rapidly, sometimes over thousands of years.
  • Nutrient Cycling: Chemical elements move from environment to organisms and back through life, death, and decomposition.
  • Energy Flow: Energy drives transformation of inorganic nutrients into organic tissues.
  • Structural relationships: Specific patterns of inter-relationships (food chains, food webs).
  • Holocoenotic environment: All factors are interconnected — if one factor changes, almost all others will change eventually.
  • Limiting factors: Light, temperature, water, wind, topography, soil, and biotic factors determine ecosystem development.
  • Variable scale: Ecosystem area can vary from tiny to vast.

4.3 Conservation of Environment

Definition: Conservation is an ethic of resource use, allocation, and protection. Its primary focus is upon maintaining the health of the natural world: its fisheries, habitats, and biological diversity. Secondary focus includes materials conservation and energy conservation. Environment conservation is the wise management and utilization of natural resources in a sustainable manner to ensure the maintenance of biodiversity.

Types of Environmental Conservation:

  1. Energy Conservation: Efforts to reduce energy consumption and preserve it for future generations. Achieved through increased efficient energy use, decreased consumption, and use of alternative energy sources (renewable resources).
  2. Water Conservation: Reducing the usage of water and recycling wastewater for different purposes (cleaning, manufacturing, agriculture). Goals include sustainability (ensuring availability for future generations) and energy conservation (reducing energy for water pumping and treatment).
  3. Soil Conservation: Management strategies for prevention of soil erosion from the earth's surface or becoming chemically altered by overuse, acidification, salinization, or other chemical contamination.
  4. Plant Conservation: Study of plant decline and its causes, and techniques to conserve rare and endangered plants. Subfields include: forest conservation, wild plant conservation, invasive species study and control, medicinal plant conservation.

Why Conservation?

  • Limited resources available on earth
  • Resources are expensive
  • Demand is continuously increasing
  • Countries face power shortages
  • Non-renewable resources may be exhausted — need to switch to alternative energy
  • For healthy environment and survival of living beings

How to Conserve:

  • Cleaner Transport: Use environment-friendly transportation; walk/cycle for short distances; check vehicles regularly.
  • Energy-Saving Features: Install energy-saving lamps; choose energy-efficient appliances; use biodegradable cleaning products.
  • Energy-Saving Habits: Turn off unused electrical appliances; use both sides of paper.
  • Water Conservation: Turn off taps while brushing/shaving; shorten showering; run full loads in washing machines; fix dripping taps.
  • Minimize Environmental Footprint: Reduce ecological, carbon, and water footprints.
  • The 3 R's: Reduce, Reuse, Recycle.
  • Other: Throw trash in bins; plant trees; use green power; close doors while using A/C; awareness programs; enact environmental policies.

4.4 Optimum Utilization of Natural Resources 2079 Jestha

Q1. What do you understand by optimum utilization of natural resources for environment conservation? [3 marks] 2079 Jestha

Answer:

Concept: Natural resources are the backbone of national economy. The current depletion rate of natural resources risks the needs of future generations. Optimum utilization means the allocation of natural resources so as to make the best use of the environment in satisfying human needs at minimum cost, on a sustainable basis.

Objectives of Optimum Utilization:

  1. Conservation, development, and sustainable management of natural resources and their utilization techniques.
  2. Restoration of ecological balance in degraded and fragile ecosystems by trees and grasses.
  3. Creation of sustained employment opportunities for rural communities.
  4. Reduction in the global use of non-renewable resources by developing different alternatives.
  5. Pollution control through policy formulation and implementation.

Types of Resources:

  • Perpetual resources: Virtually inexhaustible for human consumption (e.g., solar energy, wind).
  • Renewable resources: Available over a period of time; can be produced by human involvement (e.g., forests, fisheries).
  • Non-renewable resources: Present in fixed amounts; get exhausted after certain time. Some can be recycled or reused. More focus must be given to proper use of renewable and non-renewable resources.

4.5 Renewable and Non-Renewable Resources 2079 Jestha, 2071 Bhadra

Q1. What are the renewable and non-renewable energy resources? [3 marks] 2079 Jestha

Answer:

Renewable Resources: A natural resource that is not depleted upon consumption or can be reproduced in a short period of time. Examples:

  1. Bio-fuel: Fuel made from living things (plants, algae). Plants like corn and switch grass are used to produce bio-ethanol. Bio-diesel is made from vegetable oils and animal fat. Burns cleaner and pollutes less.
  2. Geothermal Energy: Uses temperature of the ground to generate power or heat/cool buildings. Water pumped through pipes absorbs ground heat (~55°F). Large plants extract hot water and convert it to steam for electricity.
  3. Solar Energy: Photoelectric (PV) panels absorb sunlight, exciting electrons to create electric flow. Can be stored in batteries. Becoming more affordable.
  4. Hydro Energy: Flow of water spins turbines connected to generators. Typically requires a dam to create pressure. Water is directed through a pen-stock.

Non-Renewable Resources: Natural resources that exist in finite quantities and cannot be reproduced at a scale comparable to consumption. Higher risk of exhaustion. Less environmentally friendly.

  1. Coal: Rock primarily of carbon; formed by compressing plant matter over thousands of years. Leading fuel source but produces about double the pollution of natural gas.
  2. Petroleum Oil: Refined into gasoline and jet fuel. Used for heating, asphalt, and plastics. World consumes ~90 million barrels per day.
  3. Natural Gas: Found deep underground in rocky areas or where organic matter has been buried. Used for cooking, heating, and power generation. Takes thousands of years to form.

4.6 Conflict of Resources 2078 Chaitra, 2078 Kartik

Q1. Describe about conflict of resources with suitable examples. [4 marks] 2078 Chaitra

Answer:

Conflict of Resources is the situation where the allocation, management, or use of natural resources results in violence, human rights abuses, or denial of access to resources that significantly diminishes human welfare.

Key Facts:

  • Natural resources are the centre of development for any country and a reason for conflicts among and within countries.
  • About 40% of conflicts among countries are due to conflict over ownership of resources.

Causes of Resource Conflicts:

  1. Rapid population growth resulting in environmental degradation and resource scarcity
  2. Social inequality resulting in skewed access to natural resources and services
  3. Failure of governing bodies to address the issue
  4. Unsustainable consumption
  5. Economic globalization

Examples:

  • Minerals: Diamonds in western Africa, amber in Russia, gold in Indonesia. Africa has been plagued by diamond-related conflicts. All minerals generate revenue and power for governments or rebel groups.
  • In Nepal: Conflicts over land, water, and forests are dominant:
    • Land conflicts: Boundary/demarcation disputes, change in ownership rights, tenant eviction, development on particular sites, obstruction of paths.
    • Forest conflicts: Ownership disputes, identification of users, access to forest products, illegal collection, hunting and poaching of wild animals, collection of medicinal plants by commercial traders.
    • Water conflicts: Source disputes, sharing of drinking water and irrigation water, compensation for damage from water-related projects.

4.7 Global Environmental Issues 2079 Chaitra, 2077 Chaitra

Q1. Define regional and global environmental issues. Discuss briefly the factors contributing to global warming. [4 marks] 2079 Chaitra

Answer:

Regional Environmental Issues: Environmental problems that affect a specific region or area. Examples: air/water pollution in Kathmandu Valley, deforestation in Terai, illegal sand mining in Nepal.

Global Environmental Issues: Environmental problems that affect the entire planet. Major issues include:

  • Pollution (air, water, soil)
  • Global Warming
  • Overpopulation
  • Natural Resource Depletion
  • Climate Change
  • Loss of Biodiversity
  • Ocean Acidification
  • Genetic Engineering

Factors Contributing to Global Warming:

  1. Greenhouse Gas Emissions: CO2, methane, N2O from burning fossil fuels, industrial processes, and agriculture.
  2. Deforestation: Reduces carbon sinks; trees that absorb CO2 are destroyed.
  3. Industrial Activity: Factories emit large quantities of GHGs and pollutants.
  4. Transportation: Vehicles burning petroleum contribute significantly to CO2 emissions.
  5. Agriculture: Methane from livestock, fertilizer use producing N2O.

Effects: Rise in ocean and land temperature, melting of polar ice, uneven precipitation patterns, sea-level rise.

Q2. Discuss about the major environmental issues of Nepal. [4 marks] 2074 Bhadra

Answer:

Nepal faces several major environmental issues:

  1. Climate Change & Global Warming: Melting of snow and glaciers, rise in sea level, change in seasonal and rainfall patterns, affects agricultural production, heavy rainfall and drought.
  2. Air & Water Pollution: Untreated sewage, industrial waste, and agrochemicals polluting water bodies. Biological contamination of water in rural areas. Arsenic, high iron, manganese, coliform bacteria in Terai. Vehicular and industrial emissions in city areas.

  6. Water-borne Diseases: Cholera, diarrhea, typhoid, dysentery, jaundice. Diarrheal deaths reached 7,882 (4.83% of total deaths in Nepal per WHO 2017 data).

Q5. Discuss the major environmental issues of Nepal. What are the causes of acid rain? [8 marks] 2072 Ashwin

Answer:

Major Environmental Issues of Nepal:

  1. Climate Change & Global Warming: Melting snow/glaciers, sea level rise, rainfall pattern changes, agricultural production impact.
  2. Air & Water Pollution: Untreated sewage, industrial waste, agrochemicals, vehicular/industrial emissions, arsenic/iron contamination in Terai.
  3. Deforestation & Land Degradation: Overgrazing, flood, soil erosion, habitat loss, agro-biodiversity threats.
  4. Illegal Mining: Sand/stone extraction causing soil instability, erosion, and lowered water tables.
  5. Poaching: Illegal hunting threatening endangered species and ecosystems.
  6. Water-borne Diseases: Cholera, diarrhea, typhoid, dysentery, jaundice.

Causes of Acid Rain:

Acid rain is caused when sulphur dioxide (SO2) and nitrogen oxides (NO2) are emitted into the atmosphere and mix with water molecules to form sulphuric acid and nitric acid. Sources include:

  • Burning of fossil fuels: Coal and petroleum combustion in power plants and factories releases SO2 and NO2.
  • Vehicular emissions: Internal combustion engines release nitrogen oxides.
  • Industrial processes: Metal smelting, oil refining, and chemical manufacturing.
  • Natural sources: Volcanic eruptions and lightning (minor contribution).

Effects of Acid Rain:

  • Serious effects on human health, wildlife, and aquatic species
  • Causes slow growth, injury, or death of forests
  • Washes away soil nutrients and releases aluminium, making water absorption difficult for plants
  • Damages buildings, monuments, and infrastructure
