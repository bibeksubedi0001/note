NEC Act 2055 BS (2000 AD) — Key Provisions 2074

The Nepal Engineering Council Act 2055 BS was enacted to regulate the engineering profession in Nepal. Key provisions include:

Formation and Composition of NEC

  • NEC is a statutory/autonomous body established under the NEC Act 2055
  • Headed by a Chairperson appointed by Government of Nepal
  • Comprises representatives from government, engineering academia, NEA, and senior engineers
  • Registrar serves as Member Secretary
  • Council tenure: 4 years

Powers and Functions of the Council

  1. Register engineers in appropriate categories
  2. Grant, renew, suspend, or cancel engineering licenses
  3. Formulate code of conduct for engineering professionals
  4. Take disciplinary action against engineers violating professional standards
  5. Recognize/accredit engineering degree programs
  6. Set minimum standards for engineering education
  7. Advise the Government on engineering policy matters
  8. Determine fees for registration, renewal, and other services
  9. Maintain a register of all licensed engineers in Nepal

Jurisdiction & Scope of NEC 2073 2072

Jurisdiction: NEC has authority over all engineering professionals practicing in Nepal, including:

  • All branches of engineering (Civil, Electrical, Mechanical, Computer, Electronics, Architecture, etc.)
  • Both government and private sector engineers
  • Foreign engineers practicing in Nepal
  • Engineering consultancy firms (ensuring licensed engineers lead projects)

Scope:

  • Registration and licensing of engineers across all engineering disciplines
  • Regulation of engineering practice standards
  • Accreditation of engineering programs in universities
  • Enforcement of code of conduct and professional ethics
  • Investigation of complaints against registered engineers
  • Conducting examinations for professional engineer certification
  • Ensuring only registered/licensed engineers undertake engineering works
  • Coordination with international engineering bodies

Why Should Engineers Not Practice Without a License? 2079

Justification:

  1. Public Safety: Engineering works (buildings, bridges, roads, electrical systems) directly affect public safety. Unlicensed practice can lead to structural failures, accidents, and loss of life.
  2. Quality Assurance: License ensures the engineer has minimum qualifications, knowledge, and competence to perform engineering work.
  3. Accountability: Licensed engineers are accountable to NEC. Disciplinary action can be taken for mistakes or malpractice. Unlicensed engineers have no such accountability.
  4. Legal Requirement: NEC Act 2055 makes it illegal to practice engineering without registration. Violation is punishable by law.
  5. Professional Standards: License binds engineers to follow the NEC Code of Conduct, ensuring ethical behavior, competence, and professionalism.
  6. Consumer Protection: Clients can verify credentials and file complaints against licensed engineers. No such protection exists for unlicensed practitioners.
  7. Insurance & Liability: Only licensed engineers can get professional indemnity insurance, which is essential for liability protection.
  8. International Recognition: License facilitates recognition of Nepali engineers internationally through mutual recognition agreements.

Labour Act 2074

Labour law is concerned with the rights, interests, facilities, and safety of workers and employees working in enterprises of various sectors.

Why Should Engineers Be Aware of Labour Law? 2079 2071

  1. Legal Compliance: Engineers working as project managers, site engineers, or contractors must comply with Labour Act provisions. Non-compliance leads to legal penalties and fines.
  2. Worker Safety Responsibility: Engineers supervise construction sites and are directly responsible for worker health and safety. Knowledge of safety provisions prevents accidents and deaths.
  3. Employer-Employee Relations: Engineers often manage teams. Understanding provisions on working hours, overtime, leave, and wages ensures fair treatment and prevents disputes.
  4. Accident Liability: If a worker is injured or dies on site, the engineer/employer has legal obligations for compensation, insurance, and reporting. Ignorance is not a defense.
  5. Construction-Specific Provisions: Labour Act has special provisions for construction business — mandatory safety arrangements, accident insurance, PPE — that engineers must implement.
  6. Contract Management: Knowledge of labour law helps in preparing fair employment contracts and avoiding disputes with workers and labour unions.
  7. Career Protection: Engineers who violate labour law can face criminal charges, license suspension, and career-ending consequences.

Scenario: What to Do If a Construction Worker Dies at Site 2071

If a construction worker dies by falling from height at your site, the following steps must be taken:

  1. Immediate Response:
    • Stop all work at the site immediately
    • Secure the accident area to prevent further injuries
    • Provide first aid if worker is still alive; call emergency services
  2. Notification:
    • Inform the police immediately
    • Notify the Labour Office/Department of Labour
    • Inform the employer/company management
    • Inform the worker’s family
  3. Documentation:
    • Preserve the accident scene for investigation
    • Record details of the incident (time, witnesses, conditions)
    • Take photographs of the scene
    • Prepare a written accident report
  4. Legal Obligations (as per Labour Act 2074):
    • Provide compensation to the family of the deceased worker
    • Accidental insurance claim must be processed
    • Cooperate fully with the investigation by Labour Office and police
    • Funeral expenses must be borne by the employer
  5. Investigation & Prevention:
    • Conduct internal investigation to determine the cause
    • Identify safety failures (missing harness, scaffold issues, guardrails)
    • Implement corrective measures before resuming work
    • Review and strengthen safety protocols & PPE requirements
    • Conduct safety training for all workers before resuming

Health and Safety Provisions 2080 2070

The manager of an enterprise is responsible for:

Disciplinary Action for Misconduct 2078

Steps: Warning → Suspension → Termination

  2. Suspension — Repeated misconduct or serious violation; temporary suspension from work with or without pay
  3. Termination — Continued or grave misconduct; dismissal from service following due process (opportunity to explain, investigation, hearing)

Definition 2079 2078 2073

Intellectual Property (IP): Creations of the mind — inventions, literary and artistic works, designs, symbols, names, and images used in commerce. Like real/physical property, creators have the right to own, use, and protect their intellectual creations. Others need permission before using IP; otherwise legal action can be taken.

Why Is Intellectual Property Right Necessary? 2079

  1. Encourages Innovation: Gives inventors/creators exclusive rights and financial incentives to invest time, effort, and money in research and creation.
  2. Protects Creators: Prevents unauthorized copying, use, or theft of original work. Without IPR, anyone could steal and profit from another’s creation.
  3. Economic Development: IP-intensive industries contribute significantly to GDP, employment, and exports. Patents and trademarks build competitive advantages.
  4. Technology Transfer: Patent disclosures make technical knowledge publicly available, enabling learning and further innovation after the patent period expires.
  5. Consumer Protection: Trademarks help consumers identify genuine products and avoid counterfeits, ensuring quality and safety.
  6. Cultural Preservation: Copyright protects literary, artistic, and cultural works, preserving them for future generations.
  7. Fair Competition: IPR creates a level playing field where businesses compete based on innovation rather than copying.
  8. International Trade: Nepal is a member of WIPO (World Intellectual Property Organization) and WTO — IPR compliance is required for international trade agreements.
  9. Engineering Relevance: Engineers create designs, software, processes, and inventions that need patent/copyright protection. Without IPR, engineering innovations cannot be commercially viable.

Copyright (Copyright Act 2059) 2081 2079 2078 2074 2070 2069

Definition: The sole right to produce or reproduce a work or any substantial part thereof in any material form. Exists in every original literary, dramatic, musical, and artistic work.

Three Basic Areas

  1. Attribution & Integrity — Author’s name must be attributed; work unchanged without permission
  2. Ownership, Reproduction & Distribution — Commercial exploitation rights shared between author and publishers
  3. Display & Performance — Rights in unique creative performance/interpretation

Ownership

  • Copyright vests in the author of the work
  • Can be transferred wholly or partially to another person

Registration Process

  1. Apply to the Office of the Registrar
  2. Submit evidence of ownership and prescribed registration fee
  3. Registrar examines and issues certificate

Term of Copyright

  • During lifetime of author + 50 years after death
  • For anonymous/pseudonymous works: 50 years from first publication
  • For applied art & photographs: 25 years from creation
  • For works published after author’s death: 50 years from publication

License Provisions

  • Copyright holder can grant license for publication (whole or part)
  • Registrar can grant license if owner fails to republish a needed work
  • Translation license for Nepali language can be granted
  • Public exhibition and library reproduction licenses available

Fair Use (Not Unauthorized)

  • Private study, research, criticism, review
  • News/magazines, radio diffusion, court proceedings
  • Academic institution use with proper attribution
  • Single copy for personal use (import)

Punishment for Infringement

  • First offense: Fine NRs 10,000–1,00,000 or 6 months imprisonment or both
  • Subsequent: Fine NRs 20,000–2,00,000 or 1 year imprisonment or both
  • Compensation for loss must also be paid to copyright owner

Patent (Patent, Design & Trademark Act 2022) 2074 2071 2069

Definition: Rights related to new inventions — theory, principle, formula, process, technology, or composition of matter.

Criteria for Patent

  • Must be new (novelty)
  • Must be inventive (non-obvious)
  • Must be useful (industrial applicability)

Application Requirements

  1. Name, address, and profession of inventor
  2. Conditions of acquiring right from inventor (if not self-invented)
  3. Method of operation/utilization
  4. Principle or formula (if applicable)
  5. Drawings and sketches (if necessary)
  6. Prescribed application fee

Un-patentable Inventions

  • Already registered in someone else’s name
  • Not invented by applicant and right not acquired from inventor
  • Adverse effect on health, conduct, morale, or national interest
  • Violates prevailing laws of Nepal

Examination: Department examines for novelty, usefulness, expert advice if needed.

Term: 7 years from registration; renewable twice (total 21 years).

Punishment: Fine NRs 2,50,000–5,00,000 + confiscation of related materials.

Industrial Design 2078 2073

Definition: Shape, form, pattern, color, or combination used for goods (aesthetic/ornamental, not purely utilitarian).

Application: Submit description, drawings, sketches, and 4 copies of model.

Un-registrable: Adverse effect on dignity/morale/national interest; already registered.

Term: 5 years from registration; renewable twice (total 15 years).

Punishment: Fine up to NRs 50,000 + confiscation.

Trademark 2077 2076 2073 2069

Definition: Word, symbol, picture, or combination used to distinguish goods of one firm from others.

Service Mark 2077 2076

Definition: A service mark is similar to a trademark but is used to identify and distinguish services rather than goods.

  • While a trademark identifies the source of tangible goods (e.g., Nike shoes, Samsung phones), a service mark identifies the source of services (e.g., banking services, consulting services, airlines).
  • Examples: FedEx® (delivery services), Hilton® (hotel services), Deloitte® (consulting services).
  • In Nepal, service marks are registered under the same Patent, Design & Trademark Act 2022 BS.
  • Registration process, term, and protection are identical to trademarks.
  • Service marks are particularly relevant for engineering consultancy firms that provide services rather than manufactured goods.

Business Law 2073 2072 2071

Business: Human activities related to production, sales/purchase, or exchange of goods/services with profit-making objectives.

Law: Norms drafted and enforced by state/local government to regulate activities. All laws regulating business are business law.

Sources of Business Law in Nepal

  1. English business law (Common law, Law of merchants, Principles of equity, Statute of legislature, Custom and usage)
  2. Nepalese statutory acts
  3. Judicial decisions
  4. Writings/opinions of scholars & commercial treaties/agreements

Types of Business Enterprises 2079

1) Sole Business (Private Registration Act 2014)

Characteristics: Sole ownership, sole management/control, unlimited liability, limited area, less formalities, voluntary origin/end.

Limitations: Unlimited liability, limited capital, uncertain future, no legal status, impractical decisions, lack of specialization, loss in absence of key person.

2) Partnership Business (Partnership Act 2020)

Characteristics: Joint ownership, unlimited liability, profit/loss sharing, based on agreement, members have no separate existence, joint management, joint agency, right cannot be transferred.

Limitations: Unlimited liability, uncertain existence, misunderstanding possibility, limited capital, difficult ownership transfer, slow decisions, lack of public faith.

3) Company Business (Company Act 2053)

Characteristics: Legal artificial person, perpetual existence, limited liability, common seal, voluntary association, capital via shares, transferable shares, management by representatives, financial statement publication, unlimited capital, public faith.

Limitations: Difficulty in formation, lack of personal interest, lack of secrecy, possibility of fraud, shareholder exploitation, group conflict, absence of prompt decision.

Necessities / Importance of Company Registration 2075

  1. Legal Identity: Registration gives the company a separate legal identity (legal person), distinct from its owners. It can own property, sue, and be sued in its own name.
  2. Limited Liability: Shareholders’ liability is limited to their share capital. Personal assets are protected from business debts.
  3. Credibility & Trust: Registered companies have greater public trust and credibility. Banks, clients, and government agencies prefer dealing with registered entities.
  4. Access to Finance: Only registered companies can open corporate bank accounts, obtain loans, issue shares, and attract investors.
  5. Government Contracts: Engineering firms must be registered to bid for government projects, tenders, and contracts.
  6. Tax Compliance: Registration enables PAN/VAT registration, which is mandatory for conducting business legally.
  7. Perpetual Existence: A registered company continues to exist even if shareholders/directors change. Unregistered businesses die with owners.
  8. Brand Protection: Company name is protected by law — no other company can register with the same name.
  9. Legal Protection: Registered companies can enforce contracts, protect intellectual property, and take legal action in courts.
  10. Professional Practice: For engineering consulting firms, registration is mandatory to practice legally and obtain NEC affiliation.

Company Registration Procedure in Nepal 2078 2075 2073 2072

  1. Name reservation — Apply to Office of Company Registrar; name must be unique
  2. Preparation of documents — Memorandum of Association (MOA), Articles of Association (AOA)
  3. Application submission — Submit to Office of Company Registrar with: MOA and AOA, details of promoters/shareholders, copy of citizenship certificates, registered office address proof, share capital details
  4. Registration fee payment — As prescribed
  5. Certificate of incorporation — Issued upon successful registration
  6. PAN/VAT registration — Register with Inland Revenue Department
  7. For public company — Additional requirement: Certificate of Commencement of Business

Registration Process of Engineering Consulting Firm 2073

To register an engineering consulting firm in Nepal, the following steps are required:

  1. Company Registration:
    • Register the firm at the Office of Company Registrar under Company Act 2063
    • Choose firm type: Private Limited (Pvt. Ltd.) is most common for consulting firms
    • Prepare MOA and AOA specifying engineering consultancy as objective
    • Obtain Certificate of Incorporation
  2. Tax Registration:
    • Register for PAN at Inland Revenue Department
    • Register for VAT if annual turnover exceeds threshold
  3. NEC Registration:
    • Register the firm with Nepal Engineering Council
    • At least one NEC-licensed engineer must be associated with the firm
    • Submit proof of engineer’s NEC registration certificate
  4. Professional Body Registration:
    • Register with Society of Consulting Architectural and Engineering Firms (SCAEF) Nepal
    • Membership in Nepal Engineers’ Association (NEA) recommended
  5. Category Classification:
    • Apply for categorization (A, B, C, D) based on technical manpower, experience, and financial capacity
    • Category determines the scale/value of projects the firm can bid for
    • Renewal required periodically
  6. Local Government:
    • Obtain local business license from concerned municipality
    • Office space/premises verification

Key Points to Remember

NEC Act

  • 3 categories — General A (BE), Professional B (ME), Foreign C (BE + 10 yrs)
  • NEA — professional association (1962), voluntary, no enforcement power
  • NEC — statutory body (2000/Act 2055), mandatory registration, disciplinary powers
  • NEC can grant, suspend, cancel registration
  • NEC Act 2055: composition (Chairperson + reps from govt/academia/NEA), 4-year tenure
  • Jurisdiction: all engineering branches, govt & private, foreign engineers in Nepal
  • Scope: registration, licensing, accreditation, code enforcement, complaints investigation
  • Why license needed: public safety, accountability, legal requirement, quality assurance, international recognition

Labour Law (Act 2074)

  • Working hours — 8 hrs/day, 48 hrs/week; no continuous > 5 hrs; 30 min break
  • Overtime — 1.5× wage; max 4 hrs/day, 24 hrs/week; cannot force
  • Leave — 1 day/week; 13 public holidays; 1 home leave per 20 working days; 12 sick leave (half-pay); 98 days maternity
  • Space — 15 cu.m. per worker (4m max height counted)
  • 5 types of employment — Regular, Work-based, Time-bound, Casual (≤7 days/month), Part-time (≤35 hrs/week)
  • Discipline — Warning → Suspension → Termination
  • Construction — special provisions for tools, safety, accident insurance
  • Why be aware — legal compliance, worker safety responsibility, accident liability, contract management
  • Worker death at site — stop work, notify police/Labour Office/family, document scene, process insurance, compensate, investigate cause, implement corrective measures

Intellectual Property

  • Copyright — lifetime + 50 years; Copyright Act 2059; penalty NRs 10K–1L / 6 months
  • Patent — 7 years, 2 renewals (21 yrs); must be novel/inventive/useful; penalty NRs 2.5L–5L
  • Design — 5 years, 2 renewals (15 yrs); penalty up to NRs 50K
  • Trademark — 7 years, indefinitely renewable; penalty up to NRs 1L; identifies goods
  • Service Mark — same as trademark but identifies services; same registration process and term
  • 35 days for opposition/complaint after publication (patent/design/trademark)
  • Why IPR necessary — encourages innovation, protects creators, economic development, technology transfer, consumer protection, international trade (WIPO/WTO)

Building Codes

  • Building Act 2055 + Building Rules 2062 + NBC 2060
  • 4 categories — International State-of-Art, Professionally Engineered (>1000 sq.ft., >3 stories, >4.5m span), MRT (<1000, ≤3, <4.5m), Rural Guidelines (≤2 stories)
  • NBC 105 — Seismic design; NBC 114 — Construction safety
  • Building permission mandatory from concerned authority

Building Bylaws

  • FAR = Total floor area ÷ Plot area
  • Set Back — distance from road/river/boundary
  • ROW — right of way for transportation
  • 3 components — Building, Planning, Enforcement

Company Registration

  • Sole — single owner, unlimited liability, Private Registration Act 2014
  • Partnership — 2+ persons, unlimited liability, Partnership Act 2020
  • Company — legal person, limited liability, Company Act 2053
  • Private — 1–50 shareholders, Pvt. Ltd., cannot offer shares to public
  • Public — min 7 shareholders, Limited, must publish financials
  • Registration: Name reservation → MOA/AOA → Apply → Fee → Certificate of Incorporation → PAN/VAT
  • Necessities: legal identity, limited liability, credibility, access to finance, govt contract eligibility, tax compliance, perpetual existence
  • Consulting firm: Company registration + PAN/VAT + NEC registration (licensed engineer) + SCAEF + category classification (A/B/C/D) + local license

Cyber Law (ETA 2063)

  • Effective December 8, 2006
  • Covers electronic records, digital signatures, certifying authority, computer crimes
  • IT Tribunal and Appellate Tribunal for dispute resolution
