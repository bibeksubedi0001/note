What is Society? 2080 2079 2077

Society is derived from the Latin word socius meaning companionship, friendship, or comrade. In sociology, society refers to a large group of individuals united by certain relations, modes of behavior, and a web of social relationships. It is not a mere collection of people but a complex pattern of norms and interactions that arise among them. Society is abstract — we cannot “see” it, only observe the relationships and patterns within it.

Definitions by Scholars

ScholarDefinition
MacIver“The system of usages and procedures, of authority and mutual aid, of many groupings and divisions of controls of human behaviors and liberties. It is the web of social relationship.”
Morris Ginsberg“A collection of individuals united by certain relations or mode of behavior which mark them off from others.”
J.F. Cuber“A group of individuals who have lived together long enough to become organized and consider themselves distinct from others.”
Cooley“A complex of forms or processes each of which is living and growing by interaction with the others, the whole being so unified that what takes place in one part affects all the rest.”

Criteria of Society 2074

  1. Population: Can be small or big.
  2. Common Territory: People occupy a shared geographic area.
  3. Government/Political Authority: Common political authority.
  4. Common Culture: Shared culture and a sense of relationship, membership, and commitment.

Characteristics (Elements) of Society 207420722079

  1. Plurality of Individuals: Composed of people of different ages, sexes, economic statuses, and cultural backgrounds.
  2. Web of Social Relationships / Mutual Interaction and Awareness: The core element. Defined roles (father, teacher, engineer) and reciprocal interactions.
  3. Likeness and Difference: Likeness (blood relation, nationality) binds people. Difference enables division of labor and mutual dependence.
  4. Interdependence: No individual is self-sufficient. Members depend on each other.
  5. Cooperation and Conflict: Cooperation holds society together. Conflict drives change. Both coexist.
  6. Permanence and Stability: Society outlives individual members.
  7. Dynamic Nature: Changeability is inherent. No society remains static.
  8. Abstract Nature: Society is intangible — it is the web of relationships, not the people themselves.
  9. Social Control: Society controls behavior through customs, traditions, laws, and institutions.
  10. Culture: Every society has its own unique traditions, values, and attitudes.

What is Community? 20802074

A community is a social group whose members reside in a specific locality, share government, and have a common cultural and historical heritage. Unlike society (abstract), a community is concrete and location-specific.

MacIver: “Whenever the members of any group, small or large, live together in such a way that they share the basic conditions of a common life, we call that group community.”

Elements of Community

  1. Group of People
  2. Definite Locality/Territory
  3. Community Sentiment (We-feeling)
  4. Cultural Similarity (Likeness)
  5. Permanency
  6. Naturality (grows spontaneously, not created by law)
  7. No Legal Status
  8. Particular Name

Theories of Origin of Society

  1. Divine Origin Theory: Society was created by God. Rooted in religious philosophies (e.g., Sanatan Hindu philosophy).
  2. Social Contract Theory (Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau): Humans contractually formed society for security and order.
  3. Evolutionary Theory (Spencer, Darwin): Society evolved gradually from simple to complex. Most scientifically accepted.

Social Institutions

Five basic institutions found in all known societies:

  1. The Family: Contributes new members, teaches expected behaviors.
  2. Religion: Motivates members to comply with responsibilities.
  3. Economy: Produces and distributes goods and services.
  4. Education: Transmits skills needed for productive membership.
  5. The State: Protects members from threats. Establishes legal codes.
Figure 1.4: Parsons’ Six Fundamental Social Needs

Fundamental Social Needs (Talcott Parsons) 20732071

  1. Organizing Activities: Obtain basic goods and services (food, clothing, shelter).
  2. Protection: From external threats (invasion, disasters) and internal threats (crime, epidemics).
  3. Replacement: Replace members lost by death or emigration.
  4. Transmission of Knowledge: Teach new members rights, obligations, and expected behaviors.
  5. Motivation: Motivate members to fulfill responsibilities.
  6. Conflict Resolution: Develop mechanisms for solving conflicts.

Social Change 20772072

Social change refers to alterations in social structure, cultural patterns, and social behaviors over time. Society is never static.

MacIver: “Social change in the human relationship.”
M.P. Jenison: “Social change may be defined as modification in ways of doing and thinking of people.”

Major Factors Causing Social Change (8 Factors)

  1. Technological Factors: Most powerful agent. Inventions alter how we live and work.
  2. Cultural Factors: New ideas, ideologies, values.
  3. Physical/Environmental Factors: Earthquakes, floods, climate change.
  4. Demographic Factors: Population size, birth/death rates, migration.
  5. Economic Factors: Industrialization, capitalism.
  6. Social Conflict: Class, religion, gender, caste divisions.
  7. Social Movements: Organized efforts to change norms.
  8. Human Ideas and Opinions: New ideas modify social structures.
Figure 1.5: Four Theories of Social Change — comparison with Nepal context

Theories of Social Change 2081207820752069

A. Evolutionary Theory (Comte, Spencer)

Society moves linearly from simple to complex. Example: Hunting/Gathering → Industrial → Information Society.

B. Functionalist Theory (Parsons)

Society is a system of interconnected parts. Change in one part forces adjustment in others to restore equilibrium.

C. Conflict Theory (Karl Marx) 20782075

Change is driven by conflict between “Haves” (Bourgeoisie) and “Have-nots” (Proletariat). Economic sequence: Primitive communism → Slavery → Feudal → Capitalist → Communist.

Relevance to Nepal: Maoist insurgency (1996–2006), 240-year monarchy abolished via political struggle (not gradual evolution), Dalit/Janajati/Madhesi movements for inclusion.

D. Cyclical Theory (Spengler, Toynbee)

Civilizations rise and fall in cycles: birth → growth → decline → death. Example: Roman Empire, dynastic cycles in China/Nepal.

  • Monarchy to Republic (2008): 240-year monarchy abolished through struggle.
  • Caste/Ethnic Tensions: Movements for Dalit, Janajati, Madhesi inclusion.

Individual Freedom vs. Societal Goals 20732070

Why conflict exists:

  1. Self-interest vs. Collective Good
  2. Resource Scarcity

What is Technology?

Technology is the proper knowledge and use of tools and techniques that facilitates the transfer of inputs into outputs efficiently. It is the practical application of scientific knowledge to create tools and systems that solve human problems. The relationship between technology and society is reciprocal — they shape each other.

Technological change is defined as the modification and alteration of current tools and techniques to bring improvement in society and improve productivity.

Types:

Ancient China (The Innovators)

  • Great Wall (220 BC): Massive defensive fortification. Advanced logistics, surveying, and masonry.
  • Grand Canal (510 AD): Longest canal in the world, connecting Yellow River and Yangtze. Lifeline for grain transport.
  • Four Great Inventions: Compass (navigation), Gunpowder (warfare), Papermaking (knowledge transmission), Movable Type Printing (before Gutenberg).
  • Other: Silk, noodles, porcelain, paper money (from 3000 BC).

Ancient India and Nepal

  • Indus Valley (2500–1700 BC): First known grid city system, advanced drainage/sanitation, flush toilets, public baths (The Great Bath), copper and bronze metallurgy.
  • Vedic / Ancient Nepal: Pagoda style architecture, Vaastu engineering (Nalanda, Takshashila).
  • Araniko (13th C): Master architect from Nepal who built White Stupa in Beijing, showcasing Nepali bronze and stone craftsmanship.

Mesopotamia (Cradle of Civilization)

  • First large-scale irrigation systems (canals, dikes) between Tigris and Euphrates rivers.
  • Ziggurats: Massive stepped pyramids of mud bricks (e.g., Ziggurat of Ur).
  • Cuneiform writing on clay tablets for construction and trade records.
  • Babylonian Gugallu (irrigation inspectors).

Ancient Persia (Iran)

  • Many bridges from the time of Shapur-I (300 AD).
  • Sassanid palace at Ctesiphon (400 AD) with the widest single span vault of unreinforced brickwork.
  • Stone building methods introduced into India (515 BC, when Darius conquered Punjab).

The First Engineers

The first engineers were irrigators, architects, and military engineers, often the same people. As agricultural surpluses grew, specialists emerged. Wealth accumulated and projects became too large for a single craftsman, giving rise to a new class: technicians and engineers who could negotiate with rulers, plan details, and direct workmen.

Spread of Engineering Knowledge

  • Inventions spread along trade routes; stopped by natural barriers (deserts, oceans).
  • Large interconnected populations meant faster invention rates.
  • “Hydraulic State”: Large irrigation projects necessitated centralized government (Nile, Tigris-Euphrates, Indus, Hwang-Ho valleys).
SOLVED: Lessons for fresh graduates from history [2079 Jestha, 5 marks]
  1. Build for Durability: Pyramids (4500+ yrs), Roman roads survived centuries.
  2. Appropriate Technology: Use local materials/culture; what works abroad may not suit Nepal.
  3. Teamwork: Mega-projects required massive community participation.
  4. Environmental Responsibility: Civilizations that mismanaged environment collapsed.
  5. Continuous Learning: From stone tools to computers — never stop learning.
  6. Ethics & Safety: Engineering failures caused loss of life when safety was ignored.
  7. Nepal Heritage: Preserve Araniko’s, Gehendra Shumsher’s legacy while adopting modern tech.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Confusing society (abstract) with community (concrete, location-specific).
  • Mixing up Evolutionary (linear) with Cyclical (rise and fall).
  • Not providing dates/names for Eastern/Western achievements.
  • Writing theories without proponents (Marx, Parsons, Spencer, Toynbee).
  • Ignoring Nepal context when specifically asked.
