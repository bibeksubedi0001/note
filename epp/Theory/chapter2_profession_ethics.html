Definition of Profession 2080 2078 2077 2076 2075 2073 2072 2071 2070

  • A profession is an occupation that requires specialized knowledge and skill acquired through systematic training or education.
  • It provides specialized services to individuals, organizations, or communities in areas they cannot perform themselves.
  • Engineering Council Act 2055 defines engineering profession as the occupation done by engineers — persons having a graduate degree in engineering from an institute recognized by the Council.

Characteristics / Features of Profession 207820732081

  1. Systematic Knowledge and Skill: Based on comprehensive study and practical training (e.g., 4 years of engineering education). Professionals deliver services based on this acquired expertise.
  2. Authenticity and Honor: Professionals possess special knowledge, hence society grants them special rights and honor. An engineer certifies safety designs — people trust what they cannot verify themselves.
  3. Public Property and Public Evaluation: Professional activities are always subject to public scrutiny. People observe and evaluate a professional’s behavior and practice.
  4. Bound by Code of Ethics: Professionals are regulated by professional bodies through licensing, codes of ethics, and disciplinary procedures. They must maintain high moral and behavioral standards.
  5. Professional Culture: Each profession develops its own culture through socialization, the nature of the work, and code of ethics. Members of the same profession share common behavioral patterns.

Professionalism 2081

  • Professionalism is the conduct, behavior, and attitude expected of a professional in the practice of their occupation.
  • It involves competence, integrity, accountability, ethical conduct, and adherence to professional standards.
  • A true professional maintains discipline, honesty, continuous learning, and respect for fellow practitioners.

Perception of General Public Towards Engineers in Nepal 2081

Positive Perceptions:

  • Engineers are seen as educated and skilled professionals capable of building infrastructure
  • Respected for their role in national development — roads, bridges, hydropower, buildings
  • Viewed as problem solvers who improve quality of life
  • Associated with modernization and technological advancement
  • Government engineering positions (Gazetted officers) carry social prestige

Negative Perceptions:

  • Perceived as involved in corruption — commission-taking in construction projects, inflated bills
  • Buildings/roads that fail quickly damage public trust (“road built today, destroyed tomorrow”)
  • Seen as prioritizing personal profit over public safety (especially in government projects)
  • Lack of visible accountability when structures fail (e.g., post-earthquake 2015 building collapses)
  • Public feels engineers and contractors are often in collusion
  • Low awareness among public about engineering vs. non-engineering work — often confused with contractors/masons

How to improve: Maintain ethical practice, deliver quality work, ensure public safety, be transparent, and participate in community development.

Why Do Engineers Behave Unethically? 2078

  1. Financial Pressure / Greed: Desire for quick money through commission-taking, inflated estimates, or substandard materials
  2. Pressure from Superiors / Employers: Forced to cut costs, compromise safety, or approve substandard work to meet deadlines or budgets
  3. Political Interference: Pressure from politicians to award contracts to favored parties or approve illegal constructions
  4. Lack of Enforcement: Weak regulatory bodies — NEC and government agencies do not effectively monitor or punish violations
  5. Peer Pressure / Culture of Corruption: “Everyone does it” mentality; ethical engineers are isolated or sidelined
  6. Inadequate Salary: Low government salaries push engineers toward unethical means to supplement income
  7. Ignorance: Some engineers are genuinely unaware of ethical codes and professional standards
  8. Lack of Professional Education: Engineering curriculum historically focused on technical skills, not ethics and professionalism
  9. Absence of Whistleblower Protection: Fear of retaliation prevents engineers from reporting unethical behavior
  10. Social Norms: In some communities, corruption is normalized and even expected

Q: Define Profession. Explain the features/characteristics of profession.

Ans: A profession is an occupation requiring specialized knowledge and skill acquired through systematic training, used to provide specialized services to the community. Its features are: (1) Systematic knowledge — based on comprehensive study and practice, (2) Authenticity and honor — society grants special rights due to specialized expertise, (3) Public evaluation — professional conduct is always subject to public scrutiny, (4) Code of ethics — regulated by professional bodies through licensing and ethical codes, and (5) Professional culture — shared behavioral patterns among members of the same profession.

SOLVED: Explain engineering morals, ethics, and professionalism [2074 Baisakh, 8 marks]

Q: Explain engineering morals, ethics and professionalism.

Ans: Engineering morals are personal principles of right and wrong that guide individual engineers — based on conscience, culture, and upbringing, they are internal standards free from external influence (e.g., refusing to certify unsafe structures on personal conviction).

Engineering ethics is the study of moral values, issues, and decisions involved in engineering practice. It comprises external guidelines and codes defining accepted behavior for engineers, set by professional bodies. Core ethical values include honesty, integrity, fidelity, charity, responsibility, and self-discipline.

Engineering professionalism is the overall conduct, attitude, and competence expected from practicing engineers. It includes systematic knowledge through education, public accountability, adherence to codes of ethics, and maintaining professional culture. A professional engineer holds public safety paramount, works within areas of competence, acts as a faithful agent, and conducts work with honor and integrity.

Together, these three concepts ensure that engineers serve society responsibly — morals provide internal guidance, ethics provide external standards, and professionalism encompasses the holistic professional behavior expected.

Evaluation of NEA’s Role (Golden Jubilee Perspective) 2069

Q: Are you satisfied with the role played by NEA in enhancing and upgrading engineering profession and welfare of engineers?

  • NEA has successfully united engineers under one professional body with 7500+ members across Nepal
  • Established international linkages — member of World Federation of Engineering Organizations (WFEO) and FEIAP
  • Organized numerous conferences, seminars, and workshops for professional development
  • Played a role in lobbying and advocacy for engineering profession recognition
  • Provided arbitration services to resolve professional disputes
  • Published journals and technical publications contributing to engineering knowledge

Shortcomings / Areas of Dissatisfaction:

  • Has not been able to effectively ensure fair compensation for engineers — many engineers remain underpaid in private sector

  • Limited role in quality monitoring of engineering works across Nepal

Verdict: While NEA has contributed to professional networking and international recognition, it has not fully succeeded in ensuring fair treatment, curbing corruption, or protecting engineers’ safety. More needs to be done in advocacy, enforcement, and reaching engineers beyond the capital.

Ethics — Definition 2081 2079 2078 2077 2073 2070

  • Ethics is the science of human character as expressed in right or wrong conduct — the science of morality.
  • A set of guidelines that define accepted practice and behavior for a group of people or society.
  • Ethics can change according to nation, society, peer group, religion, or profession.
  • It is not just doing the right thing — it includes making tough decisions about ambiguous situations.
  • Things that are legal may not be ethical.
  • Ethics are externally derived (from profession, society), whereas morals are internally held.

Moral — Definition 2081 2079 2074

  • Concerned with goodness and badness of human character
  • Measures standard of good behavior by which people are judged
  • Standards of behavior accepted by culture and religion of society
  • Basic human marker of right conduct; deeper level, both personal and spiritual
  • Usually do not change; defines personal character at a subliminal level

SOLVED: AI ethics in engineering [2082 Baisakh, 4 marks]

Q: Discuss the ethical considerations related to the extensive use of AI in the engineering field.

Ans:

  1. Safety and reliability: AI-based designs must be verified for safety; blind reliance without human validation can endanger public welfare
  2. Accountability: When AI-generated designs fail, determining liability (developer, engineer, or AI itself) becomes ambiguous
  3. Bias and fairness: AI trained on biased data can produce discriminatory outcomes in infrastructure planning
  4. Job displacement: Automation may replace skilled engineering jobs, raising concerns about livelihood
  5. Transparency: AI “black box” decision-making lacks transparency, conflicting with the requirement for engineers to justify decisions
  6. Data privacy: AI systems require large datasets, raising concerns about privacy and confidentiality
  7. Professional competence: Over-reliance on AI may erode engineers’ core skills and judgment
SOLVED: Define ethics, moral and non-moral actions, perspectives of professional ethics [2074 Reg, 5 marks]

Q: Define Ethics. What is moral and non-moral action? Write briefly the perspectives of Professional ethics.

Ans: Ethics is the science of morality — a set of guidelines defining accepted behavior for a group, governing right and wrong in human conduct.

Moral actions are conscious, deliberate actions guided by principles of right and wrong (e.g., reporting safety hazards). Non-moral actions are those that fall outside moral judgment — natural calamities, animal actions, children’s actions, actions of mentally unsound persons, and actions under compulsion.

Perspectives of professional ethics:

  1. Public interest — professionals must prioritize public safety, health, and welfare above all
  2. Truth and honesty — maintain integrity and fairness in all professional dealings
  3. Professional performance — deliver competent services within one’s area of expertise
  4. Faithful service — act as trustworthy agents for employers and clients
  5. Self-regulation — governed by professional codes and standards, not just legal requirements

Dilemma 2080 2071 2070

  • A situation requiring a choice between two equally unpleasant or undesirable alternatives
  • A problem that seems incapable of a satisfactory solution

Moral Dilemma 208020712070

  • A painful decision where every solution involves some kind of loss
  • Whichever option is chosen, someone or something will suffer
  • The decision must be made on the morally correct course of action, not just personal preference
Example: Disposing industrial waste into a river saves costs (economic benefit) but harms the environment and health of downstream communities. Both options involve loss.

Laws of Ethics for Overcoming Moral Dilemmas 20792071

  1. Eternal law: Based on nature and scriptures; common moral standards shared across cultures
  2. Utilitarianism: Greatest amount of benefit for the largest number of people
  3. Universalism: Act as if everyone faced with the same circumstances should do the same
  4. Distributive justice: Belief in the primacy of justice; fair distribution of benefits and burdens
  5. Personal liberty: Value of individual liberty as primary

Ethical Decision Making — Complexity

Ethical decisions in management and engineering have:

  1. Extended consequences: Decisions impact others beyond the decision-maker’s control (e.g., bribery changes governmental processes, pollution affects health)
  2. Multiple alternatives: Rarely a simple yes/no; many options must be evaluated
  3. Mixed outcomes: Social benefits/costs alongside financial benefits/costs (e.g., slightly unsafe product but reduced manufacturing cost)
  4. Uncertain consequences: Outcomes are not always predictable; risks and doubts exist
  5. Personal implications: Decisions affect the individual’s salary, promotion, and career prospects

Conflict of Interest in Profession 2079

Definition: A conflict of interest occurs when a professional’s personal interests (financial, familial, political) compete with or undermine their professional obligations and duties to clients, employers, or the public.

Types of Conflict of Interest in Engineering:

  1. Financial Conflict: An engineer who has shares in a construction company recommends that company for a project — personal financial gain conflicts with duty to select the best contractor
  2. Familial Conflict: Awarding contracts or jobs to relatives or friends regardless of qualification
  3. Professional Conflict: An engineer working for two competing firms simultaneously
  4. Client vs. Public Interest: Client wants to cut costs by reducing safety features, but the engineer’s duty is to protect public safety
  5. Employer vs. Professional Judgment: Employer pressures engineer to approve substandard work to save time/money

How to Handle Conflict of Interest:

  • Disclosure: Declare the conflict openly to all concerned parties
  • Recusal: Withdraw from decision-making where conflict exists
  • Prioritize public safety: Always place public welfare above personal gain
  • Follow code of ethics: Adhere to NEC and NSPE codes
  • Seek advice: Consult professional bodies or ethics committees
  • Document: Keep records of decisions and reasoning for transparency

Secular Methods of Ethical Decision Making 2081 2079

  • Apply the five laws of ethics (eternal, utilitarianism, universalism, distributive justice, personal liberty)
  • Weigh consequences for all stakeholders
  • Consider long-term impact over short-term gain
  • Follow established codes of ethics and professional standards
  • Seek peer consultation and expert advice
  • Maintain transparency and accountability

Vicarious liability occurs when an employer is held liable for the tort committed by their employee in the course of employment. Example: If a junior engineer negligently calculates structural loads causing building damage, the employing firm is also vicariously liable.

Priority while making ethical decisions:

  1. Public safety and welfare — always the highest priority
  2. Utilitarianism — greatest benefit for the largest number
  3. Universalism — apply standards consistently across similar situations
  4. Distributive justice — ensure fair distribution of benefits and burdens

Q: Why is ethics and moral necessary in engineering profession? Write and explain the bases of making ethical decision.

Ans: Engineers design and build infrastructure affecting public safety and welfare. Ethical conduct ensures public protection, prevents corruption and malpractice, builds trust, maintains professional standards, and ensures accountability. Without ethics and morals, engineering works can lead to unsafe structures, environmental damage, and public harm.

Bases of making ethical decisions:

  1. Eternal law: Based on nature and scriptures; common moral standards shared across cultures (e.g., do not harm others)
  2. Utilitarianism: Choose the option that provides the greatest benefit for the largest number of people
  3. Universalism: Act in a way that could be universally applied; if everyone in the same circumstances should do the same thing, then it is ethical
  4. Distributive justice: Decisions should be based on principles of fairness and justice; benefits and burdens should be distributed equitably
  5. Personal liberty: Decisions should respect individual freedom and rights; the value of liberty is primary

Each ethical decision has extended consequences, multiple alternatives, mixed outcomes, uncertain consequences, and personal implications, making ethical decision-making a complex process.

Negligence 20742078

What Kind of Liability Do Engineers Most Suffer From? 2070

Engineers most commonly suffer from Liability in Tort (Negligence Liability).

Reasons:

  1. Nature of Work: Engineers design and supervise structures affecting public safety (buildings, bridges, roads). Any failure in design or supervision can cause injury/death, making them liable for negligence.
  2. Duty of Care: Engineers inherently owe a duty of care to the public, clients, and workers. Any breach — even unintentional — can result in tort liability.
  3. Third-Party Claims: Unlike contractual liability (limited to parties in the contract), tort liability extends to any person harmed by the engineer’s negligence, including the general public and workers.
  4. Professional Standard: Engineers are held to a higher standard of care (“reasonable professional” standard). Mistakes that a layperson might make without liability become actionable negligence for engineers.
  5. Common Scenarios:
    • Structural design error leading to building collapse
    • Inadequate site supervision causing construction accidents
    • Faulty quantity survey leading to cost overruns
    • Improper soil investigation leading to foundation failure
    • Negligent certification of unsafe structures

Protection: Professional indemnity insurance, thorough documentation, peer review, and adherence to codes of practice.

Vicarious Liability 207320792080

  • When an employer is held liable for a tort committed by their employee in the course of employment, even though the employer did not commit it personally
  • Based on the principle that an employee acts as an agent of the employer

Liability of Partners in Tort

  • All partners of a firm are liable for tort committed by any one partner in the ordinary course of business
  • Arises on the basis of vicarious liability — each partner is an agent of co-partners
  • If one partner cannot pay their share, others must cover the shortfall
SOLVED: Tort and Liability [2081 Reg, 4 marks]

Q: Write short notes on Tort and Liability.

Ans: Tort is an action or inaction by one or more individuals that causes loss or damage to another person. It is a private civil wrong, independent of any contract, for which the court provides compensation. The objectives of tort law are: compensation to victim, transferring cost of injury to the responsible person, prevention of harmful actions, and defending victim’s rights. Tort cases can be brought on grounds of negligence, intentional wrong, or strict liability.

Liability is the legal obligation to pay penalty for fulfilling or not fulfilling predefined professional duties. It is the consequence of negligence. Types include: (1) Liability under contract — works not meeting specifications, (2) Liability in tort — causing injury through negligence, and (3) Liability under statute — violating applicable laws. Professionals can be liable for incorrect advice, design, or reports.

SOLVED: Differentiate tort liability and vicarious liability + prove negligence [2079 Baisakh, 4 marks]

Q: Differentiate tort liabilities and vicarious liabilities. What are the conditions necessary to prove professional negligence?

Ans: Tort liability arises when a person’s own action or inaction causes loss or damage to another — the person who committed the tort is directly liable. Vicarious liability arises when an employer is held liable for a tort committed by their employee in the course of employment, even though the employer did not personally commit the wrongful act. In tort liability, the wrongdoer is directly responsible; in vicarious liability, the responsibility extends to a third party (employer).

Conditions to prove professional negligence:

  1. Duty of care — the professional had a legal obligation to exercise reasonable care toward the plaintiff
  2. Breach of duty — the professional failed to meet the expected standard of care or acted inappropriately
  3. Resulting damage — the plaintiff suffered actual loss, injury, or damage as a direct consequence of the breach

Q: What do you understand by Negligence, Tort and Liability? What are the elements of Negligence?

Ans: Negligence is the failure to exercise proper care or attention that a reasonable person would in similar circumstances. When a person fails to meet the standard of behavior expected by law, they may be guilty of negligence.

Tort is an action or inaction causing loss or damage to another, independent of any contract. It is a private civil wrong for which the court provides compensation.

Liability is the legal obligation to compensate for harm caused. It is the consequence of negligence. Types: contractual liability, tort liability, and statutory liability.

Elements of Negligence:

  1. Duty of care — the defendant had a legal obligation to behave in a certain reasonable way
  2. Breach of duty — the defendant failed to fulfill this obligation by acting inappropriately
  3. Resulting damage — the plaintiff suffered injury, loss, or damage directly caused by the defendant’s breach of conduct
SOLVED: Ethics, negligence, tort, liability with examples [2077 Reg, 2+2 marks]

Q: Define ethics. Explain negligence, tort and liability with examples.

Ans: Ethics is a set of guidelines defining accepted practice and behavior for a group of people or profession — the science of distinguishing right from wrong in human conduct.

Negligence: Failure to exercise proper care or meet expected standards. Example: An engineer failing to check soil bearing capacity before designing a foundation, resulting in structural failure.

Tort: An action or inaction causing loss or damage to another, independent of any contract. Example: A defectively designed bridge collapses and injures passersby — the designer can be sued in tort.

Liability: Legal obligation to compensate for harm caused by fulfilling or not fulfilling professional duties. Example: An engineer who certifies a building as safe when it is not is liable for any resulting damage or injury.
