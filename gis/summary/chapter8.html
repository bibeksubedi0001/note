1 DEM / DTM / DSM

  • DEM (Digital Elevation Model): Grid of elevation values representing bare earth.
  • DTM (Digital Terrain Model): DEM plus additional terrain features (breaklines, ridges, rivers).
  • DSM (Digital Surface Model): Includes everything on the surface — buildings, trees, terrain.

2 DEM Data Sources

  • Topographic surveys and contour digitisation.
  • GPS ground surveys and total station.
  • LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging).
  • SRTM (90 m resolution), ASTER GDEM (30 m).
  • Photogrammetry from stereo aerial photographs or satellite imagery.
  • InSAR (Interferometric Synthetic Aperture Radar).

3 DEM Structures

  • Grid (raster): Regular matrix. Simple, efficient for computation.
  • TIN: Irregular triangles. Variable resolution — dense in complex terrain.
  • Contour lines: Lines of equal elevation. Traditional representation.

4 Creating DEM in Raster GIS

  1. Collect source data (contour lines, spot heights, GPS, LiDAR).
  2. Input data into GIS (digitise or import).
  3. Apply interpolation (IDW, Kriging, or Spline).
  4. Convert to raster grid (choose cell size).
  5. Validate against known control points.

5 Slope and Aspect from DEM

slope = arctan(√((∂z/∂x)² + (∂z/∂y)²))

Slope: Rate of elevation change from 3×3 window. Output in degrees (0–90) or percent.

Aspect: Compass direction of steepest descent. 0–360 (N=0, E=90, S=180, W=270). Flat = −1.

6 Applications of DEM

  • Watershed delineation, flow direction, stream network.
  • Slope/aspect analysis for engineering and agriculture.
  • Visibility analysis: viewshed, line of sight.
  • Volume calculation: cut-fill for construction.
  • 3D visualisation and contour generation.
