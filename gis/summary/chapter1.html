1 Definition of GIS

A Geographic Information System (GIS) is a computer-based tool designed to capture, store, manipulate, analyse, manage, and present spatial or geographic data. It links where things are with what things are like.

  • Handles both spatial data (location — coordinates, geometry) and non-spatial / attribute data (characteristics — name, population, type).
  • Think of it as a smart map that can query, analyse, and model real-world phenomena.

2 Components of GIS

  • Hardware: Computers, GPS receivers, digitisers, plotters, servers.
  • Software: ArcGIS, QGIS, GRASS GIS, PostGIS.
  • Data: Spatial (vector/raster) and attribute (tables).
  • People: Analysts, developers, decision-makers.
  • Methods/Procedures: Workflows, standards, analysis techniques.

3 Features and Functions of GIS

  • Data Input: Digitising, scanning, GPS import, remote sensing.
  • Data Storage: Geodatabases, shapefiles, raster formats.
  • Data Manipulation: Projection, transformation, editing.
  • Data Analysis: Overlay, buffer, proximity, network analysis.
  • Data Output: Maps, charts, reports, web services.

4 Four Ms of GIS

  • Measurement: Distance, area, perimeter calculations.
  • Mapping: Thematic maps, choropleth maps, cartographic output.
  • Monitoring: Change detection, temporal analysis.
  • Modelling: Suitability analysis, simulation, prediction.

5 Importance and Applications

GIS enables spatial decision-making across virtually every discipline.

  • Urban planning: Zoning, land-use mapping, infrastructure.
  • Environmental management: Deforestation monitoring, pollution tracking.
  • Disaster management: Flood modelling, evacuation planning.
  • Civil engineering: Route selection, cut-fill analysis, site suitability.
  • Agriculture: Precision farming, crop health monitoring.
  • Public health: Disease outbreak mapping, facility siting.

7 GIS Data Feeds

  • Topographic maps and survey data.
  • GPS observations.
  • Remote sensing / satellite imagery.
  • Census and statistical databases.
  • Field surveys and ground-truth data.
  • Existing digital databases and scanned documents.

8 Historical Development

  • Pre-1960s: Manual cartography, transparent overlay analysis (Ian McHarg).
  • 1960s: Canada Geographic Information System (CGIS) — Roger Tomlinson.
  • 1970s–80s: ESRI founded (1969); ARC/INFO released (1982).
  • 1990s: Desktop GIS (ArcView, MapInfo); GPS civilian access.
  • 2000s: Google Earth (2005), open-source GIS (QGIS, PostGIS).
  • 2010s–present: Cloud GIS, mobile mapping, AI in spatial analysis.

+Define GIS. Explain its key features and functions. (2076, 2074, 2072, 2081)

+What are the components of a GIS? (2073, 2078, 2079)

Five components: Hardware, Software, Data, People, Methods. Explain each briefly with examples. Draw a diagram with GIS at centre connected to all five.

+Differentiate between spatial and non-spatial data with examples. (2071 Bhadra, 2075)

Spatial: Data with location — coordinates, geometry (point for well, line for road, polygon for district). Non-spatial: Descriptive attributes — name, population, elevation. In GIS these are linked.

+What are the Four Ms of GIS? (2078, 2082)

Measurement, Mapping, Monitoring, Modelling. Give one application example per M.

+Why is GIS important in civil engineering? List applications. (2071, 2073, 2079, 2080)

Route planning, site suitability, flood modelling, cut-fill analysis, land-use mapping, infrastructure management. Emphasise spatial decision-making.

+Compare GIS with Cartography and CAD. (2072, 2074, 2075)

Use a comparison table. Key: GIS has topology and analytical capability; Cartography is static; CAD lacks attribute linkage.

+What are the data feeds of GIS? (2071, 2073, 2076, 2081)

Maps, GPS, remote sensing, census/statistics, field surveys, digitised documents. Explain how each feeds data into the system.

+Describe the historical development of GIS. (2075)

Key milestones: CGIS (1960s), ARC/INFO (1982), Desktop GIS (1990s), Google Earth (2005). Mention Roger Tomlinson as "Father of GIS."
