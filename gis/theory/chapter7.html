1. What is Spatial Interpolation?

Spatial interpolation is the mathematical and statistical process of estimating the values of a continuous geographic phenomenon at unknown, unsampled locations based on the known values of surrounding sampled points.

2. What is the purpose of spatial interpolation?

In reality, it is impossible, physically and financially, to measure data (like elevation, rainfall, or soil strength) at every single square meter of a study area. The primary purpose of interpolation is to take isolated sample points (like weather stations, borehole logs, or GPS spot heights) and create a continuous surface (a raster grid) that models the entire region.

3. Why is interpolation needed for producing a DEM?

A Digital Elevation Model (DEM) is a continuous raster grid where every single pixel contains an elevation value. However, field surveyors and LiDAR point clouds only capture elevation at discrete, scattered points. Interpolation is absolutely strictly required to calculate the elevation of the "empty" spaces between the surveyed points, effectively creating a solid, continuous topographic surface from isolated data.

Bilinear Interpolation (Lectures p.26)

Bilinear interpolation calculates values at a grid location using the four closest cell centres. It assigns output values by taking the weighted average based on distance from each neighbouring cell centre. It produces a smoother surface than nearest-neighbour but less smooth than cubic convolution (which uses 16 cells).

Best for: Continuous data without distinct boundaries (e.g., elevation, temperature). Not suitable for categorical/classified data.

TIN Interpolation (Lectures pp.27–28)

TIN interpolation relies on constructing a Delaunay triangulation from sample points. Each new prediction point is located within a triangle, and its value is calculated as a weighted combination of the three triangle vertices. The method is fast, efficient, and works well with many observations. However, it does not smooth the data, and predictions depend only on the three vertices of the enclosing triangle.

Limitations: Small-scale variations (e.g., abrupt elevation changes) may create unrealistic discontinuous areas. Accuracy is strongly dependent on the density of observations.

Part 2: Classification and Types of Interpolation

  2. Explain different types of interpolation methods in raster GIS and their application in different engineering fields [4] (2074 Bhadra, Q3a)
  3. Explain different types of interpolation in Raster GIS (2075 Magh, Q5a)

If asked to classify or explain the different types break them down into two main families:

A. Deterministic Methods

These use predefined mathematical equations based on the distance or geometric layout of the known points. They do not account for statistical probability.

  • Inverse Distance Weighted (IDW): Assigns values to unknown points by using a weighted average of the surrounding known points. The closer a known point is the more heavily it influences the unknown value.
  • Spline (Minimum Curvature): Fits a mathematically smooth flexible "rubber sheet" through all the known data points minimizing the overall curvature of the surface.
  • Thiessen Polygons (Nearest Neighbor): Creates sharp boundaries. Any unknown location simply takes the exact value of the single nearest known point.

B. Geostatistical Methods

These rely on both the distance and the statistical relationship (spatial autocorrelation) among the measured points.

  • Kriging: A highly advanced method that not only estimates the unknown values but also provides a map of the estimated error/uncertainty. It looks at the overall spatial trend and variance in the dataset.

Kriging Interpolation (Lectures p.29)

Kriging is a geostatistical technique that considers both the distance and the degree of variation between known data points. Unlike IDW, Kriging is statistically more sophisticated because it allows identifying distortions (possible errors) in the data through the variance output. It produces both a prediction surface and a confidence/error surface, making it the most scientifically rigorous interpolation method.

Part 3: Detailed Method Comparison (IDW vs. Spline)

The examiners frequently ask you to describe one method in detail discuss its pros and cons or compare IDW and Spline directly.

Inverse Distance Weighted (IDW)

  • Concept: Assumes that "things that are closer are more alike."
  • Advantages: Very simple to understand fast to compute and respects the exact values of the sampled points.
  • Disadvantages: It cannot predict a value higher than the highest known point or lower than the lowest known point (no extrapolation). It often creates artificial "bullseye" or circular patterns around isolated data points.

Spline (Minimum Curvature)

  • Concept: Fits a smooth curve through the points much like bending a flexible ruler.
  • Advantages: Creates highly visually appealing smooth surfaces. Unlike IDW a Spline can predict values outside the range of the input data (e.g. predicting a mountain peak even if surveyors only measured points on the slopes).
  • Disadvantages: Highly sensitive to outliers. If one data point is incorrectly recorded as extremely high the Spline will wildly distort the surrounding surface to force the "rubber sheet" through that error.

1. What is Raster Analysis?

Raster analysis is the process of performing spatial operations on raster (grid-based) data to derive new information. Unlike vector analysis which works with discrete features, raster analysis processes continuous surfaces where each cell holds a numeric value.

Advantages and Disadvantages of Raster-Based Analysis

AdvantagesDisadvantages
Simple, uniform data structure (grid of cells)Large file sizes — every cell stores a value even in empty areas

2. Types of Raster Operations (Local, Focal, Zonal, Global)

Raster operations are classified by the spatial extent of cells involved in each calculation:

Operation TypeDescriptionInputExample
LocalOperates on each cell independently, using corresponding cells from one or more layersIndividual cell valuesNDVI calculation: (NIR − Red)/(NIR + Red) for each pixel
Focal (Neighborhood)Operates on a cell and its surrounding neighbors (typically 3×3 window)Cell + its neighborhoodSlope, Aspect, Hillshade — use 3×3 window around each cell
ZonalOperates on groups of cells that share a common zone valueAll cells within each zoneAverage elevation per land use category; total rainfall per watershed
GlobalOperates using all cells in the entire rasterEntire raster datasetEuclidean distance from each cell to nearest source; flow accumulation

Why NDVI is Local and Slope is Focal: NDVI is a local function because it calculates each pixel's value using only the corresponding NIR and Red band values at that same pixel location. Slope is a focal (neighborhood) function because calculating the slope at any cell requires reading the elevation values of its 8 surrounding neighbors in a 3×3 moving window.

Map Algebra (Tomlin's Framework)

Map algebra is a set of rules and operators for performing cell-by-cell mathematical operations on raster data, first formalized by Dana Tomlin. It is the raster equivalent of SQL for vector data.

  • Arithmetic operations: Addition, subtraction, multiplication, division of raster layers (e.g., Weighted Overlay = 0.5 × Slope + 0.3 × Soil + 0.2 × LandUse)
  • Boolean operations: AND, OR, NOT — used for suitability queries (e.g., Slope < 10 AND LandUse = "Grassland")
  • Relational operations: Greater than, less than, equal to — create binary rasters (1/0)
  • Conditional operations: IF-THEN-ELSE — reclassify based on conditions

Map Algebra Operators (Lectures p.29)

Algebraic expressions create new raster data from existing ones. The basic operator types:

  • Arithmetic: +, −, ×, ÷ (e.g., NDVI = (NIR − Red) / (NIR + Red))
  • Relational: >, ≥, =, <, ≤, ≠ (e.g., Select cells where elevation > 500m)
  • Boolean: AND, OR, NOT (e.g., slope < 15 AND land_use = “forest”)
  • Assignment: = (assign new values based on conditions)
  • Accumulative: Running totals (e.g., flow accumulation)

Reclassification

Reclassify is the process of reassigning cell values in a raster to new values based on defined criteria. It converts continuous data into discrete categories for analysis.

  • Example: Reclassify a slope raster: 0–5° → 1 (Flat), 5–15° → 2 (Moderate), 15–30° → 3 (Steep), >30° → 4 (Very Steep)
  • Purpose: Simplifies complex data for overlay analysis. Essential in suitability modeling where continuous data must be converted to a common ranking scale (e.g., 1–10).
  • Types:
    • Range-based: Group values into ranges (0–100 → Low, 100–200 → Medium)
    • Individual value: Replace specific values (NoData → 0)

Hillshade

Hillshade creates a 3D shaded relief representation of a terrain surface by simulating the illumination of a surface from a hypothetical light source (the sun). It is a focal function that uses a 3×3 neighborhood window.

  • Input: DEM (Digital Elevation Model)
  • Parameters:
    • Altitude (Sun Elevation Angle): Height of the sun above the horizon (default: 45°)
    • Azimuth (Sun Direction): Compass direction of the sun (default: 315° = northwest)
  • Formula: Hillshade = 255 × [(cos(Zenith) × cos(Slope)) + (sin(Zenith) × sin(Slope) × cos(Azimuth − Aspect))]
  • Output: Grayscale raster (0–255) where bright areas face the sun and dark areas are in shadow
  • Application: Visual enhancement of terrain maps; used as a background layer beneath semi-transparent thematic layers for better 3D perception

Viewshed (View Shade)

Viewshed analysis determines which areas on a terrain surface are visible from one or more observation points. It uses line-of-sight calculations across a DEM.

  • Input: DEM + one or more observer points (with optional height offset)
  • Output: Binary raster where 1 = visible from the observer, 0 = not visible (hidden by terrain)
  • How it works: For each cell in the DEM, a straight line is drawn from the observer to that cell. If any intermediate cell's elevation blocks the line-of-sight, the target cell is marked as not visible.
  • Applications:
    • Locating telecom towers to maximize signal coverage
    • Road alignment to identify scenic routes
    • Military: Finding hidden positions not visible from enemy locations
    • Environmental: Visual impact assessment of proposed buildings or wind turbines

Euclidean Distance

Euclidean Distance calculates the straight-line distance from each raster cell to the nearest source feature. It is a global function.

  • Input: Source raster or feature (e.g., river, road, hospital locations)
  • Output: Distance raster where each cell value = its distance to the nearest source
  • Application: Finding areas within a certain distance of a river (for flood analysis) or determining accessibility to hospitals

Cost Path Analysis

Cost Path finds the least-cost route between a source and destination across a surface where movement cost varies by location.

  • Input:
    • Cost Surface: Raster where each cell value represents the cost of traversing that cell (based on slope, land cover, etc.)
    • Source point: Starting location
    • Destination point: Ending location
  • Process: (1) Create a Cost Distance raster showing accumulated cost from source, (2) Create a Back Link raster recording direction of cheapest path, (3) Trace back from destination to source using the back link
  • Application: Finding optimal road alignment that minimizes construction cost across varying terrain; pipeline routing; wildlife corridor planning
  • Concept: This function calculates summary statistics (like Area, Sum, Mean, Maximum, Minimum) for a continuous "Value Raster" strictly within the boundaries defined by a separate "Zone Dataset". The Zone Dataset can be either a vector polygon shapefile or an integer raster.
  • Mechanism: The software identifies all the pixels of the Value Raster that physically fall inside Zone A, lumps them together, calculates the requested statistic (e.g., the average), and then moves on to Zone B.
  • Exam Example: For your final year project on Site Response Analysis, you have a highly detailed, continuous raster grid showing Peak Ground Acceleration (PGA) across the Kathmandu Valley. You also have a polygon shapefile of the municipal wards. Using the Summarize Zone tool, the software will calculate and output a table showing the exact Mean and Maximum PGA specifically for each individual ward, allowing you to rank the wards by seismic vulnerability.

3. Proximity Map Calculation & Distance

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ON THIS TOPIC:

  1. Explain proximity map calculation and summarize zone functions of Raster GIS [4] (2075 Magh, Q5b)
  2. Explain the following Raster function with relevant example (a) View shade (b) Euclidean Distance (c) Reclassify (d) Slope and aspect (e) Hillshade [8] (2076 Bhadra, Q7a)

Distance and Proximity are often used interchangeably in raster analysis, but they represent slightly different ways of modeling "how close" something is.

Distance (Euclidean Distance)

  • Concept: Calculates the straight-line distance from every single cell center to the closest source feature.
  • Mechanism: It generates a continuous raster where the numerical value of every single pixel represents its distance (in meters or feet) to the target.
  • Exam Example: When mapping the "Smart Village" initiative, you need a general overview of water accessibility. You run a Distance function on the "Water Tap" point layer. The output is a gradient raster where pixels right next to the taps have values near 0m, and pixels on the outskirts of the village have values of 500m or more.

Proximity (Raster Buffering / Allocation)

  • Concept: Instead of a continuous distance gradient, a Proximity calculation often groups cells into distinct bands or allocates them to the nearest source.
  • Mechanism: It classifies pixels based on a threshold. For example, assigning a value of "1" to all pixels within 30 meters of a source, and "0" to everything else (acting like a vector buffer, but in grid format).
  • Exam Example: To enforce sanitation regulations for a new rural toilet, it must be located at least 30 meters away from any drinking water well. You run a Proximity map calculation on the wells, instructing the tool to flag all raster cells within a 30m radius. This instantly generates a restricted "no-build" pixel zone across your study area.

2. The Reservoir Site Selection Scenario

Question: You are tasked with identifying suitable locations for building a new reservoir in a region. The ideal location must meet the following criteria:

  1. The area must have a slope of less than 10 degrees to ensure stability.
  2. It must be within 1 kilometer of a river to allow for water supply.
  3. The land cover must be non-forested (e.g., grassland or bare soil) to minimize environmental impact.
  4. The area must have a high water-retention capacity, indicated by soil types classified as "clay" or "silt".

The data provided are DEM, Soil type, River, and Land cover data. Using raster data analysis, describe the steps you would take to identify suitable locations. Include at least four raster analysis functions in your explanation.

Approach: This is a classic Boolean Overlay (True/False) suitability model. Each dataset is independently processed, reclassified into binary (1=Suitable, 0=Unsuitable), and then multiplied cell-by-cell. The multiplication acts as a logical AND — a cell must pass all four tests to be marked suitable.

The Four Raster Analysis Functions Used

  1. Slope (from DEM): A Focal/Neighborhood function that computes the rate of elevation change using a 3×3 moving window. Input: DEM raster. Output: Continuous slope raster in degrees (0°–90°). This is a neighborhood function because each pixel's slope depends on its 8 surrounding neighbors.
  2. Euclidean Distance (from River): A Distance function that calculates straight-line distance from every cell center to the nearest river feature. Input: River vector (or raster). Output: Continuous distance raster in meters. Cells near rivers have low values; distant cells have high values.
  3. Reclassify: A Local function that reassigns ranges of continuous values into discrete binary classes. Applied independently to all four datasets:
    • Slope: 0°–10° → 1, >10° → 0
    • Distance: 0–1000 m → 1, >1000 m → 0
    • Land Cover: Grassland/Bare soil → 1, Forest → 0
    • Soil Type: Clay/Silt → 1, Sand/Gravel → 0
  4. Raster Calculator / Map Algebra: A Local function that performs cell-by-cell arithmetic across multiple aligned rasters. Here we multiply all four binary grids: Final = Slope_Rec × Distance_Rec × LandCover_Rec × Soil_Rec. Any cell with a 0 in any layer becomes 0 in the output (Boolean AND logic).

Workflow Overview:

Step 1: Calculate Slope (Tool: Slope)

Input the DEM raster and run the Slope function to generate a continuous raster showing the steepness in degrees.

Step 2: Calculate Proximity (Tool: Euclidean Distance)

Input the River vector data and run the Euclidean Distance tool to create a raster where every pixel value represents its distance from the river.

Step 3: Standardize the Criteria (Tool: Reclassify)

You must convert all datasets into a binary scale where 1 = Suitable and 0 = Unsuitable.

  • Reclassify Slope: 0° to 10° = 1. Greater than 10° = 0.
  • Reclassify Distance: 0 to 1000m = 1. Greater than 1000m = 0.
  • Reclassify Land Cover: Grassland/Bare soil = 1. Forested = 0.
  • Reclassify Soil: Clay/Silt = 1. Sand/Gravel = 0.

// 1 * 1 * 1 * 1 = 1 → All criteria met
// 1 * 1 * 0 * 1 = 0 → One criteria failed
Output:

Because you are multiplying, if any single criteria fails (has a 0), the final pixel value becomes 0. The output raster will only have a value of 1 where all criteria are perfectly met.

4. Rice Crop Suitability — Weighted Overlay (14 Marks Numerical)

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ON THIS TOPIC:

  1. Grid Code of a raster data with 25 m grid resolution is shown below. Derive a suitable map and suitable area for Rice Crop. Suitable parameters on 1 to 10 scale for slope, soil, and landuse are given in Table 1. The weightage for slope, soil, and landuse are 50%, 30%, and 20% respectively. Assume the value greater than 6 is suitable. [14] (2074 Magh, Q6)

This is a Weighted Overlay problem. Unlike the Boolean (AND) approach above, here each input layer has a different importance (weight), and we assign suitability scores on a 1–10 scale, then compute a weighted sum. The final value determines suitability.

The Formula

Suitability = (Slope Score × 0.50) + (Soil Score × 0.30) + (LandUse Score × 0.20)

If Suitability > 6 → Suitable for rice crop. Otherwise → Not suitable.

Step-by-Step Solution Method

The question provides three input grids (slope grid, soil grid, landuse grid) and a reclassification table. Follow these steps:

Step 1: Reclassify each input grid using the given Table 1 (which maps grid codes to suitability scores 1–10).

Step 2: Apply weights — multiply each reclassified cell by its weight factor (0.50, 0.30, 0.20).

Step 3: Add — sum the three weighted grids cell-by-cell to get the final suitability score per cell.

Step 4: Threshold — any cell with suitability > 6 is suitable; others are not suitable.

Step 5: Calculate area — count suitable cells × cell area (25m × 25m = 625 m² per cell).

Chapter Summary

This chapter focuses on raster-based spatial analysis techniques. Spatial interpolation methods estimate values at unsampled locations from known sample points — key methods include IDW (Inverse Distance Weighting), Kriging (geostatistical, uses semivariograms), Spline (smooth surface fitting), and Thiessen/Voronoi polygons (nearest-neighbor assignment). Raster analysis covers local operations (cell-by-cell calculations), focal/neighborhood operations (using moving windows/kernels), zonal operations (statistics within defined zones), and global operations (entire raster). Topological overlay in raster involves combining multiple raster layers through arithmetic (add, subtract, multiply) and logical (AND, OR, NOT) operations for suitability analysis. Map algebra provides the mathematical framework for raster calculations. Statistical analysis includes descriptive statistics, correlation, and regression on spatial data. Integrated spatial analysis combines vector and raster methods for complex multi-criteria decision making like site selection and land suitability analysis.
