1. The Relationship: Datums, GCS, and Projections (4 Marks)

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ON THIS TOPIC:

  1. Explain the relationship between datums, projections, and coordinate systems in GIS. What are the UTM zones used in Nepal and which areas are covered by each of the zones? [4+2] (2080 Chaitra, Q2)

To build a coordinate system, you must follow a strict three-step mathematical sequence:

  1. Datum: You must first define the exact size and shape of the Earth (which is bumpy and irregular). A datum fits a smooth mathematical ellipsoid to the Earth and anchors it to a specific origin point.
  2. Geographic Coordinate System (GCS): Once you have a Datum, you wrap a 3D grid of intersecting lines (Latitudes and Longitudes) around it to assign angular coordinates to every place on Earth.
  3. Projected Coordinate System (PCS): Because computer screens and printed maps are flat, you use a Map Projection to mathematically flatten that 3D GCS grid into a 2D Cartesian (X, Y) plane.

Key Definitions (Textbook pp.192–200)

  • Geoid: An equipotential surface of the Earth’s gravity field that closely approximates mean sea level. It is an undulated (irregular) surface perpendicular to the direction of gravity at every point. The Geoid serves as the reference for vertical measurements (orthometric heights).
  • Ellipsoid (Reference Ellipsoid): A mathematically defined oblate ellipsoid used to approximate the shape of the Earth. It is defined by two parameters: semi-major axis a and flattening f.
  • Flattening: f = (a − b) / a where a = semi-major axis, b = semi-minor axis.
  • WGS84 Parameters: a = 6,378,137 m, b = 6,356,752 m, f = 1/298.257 (used globally by GPS).
  • Local Horizontal Datum: An ellipsoid positioned and oriented relative to local mean sea level using a fundamental point’s geographic coordinates (φ, λ, h). Different countries use different datums (e.g., Everest 1830 for Nepal/India, Clarke 1866 for North America).
  • ITRS/ITRF: International Terrestrial Reference System — origin at Earth’s centre of mass, Z-axis toward mean north pole, X-axis toward Greenwich meridian. Realized through the ITRF network of ground control stations.

2. Why is a Datum Important? (2 Marks)

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ON THIS TOPIC:

  1. Why is datum important in GIS? Describe about two common datums? Describe in brief three types of map projections [1+2+] (2072 Magh, Q2)

Without a datum, coordinates mean nothing. A datum provides the foundational reference frame for all measurements. If two engineers use different datums (e.g., one uses WGS84 and the other uses Everest 1830), the exact same GPS coordinate could land hundreds of meters apart on the ground.

  • Two common datums: WGS 1984 (the global standard used by GPS) and Everest 1830 (a local datum optimized for the Indian subcontinent and Nepal).

3. The Significance of the Prime Meridian and Equator in GCS

In a 3D Geographic Coordinate System, you need a starting point (an origin of 0°, 0°) to measure angles from the center of the Earth.

  • The Equator: The 0° line of Latitude. It physically divides the Earth into the Northern and Southern Hemispheres.
  • The Prime Meridian (Greenwich): The 0° line of Longitude. It mathematically divides the Earth into the Eastern and Western Hemispheres.

1. What is Map Projection and Why is it Necessary?

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ON THIS TOPIC:

  1. Define Projection. [2] (2071 Bhadra, Q2a)
  2. Why is map projection important? Explain which map projection system is used for preparing maps of Nepal? [2+3] (2072 Ashwin, Q1b)
  3. Why is map projection necessary? State two main differences between a Geographic Coordinate System and Projected Coordinate System [4] (2073 Magh, Q2a)
  • Definition: A map projection is a mathematical formula used to systematically transform the 3D spherical surface of the Earth onto a flat, 2D surface (a map).
  • Why it is necessary: You cannot peel an orange and lay its skin perfectly flat without stretching or tearing it. Similarly, you cannot represent the 3D Earth on a 2D computer screen without introducing distortion. Projections are necessary to control that distortion, allowing us to print maps and perform linear mathematics on a flat Cartesian plane.

2. GCS vs. PCS & Why Civil Engineering Requires PCS (3 to 4 Marks)

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ON THIS TOPIC:

  1. Why is map projection necessary? State two main differences between a Geographic Coordinate System and Projected Coordinate System [4] (2073 Magh, Q2a)
  2. Why civil engineering projects requires to use projected coordinate system for the analysis? [3] (2078 Chaitra, Q2b)

The Engineering Need: You cannot accurately calculate the area of a watershed, the length of a highway alignment, or the cut-and-fill volume of a construction site in "degrees." Civil engineering strictly requires linear measurements in meters on a flat Cartesian (X, Y) grid, which is only possible using a Projected Coordinate System (PCS).

3. Three Main Categories of Map Projections

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ON THIS TOPIC:

  1. What is map projection? List the three main categories of map projection. Explain Nepalese Projection System with all parameters. [1+1+4] (2071 Magh, Q1b)
  2. Why is datum important in GIS? Describe about two common datums? Describe in brief three types of map projections [1+2+] (2072 Magh, Q2)
  1. Cylindrical (e.g., Mercator): Projects the Earth onto a cylinder wrapped around the equator. Advantage: Preserves straight lines for navigation and accurate shapes at the equator. Disadvantage: Massively distorts the size of landmasses near the poles (makes Greenland look huge).
  2. Conic (e.g., Lambert Conformal Conic): Projects the Earth onto a cone placed over a pole. Advantage: Highly accurate for mapping mid-latitude countries that stretch East-to-West (like the USA or Russia).
  3. Planar / Azimuthal: Projects the Earth onto a flat plane touching a single point. Advantage: Excellent for mapping the North and South Poles and calculating shortest-distance flight paths.

Projection Distortion Properties (Textbook p.223)

  • Conformal (Orthomorphic): Preserves local angles and shapes. Examples: Mercator, Transverse Mercator, Lambert Conformal Conic. Used for navigation and large-scale engineering surveys.
  • Equal-Area (Equivalent): Preserves area relationships. Examples: Albers Equal-Area Conic, Mollweide. Used for thematic maps showing density or distribution.
  • Equidistant: Preserves distances along certain lines (e.g., from centre point or along meridians). Examples: Equidistant Conic.
  • Important Rule: A projection cannot be both conformal and equal-area simultaneously. Choosing a projection always involves a trade-off between these properties.

False Origin / False Easting & Northing (Textbook p.213)

In many coordinate systems, large positive values are added to the origin to avoid negative coordinates. For example, in UTM, a false easting of 500,000 m is added to the central meridian. This ensures all coordinates within a zone are positive, simplifying calculations and data storage.

1. What is UTM?

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ON THIS TOPIC:

  1. Write short notes on: UTM and MUTM coordinate system [2] (2074 Bhadra, Q6iv)
  2. Explain the relationship between datums, projections, and coordinate systems in GIS. What are the UTM zones used in Nepal and which areas are covered by each of the zones? [4+2] (2080 Chaitra, Q2)

Universal Transverse Mercator (UTM) is a global projected coordinate system. It divides the Earth into 60 vertical zones, each being 6° of longitude wide.

  • UTM in Nepal: Nepal falls across two UTM zones: Zone 44N (covers Western Nepal) and Zone 45N (covers Central and Eastern Nepal). The dividing line is the 84°E meridian.

2. Why does Nepal use a different system (MUTM) instead of standard UTM?

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ON THIS TOPIC:

  1. Why Nepal uses a different coordinate system than the UTM? [2] (2073 Magh, Q2b)
  2. Write the differences between UTM coordinate system and coordinate system(s) used in Nepal? [3] (2076 Bhadra, Q2a)

While UTM is great globally, a 6° wide zone introduces scale distortion at its extreme eastern and western edges. Nepal is a highly mountainous country that requires extreme precision for cadastral (land ownership) and civil engineering surveys. To eliminate this distortion, Nepal "modified" the UTM system by cutting the zone width in half.

3. Why does Nepal's MUTM use a 3° zone instead of 6°?

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ON THIS TOPIC:

  1. Why Nepal's Coordinate system (MUTM) uses a 3░ zone instead of 6░ compared to UTM? [2] (2073 Bhadra, Q2c)

By narrowing the projection zone from 6° to 3° of longitude, the mapped surface stays much closer to the central meridian line where distortion is mathematically zero. This modification practically eliminates scale distortion, ensuring that a 100-meter pipeline drawn on the GIS map exactly matches 100 meters on the rugged physical ground.

2.2 Maps and Their Characteristics (Scale and Abstraction)

1. What is Scale? Types of Scales with Examples (6 Marks)

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ON THIS TOPIC:

  1. What is scale and write down different types of scales with example? [6] (2071 Bhadra, Q10)

To secure the full 6 marks, you must define scale and explicitly list the three ways it is represented on a map.

  • Definition: Map scale is the mathematical ratio between a distance measured on the map and the corresponding actual distance on the surface of the Earth. It tells the user how much the real world has been "shrunk" to fit on the paper.

Type 1: Representative Fraction (RF) or Ratio Scale

  • Concept: A unitless fraction or ratio. Whatever unit you measure with on the map is the same unit on the ground.
  • Example: 1:50,000. (This means 1 centimeter on the map equals 50,000 centimeters on the ground, or 1 inch equals 50,000 inches).

Type 2: Verbal Scale (Word Statement)

  • Concept: A simple text sentence that equates a specific map unit to a different, more understandable ground unit.
  • Example: "One centimeter equals one kilometer" or "One inch equals one mile."

Type 3: Graphical Scale (Bar Scale)

  • Concept: A visual line or bar drawn on the map, divided into equally spaced segments marked with real-world distances.
  • Example:
  • Major Advantage: If a printed map is photocopied and enlarged or shrunk, the RF scale becomes completely invalid, but the graphical scale changes size with the map and remains mathematically correct.

    • 2. Map is a 2D model of reality. Explain this with examples.

    A map is not a perfect photograph of the Earth; it is a highly simplified, 2D mathematical model. You must explain this using the concepts of Selection and Abstraction:

    • Selection: A mapmaker cannot show every single rock, tree, and house, or the map would be too cluttered to read. They select only the features relevant to the map's theme.
    • Abstraction (Dimensional Reduction): Complex 3D real-world objects are flattened into simple 2D geometric symbols (Points, Lines, and Polygons).
    • The Example: A sprawling, multi-story 3D hospital building is abstracted into a single 2D point (a red cross symbol). A complex 3D river with varying depths and flow volumes is abstracted into a single, flat 2D blue line. A massive 3D mountain is reduced to flat 2D contour lines.

    Map Characteristics (Lectures pp.9–10)

    • Selection: Maps can only depict selected geographic phenomena essential to the reader. It is impossible to display everything exactly as it appears on the Earth’s surface.
    • Abstraction: The process of explicitly defining and representing real-world features using three basic geometric forms: point, line, and polygon (area).
    • Symbolization: Maps use symbols (dots for cities, lines for roads, colour fills for regions) to represent geographic features. A legend/key explains what symbols mean.
    • Accuracy: Whether features are represented in the correct location and as the correct type. Larger symbols may shift feature positions to maintain visibility.
    • Resolution: How accurately location and shape of map features can be depicted for a given scale. As scale decreases, smaller features must be generalized to points or lines. The smallest representable size is called the Minimum Mapping Unit.

    2.3 Automated Cartography versus GIS

    No direct past exam questions have been asked from this section, but it is part of the syllabus and may appear in future exams.

    Automated Cartography (also called Computer-Assisted Cartography or CAC) refers to using computers to automate the traditional map-making process — drawing, lettering, symbolizing, and printing maps. While it produces high-quality maps efficiently, it is fundamentally a drawing tool, not an analytical tool.

    Key Differences

    AspectAutomated CartographyGIS
    Primary purposeMap production and displaySpatial analysis and decision support
    Data modelGraphics (lines, symbols, text)Spatial objects with linked attributes
    TopologyNot maintained; lines are just drawingsTopology maintained; spatial relationships are encoded
    Analysis capabilityNone — cannot answer spatial queriesFull spatial analysis (overlay, buffer, network, etc.)
    Database linkageNo attribute databaseIntegrated attribute database (RDBMS)
    Query exampleCannot query — only displays"Find all parcels > 500 m² within flood zone"
    OutputStatic mapsMaps + reports + models + 3D visualizations

    In simple terms: Automated cartography draws maps; GIS thinks with maps. Automated cartography is like a fancy printer — it produces beautiful output but cannot answer questions about the data. GIS is like a spatial calculator that can both analyze and display.

    Why the Distinction Matters: Many early "GIS" systems were actually just automated cartography tools. The true power of GIS emerged when spatial databases and analytical capabilities were added on top of the cartographic display. Modern GIS software (ArcGIS, QGIS) combines both cartographic excellence and analytical power.

    2.6 Precision and Error in GIS

    1. Accuracy vs. Precision in GIS (3 to 5 Marks)

    PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ON THIS TOPIC:

    1. Explain about accuracy and precision [Part of 3] (2076 Bhadra, Q2b)
    2. Explain accuracy and precision in GIS [Part of 5] (2074 Magh, Q2b)

    Examiners love this question because engineers must know the difference. They are not the same thing!

    • Accuracy: This refers to the degree of truth. It is how closely a measured GIS coordinate matches the actual, true location of that object in the real physical world.
      • Example: If a manhole is physically located exactly at coordinate X = 100, Y = 100, and your GPS records it at X = 100.1, Y = 100.1, your data is highly accurate.
    • Precision: This refers to the degree of exactness, detail, or repeatability. It is usually dictated by how many decimal places the system records, regardless of whether those numbers are actually correct.
      • Example: If you use a cheap smartphone GPS and it records the manhole's location as X = 542.123456, Y = 890.123456, the data is highly precise (six decimal places of exactness!), but it is completely inaccurate because it is hundreds of meters away from the true location.

    (For the exam, draw the classic "bullseye" target. Draw one target with dots tightly clustered together but far from the center—label it "High Precision, Low Accuracy". Draw another target with dots scattered widely but evenly around the center bullseye—label it "Low Precision, High Accuracy".)

    Types of Error (Lectures pp.11–12)

    • Positional inaccuracy & imprecision: Accuracy is a function of the scale at which a map was created. A point on a map represents a probable location within a certain area.
    • Attribute inaccuracy: Non-spatial data linked to locations may be inaccurate (e.g., misspelled place names, rounded population figures).
    • Temporal inaccuracy: Data age is a source of error. When data sources are too old, the information base may have changed significantly.
    • Logical inaccuracy: Information stored illogically during digitizing (e.g., political boundary polygons crossing, contour lines meeting, rivers not connecting at confluences).

    The Formula

    The allowable RMS (Root Mean Square) planimetric error defines the maximum acceptable positional error when digitizing or georeferencing a map. The US National Map Accuracy Standard (NMAS) states:

    Allowable RMS Error = 0.5 mm × Scale Denominator

    The 0.5 mm represents the smallest mark a human can make on paper (the width of a fine pen line).

    Distance Between Two Geographic Coordinates

    PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ON THIS TOPIC:

    1. Calculate the distance between two geographic coordinates. (2076 Bhadra, 2079 Chaitra)

    The distance between two geographic coordinates can be computed using the simplified formulas:

    • Latitude difference: 1° latitude ≈ 111.32 km (constant everywhere on Earth)
    • Longitude difference: 1° longitude ≈ 111.32 × cos(φ) km, where φ is the average latitude
    • Total Distance: √(dN² + dE²) using Pythagorean theorem

    3. Errors in GIS data made through "Actual Changes" (Temporal Error)

    PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ON THIS TOPIC:

    1. Describe briefly on errors in GIS data made through actual changes [2] (2075 Bhadra, Q2c)

    This question asks about a very specific type of error: Temporal (Time-based) Error.

    • Concept: The physical world is constantly changing, but a GIS database is a static snapshot of the exact moment the data was collected. If reality changes, the GIS data instantly contains an error unless it is manually updated.
    • Examples to write down:
      1. A massive monsoon flood causes the Koshi River to physically change its course. If your GIS uses a river shapefile from three years ago, your analysis will be completely wrong because of this "actual change."
      2. A new highway is constructed through a forest. Your five-year-old Land Use raster will still show that area as "Dense Forest," creating a major error in any environmental impact calculation.
