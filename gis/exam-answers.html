Question 1: GNSS Definition & GIS Database for Water Supply Infrastructure [5 + 25 Marks]

Part (a): Define GNSS [5 Marks]

GNSS (Global Navigation Satellite System) is a general term describing any satellite constellation that provides positioning, navigation, and timing (PNT) services on a global or regional basis. Unlike "GPS," which refers only to the U.S. system, GNSS is an umbrella term covering all such systems worldwide.

Key Point: GNSS = the entire family of satellite navigation systems. GPS is just one member of that family.

How GNSS Works

Three Segments of GNSS

SegmentDescription
Space SegmentThe satellites orbiting Earth, continuously broadcasting navigation signals.
Control SegmentGround stations that monitor satellite health, update orbits, and synchronize clocks.
User SegmentGNSS receivers (survey-grade, smartphones, vehicles) that compute position from satellite signals.

Advantages of Multi-Constellation GNSS

  • More satellites visible = better accuracy, especially in urban canyons and hilly terrain
  • Faster fix — time-to-first-fix (TTFF) is reduced
  • Redundancy — if one system degrades, others maintain positioning
  • Better coverage in high-latitude and obstructed environments

Question 2: Interpolation & IDW (Inverse Distance Weighted) [10 Marks]

1. What is Interpolation?

Interpolation is the process of estimating unknown values at unsampled locations based on known values at surrounding sampled points. In GIS, it is the primary technique for creating continuous surfaces (rasters) from discrete point observations.

Why do we need interpolation? We can never measure a phenomenon (e.g., elevation, rainfall, temperature) at every single location. Interpolation fills the gaps using mathematical models.

Types of Interpolation

CategoryMethodKey Idea
DeterministicIDWCloser points have more influence (weight decreases with distance)
SplineFits a smooth surface through points like bending a rubber sheet
Natural NeighborUses Voronoi tessellation to assign weights based on area
GeostatisticalKrigingUses a semivariogram model to account for spatial dependency
Co-KrigingUses a correlated secondary variable (e.g., elevation for rainfall)

2. Inverse Distance Weighted (IDW) Method

IDW is a deterministic interpolation technique based on Tobler's First Law of Geography: "Everything is related to everything else, but near things are more related than distant things."

The value at an unknown point is estimated as a weighted average of nearby known values, where the weights decrease as the distance increases.

IDW Formula

$$\hat{Z}(x_0) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{Z(x_i)}{d_i^p}}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{d_i^p}}$$

Where:

  • $\hat{Z}(x_0)$ = estimated value at the unknown point
  • $Z(x_i)$ = known value at point $i$
  • $d_i$ = distance between point $i$ and $x_0$
  • $p$ = power parameter (commonly $p = 2$)
  • $n$ = number of nearby sample points used

Effect of the Power Parameter (p)

Power (p)Behavior
p = 1Gentle decay — distant points still have moderate influence. Smoother surface.
p = 2 (default)Balanced decay — standard choice in most GIS software.
p > 2Steep decay — only the very closest points dominate. More "peaky" surface.

Advantages & Limitations

Advantages

  • Simple, intuitive, easy to understand
  • Fast computation
  • No assumptions about data distribution
  • Works well when sample points are evenly distributed
  • Available in all GIS software

Limitations

  • Bull's eye effect — concentric patterns around sample points
  • Cannot extrapolate beyond data range
  • Sensitive to clustered data
  • Does not consider spatial autocorrelation
  • No estimate of prediction uncertainty

Question 4: Parameters Extracted from Elevation Data [10 Marks]

Elevation data (DEM/DTM) is one of the most powerful GIS datasets. By analyzing how elevation values change across a surface, we can derive numerous terrain parameters.

1. Slope

Slope measures the steepness or gradient of a terrain surface — the rate of change of elevation over horizontal distance, expressed in degrees (0°–90°) or percent.

Slope RangeTerrain TypeApplication
0°–5°Flat / GentleSuitable for agriculture, construction
5°–15°ModerateRoads feasible, terrace farming
15°–30°SteepErosion risk, needs engineering measures
> 30°Very SteepLandslide prone, unsuitable for development

2. Aspect

Aspect is the compass direction a slope faces — measured 0°–360° (0°/360° = North, 90° = East, 180° = South, 270° = West).

3. Contour Lines

Contour lines are lines connecting points of equal elevation — generated from a DEM for topographic mapping, engineering design, and volume estimation.

4. Hillshade

Hillshade simulates how terrain looks illuminated from a specific sun angle — creating realistic 3D shaded relief useful for visualization and map backgrounds.

Question 5: Flow Accumulation in River Channel Analysis [10 Marks]

1. What is Flow Accumulation?

Flow accumulation calculates, for each cell in a DEM, the total number of upstream cells whose water would drain through it. It answers: "How much water collects at each point on the landscape?"

Key Concept: High flow accumulation = stream/river channel. Flow accumulation of 0 = ridgeline/hilltop (no upstream contributors).

Question 6: Low Pass & High Pass Filters in Remote Sensing [5 + 5 Marks]

Spatial filters are convolution-based image processing techniques that replace each pixel's value with a weighted combination of its neighbors using a moving kernel window.

Part (a): Low Pass Filter [5 Marks]

A low pass filter (smoothing/averaging filter) allows low spatial frequencies to pass through while suppressing high frequencies. It replaces each pixel with the average of its neighbors.

  • Smooths the image, reduces sharp variations
  • Reduces noise (salt-and-pepper)
  • Blurs edges — boundaries become less distinct
  • Generalizes — reveals broad patterns

Part (b): High Pass Filter [5 Marks]

A high pass filter (edge enhancement/sharpening filter) allows high spatial frequencies to pass through while suppressing low frequencies. It highlights edges, boundaries, and fine details.

  • Sharpens the image — enhances edges and boundaries
  • Highlights rapid changes — roads, field edges, lineaments
  • Removes gradual trends — suppresses smooth areas
  • May amplify noise (side effect)
