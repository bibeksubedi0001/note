Key Formulas & Process

1. RMS Planimetric Error

The allowable RMS (Root Mean Square) planimetric error specifies how accurate a map must be on the ground.

Formula: RMS Error = 0.5 mm × Scale Denominator

Convert to metres: RMS (m) = Scale Denominator / 2000

Why 0.5 mm? This is the minimum plottable width on paper — the smallest mark a person can reliably draw or read on a map. Multiplying by the scale denominator converts this paper error to ground distance.

2. Distance Between Geographic Points

To find the distance between two points given in latitude/longitude:

North-South distance: dNS = Δlat × (π/180) × R

East-West distance: dEW = Δlon × (π/180) × R × cos(mean_lat)

Total distance: D = √(dNS² + dEW²)

Where: R = 6,378,137 m (WGS-84 equatorial radius), Δlat and Δlon in degrees.

The cos(mean_lat) factor accounts for meridians converging toward the poles — 1° of longitude covers less ground distance at higher latitudes.

3. Length of 1° Latitude vs 1° Longitude

1° latitude: 2π × Rpole / 360 ≈ 110,946 m (nearly constant everywhere)

1° longitude: 2π × Req / 360 ≈ 111,319 m at equator, decreases toward poles

The difference arises because Earth is an oblate spheroid — the equatorial radius (6,378,137 m) is larger than the polar radius (6,356,752 m).

Process Steps

  1. Identify the scale denominator — extract the number after “1:” (e.g., 1:25,000 → 25,000)
  2. Apply the RMS formula — multiply 0.5 mm by the denominator to get error in mm
  3. Convert units — divide by 1,000 to get metres, or by 1,000,000 to get km
  4. For distance problems — compute Δlat and Δlon, find N-S and E-W components, then Pythagoras
  5. State the answer with appropriate units and precision

Q6. Distance Between Two Geographic Points VERIFIED

Exam: 2079 Chaitra Q2b [3 Marks]

Question: Find the distance between points A(24.5°N, 83.5°E) and B(24.6°N, 84.5°E). Radius of Earth = 6,378 km.

Concept: We decompose the problem into a North-South component (along meridians) and an East-West component (along parallels). The E-W distance must account for the convergence of meridians toward the poles by multiplying by cos(mean latitude). The final distance uses the Pythagorean theorem.

// Distance between two geographic points
Given:
A = (24.5°N, 83.5°E)
B = (24.6°N, 84.5°E)
R = 6,378 km
// Step 1: Compute differences
Δlat = 24.6 − 24.5 = 0.1°
Δlon = 84.5 − 83.5 = 1.0°
// Step 2: Mean latitude & cosine factor
Mean lat = (24.5 + 24.6) / 2 = 24.55°
cos(24.55°) = 0.9096
// Step 3: N-S and E-W distances
d_NS = 0.1 × (π/180) × 6378 = 11.13 km
d_EW = 1.0 × (π/180) × 6378 × 0.9096 = 101.25 km
// Step 4: Pythagorean distance
D = √(11.13² + 101.25²) = √(123.88 + 10,251.56)
D = √10,375.44 = 101.86 km

Answer: The distance between A and B is approximately 101.86 km.

Note: The E-W component dominates (101.25 km) because the longitude difference (1°) is 10× the latitude difference (0.1°). The direction from A to B is nearly due east.

Q7. Difference: 1° Latitude vs 1° Longitude VERIFIED

Exam: 2076 Bhadra Q2c [3 Marks]

Question: Find the difference between 1° of latitude and 1° of longitude if the equatorial radius is 6,378,137 m and the polar radius is 6,356,752.31 m.

Concept: Earth is not a perfect sphere — it bulges at the equator. The equatorial circumference (used for 1° longitude at equator) is larger than the polar circumference (used for 1° latitude). This means 1° of longitude at the equator is slightly longer than 1° of latitude.

Answer: 1° longitude at equator (111,319 m) is longer than 1° latitude (110,946 m) by approximately 373 m.

Why? Because Earth is an oblate spheroid — the equatorial radius is 21,385 m larger than the polar radius, making the equatorial circumference longer.
