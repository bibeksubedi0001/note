GIS and Maps

Part 1: The Fundamentals (Datums, GCS, and Projections) Covers: 2071 Bhadra Q2a, 2073 Bhadra Q2a, 2072 Magh Q2, 2071 Magh Q1b, 2080 Chaitra Q2, 2082 Shrawan, 2071 Magh Q6c

1. The Relationship: Datums, GCS, and Projections (4 Marks) Tested in: 2080 Chaitra Q2

To build a coordinate system, you must follow a strict three-step mathematical sequence:

  1. Datum: You must first define the exact size and shape of the Earth (which is bumpy and irregular). A datum fits a smooth mathematical ellipsoid to the Earth and anchors it to a specific origin point.
  2. Geographic Coordinate System (GCS): Once you have a Datum, you wrap a 3D grid of intersecting lines (Latitudes and Longitudes) around it to assign angular coordinates to every place on Earth.
  3. Projected Coordinate System (PCS): Because computer screens and printed maps are flat, you use a Map Projection to mathematically flatten that 3D GCS grid into a 2D Cartesian (X, Y) plane.

2. Why is a Datum Important? (2 Marks) Tested in: 2072 Magh Q2

Without a datum, coordinates mean nothing. A datum provides the foundational reference frame for all measurements. If two engineers use different datums (e.g., one uses WGS84 and the other uses Everest 1830), the exact same GPS coordinate could land hundreds of meters apart on the ground.

  • Two common datums: WGS 1984 (the global standard used by GPS) and Everest 1830 (a local datum optimized for the Indian subcontinent and Nepal).

3. The Significance of the Prime Meridian and Equator in GCS Tested in: 2082 Shrawan

In a 3D Geographic Coordinate System, you need a starting point (an origin of 0°, 0°) to measure angles from the center of the Earth.

  • The Equator: The 0° line of Latitude. It physically divides the Earth into the Northern and Southern Hemispheres.
  • The Prime Meridian (Greenwich): The 0° line of Longitude. It mathematically divides the Earth into the Eastern and Western Hemispheres.

Part 2: Map Projections and Engineering Needs Covers: 2072 Ashwin Q1b, 2073 Magh Q2a, 2078 Chaitra Q2b, 2071 Magh Q1b, 2081 Chaitra

1. What is Map Projection and Why is it Necessary? Tested in: 2071 Bhadra Q2a, 2072 Ashwin Q1b, 2073 Magh Q2a, 2081 Chaitra

  • Definition: A map projection is a mathematical formula used to systematically transform the 3D spherical surface of the Earth onto a flat, 2D surface (a map).
  • Why it is necessary: You cannot peel an orange and lay its skin perfectly flat without stretching or tearing it. Similarly, you cannot represent the 3D Earth on a 2D computer screen without introducing distortion. Projections are necessary to control that distortion, allowing us to print maps and perform linear mathematics on a flat Cartesian plane.

2. GCS vs. PCS & Why Civil Engineering Requires PCS (3 to 4 Marks) Tested in: 2073 Magh Q2a, 2078 Chaitra Q2b

  • Differences: GCS uses a 3D spherical model and measures locations in angular units (Degrees/Minutes/Seconds). PCS uses a flat 2D model and measures locations in linear units (Meters or Feet).
  • The Engineering Need: You cannot accurately calculate the area of a watershed, the length of a highway alignment, or the cut-and-fill volume of a construction site in "degrees." Civil engineering strictly requires linear measurements in meters on a flat Cartesian (X, Y) grid, which is only possible using a Projected Coordinate System (PCS).

3. Three Main Categories of Map Projections Tested in: 2071 Magh Q1b, 2072 Magh Q2, 2081 Chaitra

  1. Cylindrical (e.g., Mercator): Projects the Earth onto a cylinder wrapped around the equator. Advantage: Preserves straight lines for navigation and accurate shapes at the equator. Disadvantage: Massively distorts the size of landmasses near the poles (makes Greenland look huge).
  2. Conic (e.g., Lambert Conformal Conic): Projects the Earth onto a cone placed over a pole. Advantage: Highly accurate for mapping mid-latitude countries that stretch East-to-West (like the USA or Russia).
  3. Planar / Azimuthal: Projects the Earth onto a flat plane touching a single point. Advantage: Excellent for mapping the North and South Poles and calculating shortest-distance flight paths.

Part 3: UTM vs. Nepal's MUTM System Covers: 2071 Bhadra Q2b, 2074 Magh Q2a, 2073 Bhadra Q2b, Q2c, 2076 Bhadra Q2a, 2073 Magh Q2b, 2080 Chaitra Q2, 2074 Bhadra Q6iv, 2075 Magh Q1c

This is the most highly tested specific topic in this chapter.

1. What is UTM? Tested in: 2074 Bhadra Q6iv, 2080 Chaitra Q2

Universal Transverse Mercator (UTM) is a global projected coordinate system. It divides the Earth into 60 vertical zones, each being 6° of longitude wide.

  • UTM in Nepal: Nepal falls across two UTM zones: Zone 44N (covers Western Nepal) and Zone 45N (covers Central and Eastern Nepal). The dividing line is the 84°E meridian.

2. Why does Nepal use a different system (MUTM) instead of standard UTM? Tested in: 2073 Magh Q2b, 2076 Bhadra Q2a

While UTM is great globally, a 6° wide zone introduces scale distortion at its extreme eastern and western edges. Nepal is a highly mountainous country that requires extreme precision for cadastral (land ownership) and civil engineering surveys. To eliminate this distortion, Nepal "modified" the UTM system by cutting the zone width in half.

3. Why does Nepal's MUTM use a 3° zone instead of 6°? Tested in: 2073 Bhadra Q2c

By narrowing the projection zone from 6° to 3° of longitude, the mapped surface stays much closer to the central meridian line where distortion is mathematically zero. This modification practically eliminates scale distortion, ensuring that a 100-meter pipeline drawn on the GIS map exactly matches 100 meters on the rugged physical ground.

4. Parameters of the Nepalese Projection System (MUTM) Tested in: 2071 Magh Q1b, 2071 Bhadra Q2b, 2074 Magh Q2a, 2073 Bhadra Q2b

If asked to list the parameters of Nepal's Modified Universal Transverse Mercator (MUTM) system, you must write down these exact specifications:

  • Datum: Everest 1830
  • Projection: Transverse Mercator
  • Zone Width: 3° Longitude
  • False Easting: 500,000 meters (To prevent negative X coordinates)
  • False Northing: 0 meters (Since Nepal is in the Northern Hemisphere)
  • Scale Factor at Central Meridian: 0.9999
  • Central Meridians Used:
    • 81°E (For Far-Western Nepal)
    • 84°E (For Mid-Western & Central Nepal)
    • 87°E (For Eastern Nepal)

2.2 Maps and Their Characteristics (Scale and Abstraction)

1. What is Scale? Types of Scales with Examples (6 Marks) Tested in: 2071 Bhadra Q10

To secure the full 6 marks, you must define scale and explicitly list the three ways it is represented on a map.

  • Definition: Map scale is the mathematical ratio between a distance measured on the map and the corresponding actual distance on the surface of the Earth. It tells the user how much the real world has been "shrunk" to fit on the paper.

Type 1: Representative Fraction (RF) or Ratio Scale

  • Concept: A unitless fraction or ratio. Whatever unit you measure with on the map is the same unit on the ground.
  • Example: 1:50,000. (This means 1 centimeter on the map equals 50,000 centimeters on the ground, or 1 inch equals 50,000 inches).

Type 2: Verbal Scale (Word Statement)

  • Concept: A simple text sentence that equates a specific map unit to a different, more understandable ground unit.
  • Example: "One centimeter equals one kilometer" or "One inch equals one mile."

Type 3: Graphical Scale (Bar Scale)

  • Concept: A visual line or bar drawn on the map, divided into equally spaced segments marked with real-world distances.

  • Major Advantage: If a printed map is photocopied and enlarged or shrunk, the RF scale becomes completely invalid, but the graphical scale changes size with the map and remains mathematically correct.

    • 2. Map is a 2D model of reality. Explain this with examples. Tested in: 2082 Shrawan

    A map is not a perfect photograph of the Earth; it is a highly simplified, 2D mathematical model. You must explain this using the concepts of Selection and Abstraction:

    • Selection: A mapmaker cannot show every single rock, tree, and house, or the map would be too cluttered to read. They select only the features relevant to the map's theme.
    • Abstraction (Dimensional Reduction): Complex 3D real-world objects are flattened into simple 2D geometric symbols (Points, Lines, and Polygons).
    • The Example: A sprawling, multi-story 3D hospital building is abstracted into a single 2D point (a red cross symbol). A complex 3D river with varying depths and flow volumes is abstracted into a single, flat 2D blue line. A massive 3D mountain is reduced to flat 2D contour lines.

    2.6 Precision and Error in GIS

    1. Accuracy vs. Precision in GIS (3 to 5 Marks) Tested in: 2076 Bhadra Q2b, 2074 Magh Q2b

    Examiners love this question because engineers must know the difference. They are not the same thing!

    • Accuracy: This refers to the degree of truth. It is how closely a measured GIS coordinate matches the actual, true location of that object in the real physical world.
      • Example: If a manhole is physically located exactly at coordinate X = 100, Y = 100, and your GPS records it at X = 100.1, Y = 100.1, your data is highly accurate.
    • Precision: This refers to the degree of exactness, detail, or repeatability. It is usually dictated by how many decimal places the system records, regardless of whether those numbers are actually correct.
      • Example: If you use a cheap smartphone GPS and it records the manhole's location as X = 542.123456, Y = 890.123456, the data is highly precise (six decimal places of exactness!), but it is completely inaccurate because it is hundreds of meters away from the true location.

    (For the exam, draw the classic "bullseye" target. Draw one target with dots tightly clustered together but far from the center—label it "High Precision, Low Accuracy". Draw another target with dots scattered widely but evenly around the center bullseye—label it "Low Precision, High Accuracy".)

    2. Errors in GIS data made through "Actual Changes" (Temporal Error) Tested in: 2075 Bhadra Q2c

    This question asks about a very specific type of error: Temporal (Time-based) Error.

    • Concept: The physical world is constantly changing, but a GIS database is a static snapshot of the exact moment the data was collected. If reality changes, the GIS data instantly contains an error unless it is manually updated.
    • Examples to write down:
      1. A massive monsoon flood causes the Koshi River to physically change its course. If your GIS uses a river shapefile from three years ago, your analysis will be completely wrong because of this "actual change."
      2. A new highway is constructed through a forest. Your five-year-old Land Use raster will still show that area as "Dense Forest," creating a major error in any environmental impact calculation.
