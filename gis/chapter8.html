Chapter 8

Surface Model

1. DTM, Its Structures, and Applications (6 Marks) Covers: 2071 Magh Q3b, 2075 Bhadra Q8

A. What is a DTM?

A Digital Terrain Model (DTM) is a 3D digital representation of the bare-earth terrain surface. Unlike a Digital Surface Model (DSM) which includes buildings and tree canopies, a DTM mathematically filters out these above-ground features to represent only the natural ground elevation.

B. Structures of DTM:

DTMs can be structured in a GIS database in three primary ways:

  1. Raster Grid: A continuous matrix of equally sized square pixels, where each pixel stores a single elevation (Z) value.
  2. TIN (Triangulated Irregular Network): A vector-based model consisting of non-overlapping triangles formed by connecting scattered elevation points. It efficiently represents sharp ridges and valleys.
  3. Contour Lines (Isolines): Vector lines connecting points of equal elevation, traditionally used in topographic maps.

C. Three Applications of DTM:

  1. Hydrological Modeling: DTMs are the foundational data required to delineate watershed boundaries, calculate flow accumulation, and model runoff networks in software like the mesoscale Hydrologic Model (mHM).
  2. Transportation Engineering: Used extensively to extract topographic profiles for highway alignment and to calculate precise cut-and-fill earthwork volumes.
  3. Hazard Mapping: Used to identify extremely steep terrain profiles, which is critical for mapping landslide susceptibility or avalanche risks on high-altitude routes.

2. Creation of DEMs and Data Sources (5 Marks) Covers: 2072 Ashwin Q2c, 2075 Bhadra Q8

A. Data Sources of DEM:

  • LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging): Highly accurate laser pulses fired from aircraft or drones.
  • Satellite Imagery (Stereo-photogrammetry): Global datasets like SRTM (Shuttle Radar Topography Mission) or ASTER.
  • Ground Surveys: Spot elevations collected using DGPS or Total Stations during field research.
  • Digitized Topo Maps: Scanning and digitizing historical paper contour maps.

B. How is a DEM created in Raster GIS?

A raster DEM is rarely captured perfectly as a grid in the field. It is created through Spatial Interpolation.

  1. First, scattered elevation data (like a GPS track from a Mardi Himal or Tsho Rolpa trek) is imported into the GIS as a point shapefile.
  2. An interpolation algorithm (such as Inverse Distance Weighted, Spline, or Kriging) is applied to these discrete points.
  3. The software calculates the estimated elevation for all the "empty" spaces between the surveyed points.
  4. The output is a continuous raster grid where every single pixel has an assigned elevation value, effectively creating a smooth digital surface.

3. Slope and Aspect Functions (5 Marks) Covers: 2076 Bhadra Q7b

In the exam, when asked to explain these with illustrations, define the concept and draw a simple grid showing a central pixel surrounded by its 8 neighbors.

A. Slope Function:

  • Concept: Slope calculates the maximum rate of change in elevation between a specific cell and its eight immediate neighbors. It identifies the steepness of the terrain.
  • Output: The output raster displays values either in Degrees (0° for flat, 90° for vertical) or Percent Rise (elevation difference divided by horizontal distance, multiplied by 100).

B. Aspect Function:

  • Concept: Aspect calculates the downslope direction of the maximum rate of change in value from each cell to its neighbors. In simple terms, it identifies which compass direction the slope is facing.
  • Output: The output raster displays values in degrees from 0° to 360°, measured clockwise from North. (e.g., 90° is an East-facing slope, 180° is a South-facing slope). Flat areas are usually assigned a value of -1.
