Technology: The application of scientific knowledge for practical purposes; the branch of knowledge dealing with the formation and use of technical resources and their interrelation with life, society, and the environment.

Environment: The totality of all external conditions affecting the life, development and survival of an organism — physical, chemical, biological surroundings.

Society: A group of individuals in persistent social interaction, sharing geographical/social territory, subject to same political authority and cultural expectations.

  5. Accumulative: Builds upon itself — transistor → IC → microprocessor → computer.
  6. Extends Capabilities: Enables humans beyond natural capacity (cranes, calculators).
  7. Inevitable: Cannot be stopped; continuously evolves.
  8. Irreversible: Once adopted, society cannot return to pre-technology state.

1.1.2 Technology as Foundation of Civilization 2072

1.5 Appropriate Technology 207920772075207420732071

Definition: Small-scale, local-level technology whose goal is to make use of local skills and manpower to provide basic human needs. Promotes decentralized, energy-efficient, locally controlled, environmentally sound solutions.

Dr. Ernst Friedrich: Should promote health, beauty, and permanence. Ideologies of Gandhi and Mao advocated village-based self-reliant technology.

8 Properties of Appropriate Technology

  1. Small scale and simple
  2. Decentralization of goods/services
  3. Labour intensive
  4. Energy efficient
  5. Self-sustaining
  6. Uses renewable resources
  7. Environmentally sound
  8. Locally controlled

Examples

  • ICS (Improved Cooking Stoves): Less firewood, less indoor smoke
  • Rope Pumps: Simple, locally manufactured hand pumps
  • Biogas Plants: Animal dung → cooking gas + organic fertilizer
  • Gabion Walls: Wire-mesh + local stones for slope stabilization
  • Micro-Hydropower: 5–100 kW for remote hill communities

Relevance to LDCs like Nepal 20752071

  1. Affordability: Low investment (biogas, solar, ICS)
  2. Local Employment: Labor-intensive, generates local jobs
  3. Self-Reliance: Uses local materials, reduces imports
  4. Environmental Sustainability: Renewable resources, minimal pollution
  5. Social Acceptability: Designed for local conditions
  6. Terrain Suitability: Nepal's rugged terrain suits small-scale tech (ropeways, trail bridges)

Elements of Sustainable Development 20742073

  1. Economic Viability
  2. Environmental Protection
  3. Social Equity
  4. Institutional Framework

Indigenous Technology as Sustainable in Nepal 20742073

  • Rajkulo: Gravity-fed canal systems — no energy cost, centuries proven
  • Mud-and-Stone Construction: Newari houses — thermally comfortable, low-carbon
  • Dhiki & Janto: Water-powered grain mills — zero fossil fuel
  • Terraced Farming: Prevents soil erosion, maximizes cultivable area on steep slopes

Role in Transforming Society 2079

  • Biogas reduced ARI by 40–50% in rural Nepal households
  • Micro-hydro brought first electricity to remote hills (Myagdi, Baglung)
  • Freed women from 3–4 hrs/day firewood collection → education, income
  • Drip irrigation enabled year-round commercial vegetable farming in Terai
  • Solar-powered learning centres in off-grid areas (Humla, Dolpa)
  • VIP latrines & eco-sanitation reduced diarrheal diseases by 30–40%

1.6 Intermediate, Labor Based & Labor Intensive Technology 20792078

  • Intermediate: Technology for developing countries using local resources; focuses on people rather than machines. E.g.: Solar bulbs, hand weaving machines, pedal-power maize sheller.
  • Labor-Based: Combines labour with light equipment; local labour preferred; quality meets standards. E.g.: Road construction with manual earthwork + small roller for compaction.
  • Labor-Intensive: Large number of workers; high labour cost relative to capital; physical/manual jobs. E.g.: Hand-excavation of irrigation canals.
