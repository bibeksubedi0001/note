3.2 Great Renaissance of Europe 20772071

Renaissance = “Re-birth” (14th–17th c) — Intellectual and cultural revival in Europe after Medieval period.

Key Features

  1. Scientific Thinking: Observation & experimentation replaced dogma (Galileo, Copernicus)
  2. Art & Architecture: Leonardo da Vinci, Michelangelo; perspective drawing
  3. Printing Press (1440): Gutenberg → mass dissemination of knowledge
  4. Humanism: Focus on individual capability and reason
  5. Exploration: Columbus, Vasco da Gama → global trade routes
  6. Universities: Oxford, Cambridge, Bologna → systematic education

Impact on Engineering

  • Systematic design principles (Brunelleschi’s dome)
  • Military engineering & fortification advances
  • Navigation instruments (compass, sextant)
  • Foundation for the Scientific Revolution → Industrial Revolution

3.3 Early Part of Industrial Revolution 2080207920732070

Industrial Revolution (1760–1840, Britain) — Transition from manual production to mechanized manufacturing, transforming society from agrarian to industrial.

Major Changes

  1. Steam Engine (James Watt, 1769): Powered factories, trains, ships
  2. Textile Mechanization: Spinning Jenny, Power Loom → mass production
  3. Iron & Steel: Bessemer process → railways, bridges, buildings
  4. Transportation: Railways, steamships → rapid trade
  5. Urbanization: Rural → city migration; factory towns

Environmental Consequences 2080

  • Massive coal burning → air pollution, smog (London “Pea-Souper”)
  • Industrial waste dumped in rivers (Thames, severely polluted)
  • Deforestation for fuel & factory sites
  • Beginning of greenhouse gas accumulation in atmosphere
  • Loss of biodiversity due to habitat destruction

Social Impact

  • Rise of working class; child labour; poor living conditions
  • Trade unions & labour movements formed
  • Middle class growth; spread of education
  • Foundation for democratic movements

3.7 Climate Change as a Threat to Human Civilization 2079

Why Climate Change Threatens Civilization

  1. Rising Temperatures: Global temp. up ~1.1°C since pre-industrial; heatwaves
  2. Sea Level Rise: Melting glaciers & ice caps → flooding of coastal cities
  3. Extreme Weather: Intense floods, droughts, cyclones (Nepal: GLOF risk)
  4. Food Security: Crop failure, water scarcity, fisheries collapse
  5. Biodiversity Loss: Species extinction, ecosystem collapse
  6. Health: Heat stress, vector-borne diseases (dengue, malaria spreading)
  7. Displacement: Climate refugees; internal migration

Nepal-Specific Threats

  • Glacial lake outburst floods (GLOF) — 21 potentially dangerous lakes
  • Erratic monsoon: floods in some years, drought in others
  • Snow-line recession in Himalayas
  • Loss of biodiversity in Sagarmatha, Chitwan
  • Agriculture-dependent economy highly vulnerable

Brief History of Human Civilization

4 Hours 17 Past Questions

3.1 Early Civilization 2070

  • Stone Age (2.5M yrs ago – 3500 BC): Began in Africa; stones as weapons; nomadic; cereal cultivation & animal domestication began; towns evolved to cities.
  • Bronze Age (3500 – 1100 BC): First civilization in Mesopotamia; copper + tin = bronze; Egyptian, Sumerian, Maya civilizations; first writing system (Sumerians); navigation developed.
  • Iron Age (1100 BC – 500 AD): Iron weapons; Gautama Buddha, Confucius, Ashoka, Jesus Christ; Greek/Roman civilizations; Plato, Aristotle, Socrates.
  • Middle Age (500 – 1450 AD): Islam; Viking & Mongol invasions; Genghis Khan; Silk Road; universities (Paris, Oxford, Bologna); inventions: windmills, compass, spectacles.
  • Towards Modern World (1450 – 1660): Da Vinci, Galileo, Newton; Columbus discovered America (1492); mechanical revolution; sawmill, microscope, telescope.

3.2 Great Renaissance of Europe (1300s–1600s) 20772071

Renaissance = “re-birth” (French). Period following the Middle Ages; renewed interest in ancient Greeks and Romans. Began in Italy.

  1. Geography: Italy's Venice & Genoa were major trade centres; trade money financed research.
  2. Science: Telescope; printing press (Gutenberg); Copernicus proved heliocentric model; Galileo sentenced to house arrest for confirming it.
  3. Music: Golden period of Acapella; graceful flowing vocal melodies.
  4. Arts: Michelangelo’s David (1501–04), Sistine Chapel; da Vinci’s Mona Lisa; da Vinci also designed helicopter, tank, parachute.
  5. Politics: Ideology of despotism; self-governing city-states (Venice, Genoa, Milan, Florence); wealthy through trade & capitalism.
  6. Philosophy: Humanism originated; Francesco Petrarch was first Humanist; great value on education.

Role in Science & Tech: Revived scientific method (observation, experimentation); Gutenberg’s printing press (~1440) democratized knowledge; da Vinci’s engineering designs anticipated modern machines; created intellectual culture of inquiry → foundation for Scientific & Industrial Revolutions.

3.3 Industrial Revolution (1660–1815) 2080207920732070

Transition from hand production to machine manufacturing. Began in UK & USA with steam power, machine tools, and the factory system.

Impacts

  • Population: Europe 100M (1700) → 400M (1900)
  • Urbanization: Massive rural → urban shift
  • Women: Middle/upper-class confined to domestic life; lower-class forced into low-paid jobs
  • Child Labour: Two-thirds of cotton mill workers in 1788 England were children

Environmental Consequences 2080

  1. Air pollution from factories & coal combustion
  2. Water pollution from industrial waste & acid rain
  3. Soil pollution from industrial waste
  4. Deforestation for fuel & factory land
  5. Resource depletion (coal & iron mining)
  6. Global warming from fossil fuel emissions

Key Technological Developments 2073

  • Steam Power: Sped up manufacturing, transport, farming
  • Iron Making: Coke replaced charcoal, lowering cost
  • Machine Tools: Screw-cutting lathe, boring machine, milling machine

3.4 Transformation: Industrial → Information Society 2081207820712069

Industrial Society: Maximum use of mechanical machines. Information Society: Creation, distribution, manipulation of information as most significant activity. Transformation via the Digital Revolution (3rd Industrial Revolution).

Key Developments

  • Phone, computer, TV, internet merged into information system
  • Digital technology enabled identical copies & remote distribution
  • Motorola — first mobile phone (DynaTac, 1983)
  • First true digital camera (1988)
  • Tim Berners-Lee — World Wide Web (1989)

Challenges 2081

  • Job displacement from automation
  • Digital divide (developed vs developing; urban vs rural)
  • Skill gap requiring retraining
  • Privacy & cybersecurity concerns
  • Cultural disruption of traditional structures

Opportunities 2081

  • Industrial efficiency (programmable machines, automation)
  • Medicine (3D surgical modelling, MRI, CT, telemedicine)
  • Global communication (internet, mobile, WWW)
  • Quick response systems (emergency, disaster)
  • Remote work & education (video conferencing, e-learning)

3.5 Impact of World War I & II 20742070

World War I (1914–1919)

  • Causes (AMIN): Alliances, Imperialism, Militarism, Nationalism
  • Trigger: Assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand (June 28, 1914)
  • End: Treaty of Versailles (June 28, 1919)
  • Deaths: 18 million; 23 million injured; 60% world economy devastated

World War II (1939–1945)

  • Causes: Treaty of Versailles, Hitler’s imperialism, Mussolini’s fascism, Japan’s expansion
  • Key Events: Germany invades Poland (Sep 1, 1939); Pearl Harbor (Dec 7, 1941); Hiroshima (Aug 6, 1945); Nagasaki (Aug 9, 1945)
  • Deaths: 50 million (22M soldiers, 28M civilians)

Positive Impacts

  • End of dictatorship, colonialism, imperialism
  • UNO established (Oct 24, 1945)
  • Tech advances: aircraft, submarines, computers, radar, nuclear energy
  • Women given opportunity to work & vote
  • IMF established; trade unions grew

Negative Impacts

  • 68M+ total deaths; 70% industry collapsed
  • 4 empires disappeared (German, Austro-Hungarian, Ottoman, Russian)
  • Germany divided; Cold War began
  • Nuclear weapons introduced — lasting threat

Population Explosion

Post-WWII advances in medicine, agriculture, sanitation → rapid global population growth → increased resource demand, urbanization, food security challenges, environmental strain.

3.6 Rise of Environmental Issues

  1. Pollution: Air (factories, fossil fuels), Water (oil spills, acid rain), Soil (industrial waste)
  2. Global Warming: GHG emissions → melting ice caps, sea level rise, extreme weather, desertification
  3. Overpopulation: Shortage of water, fuel, food; intensive farming damages environment
  4. Waste Disposal: Plastics crisis; nuclear waste hazards
  5. Loss of Biodiversity: Ecosystems endangered; pollination threatened; coral reef destruction
  6. Deforestation: Destroys carbon sinks, animal shelters; causes drought
  7. Ocean Acidification: CO₂ uptake → coral bleaching, marine organism damage
  8. Ozone Depletion: CFCs → skin cancer, immune damage, plant damage
  9. Acid Rain: SO₂ + NO₂ → forest damage, soil nutrient loss
  10. Urban Sprawl: Land degradation, flora/fauna displacement
  11. Genetic Engineering: Increased toxins, antibiotic-resistant organisms, allergens

3.7 Climate Change as Threat to Civilization 2079

  1. Widespread Drought: GHG → increased evaporation (e.g., Sub-Saharan Africa)
  2. Infectious Diseases: Rising temperatures aid virus replication (e.g., Dengue +3–5.9%)
  3. Food Insecurity: Higher CO₂ disrupts vegetation; extreme weather destroys crops
  4. Extreme Weather: Every 1°C sea temp increase = 31% more strong storms
  5. Rising Sea Levels: Melting ice threatens coastal cities (White Chuck Glacier retreated 1.9 km in 30 yrs)
  6. Health Impacts: Ozone → asthma; disasters → PTSD, depression
