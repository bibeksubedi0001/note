CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION TO GROUNDWATER
1.1 Global Water Distribution
Water is the most abundant substance on Earth, covering approximately 71% of the Earth’s
surface. The breakdown of global water reserves is as follows:
• Oceans: 96.5%
• Freshwater: 2.5%
• Other Saline Water: 0.9%
Of the 2.5% that is Freshwater:
• Glaciers and Ice Caps: 68.7%
• Groundwater: 30.1%
• Surface and Other Freshwater: 1.2% (includes lakes, soil moisture, atmosphere, and rivers)
1.2 Definition of Groundwater
Groundwater (also known as sub-surface water) is defined as the water present beneath the
Earth’s surface in the pore spaces of soil and in the fractures of rock formations. It exists in
the zone of saturation.
1.3 Classification of Groundwater (Based on Origin)
Groundwater is classified into the following types based on its source:
• Meteoric Water: Derived from atmospheric precipitation (rain and snow). Generally, this
water originates from infiltration.
• Connate Water: Water entrapped in the interstices (pores) of sedimentary rock at the time
of its deposition. It is often highly saline due to long contact with minerals.
• Juvenile Water (Magmatic Water): Derived from the interior of the Earth, typically asso-
ciated with magma or volcanic activity. It is formed from the condensation of steam and
magmatic fluids entrapped in rock pores.
• Metamorphic Water: Water that is squeezed or drained out of rocks during the process of
metamorphism.
• Fossil Water: A form of connate water that has been preserved in an aquifer for a very long
period of time.

1.4 The Hydrological Cycle
The Hydrological Cycle is the continuous storage and movement of water between the bio-
sphere, atmosphere, and lithosphere. Throughout this process, water changes between dif-
ferent states, such as evaporation to water vapor, water freezing into ice, and water vapor
condensing to water.
Key Components of the Cycle:
• Evaporation
• Transpiration
• Sublimation
• Condensation
• Precipitation
• Runoff
• Infiltration and Percolation
• Groundwater Flow
1.5 Surface Water vs. Groundwater
Both surface water and sub-surface water are related through the hydrological cycle (contin-
uous circulation of water).
Comparison of Characteristics:
• Availability & Location
• Surface Water: Available on the surface in the form of oceans, seas, and ponds.
• Groundwater: Located below the earth’s surface.
• Contamination
• Surface Water: Generally more contaminated.
• Groundwater: Less contaminated generally.
• Mineral Content
• Surface Water: Low in minerals; considered as soft water.
• Groundwater: High in minerals; considered as hard water.
• Primary Uses
• Surface Water: Uses include drinking (usually requires treatment), irrigation, and daily
life.
• Groundwater: Uses include domestic supply, industrial purpose, and irrigation.

CHARACTERISTICS OF GROUNDWATER
General Characteristics
• Movement: Groundwater includes slow movement and has a stable temperature.
• Natural Filtration: Groundwater is generally purer than surface water.
• Quality Variations:
• Quality varies with local geology and potential contaminants.
• Dissolved minerals, pH, and hardness vary significantly by location.
Age of Groundwater
• Definition: The time since water entered an aquifer.
• Range: Ranges from days to years.
• Why Age Matters?
• To identify the pollution of GW.
• To understand how quickly it moves through aquifers.
• To identify recharge zones.
• Measurement: Scientists use natural tracers to measure age, such as:
• Carbon-14 (C-14)
• Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs)
• Tritium
• Sulfur
SPRINGS
Definition
A spring is an outlet for groundwater stored in the rock which is released onto the surface at
points where the water table meets the surface.
3

Types of Springs
1. Tilted Spring
• Formation: Occurs where permeable and impermeable rocks alternate.
• Mechanism: Water emerges at the base of permeable rocks. Water may percolate down-
wards until it reaches a rock joint which emerges at the surface.
• Result: Causes the water table to reach the surface.
2. Dyke Spring
• Formation: Involves a dyke (impermeable rock intrusion).
• Mechanism: The dyke acts as a barrier to the flow, forcing water to surface.
3. Karst Spring
• Location: Limestone regions.
• Composition: Involves calcium carbonate rock.
• Structure: Often associated with underground tunnels.
HYDROGEOLOGICAL FORMATIONS (CONTINUED)
• Aquiclude
• Definition: An impermeable layer that has good storage capacity and low transmitting ca-
pacity.
• Function: It generally acts as a barrier to groundwater flow.
• Example: Clay.
• Note: It allows water through it but doesn’t provide enough available water.
• Aquifuge
• Definition: A relatively impermeable formation that neither holds (contains) nor transmits
water.
• Characteristics: It has no interconnected pores.
• Examples: Granite, Basalt.
TYPES OF AQUIFERS
Groundwater aquifers are classified into four main types based on their geological confine-
ment:
1. Unconfined Aquifer
• Definition: An aquifer that has no overlying impermeable layer and is open to the at-
mosphere.
• Recharge: Recharged directly through the surface by precipitation.
• Upper Boundary: The unconfined upper boundary is the water table.
• Dynamics: The water level can rise and fall based on rainfall.
4

• Pressure: Water is at atmospheric pressure.
2. Confined Aquifer (Artesian Aquifer)
• Definition: An aquifer that is sandwiched between two impermeable layers.
• Pressure: Water is usually under pressure. It is also known as a pressure aquifer or
artesian aquifer.
• Characteristics: It is completely filled with water and has no free water table.
• Flow: Without pumping, the pressure can cause water to rise naturally.
3. Leaky Aquifer (Semi-Confined)
• Definition: An aquifer bounded by one or two aquitards.
• Mechanism: It allows vertical water movement or leakage through the bounding layers.
4. Perched Aquifer
• Definition: A special type of unconfined aquifer that occurs above the regional water
table.
• Formation: It sits on a localized impermeable layer (aeration zone saturation) separate
from the main aquifer below.
PROPERTIES OF SOIL/ROCK AFFECTING GROUNDWATER FLOW
The flow of groundwater is heavily influenced by the properties of the soil and rock forma-
tions:
• Porosity:
• The total number of voids (empty spaces) in the material.
• It defines the storage capacity of the formation.
• Permeability:
• The ability of the material to transmit fluids.
• Comparison:
• Clay: Low permeability, but high porosity (holds water but doesn’t let it flow).
• Sand: High permeability.
• Geological Structures:
• Features such as rock structures, faults, and fractures significantly impact flow paths.
• Texture & Grain Size:
• Coarse materials: Highly permeable and have better water-holding capacity than silt and
clay.
• Specific Yield:
• The actual volume of water that drains out of a saturated rock due to gravity.
• Rock Joint Fractures:
• Fractures in the rock that serve as conduits for water movement.

CHAPTER 8: LIMITATIONS & VALUES
8.1 Limitations of Darcy’s Law
Darcy’s law is not a universal law like Newton’s laws. It has specific limitations:
• Laminar Flow Only: It is only valid for laminar flow regimes where the Reynolds number
(Re) is typically less than 1 (some sources say 1-10).
• Newtonian Fluids: It is not applicable for Non-Newtonian fluids.
• Application: It is most common in groundwater flow because flow velocities there are gen-
erally very slow.

CHAPTER 10: INTRINSIC PERMEABILITY (KI)
10.1 Definition
Intrinsic Permeability (ki) is the property of a porous medium that characterizes its ability to
transmit a fluid.
• It is dependent only on the physical properties of the porous medium itself, such as:
• Grain size
• Grain shape and arrangement
• Pore interconnection
10.2 Difference from Hydraulic Conductivity (K)
While Intrinsic Permeability (ki) depends only on the medium (rock/soil), Hydraulic Conduc-
tivity (K) depends on the properties of both the porous media and the fluid (viscosity and
density).
10.3 The Formula
The relationship between Intrinsic Permeability and Hydraulic Conductivity is:
K = ki

µ

Where:
• K = Hydraulic Conductivity (m/s)
• µ = Dynamic Viscosity of the fluid (kg/m · s)
• ρ = Density of the fluid (kg/m3)
• g = Acceleration due to gravity (m/s2)
Units: Intrinsic Permeability is measured in Darcy or m2.

CHAPTER 11: NON-DARCYIAN FLOW
11.1 Definition
Non-Darcyian Flow refers to flow regimes where the linear relationship between hydraulic
gradient (i) and flow rate (q) (Darcy’s Law) deviates. This typically happens when flow becomes
turbulent.
11.2 Causes of Deviation
Typical Darcy flow is laminar. Deviation occurs due to:
• Turbulent Effect: High velocities cause eddies and turbulence.
• Inertial Effect: At high speeds, water cannot negotiate tight pore turns smoothly.
• Heterogeneous Effect: Complex flow paths in fractured rocks.
11.3 Examples of Non-Darcyian Flow
• Near Well Bores: Water flowing rapidly towards a well bore during pumping often exceeds
laminar conditions and enters turbulent conditions.
• Oil & Gas Extraction: Gas flows rapidly towards the extraction point.
• Fractured Rock: Water flowing through large complex fractures or karst conduits.
