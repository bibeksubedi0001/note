Introduction

Groundwater Exploration

Course Objective: To understand how surface geophysical methods can characterize subsurface hydrogeology without invasive drilling.

Groundwater is difficult to observe directly from the surface, so engineers use geophysical methods to locate aquifers and assess water quality (Alisiobi, 2012). These techniques measure physical properties like electrical resistance or wave velocity to identify geological features without invasive drilling (Binley et al., 2015; Farfour & Al-Hosni, 2024).

Electrical Resistivity Theory

1. Fundamental Principles

Geophysical resistivity methods differentiate subsurface materials by exploiting the contrast between the insulating rock matrix and the conductive pore electrolytes (Telford et al., 1990). The theoretical foundation for these measurements is established by Ohm’s Law, which relates resistance to intrinsic resistivity, and the derivation of electric potential for a point source in a semi-infinite medium (Sharma, 1997).

Ohm's Law Foundation:

$$ R = \rho \frac{L}{A} $$

Where $\rho$ is Resistivity ($\Omega \cdot m$), the intrinsic material property.

Potential in Semi-Infinite Medium:

$$ V = \frac{I \rho}{2\pi r} $$

For current $I$ injected into the Earth surface at distance $r$.

The 4-Electrode Concept

2. Current Flow in Earth

Current injected into a homogeneous half-space flows radially outward in hemispherical shells.

1. Surface Area: $A = 2\pi r^2$ (Hemisphere)

2. Current Density ($J$): $J = \frac{I}{A} = \frac{I}{2\pi r^2}$

3. Electric Field ($E$): $E = \rho J = \rho \frac{I}{2\pi r^2}$

4. Potential ($V$): $V = \int_{r}^{\infty} E \, dr = \frac{\rho I}{2\pi r}$

Why 4 Electrodes?

Using only 2 electrodes measures the Contact Resistance of the pegs themselves (which is high) rather than the Earth's resistance.

Solution: Separate circuits.
1. Outer Pair (C1, C2): Inject Current.
2. Inner Pair (P1, P2): Measure Voltage.

Result: No current flows through the potential circuit, eliminating contact resistance errors.

Apparent Resistivity ($\rho_a$)

3. The Concept of Apparent Resistivity

The inherent heterogeneity and stratification of the subsurface preclude the direct measurement of a specific point's "true" resistivity (Telford et al., 1990). Instead, geophysical instruments record an "apparent resistivity," which effectively functions as a volumetric weighted average of the electrical properties within the zone influenced by the current lines (Loke, 2000; Reynolds, 2011).

General Field Equation

$$ \rho_a = K \left( \frac{\Delta V}{I} \right) $$

What is K?

$K$ is the Geometric Factor. It depends only on the arrangement (spacing) of the electrodes. It allows us to normalize our readings regardless of the array type used.

Wenner Electrode Configuration

Wenner Array

Standard for Lateral Profiling (CST)

$$ K = 2 \pi a $$
C1 - P1 - P2 - C2
Configuration Rules

Four electrodes are placed in a straight line with equal spacing ($a$) between all adjacent electrodes.

AM = MN = NB = $a$
Advantages
  • High signal strength (large voltage potential).
  • Simple calculation of Geometric Factor ($K$).
  • Excellent for resolving horizontal changes (Lateral Profiling).

Schlumberger Electrode Configuration

Schlumberger Array

Standard for Vertical Sounding (VES)

$$ K = \frac{\pi (L^2 - b^2)}{2b} $$
C1 ... P1 P2 ... C2
Configuration Rules

The potential electrodes (P1, P2) are kept close together ($b$), while current electrodes (C1, C2) are moved outwards ($L$).

MN($b$) $\ll$ AB/2($L$) typically $L \ge 5b$
Advantages
  • Less manpower required (only outer electrodes move).
  • Greater depth of penetration per spread length.
  • Less sensitive to lateral inhomogeneities near surface.

Physics of Seismic Refraction

Snell's Law & Critical Refraction

Seismic refraction surveys rely on the principle of critical refraction, which governs how elastic waves propagate through layered subsurface media (Telford et al., 1990). When seismic waves travel from a low-velocity upper layer ($V_1$) into a high-velocity underlying layer ($V_2$), they bend away from the normal according to Snell's Law until they reach a critical angle and travel parallel to the geological interface (Burger et al., 2006; Mussett & Khan, 2000).

$$ \frac{\sin i}{\sin r} = \frac{V_1}{V_2} $$

Condition: $V_2 > V_1$ (Velocity must increase with depth)

The Critical Angle ($i_c$)

When the angle of refraction ($r$) reaches 90°, the wave travels along the interface at speed $V_2$. This specific incident angle is $i_c$.

$$ \sin i_c = V_1 / V_2 $$

Depth Calculation: Method 1

Intercept Time ($t_i$) Method

Procedure

  1. Plot First Arrival Time ($t$) vs Distance ($x$).
  2. Fit straight lines to the Direct segment and Refracted segment.
  3. Calculate slopes to get velocities: $V_1 = 1/\text{slope}_1$, $V_2 = 1/\text{slope}_2$.
  4. Project the Refracted line back to $x=0$ to find Intercept Time ($t_i$).

Depth Formula (Intercept Method)

$$ z = \frac{t_{i} V_1 V_2}{2\sqrt{V_2^2 - V_1^2}} $$

Depth Calculation: Method 2

Critical Distance Method ($X_c$)

Instead of using the intercept time ($t_i$), we can use the Crossover Distance ($X_c$). This is the exact distance on the graph where the Direct Wave and Refracted Wave arrive at the same time (the "kink" in the curve).

Formula using $X_c$
$$ z = \frac{X_c}{2} \sqrt{\frac{V_2 - V_1}{V_2 + V_1}} $$
* $X_c$ is easier to identify visually on the T-X plot than projecting back for $t_i$.

Application: Groundwater Depth

How to calculate Groundwater Depth?

The seismic refraction method is excellent for locating the water table because P-wave velocity increases significantly in water-saturated soil compared to dry soil.

Velocity Indicators

  • Unsaturated Topsoil ($V_1$) 300 - 800 m/s
  • Saturated Soil ($V_2$) 1400 - 1600 m/s

Procedure:

1. Conduct survey and plot T-X graph.
2. Identify $V_1$ and $V_2$.
3. If $V_2 \approx 1500$ m/s, the interface is the Water Table.
4. Calculate depth $z$ using the standard formula.

Three-Layer Earth Model
Layer 1: Topsoil ($V_1$, $z_1$)
Layer 2: Weathered Rock ($V_2$, $z_2$)
Layer 3: Bedrock ($V_3$, $\infty$)
V3 > V2 > V1

Depth to Interface 1 ($z_1$)

Boundary between Topsoil and Weathered Rock.

$$ z_1 = \frac{t_{i1} V_1 V_2}{2\sqrt{V_2^2 - V_1^2}} $$

Depth to Interface 2 ($z_2$)

Boundary between Weathered Rock and Sound Bedrock.

$$ z_2 = \frac{1}{2} \left[ t_{i2} - \frac{2z_1 \sqrt{V_3^2 - V_1^2}}{V_3 V_1} \right] \frac{V_3 V_2}{\sqrt{V_3^2 - V_2^2}} $$
* The formula for $z_2$ uses "Time Stripping" - subtracting the travel time through the first layer.

Determine Depths ($Z$) & Lithology

Method Limitations

Velocity Inversion

A "Hidden Layer" occurs if a slow layer ($V_{low}$) is sandwiched between fast layers. Rays bend down, making the layer undetectable. Depth is overestimated.

Blind Zone

Thin layers with small velocity contrasts may not produce a distinct first arrival on the T-X graph. The method "skips" them. Depth is underestimated.

Geometric Constraints

To detect deep layers, the survey line must be 3-5 times the target depth ($L \approx 5Z$). Confined urban spaces limit the depth of investigation.

2073 Bhadra Explain with necessary sketches the Electrical Resistivity method.

Sketch 1: Field Arrangement (Wenner Array)

Detailed Explanation:

The Electrical Resistivity method is a major geophysical technique used to determine the depth of groundwater and lithology.

  1. Working Principle:

    It utilizes the variation in electrical resistance of ground materials. Dry soil and hard rock are resistive (insulators), while water-saturated soil and clay are conductive. By measuring resistance, we can infer the material type.

  2. Field Procedure (See Sketch 1):
    • Four electrodes are driven into the ground in a straight line.
    • Current Electrodes (C1, C2): The outer two electrodes inject a DC current ($I$) into the ground from a battery.
    • Potential Electrodes (P1, P2): The inner two electrodes measure the potential difference ($V$) caused by the current.
  3. Formula & Calculation:

    The instrument measures $R = V/I$. We calculate the Apparent Resistivity ($\rho_a$): $$ \rho_a = 2 \pi a \frac{V}{I} $$ Where '$a$' is the equal spacing between electrodes (Wenner Configuration).

Key Concepts:

  • Basis: Difference in elastic wave velocity between overburden (slow) and bedrock (fast).
  • Critical Angle: Uses Snell's law. Requires $V_2 > V_1$.
  • Data: Time-Distance Graph.
