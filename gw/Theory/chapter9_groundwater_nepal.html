9.1 INTRODUCTION — PHYSIOGRAPHIC OVERVIEW

Nepal can be divided into five major physiographic zones from south to north, each having distinct groundwater characteristics:

Zone Elevation Lithology GW Potential
Terai (Southern Plain) 60–300 m Alluvial sand, gravel, clay (Quaternary) Very High — most productive aquifers
Siwalik (Churia) Hills 300–1,500 m Sandstone, mudstone, conglomerate Moderate — fractured aquifers, springs
Middle Hills (Mahabharat) 1,500–2,700 m Phyllite, schist, quartzite, gneiss Low to moderate — spring-fed
Intermontane Valleys Variable Lacustrine sediments (clay, silt, sand, gravel) Moderate to high (e.g., Kathmandu, Pokhara)
High Himalayas > 4,000 m Crystalline rocks, glacial deposits Very low — limited springs

Key Exam Point: Nepal’s groundwater is overwhelmingly concentrated in two settings: (1) the Terai alluvial plain and (2) intermontane valleys like Kathmandu and Pokhara. Together these account for >90% of the nation’s extractable groundwater.

9.2 TERAI BELT — GROUNDWATER OCCURRENCE AND DEVELOPMENT

PAST EXAM QUESTION ADDRESSED:

2072 Magh Q10 [6]: Discuss the groundwater development possibilities of the Terai belt.

9.2.1 Geological Setting

The Terai is a narrow strip of flat alluvial plain, 20–50 km wide, along the southern border of Nepal. It is the northern extension of the Indo-Gangetic Plain. The sediments are Quaternary alluvium deposited by rivers originating in the Himalayas.

The typical geological succession from top to bottom is:

  • Topsoil: 1–3 m of clayey/silty soil (low permeability)
  • Shallow aquifer (unconfined): 10–30 m of fine to medium sand — most widely used for hand pumps and shallow tube wells
  • Clay/silt aquitard: Acts as a confining layer
  • Deep aquifer (confined/semi-confined): 50–300+ m of coarse sand and gravel — high-yield aquifer for deep tube wells

9.2.2 Aquifer Characteristics

Parameter Shallow Aquifer Deep Aquifer
Type Unconfined to semi-confined Confined (artesian in places)
Depth range 5–30 m 50–300+ m
Material Fine to medium sand Coarse sand and gravel
Transmissivity (T) 100–500 m²/day 500–3,000 m²/day
Storage coefficient Sy = 0.05–0.20 S = 10−4–10−3
Well yield 5–20 m³/hr 30–100+ m³/hr
Water quality Generally good; arsenic risk in some areas Good; low iron in most areas
Primary use Domestic supply (hand pumps) Irrigation, municipal supply

9.2.3 Groundwater Development Status

  • Over 40,000 shallow tube wells and hand pumps installed for community drinking water supply
  • Over 10,000 deep tube wells for irrigation, particularly in the Central and Eastern Terai
  • The Terai contains approximately 70–80% of Nepal’s total groundwater resources
  • Only about 20–25% of available groundwater has been developed so far — significant potential remains

9.2.4 Development Possibilities

  1. Irrigation expansion: Large-scale deep tube well irrigation in the western Terai where surface water irrigation coverage is insufficient
  2. Conjunctive use: Combining surface water (monsoon) and groundwater (dry season) for year-round agriculture
  3. Urban water supply: Deep wells for rapidly growing Terai cities (Birgunj, Biratnagar, Nepalgunj, Butwal, Bharatpur)
  4. Industrial use: Groundwater for agro-industries, dairy, and manufacturing in industrial corridors
  5. Recharge augmentation: Managed aquifer recharge (MAR) using monsoon surplus to sustain long-term yields

9.2.5 Challenges

  • Arsenic contamination: Shallow aquifers in parts of the Central and Eastern Terai (Nawalparasi, Parsa, Bara, Rautahat) have arsenic > 50 μg/L (WHO limit = 10 μg/L)
  • Iron and manganese: Elevated levels in some shallow wells cause aesthetic and health issues
  • Over-extraction: Localized drawdown near urban centres due to unregulated pumping
  • Land subsidence: Potential risk if deep aquifer heads are excessively lowered
  • Lack of data: Insufficient monitoring wells and hydrogeological data for optimal management

9.3 KATHMANDU VALLEY AQUIFER SYSTEM

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED:

9.3.1 Geological Background

The Kathmandu Valley is an intermontane basin (tectonic graben) approximately 25 km × 20 km in extent, filled with lacustrine and fluvial sediments of Pliocene–Quaternary age. The valley was once occupied by a lake (the ancient Paleo-Kathmandu Lake) that gradually drained, leaving behind thick sequences of clay, silt, sand, gravel, and peat.

9.3.2 Three-Layer Aquifer Model

The hydrogeology of the Kathmandu Valley is traditionally described as a three-layer system:

Figure 9.3: Schematic three-layer aquifer model of the Kathmandu Valley. The Kalimati Clay (black clay) is the principal aquitard separating the shallow and deep systems.

(a) Layer 1 — Shallow Aquifer (Unconfined)

  • Comprised of recent alluvial deposits: sand, gravel, silt
  • Depth: 5–20 m below ground surface
  • Recharged by local precipitation, infiltration, and river leakage
  • Tapped by dug wells and shallow tube wells
  • Yield: 1–10 m³/hr (limited)
  • Water quality often degraded due to organic contamination, high ammonia, and bacteriological pollution in urban areas

(b) Kalimati Clay (Aquitard)

  • The Kalimati Formation is a thick layer (up to 200 m) of black, organic-rich, lacustrine clay deposited in the Paleo-Kathmandu Lake
  • Very low hydraulic conductivity — acts as the primary confining layer
  • Separates the shallow aquifer from the deep aquifer
  • Thickest in the central valley, thins towards the margins
  • Contains peat layers with high ammonia and methane — affects deep aquifer water quality through slow leakage

(c) Layer 2 — Deep Confined Aquifer

  • Comprised of Pliocene-age coarse sand, gravel, and boulder deposits
  • Depth: 60–300 m (varies across the valley)
  • Confined by the overlying Kalimati Clay — artesian conditions in places
  • Higher yield than the shallow aquifer: 10–50 m³/hr
  • Principal source for municipal water supply (KUKL/Melamchi supplement)
  • Faces declining water levels due to over-extraction

(d) Layer 3 — Basal Gravel

  • Boulder and cobble deposits at the base of the valley-fill, overlying the bedrock
  • Most productive aquifer where encountered — yields up to 100+ m³/hr
  • Not uniformly present — limited to paleochannel locations

9.3.3 Northern vs. Southern Groundwater Zones

Northern Groundwater Zone:
  • Alluvial fan deposits from rivers draining the Shivapuri range
  • Coarse material — high permeability
  • Main recharge zone for the valley’s deep aquifers
  • Water table is shallow; flows southward
  • Good quality, low contamination historically
Central/Southern Zone:
  • Thick Kalimati Clay dominates the central valley floor
  • Deep aquifer is confined — artesian in Patan/Thimi
  • Limited direct recharge to the deep aquifer
  • Water quality issues: ammonia, iron, hardness
  • Over-pumping has caused 1–3 m/year water level decline

9.3.4 Current Issues

  1. Over-extraction: Abstraction exceeds recharge by 2–3 times. Deep aquifer heads have declined by 20–30 m since the 1980s.
  2. Water quality degradation: Ammonia (>1.5 mg/L in many deep wells), iron, and coliform contamination.
  3. Land subsidence: Reports of 2–5 cm/year subsidence in parts of inner Kathmandu, attributed to groundwater depletion compressing the Kalimati Clay.
  4. Unregulated extraction: Thousands of private tube wells without permits — no metering or monitoring.
  5. Spring discharge decline: Reduced spring flows in the surrounding hills due to lowered regional water table.

9.3.5 Sustainable Development Strategies

  • Managed Aquifer Recharge (MAR): Injecting treated surface water or harvested rainwater into the deep aquifer through recharge wells, particularly in the northern recharge zone
  • Groundwater regulation: Licensing and metering of tube wells; establishment of groundwater protection zones
  • Conjunctive use: Integrating Melamchi surface water supply with strategic groundwater use for dry-season buffering
  • Monitoring network: Expansion of piezometric monitoring wells across the valley with real-time data logging
  • Rainwater harvesting: Mandatory in new buildings to reduce demand on groundwater

9.4 MIDDLE HILLS AND HIGH MOUNTAINS

9.4.1 Middle Hills (Mahabharat Zone)

The Middle Hills comprise metamorphic rocks (phyllite, schist, quartzite, gneiss) with limited primary porosity. Groundwater occurs primarily in:

  • Fractured rock aquifers: Water stored in joints, fractures, and faults. Limited yield (0.5–5 m³/hr).
  • Colluvial deposits: Weathered rock and debris on slopes — seasonal springs.
  • River terraces: Alluvial deposits along river valleys — moderate yields for dug wells.

The primary water source in the Middle Hills is springs. Nepal has an estimated 6,000+ springs, many of which are declining due to deforestation, climate change, and land-use changes.

9.4.2 High Himalayas

  • Crystalline rocks (granite, gneiss) with very low permeability
  • Groundwater is negligible except in glacial moraines and talus deposits
  • Springs are fed by snowmelt and glacial melt — highly seasonal
  • Not a focus for groundwater development

9.5 URBAN GROUNDWATER EXPLOITATION — ISSUES AND MITIGATION

PAST EXAM QUESTION ADDRESSED:

2078 Chaitra Q8 [6]: Discuss the overall condition of groundwater exploitation in urban areas with reference to Butwal and suggest mitigation measures.

9.5.1 General Urban Groundwater Issues in Nepal

Rapid urbanization in Nepal’s Terai cities (Butwal, Bharatpur, Birgunj, Biratnagar, Nepalgunj) and the Kathmandu Valley has led to increasing groundwater exploitation. Common issues include:

  • Declining water tables: Due to pumping exceeding natural recharge, particularly during the dry season (November–May)
  • Poor water quality: Faecal contamination from lack of sewerage; chemical contamination from industrial effluents
  • Well interference: Closely spaced wells competing for the same aquifer, reducing individual yields
  • Loss of recharge area: Paving and construction reduce infiltration; stormwater is drained away rather than recharged
  • Unregulated extraction: No licensing, no metering, no pumping limits

9.5.2 Case Study: Butwal

Butwal is a rapidly growing Terai city at the foot of the Siwaliks (Rupandehi District). Key groundwater features:

  • Aquifer type: Alluvial sand and gravel aquifers along the Tinau River fan
  • Depth range: Shallow wells at 10–30 m; deep wells at 60–120 m
  • Current status: Groundwater is the primary source of municipal water supply; Nepal Water Supply Corporation (NWSC) and private tankers rely heavily on bore wells
  • Problems:
    • Dry-season drawdown of 3–8 m in shallow wells
    • Reduction in Tinau River baseflow due to upstream and local pumping
    • Coliform contamination in shallow wells from inadequate sanitation
    • Increasing number of private tube wells without regulation

9.5.3 Mitigation Measures

  1. Groundwater regulation: Establish a licensing system for all tube wells; mandatory registration and periodic monitoring
  2. Managed Aquifer Recharge (MAR): Construct recharge pits, injection wells, and percolation ponds along the Tinau River to replenish aquifers during monsoon
  3. Rainwater harvesting: Promote household and institutional rainwater collection and recharge
  4. Conjunctive use: Develop surface water sources (Tinau diversion) for dry-season supplementation
  5. Wellhead protection zones: Establish protective zones around public supply wells to prevent contamination
  6. Monitoring network: Install piezometers across the city for continuous water level and quality monitoring
  7. Public awareness: Educate communities about water conservation and the finite nature of groundwater
  8. Sanitation improvement: Construct proper sewerage and wastewater treatment to prevent groundwater pollution
Exam tip: For a 6-mark question on urban groundwater (Butwal), organize your answer as: (a) Brief setting — 2–3 lines, (b) Current problems — 4–5 bullet points, (c) Mitigation measures — 5–6 numbered points. This structure ensures complete coverage.

9.6 GROUNDWATER STORAGE AND WELL-FIELD PLANNING (NUMERICAL)

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED:

2072 Ashwin Q3 [8]: For a confined aquifer in Pokhara, estimate annual groundwater storage and determine the number of wells. (Numerical)

2072 Magh Q3 [8]: For a confined aquifer in Janakpur, estimate annual groundwater storage and determine the number of wells. (Numerical)

9.6.1 Key Formulae

A. Annual Groundwater Storage Volume:

\[ \boxed{V = S \times A \times \Delta h} \]
  • \( S \) = storage coefficient (dimensionless) — for confined aquifer, typically 10−4–10−3
  • \( A \) = aquifer plan area (m²)
  • \( \Delta h \) = annual fluctuation of piezometric surface (m)

B. Number of Wells:

\[ \boxed{n = \frac{V}{q \times t}} \]
  • \( q \) = average yield per well (m³/hr)
  • \( t \) = annual pumping hours = pumping days × 24 hr/day
Physical Meaning: The storage coefficient \( S \) for a confined aquifer represents the volume of water released per unit area per unit decline in head. It is very small because water is released only by elastic compression of the aquifer skeleton and expansion of water. For unconfined aquifers, the specific yield \( S_y \) is 100–1000× larger because water drains from pore spaces by gravity.

9.6.2 Solved Example 1: Pokhara (2072 Ashwin Q3)

PROBLEM STATEMENT

In Pokhara, a confined aquifer of 30 m average thickness extends over 800 km². The piezometric surface fluctuates annually from 19 m to 9 m above the top of the aquifer. Storage coefficient S = 0.0008. Average well yield = 30 m³/hr, pumping duration = 200 days/year. Find: (a) annual groundwater storage, (b) number of wells that can be drilled.

Given Data:
Aquifer thickness = 30 m,  A = 800 km² = 800 × 106 m²
Piezometric fluctuation: 19 m → 9 m ⇒ Δh = 10 m
S = 0.0008,  q = 30 m³/hr,  Pumping = 200 days/year

SOLUTION

Step 1 — Annual groundwater storage:

\[ V = S \times A \times \Delta h = 0.0008 \times 800 \times 10^6 \times 10 \]
\[ V = 0.0008 \times 8 \times 10^9 = 6.4 \times 10^6 \text{ m}^3 \]

\( V = 6.4 \text{ million m}^3/\text{year} \)

Step 2 — Annual yield per well:

\[ q_{annual} = 30 \;\text{m}^3/\text{hr} \times 24 \;\text{hr/day} \times 200 \;\text{days} = 144{,}000 \;\text{m}^3/\text{year} \]

Step 3 — Number of wells:

\[ n = \frac{V}{q_{annual}} = \frac{6{,}400{,}000}{144{,}000} = 44.4 \]

\( \boxed{n \approx 44 \text{ wells}} \)

9.6.3 Solved Example 2: Janakpur (2072 Magh Q3)

PROBLEM STATEMENT

In Janakpur, a confined aquifer of 20 m average thickness extends over 850 km². Groundwater table fluctuates from 15 m to 10 m above the top. S = 0.0009. Average well yield = 30 m³/hr, pumping = 210 days/year. Find: (a) annual groundwater storage, (b) number of wells.

Given Data:
Aquifer thickness = 20 m,  A = 850 km² = 850 × 106 m²
Piezometric fluctuation: 15 m → 10 m ⇒ Δh = 5 m
S = 0.0009,  q = 30 m³/hr,  Pumping = 210 days/year

SOLUTION

Step 1 — Annual groundwater storage:

\[ V = S \times A \times \Delta h = 0.0009 \times 850 \times 10^6 \times 5 \]
\[ V = 0.0009 \times 4.25 \times 10^9 = 3.825 \times 10^6 \text{ m}^3 \]

\( V = 3.825 \text{ million m}^3/\text{year} \)

Step 2 — Annual yield per well:

\[ q_{annual} = 30 \times 24 \times 210 = 151{,}200 \;\text{m}^3/\text{year} \]

Step 3 — Number of wells:

\[ n = \frac{3{,}825{,}000}{151{,}200} = 25.3 \]

\( \boxed{n \approx 25 \text{ wells}} \)
