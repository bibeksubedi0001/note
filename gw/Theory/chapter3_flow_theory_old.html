3.1 STREAM LINES, EQUIPOTENTIAL LINES, AND FLOW NETS

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Define flow net with a neat sketch. State applications of flow net in geotechnical engineering and derive the expression for seepage discharge from a flow net. [3+3+4] (2078 Chaitra, Q3a)
  2. Derive an expression for seepage discharge from a flow net for an isotropic aquifer. [6] (2071 Bhadra, Q3)
DEFINITION: A flow net is a graphical representation of two-dimensional groundwater flow consisting of two families of curves — stream lines (flow lines) and equipotential lines — that intersect each other at right angles in isotropic media.

Key Terminology

  • Stream line (flow line): A line that is everywhere tangent to the velocity vector. Water particles move along stream lines. No flow crosses a stream line.
  • Equipotential line: A line connecting points of equal hydraulic head. If a piezometer is installed at any point along an equipotential line, the water would rise to the same elevation.
  • Flow channel: The zone between two adjacent stream lines through which a constant fraction of the total flow passes.
  • Potential drop: The difference in head between two successive equipotential lines.
Figure 3.1: A flow net beneath a dam showing stream lines (solid) and equipotential lines (dashed) intersecting at right angles.

Properties of a Flow Net

  1. Stream lines and equipotential lines are orthogonal (perpendicular) in isotropic media.
  2. The head drop between successive equipotential lines is constant: \(\Delta h = \frac{H}{N_d}\), where \(H\) is the total head loss and \(N_d\) is the number of potential drops.
  3. The discharge through each flow channel is the same: \(\Delta q = K \Delta h \frac{b}{a}\), where \(a\) and \(b\) are the dimensions of a flow-net cell.
  4. For a square flow net (\(a = b\)), the cell dimensions are equal, simplifying the computation.
  5. Neither stream lines nor equipotential lines can intersect themselves.
  6. Impervious boundaries are stream lines; constant-head boundaries are equipotential lines.

Applications of Flow Nets in Engineering

  1. Determination of seepage discharge beneath dams, sheet piles, and cofferdams.
  2. Estimation of uplift pressure on the base of hydraulic structures.
  3. Assessment of exit gradient at the downstream toe to check piping safety.
  4. Computation of pore-water pressure distribution for slope stability analysis.
  5. Analysis of dewatering and well-field problems.

3.2 SEEPAGE DISCHARGE FROM A FLOW NET

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Derive the expression for determining seepage discharge from flow net. [Part of Q3a] (2078 Chaitra)
  2. Derive an expression for seepage discharge from a flow net for an isotropic aquifer. [6] (2071 Bhadra, Q3)
Figure 3.2: Derivation of seepage from a single curvilinear square of a flow net.

Consider a single cell of the flow net with length \(a\) along the flow direction and width \(b\) perpendicular to flow, both measured in the plane of the flow net. By Darcy's law the discharge per unit thickness (into the page) through this cell is:

$$ \Delta q = K \frac{\Delta h}{a} \cdot b \cdot 1 $$

For a square flow net, \(a = b\), so \(\Delta q = K \Delta h\). The total head loss across the system equals \(H\), and it is divided equally among \(N_d\) equipotential drops, giving \(\Delta h = H / N_d\). There are \(N_f\) flow channels, each carrying \(\Delta q\). Therefore:

$$ \boxed{Q = K H \frac{N_f}{N_d}} $$

ISOTROPIC CASE

\(Q = K H \frac{N_f}{N_d}\)

where \(K\) is the uniform hydraulic conductivity.

ANISOTROPIC CASE

Transform the domain by: \(x' = x \sqrt{K_z/K_x}\).

Then: \(Q = H \frac{N_f}{N_d} \sqrt{K_x K_z}\)

Symbol Meaning
\(Q\) Total seepage discharge per unit width of structure (m³/s per m)
\(K\) Hydraulic conductivity (m/s or m/day)
\(H\) Total head loss = upstream head − downstream head
\(N_f\) Number of flow channels (spaces between stream lines)
\(N_d\) Number of equipotential drops
Figure 3.3: Flow net around a sheet pile showing stream lines (solid), equipotential lines (dashed), and the seepage formula.

3.3 WATER TABLE CONTOUR MAPS AND DIRECTION OF FLOW

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. State the uses of water table contour. Prepare a water table contour map and show the direction of groundwater flow from four observation wells. [3+4] (2073 Bhadra, Q3a)
  2. Explain how water table contour maps are prepared, state their uses, and prepare a groundwater contour map with flow direction. [8] (2072 Ashwin, Q4)
  3. Explain how a water table contour map is prepared. [4] (2072 Magh, Q5a)
DEFINITION: A water table contour map is a plan view showing lines of equal water-table elevation (groundwater equipotentials). It is the groundwater equivalent of a topographic contour map.

How a Water Table Contour Map Is Prepared

  1. Install observation wells (piezometers) at multiple locations across the study area.
  2. Measure the static water level in each well at a common reference time to eliminate tidal and pumping transients.
  3. Convert water levels to elevation above a common datum (e.g., mean sea level) by subtracting the depth-to-water from the surveyed ground elevation.
  4. Plot the well locations on a scaled plan and annotate each with its water-table elevation.
  5. Interpolate contour lines of equal head between the measurement points, using a chosen contour interval.
Figure 3.4: Water table contour map from four observation wells with 5 m contour interval, showing groundwater flow direction perpendicular to contour lines towards lower head.

Uses of Water Table Contour Maps

  1. Determine the direction of groundwater flow at any point (perpendicular to contours, pointing toward decreasing head).
  2. Estimate the hydraulic gradient at any location from the spacing of contour lines.
  3. Identify recharge and discharge zones (closely spaced contours near discharge, widely spaced in recharge areas).
  4. Assist in well-field planning by locating wells where flow converges.
  5. Evaluate the potential path of contaminant plumes in the subsurface.
  6. Calculate seepage flow through any cross section using Darcy's law with the gradient obtained from the map.
Figure 3.5: Three-well method for finding groundwater flow direction from a triangular network of piezometers (2072 Ashwin type problem).

3.4 DERIVATION OF LAPLACE EQUATION FOR STEADY GROUNDWATER FLOW

OBJECTIVE: The governing partial differential equation for steady-state groundwater flow in a homogeneous isotropic aquifer is the Laplace equation, which provides the theoretical foundation for flow-net analysis.

Consider a small control volume within a saturated aquifer. Under steady-state conditions, inflow equals outflow (no storage change). Using Darcy's law for the flux components and applying conservation of mass:

$$ \frac{\partial q_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial q_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial q_z}{\partial z} = 0 $$

Substituting Darcy's law \(q_i = -K_i \frac{\partial h}{\partial x_i}\):

$$ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(K_x \frac{\partial h}{\partial x}\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(K_y \frac{\partial h}{\partial y}\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\left(K_z \frac{\partial h}{\partial z}\right) = 0 $$

For a homogeneous isotropic medium (\(K_x = K_y = K_z = K\) = constant):

$$ \boxed{\frac{\partial^2 h}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 h}{\partial y^2} + \frac{\partial^2 h}{\partial z^2} = 0 \qquad (\text{Laplace Equation})} $$

Or in compact notation: \(\nabla^2 h = 0\). This states that for steady flow in homogeneous isotropic media, the field of hydraulic head satisfies Laplace's equation. Solutions to this equation are called harmonic functions.

diffusion equation.
Figure 3.6: Derivation pathway from conservation of mass and Darcy's law to the Laplace equation for steady flow.

Anisotropic Media

When \(K_x \neq K_z\), a coordinate transformation \(x' = x \sqrt{K_z / K_x}\) converts the anisotropic medium into an equivalent isotropic medium where the Laplace equation applies. After solving the transformed problem, the geometry is stretched back to physical coordinates. The effective conductivity for seepage computation is \(K_e = \sqrt{K_x K_z}\).

3.5 STEADY ONE-DIMENSIONAL FLOW IN AN UNCONFINED AQUIFER (DUPUIT THEORY)

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Two rivers are separated by a homogeneous unconfined aquifer. Compute seepage flow per unit river length and obtain the equation of the phreatic line. [7] (2073 Bhadra, Q3b)
DUPUIT ASSUMPTIONS: (1) Hydraulic gradient equals the slope of the water table. (2) Flow is essentially horizontal — all stream lines are horizontal and equipotential lines are vertical.

These assumptions are reasonable when the water table slope is small. They permit reduction of the two-dimensional free-surface problem to a one-dimensional equation.

Unconfined Flow Between Two Water Bodies (No Recharge)

Figure 3.7: Steady unconfined flow between two water bodies resting on an impervious base (Dupuit parabola).

Applying Darcy's law with the Dupuit assumption (\(q = -K h \frac{dh}{dx}\)) and integrating from \(x = 0\) where \(h = h_1\) to \(x = L\) where \(h = h_2\):

$$ Q = \frac{K}{2L}(h_1^2 - h_2^2) \qquad \text{(Dupuit equation)} $$

The water-table profile (phreatic surface equation) is:

$$ h^2 = h_1^2 - \frac{h_1^2 - h_2^2}{L} x $$

This is a parabolic profile — the water table drops more steeply near the discharge boundary.

3.6 UNCONFINED AQUIFER BETWEEN TWO WATER BODIES WITH RECHARGE

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Two irrigation canals are separated by a land strip underlain by an impervious layer. Determine seepage discharge into both canals, and obtain the phreatic surface equation and water table divide. [8] (2078 Chaitra, Q3b)
  2. Rain falls on a strip of land between two canals over an impervious stratum. Compute discharge into both canals, determine phreatic surface equation, water table divide, and seepage when rainfall is absent. [8] (2071 Bhadra, Q4)

When a uniform recharge \(p\) (rainfall that fully infiltrates) is applied to the land surface between two canals of depth \(h_1\) and \(h_2\) (with \(h_1 > h_2\)), the governing 1-D Dupuit equation becomes:

$$ \frac{d}{dx}\left(K h \frac{dh}{dx}\right) + p = 0 $$
Figure 3.8: Unconfined aquifer between two canals receiving uniform recharge, showing the water table divide and the two seepage components q1 and q2.

Integrating the governing equation with boundary conditions \(h = h_1\) at \(x=0\) and \(h = h_2\) at \(x = L\):

Phreatic Surface Equation

$$ h^2 = h_1^2 - \frac{h_1^2 - h_2^2}{L} x + \frac{p}{K} x(L - x) $$

Seepage to Each Canal (per unit length)

$$ q_1 = \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2L} + \frac{pL}{2} \qquad \text{(seepage into the deeper canal)} $$
$$ q_2 = \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2L} - \frac{pL}{2} + pL = \frac{pL}{2} - \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2L} $$

Note: The total recharge on unit strip is \(pL\), which equals \(q_1 + q_2\).

Location of the Water Table Divide

At the divide, \(dh/dx = 0\). Setting the gradient to zero:

$$ d = \frac{L}{2} - \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2pL} $$
Figure 3.9: The Darcy flux varies linearly from positive (toward the deeper canal) to negative (toward the shallower canal). The zero-crossing locates the water table divide.

SPECIAL CASE: NO RECHARGE (p = 0)

When there is no rainfall, the equations reduce to the simple Dupuit case:

$$ q = \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2L} $$

All flow moves toward the canal with lower head, and there is no water divide.

3.7 STEADY FLOW IN A CONFINED AQUIFER

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Determine the flow in the confined aquifer shown in the figure. [8] (2072 Ashwin, Q5)
  2. Determine the flow in the confined aquifer shown in the figure. [8] (2072 Magh, Q7)

A. Constant Thickness

For a confined aquifer of constant thickness \(b\) bounded by impervious layers, with head \(h_1\) at one end and \(h_2\) at the other, the one-dimensional Darcy equation gives:

$$ Q = T \frac{h_1 - h_2}{L} = K b \frac{h_1 - h_2}{L} $$
Figure 3.10: Steady one-dimensional flow in a confined aquifer of constant thickness b.

B. Variable Thickness (Wedge-Shaped Aquifer)

When the aquifer thickness varies linearly from \(b_1\) at \(x = 0\) to \(b_2\) at \(x = L\), the thickness at any section is \(b(x) = b_1 + \frac{b_2 - b_1}{L} x\). Applying Darcy's law and integrating:

$$ Q = K \frac{h_1 - h_2}{\ln(b_2/b_1)} \cdot \frac{b_2 - b_1}{L} $$
Figure 3.11: Steady flow in a confined aquifer of variable (wedge-shaped) thickness.

Horizontal Gallery (Infiltration Gallery)

A horizontal gallery extending up to impervious rock in an unconfined aquifer draws water equally from both sides. The governing equations are similar to the Dupuit analysis but with a line-sink boundary condition at the gallery location instead of a water-body boundary.
