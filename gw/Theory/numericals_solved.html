Problem 1: Darcy Validity Domain Around a Pumping Well

Ch 2 2078 Chaitra Q2(b) — 6 marks

Question: Determine the valid domain around a pumping well for which Darcy's law is applicable.

GIVEN DATA

Aquifer thickness, \(b\)18 m
Pumping rate, \(Q\)0.3 m³/s
Average grain diameter, \(d_m\)1 mm = 0.001 m
Critical Reynolds number, \(Re_{cr}\)8
Kinematic viscosity, \(\nu\)1 centistoke = \(1\times10^{-6}\) m²/s
Radial flow to a pumping well in a confined aquifer. Near the well, velocity is high and flow may become non-Darcian.

KEY FORMULAE USED

$$ q_r = \frac{Q}{2\pi r b} \qquad Re = \frac{q_r \cdot d_m}{\nu} \qquad r_{min} = \frac{Q \cdot d_m}{2\pi \cdot b \cdot \nu \cdot Re_{cr}} $$

Step 1 — Radial Darcy flux. For a fully penetrating well in a confined aquifer of thickness \(b\):

$$ q_r = \frac{Q}{2\pi r b} $$

Step 2 — Reynolds number at distance r.

$$ Re = \frac{q_r \cdot d_m}{\nu} = \frac{Q \cdot d_m}{2\pi \cdot r \cdot b \cdot \nu} $$

Step 3 — At the boundary of Darcy validity, set \(Re = Re_{cr}\).

$$ r_{min} = \frac{Q \cdot d_m}{2\pi \cdot b \cdot \nu \cdot Re_{cr}} $$

Step 4 — Substitute numerical values.

$$ r_{min} = \frac{0.3 \times 0.001}{2\pi \times 18 \times 1\times10^{-6} \times 8} = \frac{3 \times 10^{-4}}{2\pi \times 144 \times 10^{-6}} $$
$$ r_{min} = \frac{3 \times 10^{-4}}{9.0478 \times 10^{-4}} $$
\( r_{min} = 0.332 \text{ m} \approx 0.33 \text{ m} \)

Conclusion: Darcy's law is valid for \(r \geq 0.33\) m from the well centre. Within this radius of 33 cm, velocities are too high and inertial (non-Darcian) effects dominate.

Problem 2: Darcy Validity Domain Around a Pumping Well

Ch 2 2073 Bhadra Q2(b) — 8 marks

Question: Determine the domain around a pumping well for which Darcy's law is applicable.

GIVEN DATA

Pumping rate, \(Q\)1000 m³/hr
Aquifer thickness, \(b\)28 m
Average grain diameter, \(d_m\)1 mm = 0.001 m
Critical Reynolds number, \(Re_{cr}\)6
Kinematic viscosity, \(\nu\)1 centistoke = \(1\times10^{-6}\) m²/s

Step 1 — Convert pumping rate to m³/s.

$$ Q = \frac{1000}{3600} = 0.2778 \text{ m}^3/\text{s} $$

Step 2 — Apply the formula. (Same derivation as Problem 1.)

$$ r_{min} = \frac{Q \cdot d_m}{2\pi \cdot b \cdot \nu \cdot Re_{cr}} = \frac{0.2778 \times 0.001}{2\pi \times 28 \times 1\times10^{-6} \times 6} $$
$$ r_{min} = \frac{2.778 \times 10^{-4}}{2\pi \times 168 \times 10^{-6}} = \frac{2.778 \times 10^{-4}}{1.0556 \times 10^{-3}} $$
\( r_{min} = 0.263 \text{ m} \approx 0.26 \text{ m} \)

Conclusion: Darcy's law is applicable for \(r \geq 0.26\) m from the pumping well. Despite the higher pumping rate, the larger aquifer thickness and lower Recr lead to a smaller critical radius than Problem 1.

Problem 3: Darcy Applicability Check and Seepage Velocity

Ch 2 2071 Bhadra Q6 — 8 marks

Question: State whether Darcy's law is applicable for the given conditions, and determine the average pore velocity (seepage velocity).

GIVEN DATA

Aquifer thickness, \(b\)25 m
Effective porosity, \(n_e\)20% = 0.20
Hydraulic conductivity, \(K\)15 m/day
Distance between wells, \(L\)1100 m
Piezometric heads, \(h_1\), \(h_2\)5.5 m and 3.0 m
Grain diameter, \(d_m\)1 mm = 0.001 m
Kinematic viscosity, \(\nu\)0.01 cm²/s = \(1\times10^{-6}\) m²/s
One-dimensional flow between two observation wells through a confined aquifer.

Step 1 — Hydraulic gradient.

$$ i = \frac{h_1 - h_2}{L} = \frac{5.5 - 3.0}{1100} = \frac{2.5}{1100} = 2.273 \times 10^{-3} $$

Step 2 — Darcy flux (specific discharge).

$$ q = K \cdot i = 15 \times 2.273 \times 10^{-3} = 0.03409 \text{ m/day} $$

Convert to m/s: \(q = \frac{0.03409}{86400} = 3.945 \times 10^{-7}\) m/s.

Step 3 — Check Darcy validity (Reynolds number).

$$ Re = \frac{q \cdot d_m}{\nu} = \frac{3.945 \times 10^{-7} \times 0.001}{1 \times 10^{-6}} = \frac{3.945 \times 10^{-10}}{10^{-6}} $$
\( Re = 3.95 \times 10^{-4} \ll 1 \) — Darcy's law is fully applicable

Since \(Re \approx 0.0004\), which is far below any commonly accepted limit (1–10), the flow is deeply in the laminar regime.

Step 4 — Average pore velocity (seepage velocity).

$$ v_s = \frac{q}{n_e} = \frac{0.03409}{0.20} $$
\( v_s = 0.170 \text{ m/day} = 1.97 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m/s} \)

Physical insight: The actual groundwater velocity in the pores is about 5× the Darcy flux. This is because water can only flow through pore openings (20% of the cross section), not through the solid grains.

Problem 4: Unconfined Aquifer with Recharge — Canal Seepage

Ch 3 2078 Chaitra Q3(b) — 8 marks

Question: Two irrigation canals are separated by a land strip underlain by an impervious layer. Rainfall infiltrates the strip. Determine seepage discharge into both canals, obtain the phreatic surface equation, and locate the water table divide.

GIVEN DATA

Strip width, \(L\)1.57 km = 1570 m
Rainfall (recharge), \(p\)25 mm/hr
Canal water depth (left), \(h_1\)12 m
Canal water depth (right), \(h_2\)7 m
Permeability, \(K\)12 m/day
Two canals separated by a land strip receiving uniform rainfall infiltration.

KEY FORMULAE

$$ q_1 = \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2L} + \frac{pL}{2}, \quad q_2 = \frac{pL}{2} - \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2L}, \quad d = \frac{L}{2} - \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2pL} $$

Step 1 — Convert rainfall to m/day.

$$ p = 25 \text{ mm/hr} = 25 \times \frac{24}{1000} = 0.6 \text{ m/day} $$

Step 2 — Compute the Dupuit gradient term.

$$ \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2L} = \frac{12(144 - 49)}{2 \times 1570} = \frac{12 \times 95}{3140} = \frac{1140}{3140} = 0.363 \text{ m}^2/\text{day} $$

Step 3 — Compute the recharge term.

$$ \frac{pL}{2} = \frac{0.6 \times 1570}{2} = \frac{942}{2} = 471.0 \text{ m}^2/\text{day} $$

Step 4 — Seepage into the deeper canal (q1, at x = 0).

$$ q_1 = 0.363 + 471.0 $$
\( q_1 = 471.36 \text{ m}^2/\text{day per m of canal length} \)

Step 5 — Seepage into the shallower canal (q2, at x = L).

$$ q_2 = 471.0 - 0.363 $$
\( q_2 = 470.64 \text{ m}^2/\text{day per m of canal length} \)

Verification: \(q_1 + q_2 = 471.36 + 470.64 = 942.0 = pL = 0.6 \times 1570\). ✓

Step 6 — Phreatic surface equation.

$$ h^2 = h_1^2 - \frac{h_1^2 - h_2^2}{L}\,x + \frac{p}{K}\,x(L - x) $$
\( h^2 = 144 - 0.0605\,x + 0.05\,x(1570 - x) \)

Expanding: \(h^2 = 144 + 78.44\,x - 0.05\,x^2\).

Step 7 — Water table divide.

$$ d = \frac{L}{2} - \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2pL} = \frac{1570}{2} - \frac{12 \times 95}{2 \times 0.6 \times 1570} = 785 - \frac{1140}{1884} $$
\( d = 785 - 0.605 = 784.4 \text{ m from the deeper canal} \)

Physical insight: The water divide is nearly at the midpoint because the intense rainfall (0.6 m/day) dominates over the small Dupuit gradient term (0.363 m²/day). Almost equal volumes seep to both canals.

Problem 5: Dupuit Equation — Unconfined Flow Without Recharge

Ch 3 2073 Bhadra Q3(b) — 7 marks

Question: Two rivers are separated by a homogeneous unconfined aquifer resting on an impervious base. Compute seepage flow per unit river length and obtain the equation of the phreatic line.

GIVEN DATA

Distance between rivers, \(L\)5 km = 5000 m
Head at left river, \(h_1\)20 m
Head at right river, \(h_2\)11 m
Permeability, \(K\)18 m/day
Unconfined aquifer between two rivers (no recharge). The phreatic surface forms a Dupuit parabola.

Step 1 — Seepage discharge per unit river length (Dupuit equation).

$$ Q = \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2L} = \frac{18(20^2 - 11^2)}{2 \times 5000} = \frac{18(400 - 121)}{10000} = \frac{18 \times 279}{10000} $$
\( Q = 0.502 \text{ m}^2/\text{day per m of river length} \)

Step 2 — Phreatic surface equation.

$$ h^2 = h_1^2 - \frac{h_1^2 - h_2^2}{L}\,x = 400 - \frac{279}{5000}\,x $$
\( h^2 = 400 - 0.0558\,x \quad \Rightarrow \quad h = \sqrt{400 - 0.0558\,x} \)

Physical insight: This parabolic profile drops more steeply near the discharge boundary (right river) because the same flow passes through a progressively thinner saturated section.

Problem 6: Confined Aquifer — Variable Thickness

Ch 3 2072 Ashwin Q5 — 8 marks

Question: Determine the flow in the confined aquifer of variable (wedge-shaped) thickness shown in the figure.

GIVEN DATA

Hydraulic conductivity, \(K\)0.4 m/day
Aquifer length, \(L\)800 m
Thickness at left, \(b_1\)8 m
Thickness at right, \(b_2\)6 m
Head at left, \(h_1\)10 m
Head at right, \(h_2\)8 m
Confined aquifer with linearly varying thickness (wedge-shaped), flow from left to right.

FORMULA FOR VARIABLE-THICKNESS CONFINED AQUIFER

$$ Q = K \frac{(h_1 - h_2)(b_2 - b_1)}{L \ln(b_2 / b_1)} $$

This is derived by integrating Darcy's law \(Q = K \cdot b(x) \cdot \frac{dh}{dx}\) where \(b(x) = b_1 + \frac{b_2-b_1}{L}x\) varies linearly.

Step 1 — Substitute values.

$$ Q = 0.4 \times \frac{(10 - 8)(6 - 8)}{800 \times \ln(6/8)} = 0.4 \times \frac{2 \times (-2)}{800 \times \ln(0.75)} $$

Step 2 — Evaluate the logarithm.

$$ \ln(0.75) = -0.2877 $$

Step 3 — Complete the computation.

$$ Q = 0.4 \times \frac{-4}{800 \times (-0.2877)} = 0.4 \times \frac{-4}{-230.16} = 0.4 \times 0.01738 $$
\( Q = 6.95 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^2/\text{day per m width} \approx 0.00695 \text{ m}^2/\text{day} \)

Conclusion: Flow is from \(h_1 = 10\) m toward \(h_2 = 8\) m (decreasing head). The low K and large length result in a very small discharge.

Problem 7: Confined Aquifer — Variable Thickness

Ch 3 2072 Magh Q7 — 8 marks

Question: Determine the flow in the confined aquifer of variable thickness shown in the figure.

GIVEN DATA

Aquifer length, \(L\)950 m
Hydraulic conductivity, \(K\)0.56 m/day
Head at left, \(h_1\)12 m
Thickness at left, \(b_1\)7 m
Head at right, \(h_2\)9 m
Thickness at right, \(b_2\)5 m

This is the same type as Problem 6. Applying the variable-thickness formula directly:

Step 1 — Substitute.

$$ Q = 0.56 \times \frac{(12 - 9)(5 - 7)}{950 \times \ln(5/7)} = 0.56 \times \frac{3 \times (-2)}{950 \times \ln(0.7143)} $$

Step 2 — Evaluate.

$$ \ln(0.7143) = -0.3365 $$
$$ Q = 0.56 \times \frac{-6}{950 \times (-0.3365)} = 0.56 \times \frac{-6}{-319.68} = 0.56 \times 0.01877 $$
\( Q = 1.051 \times 10^{-2} \text{ m}^2/\text{day per m width} \approx 0.0105 \text{ m}^2/\text{day} \)

Comparison with Problem 6: Despite a longer aquifer (950 m vs 800 m), the higher K (0.56 vs 0.4), larger head difference (3 m vs 2 m) result in a 50% greater discharge.

Problem 8: Unconfined Aquifer with Recharge + No-Rain Case

Ch 3 2071 Bhadra Q4 — 8 marks

Question: Rain falls on a strip of land between two parallel canals over an impervious stratum. Compute discharge into both canals, determine the phreatic surface equation, locate the water table divide, and compute seepage when rainfall is absent.

GIVEN DATA

Rainfall (recharge), \(p\)12 mm/hr
Strip width, \(L\)1200 m
Canal depth (left), \(h_1\)10 m
Canal depth (right), \(h_2\)8 m
Permeability, \(K\)10 m/day
Land strip receiving rainfall between two canals of different depth.

Step 1 — Convert rainfall.

$$ p = 12 \text{ mm/hr} = \frac{12 \times 24}{1000} = 0.288 \text{ m/day} $$

Step 2 — Compute the two terms.

$$ \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2L} = \frac{10(100 - 64)}{2400} = \frac{360}{2400} = 0.15 \text{ m}^2/\text{day} $$
$$ \frac{pL}{2} = \frac{0.288 \times 1200}{2} = \frac{345.6}{2} = 172.8 \text{ m}^2/\text{day} $$

Step 3 — Seepage into the deeper canal (q1).

\( q_1 = 0.15 + 172.8 = 172.95 \text{ m}^2/\text{day} \)

Step 4 — Seepage into the shallower canal (q2).

\( q_2 = 172.8 - 0.15 = 172.65 \text{ m}^2/\text{day} \)

Verification: \(q_1 + q_2 = 172.95 + 172.65 = 345.6 = pL\). ✓

Step 5 — Phreatic surface equation.

\( h^2 = 100 - 0.03\,x + 0.0288\,x(1200 - x) \)

Step 6 — Water table divide.

$$ d = \frac{1200}{2} - \frac{10 \times 36}{2 \times 0.288 \times 1200} = 600 - \frac{360}{691.2} = 600 - 0.521 $$
\( d = 599.5 \text{ m from the deeper canal (essentially at centre)} \)

Step 7 — Seepage when rainfall is absent (p = 0).

Setting \(p = 0\), the recharge terms vanish and we get the simple Dupuit equation:

$$ q = \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2L} = 0.15 \text{ m}^2/\text{day} $$
\( q_{p=0} = 0.15 \text{ m}^2/\text{day} \) — all flow goes toward the lower canal only. No water divide.

Physical insight: Rainfall contributes 345.6 m²/day compared to only 0.15 m²/day from the Dupuit gradient. The recharge dominates the system by a factor of 2300. Without rain, the flow drops by 99.96%.

Problem 9: Water Table Contour Map Construction

Ch 3 2073 Bhadra Q3(a) — 7 marks

Question: State the uses of a water-table contour map. Prepare a water table contour map and show the direction of groundwater flow from four observation wells.

GIVEN DATA

Well A (bottom-left)56 m
Well B (bottom-right)51 m
Well C (top-right)66 m
Well D (top-left)76 m
Contour interval5 m
Completed water table contour map with interpolated 5 m contours and flow direction arrows perpendicular to the contour lines.

Step 1 — Assign well positions. Place wells at rectangle corners: D = 76 m (top-left), C = 66 m (top-right), A = 56 m (bottom-left), B = 51 m (bottom-right).

Step 2 — Interpolate contour crossings on each side.

  • Side AD (56 → 76 m, left edge): 60 m at \(\frac{60-56}{76-56} = 20\%\), 65 m at 45%, 70 m at 70%, 75 m at 95% from A.
  • Side AB (56 → 51 m, bottom edge): 55 m at \(\frac{56-55}{56-51} = 20\%\) from A.
  • Side BC (51 → 66 m, right edge): 55 m at 27%, 60 m at 60%, 65 m at 93% from B.
  • Side DC (76 → 66 m, top edge): 75 m at 10%, 70 m at 60% from D.

Step 3 — Draw contour lines. Connect points of equal head across the rectangle (see figure above). The contours run roughly from upper-left to lower-right.

Step 4 — Determine flow direction.

Flow is perpendicular to contour lines in the direction of decreasing head — from the 76 m corner (D) toward the 51 m corner (B), roughly along the rectangle diagonal.

Step 5 — Estimate hydraulic gradient.

$$ i = \frac{\Delta h}{\text{perpendicular distance between contours}} $$

Closely spaced contours = steep gradient (fast flow); widely spaced = gentle gradient (slow flow).

Uses of Water Table Contour Maps

  1. Determine groundwater flow direction at any point.
  2. Estimate hydraulic gradient from contour spacing.
  3. Identify recharge and discharge zones.
  4. Assist in well-field planning and contamination tracking.
  5. Calculate seepage flow through any cross section using Darcy's law.
