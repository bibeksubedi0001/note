5.1 INTRODUCTION — WHY PUMPING TESTS?

PAST EXAM QUESTION ADDRESSED:

  1. Why is the pumping test carried out in groundwater studies? [2] (2073 Bhadra, Q5a)
CORE IDEA: A pumping test is the most reliable field method for determining aquifer hydraulic properties — transmissivity (T), storage coefficient (S), and hydraulic conductivity (K) — at realistic field scale.

Laboratory tests on soil samples (e.g., constant-head or falling-head permeameter) provide values of hydraulic conductivity at a point, but they cannot capture the variability, layering, and effective connectivity of a real aquifer. A pumping test stresses the aquifer over a large volume and yields integrated (field-scale) parameters that directly govern well yield, drawdown prediction, and groundwater resource planning.

Objectives of a Pumping Test

  1. Determine transmissivity (T = Kb) — the rate at which water is transmitted through a unit width of the aquifer under a unit hydraulic gradient. This controls well yield and drawdown.
  2. Determine storage coefficient (S) — the volume of water released from (or taken into) storage per unit area per unit change in head. This controls how quickly the cone of depression expands.
  3. Evaluate well performance — measure well efficiency, specific capacity, and well losses.
  4. Assess aquifer boundaries — detect impermeable barriers or recharge boundaries via deviations in the drawdown curve.
  5. Estimate radius of influence (R) — the distance beyond which pumping has negligible effect.
  6. Design production wells — determine sustainable pumping rate, well spacing, and predict long-term drawdown.
Figure 5.1: Typical pumping test setup in a confined aquifer — one pumping well at constant rate Q and two observation wells (OW) at distances r1 and r2. Drawdowns s1 and s2 are measured over time.

5.2 TYPES OF PUMPING TESTS

PAST EXAM QUESTION ADDRESSED:

  1. Explain the types of pumping test used in the field to determine the permeability of soil. [8] (2072 Magh, Q6)

Pumping tests are classified according to the flow regime achieved during the test and the method of analysis used:

A. Constant-Rate Pumping Test (Aquifer Test)

The well is pumped at a constant discharge Q and drawdown is measured in one or more observation wells at increasing time intervals. This is the standard test for determining T and S. Two analysis approaches:

(i) Transient (Unsteady-State) Analysis

Drawdown changes with time. Used when the test has not reached equilibrium. Yields both T and S.

  • Theis type-curve method — matches field data to the Theis well function W(u)
  • Cooper-Jacob time-drawdown — semi-log straight-line method (late-time data)

(ii) Steady-State (Equilibrium) Analysis

Drawdown stabilizes (does not change further with time). Yields only T (not S).

  • Thiem’s method — uses drawdown difference between two observation wells
  • Distance-drawdown method — plots s vs log(r) from multiple wells

B. Step-Drawdown Test

The pumping rate is increased in steps (e.g., Q, 2Q, 3Q), each maintained until drawdown stabilizes. Determines:

  • Well efficiency — ratio of theoretical to actual drawdown
  • Well loss coefficient (C) in the equation \(s_w = BQ + CQ^2\), where BQ is the aquifer (Darcian) loss and CQ² is the well (non-Darcian) loss
  • Optimum pumping rate for the well

C. Recovery Test

After the pump is shut off, the water level recovers (rises) toward its original position. Substituting “residual drawdown” into the Theis/Cooper-Jacob equations yields T independently. This is a valuable check on T obtained during the pumping phase.

D. Slug Test (Bail Test)

A known volume of water is instantaneously added to or removed from a well, and the subsequent recovery is monitored. No pumping is required. Used in low-permeability formations or when observation wells are not available. Yields K at the well screen, but not T or S at field scale.

5.3 THEIS EQUATION — UNSTEADY RADIAL FLOW TO A WELL

PAST EXAM QUESTION ADDRESSED:

  1. Explain the Theis solution for determination of storage coefficient and transmissibility of an aquifer. [6] (2072 Ashwin, Q11)
SIGNIFICANCE: The Theis (1935) equation was the first analytical solution for transient (time-dependent) flow to a well. It allows determination of both T and S from a single pumping test — the foundation of modern aquifer test analysis.

Assumptions

  1. The aquifer is confined, homogeneous, isotropic, and of uniform thickness b.
  2. The aquifer extends infinitely in all horizontal directions (no boundaries).
  3. The well is fully penetrating and pumps at a constant rate Q.
  4. Flow is horizontal and radially symmetric toward the well.
  5. Water is released from storage instantaneously with decline in head.
  6. The well has infinitesimal diameter (no well storage effects).
  7. No recharge occurs during the test.

Governing Equation

Starting from the radial form of the confined flow equation (combining Darcy’s law with continuity):

$$ \frac{\partial^2 h}{\partial r^2} + \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial h}{\partial r} = \frac{S}{T}\frac{\partial h}{\partial t} $$

With boundary conditions: \(h(r, 0) = h_0\) (initial), \(h(\infty, t) = h_0\) (infinite boundary), and \(\lim_{r \to 0} r\,\partial h/\partial r = -Q/(2\pi T)\) (well discharge).

Theis Solution

Theis solved this by recognising an analogy with heat conduction in solids. The drawdown at distance r from the well at time t is:

$$ \boxed{s(r,t) = \frac{Q}{4\pi T}\,W(u)} $$

where \(W(u)\) is the Theis well function:

$$ W(u) = \int_u^{\infty} \frac{e^{-y}}{y}\,dy = -0.5772 - \ln u + u - \frac{u^2}{2 \cdot 2!} + \frac{u^3}{3 \cdot 3!} - \cdots $$

and the dimensionless time parameter u is:

$$ \boxed{u = \frac{r^2 S}{4Tt}} $$

Physical Meaning of Parameters

Transmissivity (T)

$$ T = K \cdot b \quad [\text{m}^2/\text{day}] $$

Rate of water flow through a unit width of the full aquifer thickness under unit gradient. High T = high yield. Typical: 10–10,000 m²/day.

Storage Coefficient (S)

Confined: S = 10−5 to 10−3 (elastic storage only). Unconfined: S = Sy = 0.01 to 0.30 (gravity drainage). Small S means the cone of depression expands rapidly.

5.4 THEIS TYPE-CURVE METHOD FOR T AND S

PURPOSE: Graphical curve-matching technique that works for all pumping times, not just late-time data. Yields both T and S.

Step-by-Step Procedure

  1. Prepare the type curve: Plot W(u) vs 1/u on log-log paper (standard Theis curve). This is a fixed reference curve.
  2. Plot field data: On a separate sheet of log-log paper at the same scale, plot drawdown s (y-axis) vs time t (x-axis) for each observation well.
  3. Overlay and match: Place the field data sheet on top of the type curve. Keep axes parallel. Slide the field data sheet until the data points align with the type curve.
  4. Pick a match point: Choose any convenient point on the overlapping sheets. Read four values:
    • From the type curve sheet: \(W(u)\) and \(1/u\)
    • From the field data sheet: \(s\) and \(t\)
  5. Calculate T:
    $$ \boxed{T = \frac{Q \cdot W(u)}{4\pi\,s}} $$
  6. Calculate S:
    $$ \boxed{S = \frac{4Tu}{r^2} = \frac{4Tt}{r^2 \cdot (1/u)}} $$
Figure 5.4: Theis type-curve matching — overlay field data on the standard type curve, find a match point, and calculate T and S.

ADVANTAGES AND LIMITATIONS

Advantages: Works for all time periods (early and late). Uses the full Theis solution without approximation. Robust for small datasets.

Limitations: Subjective curve matching (different analysts may get slightly different match points). Requires log-log paper at identical scales. May be difficult when data scatter is large.

5.5 THIEM’S EQUILIBRIUM (STEADY-STATE) METHOD FOR T

PAST EXAM QUESTION ADDRESSED:

  1. Write the step-by-step procedure to determine transmissivity (T) by Thiem’s method with the necessary mathematical expression. [6] (2078 Chaitra, Q5a)
APPLICABILITY: Thiem’s method applies when pumping has continued long enough that drawdown no longer changes with time (steady state). It yields T but not S.

Derivation

At steady state, for radial flow in a confined aquifer, by Darcy’s law at distance r from the well:

$$ Q = 2\pi\,r\,b\,K\,\frac{dh}{dr} = 2\pi\,T\,r\,\frac{dh}{dr} $$

Separating variables and integrating between two observation wells at distances \(r_1\) and \(r_2\) (with heads \(h_1\) and \(h_2\)):

$$ Q \int_{r_1}^{r_2} \frac{dr}{r} = 2\pi T \int_{h_1}^{h_2} dh $$
$$ Q \ln\frac{r_2}{r_1} = 2\pi T (h_2 - h_1) $$

Since drawdown \(s = h_0 - h\), we have \(h_2 - h_1 = s_1 - s_2\). Therefore:

$$ \boxed{T = \frac{Q \ln(r_2/r_1)}{2\pi(s_1 - s_2)} = \frac{2.303\,Q\,\log_{10}(r_2/r_1)}{2\pi(s_1 - s_2)}} $$
Figure 5.5: Thiem’s steady-state method — drawdowns s1 and s2 at distances r1 and r2 from the pumping well, with Q = constant.

Step-by-Step Procedure (Exam Answer for 2078 Q5a)

THIEM’S METHOD — PROCEDURE TO DETERMINE T

  1. Install observation wells at two or more known distances \(r_1, r_2, \ldots\) from the pumping well in the confined aquifer.
  2. Pump at constant rate Q until drawdowns \(s_1, s_2, \ldots\) stabilize (stop changing with time). This confirms steady-state conditions.
  3. Record the steady-state drawdowns \(s_1\) at distance \(r_1\) and \(s_2\) at distance \(r_2\) (where \(r_2 > r_1\), so \(s_1 > s_2\)).
  4. Apply Thiem’s equation:
    $$ T = \frac{Q}{2\pi(s_1 - s_2)} \ln\!\left(\frac{r_2}{r_1}\right) $$
    Or equivalently, using log10:
    $$ T = \frac{2.303\,Q}{2\pi(s_1 - s_2)} \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{r_2}{r_1}\right) $$
  5. Calculate K from \(T = Kb\), i.e., \(K = T/b\), where b is the aquifer thickness.

Alternative (distance-drawdown graph): If data from multiple observation wells is available, plot s vs log10(r). The data should form a straight line. From the slope (Δs per log cycle of r):

$$ T = \frac{2.303\,Q}{2\pi\,\Delta s} $$

The line extended to s = 0 gives the radius of influence R0.

Limitations of Thiem’s Method

  • Requires true steady state — often takes hours to days to achieve (may never be reached in practice).
  • Cannot determine S (storage coefficient), only T.
  • Requires at least two observation wells (or the pumping well data with one observation well, although this introduces well losses).
  • Assumes confined aquifer with no leakage, no boundaries.

5.6 COOPER-JACOB STRAIGHT-LINE APPROXIMATION

PAST EXAM QUESTION ADDRESSED:

  1. Explain the Cooper and Jacob method for determination of storage coefficient and transmissivity of an aquifer using time-drawdown data. [6] (2072 Magh, Q7)
KEY INSIGHT: For small values of u (i.e., large time t or small distance r), the Theis well function W(u) can be approximated by a logarithmic expression, transforming the Theis curve into a straight line on semi-log paper.

Mathematical Basis

Recall the series expansion of W(u):

$$ W(u) = -0.5772 - \ln u + u - \frac{u^2}{4} + \cdots $$

When \(u < 0.01\), the terms beyond \(-\ln u\) become negligible (\(u < 0.01\) contributes less than 1%), so:

$$ W(u) \approx -0.5772 - \ln u = \ln\frac{1}{u} - 0.5772 = \ln\frac{e^{-0.5772}}{u} = \ln\frac{0.5625}{u} $$

Substituting \(u = r^2S/(4Tt)\) into the Theis equation:

$$ s = \frac{Q}{4\pi T}\ln\frac{0.5625 \times 4Tt}{r^2 S} = \frac{Q}{4\pi T}\ln\frac{2.25Tt}{r^2 S} $$

Converting to log10 (multiply by 2.303):

$$ \boxed{s = \frac{2.303\,Q}{4\pi\,T}\,\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{2.25\,T\,t}{r^2\,S}\right)} $$

Why This Is a Straight Line

For a fixed observation well at distance r:

$$ s = \frac{2.303\,Q}{4\pi T}\,\log_{10}(t) + \frac{2.303\,Q}{4\pi T}\,\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{2.25\,T}{r^2 S}\right) $$

This is of the form \(s = m \log_{10}(t) + c\), which is a straight line when s is plotted against log10(t). The slope m per log cycle equals:

$$ \Delta s \text{ (per log cycle)} = \frac{2.303\,Q}{4\pi\,T} $$

Validity Condition

COOPER-JACOB VALIDITY

The approximation is valid when:

$$ u = \frac{r^2 S}{4Tt} < 0.01 $$

In practice, this means:

  • For an observation well at r = 100 m with T = 1000 m²/day and S = 10−4: u < 0.01 when t > 0.0025 day = 3.6 min. So the method is valid almost from the start!
  • For low-T aquifers or distant observation wells, early-time data may have u > 0.01 and will deviate from the straight line.

5.7 COOPER-JACOB TIME-DRAWDOWN METHOD

This is the most widely used pumping test analysis method and the most frequently examined in this course.

Step-by-Step Procedure

PROCEDURE — COOPER-JACOB TIME-DRAWDOWN METHOD

  1. Tabulate data: Record time t and drawdown s from the observation well at distance r from the pumping well.
  2. Plot on semi-log paper: Plot s (linear y-axis, increasing downward) vs t (logarithmic x-axis). Alternatively, s on y vs log10(t) on x with both axes linear.
  3. Fit a straight line through the late-time data points (where u < 0.01). Early points that deviate from the line are expected and should be ignored for fitting.
  4. Determine Δs = drawdown per log cycle of time — the vertical rise of the line over one log cycle of the t-axis.
  5. Calculate T:
    $$ \boxed{T = \frac{2.303\,Q}{4\pi\,\Delta s}} $$
  6. Determine t0 = the time at which the best-fit straight line intersects s = 0 (extrapolate back if needed).
  7. Calculate S:
    $$ \boxed{S = \frac{2.25\,T\,t_0}{r^2}} $$
  8. Check validity: Compute \(u = r^2S/(4Tt)\) for the earliest data point used. It should be less than 0.01.
Figure 5.6: Cooper-Jacob time-drawdown plot. The slope (Δs per log cycle) gives T; the zero-drawdown intercept t0 gives S.

Physical Interpretation

  • Δs is small → the line is flat → high T (aquifer transmits water easily, so drawdown increases slowly).
  • Δs is large → steep line → low T (aquifer resists flow, drawdown grows rapidly).
  • t0 is small → small S (little storage, cone expands quickly, intersecting s = 0 at a very early time).
  • t0 is large → large S (ample storage, takes more time for drawdown to manifest).

5.8 COOPER-JACOB DISTANCE-DRAWDOWN METHOD

When data from multiple observation wells at different distances is available (typically at steady or quasi-steady state), the same Cooper-Jacob logic can be applied spatially.

Procedure

  1. Plot s (linear y-axis) vs r (logarithmic x-axis). Or equivalently, s vs log10(r).
  2. Fit a straight line through the data points.
  3. Read Δs = drawdown change per log cycle of distance.
  4. Calculate T:
    $$ \boxed{T = \frac{2.303\,Q}{2\pi\,\Delta s}} $$

    (Note: \(2\pi\) in denominator, not \(4\pi\), because this is the steady-state Thiem equation.)

  5. Extend the line to s = 0 → the r-intercept is the radius of influence R0 (the distance at which the aquifer is unaffected by pumping).
Figure 5.7: Distance-drawdown plot. The slope gives T via the Thiem formula; the zero-drawdown intercept gives the radius of influence R0.

5.9 RECOVERY TEST

After the pump is stopped (at time tp since pumping started), the water level recovers. By the principle of superposition, the recovery can be modelled as continuing the original pumping AND injecting at rate Q from time tp. The residual drawdown s’ at time t’ after pump shutdown is:

$$ s' = \frac{2.303\,Q}{4\pi\,T}\,\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{t}{t'}\right) $$

where \(t = t_p + t'\) = total time since pumping started, and \(t'\) = time since pumping stopped.

Procedure

  1. Plot residual drawdown s’ (y-axis, linear) vs \(t/t'\) (x-axis, log scale).
  2. Fit a straight line through the data.
  3. Determine Δs’ per log cycle of \(t/t'\).
  4. Compute:
    $$ \boxed{T = \frac{2.303\,Q}{4\pi\,\Delta s'}} $$
Figure 5.8: Recovery test — after the pump is stopped, residual drawdown s’ is plotted against t/t’ on semi-log paper. The slope yields T.

Advantages of the Recovery Test

  • Independent check on T obtained during the pumping phase.
  • No flow meter needed during recovery (Q is known from the pumping phase).
  • Not affected by well losses (there is no discharge during recovery, so the non-Darcian turbulence zone disappears).
  • Simpler data collection — only one well (even the pumping well) is needed.

5.10 IMAGE WELL THEORY AND BOUNDARY EFFECTS

Real aquifers are not infinite — they encounter boundaries such as impermeable barriers (faults, clay layers) and recharge boundaries (rivers, lakes). The method of images handles these by placing imaginary wells symmetrically across the boundary:

Impermeable Boundary (Barrier)

An image pumping well (same sign, +Q) is placed at the mirror position across the barrier. This doubles the drawdown near the boundary.

Effect: The drawdown curve steepens — a second straight line with double the slope (Δs doubles) appears on the Cooper-Jacob plot.

Recharge Boundary (River)

An image recharge well (opposite sign, −Q) is placed at the mirror position across the river. This reduces the drawdown.

Effect: The drawdown curve flattens and eventually stabilizes (reaches steady state even without true equilibrium) — the Cooper-Jacob line flattens to zero slope.

Detecting Boundaries from Pumping Test Data

  • On a Cooper-Jacob time-drawdown plot, a barrier causes the late-time data to steepen (upward break in the line, slope doubles).
  • A recharge boundary causes the data to flatten below the original line (downward break, slope approaches zero).
  • Distance to the boundary can be estimated from the time at which the break occurs using \(r_i = \sqrt{4Tt_b/S}\), where tb is the break time.
