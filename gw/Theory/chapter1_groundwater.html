1.1 INTRODUCTION TO GROUNDWATER ENGINEERING

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. What is Groundwater Engineering? What is the importance of groundwater in the context of Nepal? Write the advantages and disadvantages of groundwater. [2+3+3] (2071 Bhadra, Q1)
  2. State the advantages of groundwater in comparison to surface water. [4] (2073 Bhadra, Q1a)
DEFINITION: Groundwater Engineering is the branch of civil engineering concerned with the physics of saturated flow in porous media and its engineering applications — including groundwater occurrence, Darcian flow, well hydraulics, pumping tests, exploration methods, tube-well design, pump selection, and the economics of groundwater utilization.

Groundwater is subsurface water that occupies the saturated zone of soil and rock formations beneath the earth's surface. It constitutes roughly 30% of the world's total freshwater and is the largest accessible reservoir of liquid freshwater on the planet. In the context of Nepal, groundwater is of particular importance because:

  • The Terai region (southern plains) is underlain by extensive alluvial aquifers that serve as the primary water source for drinking, irrigation, and industry for over 50% of Nepal's population.
  • The Kathmandu Valley depends heavily on groundwater to supplement inadequate surface water supply, with deep tube wells tapping confined aquifers within lacustrine sediments.
  • In hilly areas, springs issuing from groundwater are often the sole source of drinking water for rural communities.

Advantages of Groundwater over Surface Water

  1. Better Quality: Groundwater is naturally filtered through soil and rock formations, making it generally free from turbidity, pathogenic organisms, and organic matter. It requires minimal treatment before use.
  2. Reliable and Drought-Resistant: Groundwater is less affected by seasonal variations and is available even during extended dry periods, unlike rivers and lakes which may dry up.
  3. Widespread Availability: Groundwater exists beneath most land areas, enabling decentralized water supply even in remote locations far from rivers or reservoirs.
  4. Low Evaporation Loss: Being stored underground, groundwater is not subject to evaporation, unlike surface reservoirs which lose large volumes, especially in hot climates.
  5. Lower Development Cost: Wells and bore holes can be drilled incrementally; they do not require massive dams, canals, or treatment plants, making the per-unit cost often lower.
  6. Constant Temperature: Groundwater temperature remains nearly constant year-round, which is advantageous for industrial processes and aquaculture.

Disadvantages of Groundwater

  1. Over-extraction and Depletion: Excessive pumping beyond recharge capacity lowers the water table, causes well failure, land subsidence, and ecological damage.
  2. Contamination Persistence: Once polluted, groundwater is extremely difficult and costly to remediate because of slow flow velocities and limited accessibility.
  3. Dissolved Minerals: Prolonged contact with rock may result in excessive hardness, iron, fluoride, or arsenic concentrations requiring treatment.
  4. Limited Visibility: Unlike surface water, groundwater quantity and quality are difficult to assess without costly exploration (drilling, geophysics, pumping tests).

1.2 THE HYDROLOGICAL CYCLE AND ORIGIN OF GROUNDWATER

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Explain briefly the hydrological cycle, focusing on components of subsurface movement of water. [4] (2078 Chaitra, Q1a)
  2. Explain the origination of groundwater and how hydrological components affect groundwater. [6] (2072 Magh, Q1b)

The hydrological cycle (or water cycle) is the continuous circulation of water within the earth–atmosphere system. Groundwater is an integral component of this cycle, recharged by infiltration and discharged through springs, rivers, and evapotranspiration.

Figure 1.1: The Hydrological Cycle — showing the role of groundwater in the continuous circulation of water through the earth–atmosphere system.

Components of the Hydrological Cycle Relevant to Groundwater

  1. Precipitation: Rain, snow, sleet, or hail falling on the earth's surface. This is the primary source of groundwater recharge.
  2. Infiltration: The process by which water on the ground surface enters the soil. The rate of infiltration depends on soil type, antecedent moisture content, slope, vegetation cover, and rainfall intensity.
  3. Percolation: The downward movement of infiltrated water through the unsaturated (vadose) zone under gravity until it reaches the water table. This is the mechanism by which aquifers are recharged.
  4. Groundwater Flow: Once water reaches the saturated zone, it moves laterally through the aquifer from areas of higher hydraulic head to areas of lower hydraulic head, governed by Darcy's law.
  5. Capillary Rise: Water moves upward from the water table into the capillary fringe due to surface tension forces in the soil pores.
  6. Discharge: Groundwater is discharged naturally through springs, seepage into rivers, lakes, and wetlands (base flow), and through evapotranspiration where the water table is shallow enough.
  7. Evapotranspiration: The combined loss of water through evaporation from the soil and transpiration from plants. Deep-rooted vegetation can directly extract groundwater.

Origin and Age of Groundwater

Based on origin, groundwater is classified into three types:

  1. Meteoric Water: Water derived from precipitation (rain and snowmelt) that infiltrates the soil and percolates down to the water table. This is the most common origin and constitutes the majority of exploitable groundwater.
  2. Connate Water: Water trapped in the pore spaces of sedimentary rocks at the time of their deposition (millions of years ago). This "fossil water" is often highly mineralized and may be saline.
  3. Juvenile (Magmatic) Water: Water that originates from deep within the earth's interior, released during volcanic activity or crystallization of magma. It has never been part of the surface water cycle before.

The age of groundwater varies enormously — from days or weeks in shallow unconfined aquifers receiving direct rainfall recharge, to tens of thousands of years for deeply confined aquifers containing connate water. Radioisotope dating methods (e.g., Carbon-14, Tritium) are used to estimate groundwater age.

1.3 SUBSURFACE ZONES OF WATER

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  2. With the help of a neat sketch, briefly discuss various subsurface zones. [4] (2073 Bhadra, Q1b)

The subsurface can be divided into two principal zones based on the degree of saturation of pore spaces. These zones are further subdivided as follows:

Figure 1.2: Distribution of water in the subsurface — showing the Zone of Aeration (vadose) and Zone of Saturation (phreatic) with their sub-divisions.

A. Zone of Aeration (Vadose Zone / Unsaturated Zone)

This is the zone between the ground surface and the water table. Pore spaces contain both air and water. It is subdivided into three sub-zones:

  1. Soil Water Zone (Belt of Soil Moisture): The uppermost sub-zone, extending from the ground surface to the depth of plant roots (typically 1–2 m). Water here is held by adhesion to soil particles and capillary forces.
  2. Intermediate Vadose Zone: Extends from the bottom of the soil water zone to the top of the capillary fringe. Gravitational water percolates downward through this zone. The thickness can range from zero (where the water table is shallow) to hundreds of metres in arid regions.
  3. Capillary Fringe: Located immediately above the water table. Water rises into this zone from the water table by capillary action through fine pores. The pores are nearly or fully saturated, but the water is held at less-than-atmospheric pressure (negative pore water pressure). Its thickness depends on pore size — fine-grained soils (clays) have thick capillary fringes (up to several metres), while coarse sands have thin ones (a few centimetres).

B. Zone of Saturation (Phreatic Zone)

Below the water table, all voids in the soil and rock are completely filled with water under hydrostatic pressure (pressure ≥ atmospheric). This is the zone from which wells draw water and where true groundwater exists.

1.4 SPRINGS AND THEIR TYPES

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Define springs, list out the types of springs, and explain each briefly. [6] (2072 Magh, Q1a)
  2. List the types of springs. [2] (2078 Chaitra, Q1b)
DEFINITION: A spring is a natural point of discharge where groundwater emerges at the earth's surface due to the intersection of the water table or a confined aquifer with the ground surface, aided by geological and topographic conditions.

Classification of Springs

A. Based on the Cause of Emergence

  1. Gravity Springs (Depression Springs): Occur where the land surface dips below the water table, such as in a valley or depression.
  2. Artesian Springs (Pressure Springs): Occur when the piezometric surface of a confined aquifer rises above the ground surface at a particular location.
  3. Contact Springs: Form at the junction between a permeable formation overlying an impervious formation.
  4. Fault Springs: Occur along geological faults where an impervious formation is juxtaposed against a permeable water-bearing formation.

C. Based on Temperature

  • Cold Springs: Water temperature is at or below the mean annual air temperature of the area.
  • Thermal (Hot) Springs: Water temperature significantly exceeds the mean annual air temperature, usually due to deep circulation or proximity to geothermal heat sources.

D. Based on Variability

  • Perennial Springs: Flow throughout the year, typically fed by large or deep aquifers.
  • Intermittent Springs: Flow only during the wet season or when the water table is sufficiently high.

1.5 BASIC DEFINITIONS AND TYPES OF AQUIFER

Water-Bearing Formations — Basic Terminology

TermDefinitionExample
AquiferA geological formation that is sufficiently permeable and porous to store and transmit significant quantities of water under ordinary field conditions.Sand, gravel, sandstone, fractured limestone
AquitardA geological formation that is less permeable than an aquifer. It can store water but transmits it only slowly (allows leakage).Sandy clay, silt, loess
AquicludeA formation that can store water but does not transmit it in significant quantities. Essentially impermeable for practical purposes.Clay, shale
AquifugeA formation that can neither store nor transmit water. Absolutely impermeable and non-porous.Massive unfractured granite, quartzite

Types of Aquifer

Figure 1.4: Types of aquifers — unconfined, confined, leaky (semi-confined), and perched — showing their geological relationships, wells, and the piezometric surface.

1. Unconfined Aquifer (Water-Table Aquifer / Phreatic Aquifer)

An unconfined aquifer has a free water table as its upper boundary. The water table rises and falls freely with recharge and discharge. Water is at atmospheric pressure at the water table. When a well is drilled into an unconfined aquifer, the water level corresponds to the water table.

2. Confined Aquifer (Artesian Aquifer / Pressure Aquifer)

A confined aquifer is bounded both above and below by impervious or nearly impervious layers. Water is under pressure greater than atmospheric, characterized by a piezometric surface that lies above the top of the aquifer. If the piezometric surface lies above the ground surface, a flowing artesian well results.

3. Leaky Aquifer (Semi-Confined Aquifer)

A leaky aquifer is bounded above or below by aquitards that allow slow vertical leakage. When pumped, the pressure difference induces water to leak through the aquitard. This is the most common type of aquifer in nature.

4. Perched Aquifer

A perched aquifer forms when infiltrating water is held up by a small, localized impervious lens within the vadose zone, creating a local saturated body above the main water table. They are typically small and seasonal.

1.6 PROPERTIES OF SOIL/ROCK AFFECTING GROUNDWATER FLOW

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

1. Porosity (\(n\))

DEFINITION: Porosity is the ratio of the volume of voids (pore spaces) in a soil or rock sample to the total volume of the sample.
$$ n = \frac{V_v}{V_T} \times 100\% $$

Effective Porosity (\(n_e\)): Only interconnected pores allow fluid flow. Clay may have very high total porosity (40–70%) but extremely low effective porosity due to its tiny, poorly connected pores.

2. Specific Yield (\(S_y\))

DEFINITION: Specific yield is the volume of water that an unconfined aquifer releases from storage per unit surface area per unit decline in the water table.
$$ S_y = \frac{\text{Volume of water drained by gravity}}{\text{Total volume of aquifer}} $$

Specific Retention (\(S_r\)): The water retained in the pores against gravity. The relationship is:

$$ n = S_y + S_r $$

Typical values: gravel = 15–30%, coarse sand = 15–25%, fine sand = 10–15%, clay = 1–5%.

3. Specific Storage (\(S_s\))

DEFINITION: Specific storage is the volume of water that a unit volume of a confined aquifer releases from storage per unit change in hydraulic head.
$$ S_s = \rho g (\alpha + n \beta) $$

where \(\rho\) = density of water, \(g\) = gravitational acceleration, \(\alpha\) = compressibility of aquifer skeleton, \(n\) = porosity, \(\beta\) = compressibility of water.

4. Storage Coefficient / Storativity (\(S\))

For a confined aquifer of thickness \(b\): \(S = S_s \cdot b\). Typical values: 10−5 to 10−3.

For an unconfined aquifer: \(S \approx S_y\). Typical values: 0.1 to 0.3.

5. Transmissivity (\(T\))

DEFINITION: Transmissivity is the rate at which water is transmitted through a unit width of the entire saturated thickness of the aquifer under a unit hydraulic gradient.
$$ T = K \cdot b $$

6. Aquifer Heterogeneity and Anisotropy

  • Homogeneous: K is the same at every point.
  • Heterogeneous: K varies from point to point.
  • Isotropic: K is the same in all directions at a given point.
  • Anisotropic: K varies with direction. In sedimentary deposits, \(K_h \gg K_v\).

1.8 GROUNDWATER BASINS

A groundwater basin is a subsurface body of permeable rock or sediment containing one or more connected aquifers that share a common source of recharge and discharge. It includes:

  • A recharge area (where precipitation infiltrates to the water table).
  • A storage body consisting of one or more aquifers.
  • A discharge area where groundwater exits (springs, seepage into rivers, wells).
  • Major groundwater basins in Nepal: Kathmandu Valley Basin (lacustrine sediments up to 500 m thick), Inner Terai Basins (Dang, Chitwan, Surkhet), and the Terai Plain (thick alluvial deposits).
