4.1 INTRODUCTION TO WELLS AND WELL TERMINOLOGY

A well is a hydraulic structure that penetrates an aquifer and allows extraction (or injection) of groundwater. Wells are the most common means of groundwater exploitation worldwide. Understanding the hydraulics of flow toward a pumping well is central to groundwater engineering.

Types of Wells

  • Fully penetrating well: A well whose screen extends through the entire thickness of the aquifer, so that water enters along the full saturated depth.
  • Partially penetrating well: A well that taps only a portion of the aquifer thickness, causing three-dimensional flow near the well.
  • Artesian (flowing) well: A well drilled into a confined aquifer where the piezometric surface is above the ground level, causing water to flow naturally without pumping.
  • Observation well (piezometer): A non-pumping well used to measure the water level or piezometric head at a given location.
Figure 4.1: Types of wells — confined (fully penetrating) and unconfined well with water levels.

Key Terminology

Term Definition
Static water level (SWL) The water level in a well before pumping begins; represents the natural piezometric surface or water table.
Pumping water level (PWL) The water level in the well during pumping; always lower than SWL.
Drawdown (\(s\) or \(s_w\)) The difference between SWL and PWL: \(s = \text{SWL} - \text{PWL}\). Represents the depression of the water surface caused by pumping.
Well radius (\(r_w\)) The radius of the well casing/screen.
Radius of influence (\(R\)) The radial distance from the well beyond which the drawdown is negligible; the boundary of the cone of depression.
Well discharge / yield (\(Q\)) The volume of water pumped per unit time (m³/s or L/s).
Specific capacity The discharge per unit drawdown: \(Q/s_w\). Indicates well efficiency.

4.2 CONE OF DEPRESSION, RADIUS OF INFLUENCE, AND AREA OF INFLUENCE

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Define cone of depression, radius of influence, well yield, and specific capacity of a well. Write down the assumptions used for the governing equation. [6] (2078 Chaitra, Q4a)
  2. Define cone of depression, radius of influence, and area of influence with a neat sketch in well hydraulics. [6] (2072 Magh, Q4)
DEFINITION: When a well is pumped, the water level drops at and around the well, forming a funnel-shaped depression in the piezometric surface (confined) or water table (unconfined). This depression is called the cone of depression (or drawdown cone).
Figure 4.2: Cone of depression around a pumping well in a confined aquifer showing drawdown sw and radius of influence R.

Cone of Depression

When pumping starts, water is first extracted from the immediate vicinity of the well. As pumping continues, the drawdown propagates outward, forming a three-dimensional conical surface. Key features:

  • The drawdown is maximum at the well face and decreases with radial distance.
  • The cone expands with time until a steady state is reached (when recharge equals discharge).
  • The steepness of the cone depends on the aquifer transmissivity: low-T aquifers produce steep, narrow cones; high-T aquifers produce flat, wide cones.
  • In unconfined aquifers, the cone represents the actual lowering of the water table, reducing the saturated thickness near the well.

Radius of Influence (R)

The radius of influence is the maximum distance from the pumping well at which measurable drawdown occurs. Beyond this distance, the aquifer is essentially undisturbed. It depends on:

  • Pumping rate (\(Q\))
  • Aquifer transmissivity (\(T\))
  • Storage coefficient (\(S\))
  • Duration of pumping (\(t\))

Empirical estimates include:

$$ R = 3000 \cdot s_w \cdot \sqrt{K} \quad \text{(Sichardt formula, R in m, K in m/s)} $$

Area of Influence

The area of influence is the circular area (in plan view) within which the drawdown due to pumping is measurable. It equals \(\pi R^2\), where \(R\) is the radius of influence.

4.3 WELL YIELD AND SPECIFIC CAPACITY

Well Yield

The well yield (or discharge, \(Q\)) is the volume of water that can be pumped from a well per unit time under specified conditions. It is typically expressed in liters per second (L/s), cubic meters per day (m³/day), or gallons per minute (gpm).

Specific Capacity

The specific capacity of a well is defined as the discharge per unit drawdown:

$$ \text{Specific capacity} = \frac{Q}{s_w} \quad \text{(m}^3\text{/s per m, or L/s per m)} $$

Significance of specific capacity:

  • A higher specific capacity indicates a more productive well (or higher transmissivity aquifer).
  • It provides a quick field estimate of aquifer transmissivity: \(T \approx 1.22 \times (Q/s_w)\) (empirical, for confined aquifers).
  • Specific capacity decreases with increasing drawdown in unconfined aquifers because the saturated thickness diminishes.
  • It is also affected by well losses (well efficiency), partial penetration, and turbulent flow near the well.

EXAM TIP

The specific capacity \(Q/s_w\) is NOT constant in unconfined aquifers because as drawdown increases, the effective saturated thickness decreases, reducing the available transmissivity. In confined aquifers (where \(b\) stays constant), the specific capacity remains approximately constant for small drawdowns.

4.4 ASSUMPTIONS FOR STEADY-STATE WELL FLOW

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Write down the assumptions used for the governing equation. [Part of Q4a] (2078 Chaitra)

The derivation of steady-state radial flow equations (Thiem / Dupuit) relies on the following assumptions:

  1. The aquifer is homogeneous and isotropic with uniform hydraulic conductivity \(K\).
  2. The aquifer has infinite areal extent (no boundary effects).
  3. The well fully penetrates the aquifer and receives water from the entire aquifer thickness.
  4. Flow toward the well is radially symmetric (horizontal, two-dimensional in plan).
  5. The aquifer is bounded below by an impermeable (confining) layer.
  6. Steady-state conditions prevail — the drawdown does not change with time, meaning recharge equals discharge.
  7. Flow obeys Darcy's law (laminar flow conditions).
  8. The well diameter is infinitesimally small (or well storage is negligible).
  9. For unconfined aquifers: the Dupuit assumptions apply — flow is horizontal and the hydraulic gradient equals the slope of the water table.

4.5 DERIVATION: STEADY RADIAL FLOW TO A WELL IN A CONFINED AQUIFER (THIEM EQUATION)

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Derive an expression for steady radial flow into a well in a confined aquifer with a neat sketch. [7] (2073 Bhadra, Q4a)
  2. A well penetrates an artesian aquifer. Determine the maximum yield for a given drawdown and the percentage change in discharge if the well diameter is reduced. [6] (2078 Chaitra, Q4b)
Figure 4.4: Steady radial flow to a fully penetrating well in a confined aquifer — definition sketch for Thiem equation derivation.

Derivation

Consider a fully penetrating well of radius \(r_w\) pumping at a constant rate \(Q\) from a confined aquifer of thickness \(b\) and hydraulic conductivity \(K\). At steady state, the flow toward the well is purely radial and horizontal.

At any radial distance \(r\) from the well, consider a cylindrical surface of height \(b\) and circumference \(2\pi r\). By continuity, all the water flowing through this surface must equal the pumping rate:

$$ Q = A \cdot v = (2\pi r \cdot b) \cdot v $$

By Darcy's law, the velocity at distance \(r\) is:

$$ v = K \frac{dh}{dr} $$

Substituting:

$$ Q = 2\pi r b K \frac{dh}{dr} $$

Separating variables:

$$ \frac{Q}{2\pi K b} \frac{dr}{r} = dh $$

Integrating between the well face (\(r = r_w\), \(h = h_w\)) and the radius of influence (\(r = R\), \(h = H\)):

$$ \frac{Q}{2\pi K b} \int_{r_w}^{R} \frac{dr}{r} = \int_{h_w}^{H} dh $$
$$ \frac{Q}{2\pi K b} \ln\left(\frac{R}{r_w}\right) = H - h_w $$

Since the drawdown at the well face \(s_w = H - h_w\) and the transmissivity \(T = Kb\):

$$ \boxed{Q = \frac{2\pi T s_w}{\ln(R/r_w)} = \frac{2\pi K b \, s_w}{\ln(R/r_w)}} $$

This is the Thiem equation for steady radial flow in a confined aquifer.

General form between two observation wells

If head measurements \(h_1\) and \(h_2\) are available at distances \(r_1\) and \(r_2\) from the pumping well:

$$ Q = \frac{2\pi T (h_2 - h_1)}{\ln(r_2/r_1)} $$

THIEM EQUATION — CONFINED

\(Q = \dfrac{2\pi K b \, s_w}{\ln(R/r_w)}\)

or equivalently: \(Q = \dfrac{2\pi T \, s_w}{\ln(R/r_w)}\)

KEY INSIGHT

Discharge \(Q\) depends on \(\ln(R/r_w)\), not on \(R/r_w\) directly. Since the logarithm varies slowly, doubling the well diameter changes \(Q\) by only a few percent.

Symbol Meaning Typical Units
\(Q\) Well discharge (pumping rate) m³/s or m³/day
\(K\) Hydraulic conductivity m/s or m/day
\(b\) Confined aquifer thickness m
\(T\) Transmissivity = \(Kb\) m²/s or m²/day
\(s_w\) Drawdown at the well face m
\(R\) Radius of influence m
\(r_w\) Well radius m

4.6 DERIVATION: STEADY RADIAL FLOW TO A WELL IN AN UNCONFINED AQUIFER (DUPUIT EQUATION)

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Derive the equation for steady radial flow in an unconfined aquifer. [8] (2072 Magh, Q5b)
  2. A fully penetrating well in an unconfined aquifer is pumped under steady conditions. Compute the steady-state discharge. [4] (2073 Bhadra, Q4b)
Figure 4.5: Steady radial flow to a fully penetrating well in an unconfined aquifer — definition sketch for Dupuit equation derivation.

Derivation

Consider a fully penetrating well of radius \(r_w\) pumping at a constant rate \(Q\) from an unconfined aquifer resting on an impervious base. The original (pre-pumping) saturated thickness is \(H\). Under the Dupuit assumptions, flow is horizontal and the hydraulic gradient equals the slope of the free surface.

At radial distance \(r\), the saturated thickness is \(h\) (which varies with \(r\)). The area of the cylindrical surface through which flow passes is \(A = 2\pi r \cdot h\). By continuity and Darcy's law:

$$ Q = 2\pi r \cdot h \cdot K \frac{dh}{dr} $$

Separating variables:

$$ \frac{Q}{2\pi K} \frac{dr}{r} = h \, dh $$

Integrating between the well face (\(r = r_w\), \(h = h_w\)) and the radius of influence (\(r = R\), \(h = H\)):

$$ \frac{Q}{2\pi K} \int_{r_w}^{R} \frac{dr}{r} = \int_{h_w}^{H} h \, dh $$
$$ \frac{Q}{2\pi K} \ln\left(\frac{R}{r_w}\right) = \frac{H^2 - h_w^2}{2} $$

Solving for \(Q\):

$$ \boxed{Q = \frac{\pi K (H^2 - h_w^2)}{\ln(R/r_w)}} $$

This is the Dupuit equation for steady radial flow in an unconfined aquifer.

General form between two observation wells

If heads \(h_1\) and \(h_2\) are measured at distances \(r_1\) and \(r_2\):

$$ Q = \frac{\pi K (h_2^2 - h_1^2)}{\ln(r_2/r_1)} $$

DUPUIT EQUATION — UNCONFINED

\(Q = \dfrac{\pi K (H^2 - h_w^2)}{\ln(R/r_w)}\)

DUPUIT VALIDITY

The Dupuit equation is most accurate when the drawdown is small relative to the saturated thickness: \(s_w / H < 0.5\). For large drawdowns, vertical flow components become significant and the equation overestimates discharge.

Comparison: Confined vs. Unconfined Well Equations

Feature Confined (Thiem) Unconfined (Dupuit)
Aquifer thickness Constant (\(b\)) Variable (\(h\), decreases near well)
Flow cross-section area \(2\pi r b\) \(2\pi r h\)
Equation form \(Q = \dfrac{2\pi Kb \, s_w}{\ln(R/r_w)}\) \(Q = \dfrac{\pi K(H^2 - h_w^2)}{\ln(R/r_w)}\)
Head term Linear in \(h\) (or \(s_w\)) Difference of squares (\(H^2 - h_w^2\))
Specific capacity Approximately constant Decreases with increasing drawdown

4.7 UNSTEADY (TRANSIENT) RADIAL FLOW — THEIS EQUATION (OVERVIEW)

In reality, when a well starts pumping, the cone of depression expands with time and steady state may not be achieved quickly (especially in confined aquifers). The Theis (1935) solution addresses this transient behavior.

Governing Equation

The radial form of the groundwater flow equation in a confined aquifer is:

$$ \frac{\partial^2 h}{\partial r^2} + \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial h}{\partial r} = \frac{S}{T}\frac{\partial h}{\partial t} $$

Theis Solution

Theis expressed the drawdown at distance \(r\) and time \(t\) as:

$$ s = \frac{Q}{4\pi T} W(u) $$

where the well function \(W(u)\) is:

$$ W(u) = \int_u^{\infty} \frac{e^{-\eta}}{\eta} d\eta = -0.5772 - \ln u + u - \frac{u^2}{2 \cdot 2!} + \frac{u^3}{3 \cdot 3!} - \cdots $$

and the dimensionless parameter \(u\) is:

$$ u = \frac{r^2 S}{4Tt} $$
Symbol Meaning
\(s\) Drawdown at distance \(r\) and time \(t\)
\(Q\) Constant pumping rate
\(T\) Transmissivity of the aquifer
\(S\) Storage coefficient (storativity)
\(W(u)\) Theis well function
\(u\) Dimensionless time parameter

Cooper–Jacob Approximation

For large time or small distance (i.e., \(u < 0.01\)), the well function simplifies:

$$ s \approx \frac{Q}{4\pi T} \left[ -0.5772 - \ln\left(\frac{r^2 S}{4Tt}\right) \right] = \frac{2.303\, Q}{4\pi T} \log_{10}\left(\frac{2.25\, Tt}{r^2 S}\right) $$

This is the basis for the Cooper–Jacob straight-line method used in pumping test analysis (covered in detail in Chapter 5).

EXAM NOTE

The Theis equation and Cooper–Jacob method are more commonly examined in Chapter 5 (Pumping Tests). In Chapter 4, the focus is primarily on steady-state equations (Thiem for confined, Dupuit for unconfined).
