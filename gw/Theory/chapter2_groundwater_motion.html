2.1 CONTINUUM APPROACH AND REPRESENTATIVE ELEMENTARY VOLUME

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Define REV and continuum approach. Explain the importance, validity, and limitations of Darcy's law. [6] (2078 Chaitra, Q2a)
  2. Explain the continuum and REV approach. Define permeability and transmissibility. [4+2] (2073 Bhadra, Q2a)
  3. What do you understand by REV? Explain. [4] (2071 Bhadra, Q2)
CORE IDEA: Groundwater actually moves through an intricate network of pores and fractures, but engineering analysis becomes possible only when the porous medium is treated as a continuum whose averaged properties represent the microscopic void system.

At pore scale, water flow is highly irregular. The continuum approach replaces the actual porous mass with an equivalent continuous material having averaged properties such as porosity, hydraulic conductivity, and storage. The averaging is performed over a Representative Elementary Volume (REV) — large compared with individual pores but still small compared with the total aquifer.

Figure 2.1: Groundwater modeling: from pore-scale complexity through REV-based averaging to a continuum description with macroscopic properties.
Figure 2.2: REV threshold — as the averaging volume increases, the measured property stops fluctuating and stabilizes at a meaningful macroscopic value.

Why the Continuum Approach Is Important

  1. It permits the use of differential equations for groundwater flow.
  2. It allows the definition of field-scale properties such as K and storativity.
  3. It links lab and pumping-test results to aquifer-scale predictions.
  4. It justifies Darcy's law as a macroscopic law.

Limitations of the REV/Continuum Assumption

  • Weak in highly fractured rock with discrete fractures.
  • May fail near cavities, karst conduits, or very coarse gravel.
  • Scale-dependent: an acceptable REV in one formation may not work in another.
  • Does not eliminate heterogeneity; it only smooths material to define local averages.

2.2 DARCY'S EXPERIMENT AND ONE-DIMENSIONAL GROUNDWATER FLOW

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. What is Darcy's law? Derive the 1D form and explain its validity. [6] (2072 Magh, Q2)
  2. Derive the 3D form of groundwater flow from Darcy's law. [6] (2072 Ashwin, Q2)

In 1856, Henry Darcy performed controlled sand-column experiments and found: discharge through a porous medium is proportional to the cross-sectional area and head loss, and inversely proportional to the flow length.

Figure 2.3: Darcy's 1856 sand-column experiment — piezometric heads h₁ and h₂ at two points along the flow path, discharge Q measured at outflow.
DARCY'S LAW: The discharge through a saturated porous medium is proportional to the hydraulic gradient and the cross-sectional area normal to flow.
$$ Q = K A \frac{h_1 - h_2}{L} = K A i $$

In sign-conscious form:

$$ q = \frac{Q}{A} = -K \frac{dh}{dl} $$

where q = Darcy flux (specific discharge), K = hydraulic conductivity, dh/dl = hydraulic gradient. The negative sign indicates head decreases in the flow direction.

DARCY FLUX

Discharge per total cross-sectional area. Does not represent real velocity in pores.

$$ q = \frac{Q}{A} $$

SEEPAGE VELOCITY

Actual average velocity through pore spaces; divide by effective porosity.

$$ v_s = \frac{q}{n_e} = \frac{K i}{n_e} $$

2.3 THREE-DIMENSIONAL DARCY LAW AND HEAD GRADIENTS

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Derive the 3D form of groundwater flow from Darcy's law. [6] (2072 Ashwin, Q2)
  2. What is Darcy's law? Derive the 1D form. [6] (2072 Magh, Q2)
$$ q_x = -K_x \frac{\partial h}{\partial x}, \qquad q_y = -K_y \frac{\partial h}{\partial y}, \qquad q_z = -K_z \frac{\partial h}{\partial z} $$

In isotropic media (\(K_x = K_y = K_z = K\)):

$$ \vec{q} = -K \nabla h $$

2.4 VALIDITY OF DARCY'S LAW AND NON-DARCIAN FLOW

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Explain validity and limitations of Darcy's law. [Part of 6] (2078 Chaitra, Q2a)
  2. Determine the valid domain around a pumping well for which Darcy's law applies. [6] (2078 Chaitra, Q2b)
  3. Determine the domain for Darcy's law applicability around a pumping well. [8] (2073 Bhadra, Q2b)
  4. State whether Darcy's law is applicable and determine pore velocity. [8] (2071 Bhadra, Q6)

Darcy's law is valid when pore flow is essentially laminar. This is checked by a Reynolds number:

$$ Re = \frac{q \, d_m}{\nu} \qquad \text{or} \qquad Re = \frac{v_s \, d_m}{\nu} $$

where \(d_m\) = average grain diameter and \(\nu\) = kinematic viscosity. Darcy's law is valid when \(Re \leq Re_{cr}\) (typically 1–10 depending on convention).

Figure 2.6: Darcy's law gives a linear relationship between q and i. At high gradients (near wells), inertial effects cause nonlinear deviation.
Figure 2.7: Radial flow into a pumping well — velocity is highest near the well. Darcy's law is valid only for r ≥ rmin where Re ≤ Recr.

STANDARD EXAM PROCEDURE FOR WELL-DOMAIN PROBLEMS

  1. Write radial Darcy flux: \( q_r = \frac{Q}{2\pi r b} \)
  2. Define Reynolds number: \( Re = \frac{q_r d_m}{\nu} = \frac{Q d_m}{2\pi r b \nu} \)
  3. Set \( Re = Re_{cr} \) to find boundary of validity
  4. Solve: \( r_{min} = \frac{Q d_m}{2\pi b \nu Re_{cr}} \). Darcy flow valid for \( r \geq r_{min} \).

2.5 HYDRAULIC CONDUCTIVITY, PERMEABILITY, AND TRANSMISSIVITY

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Define permeability and transmissibility. [2] (2073 Bhadra, Q2a)
  2. Define specific storage, specific yield, transmissibility, and anisotropy. [4] (2072 Ashwin, Q1)
KEY DISTINCTION: Permeability (k) is a property of the medium alone; hydraulic conductivity (K) depends on both medium and fluid.
$$ K = \frac{k \rho g}{\mu} = \frac{k g}{\nu} \qquad \text{and} \qquad T = K b $$
Figure 2.8: Typical range of hydraulic conductivity K for common geological materials (log scale).
Figure 2.9: Relationship chain — intrinsic permeability (medium only) → hydraulic conductivity (+ fluid) → transmissivity (× aquifer thickness).

2.6 AQUIFER HETEROGENEITY AND ANISOTROPY

Homogeneous: Same K everywhere. Heterogeneous: K varies spatially. Isotropic: K same in all directions at one point. Anisotropic: K depends on direction.

2.8 WORKED NUMERICAL SOLUTIONS

SOLUTION 1: 2078 Chaitra Q2(b) — Darcy Validity Domain

Given: \(Q = 0.3\) m³/s, \(b = 18\) m, \(d_m = 1\) mm = \(0.001\) m, \(Re_{cr} = 8\), \(\nu = 1\) centistoke = \(1 \times 10^{-6}\) m²/s.

Required: Minimum radius \(r_{min}\) from the well beyond which Darcy's law is valid.

Step 1: For a fully penetrating well in a confined aquifer, the Darcy flux at radial distance \(r\) is:

$$ q_r = \frac{Q}{2\pi r b} $$

Step 2: Reynolds number defined using Darcy flux:

$$ Re = \frac{q_r \cdot d_m}{\nu} = \frac{Q \cdot d_m}{2\pi \cdot r \cdot b \cdot \nu} $$

Step 3: At the boundary of Darcy validity, \(Re = Re_{cr}\). Solve for \(r_{min}\):

$$ r_{min} = \frac{Q \cdot d_m}{2\pi \cdot b \cdot \nu \cdot Re_{cr}} $$

Step 4: Substitute values:

$$ r_{min} = \frac{0.3 \times 0.001}{2\pi \times 18 \times 1 \times 10^{-6} \times 8} $$
$$ r_{min} = \frac{3 \times 10^{-4}}{2\pi \times 144 \times 10^{-6}} = \frac{3 \times 10^{-4}}{9.0478 \times 10^{-4}} $$
$$ \boxed{r_{min} = 0.332 \text{ m} \approx 0.33 \text{ m}} $$

Conclusion: Darcy's law is valid for \(r \geq 0.33\) m from the well centre. Within 0.33 m, flow velocities are too high and non-Darcian effects dominate.

SOLUTION 2: 2073 Bhadra Q2(b) — Darcy Validity Domain

Given: \(Q = 1000\) m³/hr, \(b = 28\) m, \(d_m = 1\) mm = \(0.001\) m, \(Re_{cr} = 6\), \(\nu = 1\) centistoke = \(1 \times 10^{-6}\) m²/s.

Step 1: Convert units: \(Q = \frac{1000}{3600} = 0.2778\) m³/s.

Step 2: Apply the formula:

$$ r_{min} = \frac{Q \cdot d_m}{2\pi \cdot b \cdot \nu \cdot Re_{cr}} = \frac{0.2778 \times 0.001}{2\pi \times 28 \times 10^{-6} \times 6} $$
$$ r_{min} = \frac{2.778 \times 10^{-4}}{2\pi \times 168 \times 10^{-6}} = \frac{2.778 \times 10^{-4}}{1.0556 \times 10^{-3}} $$
$$ \boxed{r_{min} = 0.263 \text{ m} \approx 0.26 \text{ m}} $$

Conclusion: Darcy's law is applicable for \(r \geq 0.26\) m from the pumping well.

SOLUTION 3: 2071 Bhadra Q6 — Darcy Applicability and Pore Velocity

Given: \(b = 25\) m, \(n_e = 0.20\), \(K = 15\) m/day, \(L = 1100\) m, \(h_1 = 5.5\) m, \(h_2 = 3.0\) m, \(d_m = 1\) mm = \(0.001\) m, \(\nu = 0.01\) cm²/s = \(1 \times 10^{-6}\) m²/s.

Step 1: Hydraulic gradient

$$ i = \frac{h_1 - h_2}{L} = \frac{5.5 - 3.0}{1100} = \frac{2.5}{1100} = 2.273 \times 10^{-3} $$

Step 2: Darcy flux (specific discharge)

$$ q = K \cdot i = 15 \times 2.273 \times 10^{-3} = 0.0341 \text{ m/day} $$

Convert to m/s: \(q = \frac{0.0341}{86400} = 3.945 \times 10^{-7}\) m/s.

Step 3: Check Darcy validity (Reynolds number)

$$ Re = \frac{q \cdot d_m}{\nu} = \frac{3.945 \times 10^{-7} \times 0.001}{1 \times 10^{-6}} = \frac{3.945 \times 10^{-10}}{10^{-6}} $$
$$ \boxed{Re = 3.95 \times 10^{-4} \ll 1} $$

Conclusion: Since \(Re \approx 0.0004\), which is far below any commonly accepted limit (1–10), Darcy's law is fully applicable.

Step 4: Average pore velocity (seepage velocity)

$$ v_s = \frac{q}{n_e} = \frac{0.0341}{0.20} $$
$$ \boxed{v_s = 0.170 \text{ m/day} = 1.973 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m/s}} $$

The actual groundwater velocity in the pores is about 5 times the Darcy flux, and the flow is deeply in the laminar regime.
