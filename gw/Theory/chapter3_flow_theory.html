3.1 STREAM LINES, EQUIPOTENTIAL LINES, AND FLOW NETS

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Define flow net with a neat sketch. State applications of flow net in geotechnical engineering and derive the expression for seepage discharge from a flow net. [3+3+4] (2078 Chaitra, Q3a)
  2. Derive an expression for seepage discharge from a flow net for an isotropic aquifer. [6] (2071 Bhadra, Q3)
DEFINITION: A flow net is a graphical representation of two-dimensional groundwater flow consisting of two families of curves — stream lines (flow lines) and equipotential lines — that intersect each other at right angles in isotropic media.

Key Terminology

  • Stream line (flow line): A line that is everywhere tangent to the velocity vector. Water particles move along stream lines. No flow crosses a stream line.
  • Equipotential line: A line connecting points of equal hydraulic head. If a piezometer is installed at any point along an equipotential line, the water would rise to the same elevation.
  • Flow channel: The zone between two adjacent stream lines through which a constant fraction of the total flow passes.
  • Potential drop: The difference in head between two successive equipotential lines.
Figure 3.1: A flow net beneath a dam showing stream lines (solid) and equipotential lines (dashed) intersecting at right angles.

Properties of a Flow Net

  1. Stream lines and equipotential lines are orthogonal (perpendicular) in isotropic media.
  2. The head drop between successive equipotential lines is constant: \(\Delta h = H / N_d\).
  3. The discharge through each flow channel is the same: \(\Delta q = K \Delta h \cdot b/a\).
  4. For a square flow net (\(a = b\)), the cell dimensions are equal, simplifying computation.
  5. Neither stream lines nor equipotential lines can intersect themselves.
  6. Impervious boundaries are stream lines; constant-head boundaries are equipotential lines.

Applications of Flow Nets in Engineering

  1. Determination of seepage discharge beneath dams, sheet piles, and cofferdams.
  2. Estimation of uplift pressure on the base of hydraulic structures.
  3. Assessment of exit gradient at the downstream toe to check piping safety.
  4. Computation of pore-water pressure distribution for slope stability analysis.
  5. Analysis of dewatering and well-field problems.

3.2 SEEPAGE DISCHARGE FROM A FLOW NET

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Derive the expression for seepage discharge from flow net. [Part of Q3a] (2078 Chaitra)
  2. Derive an expression for seepage discharge from a flow net for an isotropic aquifer. [6] (2071 Bhadra, Q3)
Figure 3.2: Derivation of seepage from a single curvilinear square of a flow net.

Consider a single cell of the flow net with length \(a\) along the flow direction and width \(b\) perpendicular to flow. By Darcy's law the discharge per unit thickness through this cell is:

$$ \Delta q = K \frac{\Delta h}{a} \cdot b \cdot 1 $$

For a square flow net, \(a = b\), so \(\Delta q = K \Delta h\). The total head loss is \(H\), divided into \(N_d\) drops giving \(\Delta h = H/N_d\). With \(N_f\) flow channels:

$$ \boxed{Q = K H \frac{N_f}{N_d}} $$
Figure 3.3: Flow net around a sheet pile showing stream lines (solid), equipotential lines (dashed), and the seepage formula.

ISOTROPIC CASE

\(Q = K H \frac{N_f}{N_d}\), where \(K\) is the uniform hydraulic conductivity.

ANISOTROPIC CASE

Transform domain: \(x' = x\sqrt{K_z/K_x}\). Then \(Q = H \frac{N_f}{N_d}\sqrt{K_x K_z}\).

SymbolMeaning
\(Q\)Total seepage discharge per unit width of structure (m³/s per m)
\(K\)Hydraulic conductivity (m/s or m/day)
\(H\)Total head loss = upstream head − downstream head
\(N_f\)Number of flow channels (spaces between stream lines)
\(N_d\)Number of equipotential drops

3.3 WATER TABLE CONTOUR MAPS AND DIRECTION OF FLOW

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. State uses of water-table contour. Prepare a contour map and show flow direction from four observation wells. [3+4] (2073 Bhadra, Q3a)
  2. Explain how water table contour maps are prepared, state their uses, and prepare a groundwater contour map. [8] (2072 Ashwin, Q4)
  3. Explain how a water table contour map is prepared. [4] (2072 Magh, Q5a)
DEFINITION: A water table contour map is a plan view showing lines of equal water-table elevation. It is the groundwater equivalent of a topographic contour map.

How a Water Table Contour Map Is Prepared

  1. Install observation wells (piezometers) at multiple locations across the study area.
  2. Measure static water level in each well at a common reference time.
  3. Convert water levels to elevation above a common datum (e.g., mean sea level).
  4. Plot the well locations on a scaled plan and annotate each with its water-table elevation.
  5. Interpolate contour lines of equal head between measurement points.
Figure 3.4: Water table contour map from four observation wells (51, 56, 66, 76 m) with 5 m contour interval, showing groundwater flow direction.

Uses of Water Table Contour Maps

  1. Determine the direction of groundwater flow at any point.
  2. Estimate the hydraulic gradient from the spacing of contour lines.
  3. Identify recharge and discharge zones.
  4. Assist in well-field planning.
  5. Evaluate potential path of contaminant plumes.
  6. Calculate seepage flow through any cross section using Darcy's law.
Figure 3.5: Three-well method for finding groundwater flow direction from a triangular network of piezometers.

3.4 DERIVATION OF LAPLACE EQUATION FOR STEADY GROUNDWATER FLOW

OBJECTIVE: The governing PDE for steady-state groundwater flow in a homogeneous isotropic aquifer is the Laplace equation, providing the theoretical foundation for flow-net analysis.

Under steady-state conditions, inflow equals outflow (no storage change). Using Darcy's law and conservation of mass:

$$ \frac{\partial q_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial q_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial q_z}{\partial z} = 0 $$

Substituting Darcy's law \(q_i = -K_i \frac{\partial h}{\partial x_i}\):

$$ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\left(K_x \frac{\partial h}{\partial x}\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\!\left(K_y \frac{\partial h}{\partial y}\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\!\left(K_z \frac{\partial h}{\partial z}\right) = 0 $$

For a homogeneous isotropic medium (\(K\) = constant):

$$ \boxed{\frac{\partial^2 h}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 h}{\partial y^2} + \frac{\partial^2 h}{\partial z^2} = 0 \qquad (\nabla^2 h = 0)} $$
Figure 3.6: Derivation pathway from conservation of mass and Darcy's law to the Laplace equation.

Anisotropic Media

When \(K_x \neq K_z\), a coordinate transformation \(x' = x\sqrt{K_z/K_x}\) converts the anisotropic medium into an equivalent isotropic one. The effective conductivity is \(K_e = \sqrt{K_x K_z}\).

3.5 STEADY ONE-DIMENSIONAL FLOW IN AN UNCONFINED AQUIFER (DUPUIT THEORY)

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Two rivers are separated by a homogeneous unconfined aquifer. Compute seepage flow per unit river length and the phreatic line. [7] (2073 Bhadra, Q3b)
DUPUIT ASSUMPTIONS: (1) Hydraulic gradient equals the slope of the water table. (2) Flow is essentially horizontal — all stream lines are horizontal and equipotential lines are vertical.
Figure 3.7: Steady unconfined flow between two water bodies on an impervious base (Dupuit parabola).

Applying Darcy's law with Dupuit assumption (\(q = -Kh\,dh/dx\)) and integrating:

$$ Q = \frac{K}{2L}(h_1^2 - h_2^2) \qquad \text{(Dupuit equation)} $$

The water-table profile (phreatic surface equation) is:

$$ h^2 = h_1^2 - \frac{h_1^2 - h_2^2}{L}\,x $$

3.6 UNCONFINED AQUIFER BETWEEN TWO WATER BODIES WITH RECHARGE

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Two irrigation canals with recharge — determine q1, q2, phreatic line, divide. [8] (2078 Chaitra, Q3b)
  2. Rain on a land strip between canals — q1, q2, divide, and no-rain case. [8] (2071 Bhadra, Q4)

When uniform recharge \(p\) is applied, the governing 1-D Dupuit equation becomes:

$$ \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(Kh\frac{dh}{dx}\right) + p = 0 $$
Figure 3.8: Unconfined aquifer between two canals with uniform recharge, showing water table divide and seepage components q1 and q2.

Phreatic Surface Equation

$$ h^2 = h_1^2 - \frac{h_1^2 - h_2^2}{L}\,x + \frac{p}{K}\,x(L - x) $$

Seepage to Each Canal (per unit length)

$$ q_1 = \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2L} + \frac{pL}{2} \qquad \text{(into the deeper canal, at } x=0\text{)} $$
$$ q_2 = \frac{pL}{2} - \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2L} \qquad \text{(into the shallower canal, at } x=L\text{)} $$

Verify: \(q_1 + q_2 = pL\) (total recharge on the strip).

Location of the Water Table Divide

$$ d = \frac{L}{2} - \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2pL} $$
Figure 3.9: Darcy flux varies linearly with distance. The zero-crossing locates the water table divide.

SPECIAL CASE: NO RECHARGE (p = 0)

Equations reduce to the simple Dupuit case:

$$ q = \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2L} $$

All flow moves toward the canal with lower head. No water divide exists.

3.7 STEADY FLOW IN A CONFINED AQUIFER

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Determine flow in the confined aquifer (variable thickness). [8] (2072 Ashwin, Q5)
  2. Determine flow in the confined aquifer (variable thickness). [8] (2072 Magh, Q7)

A. Constant Thickness

$$ Q = T \frac{h_1 - h_2}{L} = Kb \frac{h_1 - h_2}{L} $$
Figure 3.10: Steady one-dimensional flow in a confined aquifer of constant thickness b.

B. Variable Thickness (Wedge-Shaped Aquifer)

When aquifer thickness varies linearly from \(b_1\) at \(x = 0\) to \(b_2\) at \(x = L\), applying Darcy's law and integrating:

$$ Q = K \frac{(h_1 - h_2)(b_2 - b_1)}{L \ln(b_2/b_1)} $$
Figure 3.11: Steady flow in a confined aquifer of variable (wedge-shaped) thickness.

3.9 WORKED NUMERICAL SOLUTIONS

SOLUTION 1: 2078 Chaitra Q3(b) — Unconfined Aquifer with Recharge

Given: \(L = 1570\) m, \(p = 25\) mm/hr \(= \frac{25}{1000 \times 24} = 0.6\) m/day, \(h_1 = 12\) m, \(h_2 = 7\) m, \(K = 12\) m/day.

Required: \(q_1\), \(q_2\), phreatic surface equation, and water table divide location.

Step 1: Convert rainfall to m/day

$$ p = 25 \text{ mm/hr} = 25 \times \frac{24}{1000} = 0.6 \text{ m/day} $$

Step 2: Seepage into the deeper canal (left, at x = 0)

$$ q_1 = \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2L} + \frac{pL}{2} $$
$$ q_1 = \frac{12(144 - 49)}{2 \times 1570} + \frac{0.6 \times 1570}{2} = \frac{12 \times 95}{3140} + \frac{942}{2} $$
$$ \boxed{q_1 = 0.363 + 471.0 = 471.36 \text{ m}^2/\text{day per m of canal}} $$

Step 3: Seepage into the shallower canal (right, at x = L)

$$ q_2 = \frac{pL}{2} - \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2L} = 471.0 - 0.363 $$
$$ \boxed{q_2 = 470.64 \text{ m}^2/\text{day per m of canal}} $$

Check: \(q_1 + q_2 = 471.36 + 470.64 = 942.0 = pL = 0.6 \times 1570\). ✓

Step 4: Phreatic surface equation

$$ h^2 = h_1^2 - \frac{h_1^2 - h_2^2}{L}\,x + \frac{p}{K}\,x(L - x) $$
$$ h^2 = 144 - \frac{95}{1570}\,x + \frac{0.6}{12}\,x(1570 - x) $$
$$ \boxed{h^2 = 144 - 0.0605\,x + 0.05\,x(1570 - x)} $$

Expanding: \(h^2 = 144 + 78.44\,x - 0.05\,x^2\).

Step 5: Water table divide

$$ d = \frac{L}{2} - \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2pL} = \frac{1570}{2} - \frac{12 \times 95}{2 \times 0.6 \times 1570} $$
$$ d = 785 - \frac{1140}{1884} = 785 - 0.605 $$
$$ \boxed{d = 784.4 \text{ m from the deeper canal}} $$

The water divide is nearly at the midpoint because the intense rainfall dominates over the Dupuit gradient term.

SOLUTION 2: 2073 Bhadra Q3(b) — Dupuit Equation (No Recharge)

Given: \(L = 5000\) m, \(h_1 = 20\) m, \(h_2 = 11\) m, \(K = 18\) m/day.

Step 1: Seepage discharge per unit river length

$$ Q = \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2L} = \frac{18(400 - 121)}{2 \times 5000} = \frac{18 \times 279}{10000} $$
$$ \boxed{Q = 0.502 \text{ m}^2/\text{day per m of river length}} $$

Step 2: Phreatic surface equation

$$ h^2 = h_1^2 - \frac{h_1^2 - h_2^2}{L}\,x = 400 - \frac{279}{5000}\,x $$
$$ \boxed{h^2 = 400 - 0.0558\,x} $$

Or equivalently: \(h = \sqrt{400 - 0.0558\,x}\). This is a parabolic profile that drops more steeply near the discharge boundary.

SOLUTION 3: 2072 Ashwin Q5 — Confined Aquifer (Variable Thickness)

Given: \(K = 0.4\) m/day, \(L = 800\) m, \(b_1 = 8\) m, \(b_2 = 6\) m, \(h_1 = 10\) m, \(h_2 = 8\) m.

Step 1: Identify the formula for variable (wedge-shaped) thickness

Since the thickness changes linearly from \(b_1\) to \(b_2\), we use:

$$ Q = K \frac{(h_1 - h_2)(b_2 - b_1)}{L \ln(b_2 / b_1)} $$

Step 2: Substitute values

$$ Q = 0.4 \times \frac{(10 - 8)(6 - 8)}{800 \times \ln(6/8)} = 0.4 \times \frac{2 \times (-2)}{800 \times \ln(0.75)} $$
$$ Q = 0.4 \times \frac{-4}{800 \times (-0.2877)} = 0.4 \times \frac{-4}{-230.16} $$
$$ Q = 0.4 \times 0.01738 $$
$$ \boxed{Q = 6.95 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^2/\text{day per m width}} $$

Note: The positive result confirms flow is from \(h_1 = 10\) m toward \(h_2 = 8\) m (in the direction of decreasing head).

SOLUTION 4: 2072 Magh Q7 — Confined Aquifer (Variable Thickness)

Given: \(L = 950\) m, \(K = 0.56\) m/day, \(h_1 = 12\) m, \(b_1 = 7\) m, \(h_2 = 9\) m, \(b_2 = 5\) m.

Step 1: Apply the variable-thickness formula

$$ Q = K \frac{(h_1 - h_2)(b_2 - b_1)}{L \ln(b_2/b_1)} $$

Step 2: Substitute

$$ Q = 0.56 \times \frac{(12 - 9)(5 - 7)}{950 \times \ln(5/7)} = 0.56 \times \frac{3 \times (-2)}{950 \times \ln(0.7143)} $$
$$ Q = 0.56 \times \frac{-6}{950 \times (-0.3365)} = 0.56 \times \frac{-6}{-319.68} $$
$$ Q = 0.56 \times 0.01877 $$
$$ \boxed{Q = 1.051 \times 10^{-2} \text{ m}^2/\text{day per m width}} $$

SOLUTION 5: 2071 Bhadra Q4 — Unconfined Aquifer with Recharge

Given: \(p = 12\) mm/hr, \(L = 1200\) m, \(h_1 = 10\) m, \(h_2 = 8\) m, \(K = 10\) m/day.

Step 1: Convert rainfall

$$ p = 12 \text{ mm/hr} = \frac{12 \times 24}{1000} = 0.288 \text{ m/day} $$

Step 2: Seepage into the deeper canal (q1)

$$ q_1 = \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2L} + \frac{pL}{2} = \frac{10(100 - 64)}{2 \times 1200} + \frac{0.288 \times 1200}{2} $$
$$ q_1 = \frac{360}{2400} + \frac{345.6}{2} = 0.15 + 172.8 $$
$$ \boxed{q_1 = 172.95 \text{ m}^2/\text{day}} $$

Step 3: Seepage into the shallower canal (q2)

$$ q_2 = \frac{pL}{2} - \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2L} = 172.8 - 0.15 $$
$$ \boxed{q_2 = 172.65 \text{ m}^2/\text{day}} $$

Check: \(q_1 + q_2 = 172.95 + 172.65 = 345.6 = pL = 0.288 \times 1200\). ✓

Step 4: Phreatic surface equation

$$ h^2 = 100 - \frac{36}{1200}\,x + \frac{0.288}{10}\,x(1200 - x) $$
$$ \boxed{h^2 = 100 - 0.03\,x + 0.0288\,x(1200 - x)} $$

Step 5: Water table divide

$$ d = \frac{L}{2} - \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2pL} = 600 - \frac{10 \times 36}{2 \times 0.288 \times 1200} $$
$$ d = 600 - \frac{360}{691.2} = 600 - 0.521 $$
$$ \boxed{d = 599.5 \text{ m from the deeper canal}} $$

Step 6: Seepage when rainfall is absent (p = 0)

$$ q = \frac{K(h_1^2 - h_2^2)}{2L} = \frac{10 \times 36}{2 \times 1200} $$
$$ \boxed{q = 0.15 \text{ m}^2/\text{day (all flow toward the lower canal)}} $$

Without rainfall, the seepage drops from 345.6 m²/day (total) to just 0.15 m²/day, and there is no water divide.

SOLUTION 6: 2073 Bhadra Q3(a) — Water Table Contour Map

Given: Four wells at rectangle vertices with water-table elevations 51 m, 56 m, 76 m, 66 m. Contour interval = 5 m.

Step 1: Assign well positions

Let the rectangle have corners: A (bottom-left) = 56 m, B (bottom-right) = 51 m, C (top-right) = 66 m, D (top-left) = 76 m.

Step 2: Interpolate contour crossings on each side

  • Side AD (56 m to 76 m): The 60, 65, 70, 75 m contours cross this side. For example, the 60 m contour crosses at \(\frac{60-56}{76-56} = 0.2\) (20% from A).
  • Side BC (51 m to 66 m): The 55, 60, 65 m contours cross. The 55 m contour at \(\frac{55-51}{66-51} = 0.267\) (26.7% from B).
  • Side AB (56 m to 51 m): The 55 m contour crosses at \(\frac{56-55}{56-51} = 0.2\) (20% from A).
  • Side DC (76 m to 66 m): The 70 m contour crosses at \(\frac{76-70}{76-66} = 0.6\) (60% from D).

Step 3: Draw contour lines

Connect interpolated points of equal elevation across the rectangle to draw the 55, 60, 65, 70, and 75 m contour lines (see Figure 3.4 for the completed map).

Step 4: Determine flow direction

Groundwater flows perpendicular to the contour lines in the direction of decreasing head. From the map, the general flow direction is from the 76 m corner (highest) toward the 51 m corner (lowest), roughly along the diagonal of the rectangle.

Step 5: Estimate hydraulic gradient

The gradient can be estimated between any two contours:

$$ i = \frac{\Delta h}{\text{distance between contours}} $$

Closely spaced contours indicate steeper gradients and faster flow; widely spaced contours indicate gentle gradients.
