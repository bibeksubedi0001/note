8.1 INTRODUCTION — NEED FOR PUMPING

Groundwater, by nature, exists below the land surface. To extract it for use in drinking water supply, irrigation, or industrial purposes, energy must be supplied to lift the water from the aquifer to the surface. This energy is typically provided by pumps.

The selection and design of a pumping system is a critical step in groundwater development. The pump must:

  • Deliver the required discharge (Q) at the required pressure head
  • Overcome the total dynamic head (TDH), which includes the static lift, drawdown, friction losses, and delivery head
  • Operate efficiently at the design point to minimize energy costs
  • Be suitable for the well type, depth, and water quality conditions
Key Exam Point: This is a theory-only chapter — no numerical problems. Questions typically ask for types of pumps (4 marks), working principle of centrifugal pump (4 marks), or pump selection factors (4 marks). Focus on clear descriptions with diagrams.

8.3 CENTRIFUGAL PUMP — WORKING PRINCIPLE

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED:

8.3.1 Description

The centrifugal pump is the most widely used type of pump for groundwater lifting. It belongs to the rotodynamic pump category and works on the principle of converting kinetic energy of water into pressure energy.

8.3.2 Main Components

  • Impeller: A rotating disc with curved vanes (blades) mounted on a shaft. It is the key component that imparts energy to the water. May be open, semi-open, or enclosed (shrouded) type.
  • Casing (volute): A spiral-shaped housing that surrounds the impeller. Its cross-sectional area gradually increases from the impeller to the discharge nozzle, converting kinetic energy to pressure energy.
  • Suction pipe with foot valve and strainer: Connects the pump to the water source. The foot valve prevents backflow when the pump is stopped. The strainer prevents debris from entering.
  • Delivery pipe: Carries water from the pump to the point of use or storage.
  • Shaft and bearings: The shaft connects the impeller to the prime mover (motor or engine). Bearings support the shaft.
  • Stuffing box / mechanical seal: Prevents leakage where the shaft passes through the casing.

8.3.3 Working Principle

The working principle of a centrifugal pump can be explained in the following steps:

  1. Priming: Before starting, the pump casing and suction pipe must be completely filled with water (primed). Air must be removed because the pump cannot create sufficient vacuum to lift water if air is present.
  2. Rotation of impeller: When the electric motor (or engine) starts, the impeller rotates at high speed (typically 1450 or 2900 rpm).
  3. Centrifugal action: As the impeller rotates, the water between the vanes is thrown outward by centrifugal force towards the periphery of the casing. This creates a region of low pressure (partial vacuum) at the centre of the impeller (eye).
  4. Suction: Due to the low pressure at the eye, atmospheric pressure acting on the water surface in the source (well/sump) pushes water up through the suction pipe into the impeller eye. This is a continuous process as long as the pump runs.
  5. Energy conversion in the volute: The water thrown to the periphery at high velocity enters the volute casing. As the cross-sectional area of the volute increases, the velocity of water decreases and its pressure increases (Bernoulli’s principle). This converts kinetic energy into pressure energy.
  6. Delivery: The high-pressure water exits through the delivery pipe to the required height or storage tank.

8.3.4 Types of Centrifugal Pumps

(a) Volute Pump

The most common type. The impeller is surrounded by a spiral volute casing that converts velocity head to pressure head. Simple construction and widely used for surface-mounted installations.

(b) Turbine (Diffuser) Pump

Uses stationary guide vanes (diffusers) around the impeller instead of a volute casing. The diffuser vanes gradually expand the flow area and convert kinetic energy more efficiently. Used in vertical turbine pumps installed in deep wells (also called deep-well turbine pumps or lineshaft turbine pumps).

(c) Multistage Centrifugal Pump

Contains two or more impellers arranged in series on the same shaft. Each impeller (stage) adds pressure. Used when a single impeller cannot provide the required total head. The total head = head per stage × number of stages.

8.3.5 Advantages and Limitations

Advantages:
  • Simple construction and compact design
  • Smooth, continuous flow (non-pulsating)
  • Can handle large discharges
  • High efficiency (60–85%)
  • Low maintenance cost
  • Can be directly coupled to electric motor
  • Suitable for a wide range of heads and discharges
Limitations:
  • Requires priming before start-up (not self-priming)
  • Limited suction lift (≤ 6–7 m due to atmospheric pressure limit and NPSH requirements)
  • Efficiency drops significantly at off-design conditions
  • Not suitable for very high heads with single stage
  • Cannot handle viscous fluids or fluids with large solids
  • Susceptible to cavitation if NPSH is insufficient
Cavitation: When the pressure at the pump inlet drops below the vapour pressure of water, dissolved gases form bubbles that implode violently when they reach higher-pressure zones. This causes pitting damage to the impeller and noise. To prevent cavitation, the available Net Positive Suction Head (NPSH) must exceed the required NPSH of the pump.
Figure 8.2c: Left: Why priming is required — air cannot create sufficient vacuum; only water can. Maximum suction lift ≈ 6–7 m. Right: Cavitation process — pressure drops below vapour pressure → bubbles form → implode violently → impeller damage. Animated bubbles show formation and collapse.

8.4 SUBMERSIBLE PUMP

A submersible pump is a centrifugal pump that has the motor and pump unit integrated into a single waterproof, sealed assembly that is lowered entirely into the well below the water level.

8.4.1 Construction

  • Motor: Hermetically sealed, water-cooled electric motor located below the pump stages. The motor is filled with oil or water for insulation and cooling.
  • Pump stages: Multiple impellers (stages) stacked in series above the motor. Each stage adds head. A typical groundwater submersible may have 5–30 stages.
  • Intake screen: Between the motor and pump section, allows water to enter while filtering sand.
  • Power cable: Waterproof electrical cable runs from the motor to the surface control panel.
  • Drop pipe (rising main): Connects the pump outlet to the surface delivery.

8.4.2 Working Principle

The working principle is the same as a multistage centrifugal pump. The motor drives the impellers, which spin and throw water outward by centrifugal force. Each stage adds pressure, and the accumulated pressure forces water up through the rising main to the surface.

Because the pump is submerged, it does not need priming and there is no suction lift limitation. The pump is always below the water level, so it pushes water upward rather than pulling it.

8.4.3 Advantages and Limitations

Advantages:
  • No priming needed — always submerged
  • No suction lift limit — can lift from any depth
  • Compact — fits inside small-diameter tube wells
  • Quiet operation (submerged, no surface noise)
  • No separate pump house required
  • High efficiency for deep-well applications
  • Freeze-proof (pump is below frost line)
Limitations:
  • Higher initial cost than surface pumps
  • Difficult to inspect and repair (must be pulled out)
  • Motor burns out if water level drops below the motor (dry running)
  • Sensitive to sand and abrasive particles
  • Electrical cable failure is difficult to detect
  • Motor winding insulation can degrade over time

8.5 JET PUMP (EJECTOR PUMP)

A jet pump (or ejector pump) combines a centrifugal pump at the surface with a jet (ejector) assembly located down in the well. It is used to lift water from depths greater than the normal suction limit of a surface centrifugal pump (which is about 6–7 m).

8.5.1 Working Principle

  1. A centrifugal pump at the surface sends a portion of the pumped water back down through a pressure pipe to the ejector assembly in the well.
  2. The ejector has a nozzle that converts the pressure energy of this recirculated water into a high-velocity jet.
  3. The high-velocity jet creates a low-pressure zone (Venturi effect) in the mixing chamber, which draws groundwater from the well into the jet stream.
  4. The mixed water (recirculated + fresh groundwater) passes through a diffuser (gradually expanding section), converting velocity to pressure.
  5. The pressurized mixture rises through the return pipe to the surface pump, which delivers the net discharge.

8.5.2 Advantages and Limitations

Figure 8.5: Animated jet pump (ejector) — red dots show pressurized water flowing down through the nozzle; blue dots show the mixed water (recirculated + fresh GW) returning to the surface. The pulsing circle at the nozzle shows the low-pressure (Venturi) zone that draws in groundwater.

8.5.2 Advantages and Limitations

Advantages:
  • All moving parts at the surface — easy maintenance
  • Can lift water from 20–60 m depth
  • Self-priming after initial prime
  • Suitable for small-diameter wells
Limitations:
  • Low efficiency (25–40%) due to energy loss in the jet
  • Part of the pumped water is recirculated (reduced net discharge)
  • Efficiency decreases with increasing depth
  • Not suitable for large yields

8.6 AIRLIFT PUMP

An airlift pump uses compressed air to lift water from a well. It has no moving parts inside the well.

8.6.1 Working Principle

  1. Compressed air is injected into the bottom of a riser pipe (eductor pipe) submerged in the well water.
  2. The air mixes with water in the riser pipe, creating an air-water mixture that is lighter than the surrounding water column.
  3. The denser surrounding water pushes the lighter air-water mixture upward through the riser pipe to the surface.
  4. At the surface, air is separated and water is collected.

The key requirement is that the submergence ratio (ratio of the submerged length of the riser pipe to the total length above the water surface) must be adequate. Typically, the submergence should be 50–70% of the total riser length for reasonable efficiency.

8.6.2 Advantages and Limitations

Figure 8.6: Animated airlift pump — white bubbles (air) rise through the riser pipe, carrying water (blue dots) upward. The air-water mixture is lighter than the surrounding water column, creating the lifting force. The pulsing red dot shows the air injection point.

8.6.2 Advantages and Limitations

Advantages:
  • No moving parts in the well — minimal wear
  • Can handle sandy or muddy water
  • Useful for well development and testing
  • Simple installation
Limitations:
  • Very low efficiency (20–35%)
  • Requires large submergence — not suitable for shallow wells
  • Requires air compressor at the surface
  • Not suitable for continuous large-scale pumping

8.7 RECIPROCATING PUMP (HAND PUMP)

A reciprocating pump is a positive displacement pump that uses a piston or plunger moving back and forth in a cylinder to draw and push water. Hand-operated versions are widely used in rural areas of Nepal and South Asia for shallow groundwater extraction.

8.7.1 Working Principle

Figure 8.7: Animated reciprocating (hand) pump — Left: Suction stroke (piston up, suction valve opens, water drawn in). Right: Delivery stroke (piston down, delivery valve opens, water pushed through and out the spout). Valves alternate green (open) / red (closed).

8.7.1 Working Principle

  1. Suction stroke: As the piston moves upward, the volume below the piston increases, creating a partial vacuum. The suction valve opens and water from the well enters the cylinder.
  2. Delivery stroke: As the piston moves downward, the suction valve closes and the delivery valve (on the piston) opens. Water is forced above the piston.
  3. On the next upstroke, the water above the piston is pushed out through the delivery spout, and fresh water enters from below.

8.9 FACTORS IN PUMP SELECTION

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED:

2078 Chaitra Q6 [4]: Explain the factors to be considered in the selection of pump sets for groundwater pumping.

2073 Bhadra Q9 [4+2]: What factors should be considered while selecting a groundwater pump?

The selection of an appropriate pump for a groundwater project involves the following factors:

A. Well Characteristics

  • Well type: Open well or tube well? Open wells typically use centrifugal surface pumps; tube wells need submersible or deep-well turbine pumps.
  • Well depth and casing diameter: The pump must physically fit inside the well. Submersible pumps require minimum 100 mm (4″) casing.
  • Static water level and drawdown: Determines the total pumping lift. If static water level is within 6–7 m, a surface centrifugal pump suffices. Deeper water levels require submersible or jet pumps.

B. Required Discharge and Head

  • Design discharge (Q): The pump must deliver the required flow rate. Centrifugal pumps handle high Q; hand pumps are for low Q.
  • Total dynamic head (TDH): The sum of:
    • Static suction lift (depth to pumping water level)
    • Static delivery head (height above pump to delivery point)
    • Friction losses in pipes and fittings
    • Velocity head at discharge

C. Power Source Availability

  • Electricity: Most pumps (centrifugal, submersible) require reliable electric supply
  • Diesel/petrol engine: Used where electricity is unavailable or unreliable
  • Solar energy: Increasingly used for submersible pumps in remote areas
  • Manual power: Hand pumps for areas without any power source

D. Water Quality and Sand Content

  • Sandy or turbid water can damage centrifugal pump impellers due to abrasion
  • Corrosive water (high iron, low pH) requires stainless steel or plastic components
  • Airlift pumps are preferred for sandy wells (no submerged moving parts)

E. Economic Factors

  • Initial cost: Purchase and installation cost of the pump set
  • Operating cost: Energy consumption, which depends on pump efficiency and hours of operation
  • Maintenance cost: Ease and frequency of repairs
  • Life cycle cost: Total cost over the expected lifespan (typically 10–20 years)

F. Reliability and Serviceability

  • Availability of spare parts and local repair expertise
  • Durability and expected service life
  • Ease of installation, removal, and repair
  • For community water supply, VLOM (Village Level Operation and Maintenance) pumps are preferred

G. Duty Conditions

  • Continuous vs. intermittent operation: Submersible pumps are well-suited for continuous operation; hand pumps are for intermittent use.
  • Future expansion: Whether the system may need higher discharge in the future
Exam writing strategy: When asked about pump selection factors, organize your answer under clear headings (Well Characteristics, Discharge & Head, Power, Water Quality, Economics, Serviceability). Write 1–2 bullet points under each heading. This structured approach scores higher marks than a loose list.

8.10 PUMP PERFORMANCE AND BASIC FORMULAE

Although Chapter 8 is theory-only, the following basic pump formulae are useful for understanding pump performance and may appear in related contexts:

8.10.1 Total Dynamic Head (TDH)

Figure 8.8: Total Dynamic Head (TDH) breakdown — the pump must overcome the sum of suction head, delivery head, pipe friction losses, and velocity head at the outlet. An animated blue particle traces the complete water path from well to storage tank.

8.10.1 Total Dynamic Head (TDH)

\[ \text{TDH} = h_s + h_d + h_f + \frac{v^2}{2g} \]

where:

  • \( h_s \) = static suction head (depth to pumping water level)
  • \( h_d \) = static delivery head (height to delivery point)
  • \( h_f \) = total friction head loss in suction and delivery pipes
  • \( v^2/2g \) = velocity head at the delivery pipe outlet

8.10.2 Power Required

\[ P_w = \frac{\gamma \cdot Q \cdot \text{TDH}}{1000} \quad \text{(kW)} \]

where \( \gamma = \rho g = 9810 \text{ N/m}^3 \) (specific weight of water), Q in m³/s.

The brake power (power input to the pump shaft) accounts for pump efficiency:

\[ P_b = \frac{P_w}{\eta_p} = \frac{\gamma \cdot Q \cdot \text{TDH}}{1000 \cdot \eta_p} \]

The motor power (if electric motor driven):

\[ P_m = \frac{P_b}{\eta_m} = \frac{\gamma \cdot Q \cdot \text{TDH}}{1000 \cdot \eta_p \cdot \eta_m} \]

where \( \eta_p \) = pump efficiency (0.6–0.85) and \( \eta_m \) = motor efficiency (0.85–0.95).

8.10.3 Specific Speed

Specific speed is a dimensionless parameter used to classify pump types and select the most efficient pump for given conditions:

\[ N_s = \frac{N \sqrt{Q}}{H^{3/4}} \]

where N = pump speed (rpm), Q = discharge (m³/s), H = head per stage (m). Low Ns indicates radial flow (centrifugal); high Ns indicates axial flow (propeller type).

Note: These formulae are typically used in pump design or selection calculations, not as separate numerical problems in the groundwater syllabus. However, understanding TDH and power is important for conceptual questions.
