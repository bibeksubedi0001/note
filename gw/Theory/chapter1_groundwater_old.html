1.1 INTRODUCTION TO GROUNDWATER ENGINEERING

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. What is Groundwater Engineering? What is the importance of groundwater in the context of Nepal? Write the advantages and disadvantages of groundwater. [2+3+3] (2071 Bhadra, Q1)
  2. State the advantages of groundwater in comparison to surface water. [4] (2073 Bhadra, Q1a)
DEFINITION: Groundwater Engineering is the branch of civil engineering concerned with the physics of saturated flow in porous media and its engineering applications — including groundwater occurrence, Darcian flow, well hydraulics, pumping tests, exploration methods, tube-well design, pump selection, and the economics of groundwater utilization.
Figure 1.0: Distribution of Earth's water — groundwater is the largest accessible freshwater source.

Groundwater is subsurface water that occupies the saturated zone of soil and rock formations beneath the earth's surface. It constitutes roughly 30% of the world's total freshwater and is the largest accessible reservoir of liquid freshwater on the planet. In the context of Nepal, groundwater is of particular importance because:

  • The Terai region (southern plains) is underlain by extensive alluvial aquifers that serve as the primary water source for drinking, irrigation, and industry for over 50% of Nepal's population.
  • The Kathmandu Valley depends heavily on groundwater to supplement inadequate surface water supply, with deep tube wells tapping confined aquifers within lacustrine sediments.
  • In hilly areas, springs issuing from groundwater are often the sole source of drinking water for rural communities.

Advantages of Groundwater over Surface Water

  1. Better Quality: Groundwater is naturally filtered through soil and rock formations, making it generally free from turbidity, pathogenic organisms, and organic matter. It requires minimal treatment before use.
  2. Reliable and Drought-Resistant: Groundwater is less affected by seasonal variations and droughts compared to rivers and lakes. Aquifers act as natural storage reservoirs that buffer against climatic fluctuations.
  3. Wide Availability: Groundwater is found almost everywhere beneath the earth's surface, even in areas where surface water is scarce.
  4. Low Evaporation Losses: Since groundwater is stored underground, it is not subject to evaporation losses, unlike reservoirs and open channels.
  5. Near Point of Use: Wells can be sited close to the point of demand, reducing transmission and conveyance costs.
  6. Lower Development Cost: In many cases, drilling a well is cheaper and faster than constructing a dam, treatment plant, and pipeline system.
  7. Uniform Temperature: Groundwater maintains a nearly constant temperature, making it suitable for industrial cooling and aquaculture.

Disadvantages of Groundwater

  1. Risk of Over-Exploitation: Excessive pumping can lower the water table permanently, leading to well failure and land subsidence.
  2. Contamination is Difficult to Remediate: Once an aquifer is contaminated (e.g., by arsenic, nitrates, or industrial pollutants), natural restoration is extremely slow and costly.
  3. Limited Yield: Individual wells have limited discharge capacity compared to surface water systems like rivers or large reservoirs.
  4. High Mineral Content: Prolonged contact with geological formations can lead to high concentrations of iron, manganese, fluoride, or arsenic (a critical issue in the Terai of Nepal).
  5. Energy Cost of Lifting: Groundwater must be pumped to the surface, incurring continuous energy costs that increase as the water table declines.
  6. Difficulty of Assessment: Unlike surface water, groundwater cannot be directly observed. Quantifying aquifer properties requires expensive exploration and testing programmes.

1.2 HYDROLOGICAL CYCLE AND GROUNDWATER

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Explain briefly the hydrological cycle, focusing on components of subsurface movement of water. [4] (2078 Chaitra, Q1a)
  2. Explain the origination of groundwater and how hydrological components affect groundwater. [6] (2072 Magh, Q1b)

The hydrological cycle (or water cycle) is the continuous circulation of water within the earth–atmosphere system. Groundwater is an integral component of this cycle, recharged by infiltration and discharged through springs, rivers, and evapotranspiration.

Figure 1.1: The Hydrological Cycle showing the role of groundwater.

Components of the Hydrological Cycle Relevant to Groundwater

  1. Precipitation: Rain, snow, sleet, or hail falling on the earth's surface. This is the primary source of groundwater recharge.
  2. Infiltration: The process by which water on the ground surface enters the soil. The rate of infiltration depends on soil type, antecedent moisture content, slope, vegetation cover, and rainfall intensity.
  3. Percolation: The downward movement of infiltrated water through the unsaturated (vadose) zone under gravity until it reaches the water table. This is the mechanism by which aquifers are recharged.
  4. Groundwater Flow: Once water reaches the saturated zone, it moves laterally through the aquifer from areas of higher hydraulic head to areas of lower hydraulic head, governed by Darcy's law.
  5. Capillary Rise: Water moves upward from the water table into the capillary fringe due to surface tension forces in the soil pores.
  6. Discharge: Groundwater is discharged naturally through springs, seepage into rivers, lakes, and wetlands (base flow), and through evapotranspiration where the water table is shallow enough.
  7. Evapotranspiration: The combined loss of water through evaporation from the soil and transpiration from plants. Deep-rooted vegetation can directly extract groundwater.

Origin and Age of Groundwater

Based on origin, groundwater is classified into three types:

  1. Meteoric Water: Water derived from precipitation (rain and snowmelt) that infiltrates the soil and percolates down to the water table. This is the most common origin and constitutes the majority of exploitable groundwater.
  2. Connate Water: Water trapped in the pore spaces of sedimentary rocks at the time of their deposition (millions of years ago). This "fossil water" is often highly mineralized and may be saline.
  3. Juvenile (Magmatic) Water: Water that originates from deep within the earth's interior, released during volcanic activity or crystallization of magma. It has never been part of the surface water cycle before.
  4. Metamorphic Water: Water that is squeezed or drained out of rocks during the process of metamorphism — the transformation of existing rock by heat and pressure deep within the earth's crust.
  5. Fossil Water: A form of connate water that has been preserved in an aquifer for a very long period of time (thousands to millions of years) without any modern recharge. It is essentially a non-renewable groundwater resource.

Age of Groundwater

The age of groundwater is defined as the time elapsed since water entered an aquifer. It varies enormously — from days or weeks in shallow unconfined aquifers receiving direct rainfall recharge, to tens of thousands of years for deeply confined aquifers containing connate or fossil water.

Why Groundwater Age Matters

  • To identify the pollution vulnerability of groundwater — younger water is more susceptible to recent contamination.
  • To understand how quickly water moves through aquifers — essential for sustainable management.
  • To identify recharge zones and quantify recharge rates.

Measurement Techniques

Scientists use natural tracers to estimate groundwater age:

  • Carbon-14 (C-14): For dating very old groundwater (up to ~50,000 years).
  • Tritium: For dating young groundwater (post-1950s, from nuclear testing).
  • Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs): For dating modern groundwater recharged since the 1940s.
  • Sulfur isotopes: To trace groundwater origin and mixing.

1.3 SUBSURFACE ZONES OF WATER

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Write about the major soil zones according to the presence of water and list the types of springs. [4+2] (2078 Chaitra, Q1b)
  2. With the help of a neat sketch, briefly discuss various subsurface zones. [4] (2073 Bhadra, Q1b)

The subsurface can be divided into two principal zones based on the degree of saturation of pore spaces. These zones are further subdivided as follows:

Figure 1.2: Various subsurface zones showing the distribution of water below ground surface.

A. Zone of Aeration (Vadose Zone / Unsaturated Zone)

This is the zone between the ground surface and the water table. Pore spaces contain both air and water. It is subdivided into three sub-zones:

  1. Soil Water Zone (Belt of Soil Moisture): The uppermost sub-zone, extending from the ground surface to the depth of plant roots (typically 1–2 m). Water here is held by adhesion to soil particles and capillary forces. Plants extract water from this zone through their root systems via transpiration.
  2. Intermediate Vadose Zone: Extends from the bottom of the soil water zone to the top of the capillary fringe. Gravitational water percolates downward through this zone. The thickness can range from zero (where the water table is shallow) to hundreds of metres in arid regions.
  3. Capillary Fringe: Located immediately above the water table. Water rises into this zone from the water table by capillary action through fine pores. The pores are nearly or fully saturated, but the water is held at less-than-atmospheric pressure (negative pore water pressure). Its thickness depends on the pore size — fine-grained soils (clays) have thick capillary fringes (up to several metres), while coarse sands have thin ones (a few centimetres).

B. Zone of Saturation (Phreatic Zone)

Below the water table, all voids in the soil and rock are completely filled with water under hydrostatic pressure (pressure ≥ atmospheric). This is the zone from which wells draw water and where true groundwater exists. Water in this zone moves laterally through the aquifer under hydraulic gradients.

1.4 SPRINGS AND THEIR TYPES

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  1. Define springs, list out the types of springs, and explain each briefly. [6] (2072 Magh, Q1a)
  2. List the types of springs. [2] (2078 Chaitra, Q1b)
DEFINITION: A spring is a natural point of discharge where groundwater emerges at the earth's surface due to the intersection of the water table or a confined aquifer with the ground surface, aided by geological and topographic conditions.

Classification of Springs

A. Based on the Cause of Emergence

  1. Gravity Springs (Depression Springs): Occur where the land surface dips below the water table, such as in a valley or depression. The water table intersects the ground surface and water flows out under the influence of gravity.
  2. Artesian Springs (Pressure Springs): Occur when the piezometric surface of a confined aquifer rises above the ground surface at a particular location (e.g., due to a fault or fracture), causing water to flow out under pressure without pumping.
  3. Contact Springs: Form at the contact (junction) between a permeable formation overlying an impervious formation. Water percolating through the upper permeable layer is forced to emerge laterally at the contact plane.
  4. Fault Springs: Occur along geological faults where an impervious formation on one side of the fault is juxtaposed against a permeable water-bearing formation on the other side, forcing groundwater to the surface along the fault plane.
Figure 1.3: Types of springs based on the cause of emergence.

B. Based on Discharge

Magnitude Discharge
1st Magnitude > 2800 litres/s (largest springs)
2nd Magnitude 280 – 2800 litres/s
3rd Magnitude 28 – 280 litres/s
4th to 8th Magnitude Progressively smaller, with 8th magnitude < 0.06 litres/s

C. Based on Temperature

  • Cold Springs: Water temperature is at or below the mean annual air temperature of the area.
  • Thermal (Hot) Springs: Water temperature significantly exceeds the mean annual air temperature, usually due to deep circulation or proximity to geothermal heat sources.

D. Based on Variability

  • Perennial Springs: Flow throughout the year, typically fed by large or deep aquifers.
  • Intermittent Springs: Flow only during the wet season or when the water table is sufficiently high.

E. Based on Geological Setting

  1. Tilted Spring: Occurs where permeable and impermeable rocks alternate in a tilted sequence. Water percolates downward through the permeable layer until it reaches the contact with the impermeable layer, emerging at the base of the permeable rock where the water table meets the surface.
  2. Dyke Spring: Formed when a dyke (an impermeable igneous rock intrusion cutting through surrounding formations) acts as a barrier to groundwater flow, forcing water to rise and discharge at the surface along the dyke.
  3. Karst Spring: Found in limestone regions composed of calcium carbonate rock. These springs are often associated with underground tunnels, caves, and solution channels formed by chemical dissolution of the rock. Karst springs can have very large and variable discharges.

1.5 BASIC DEFINITIONS AND TYPES OF AQUIFER

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  2. Define specific storage, specific yield, transmissibility, and anisotropy of an aquifer. [4] (2072 Ashwin, Q1)

Water-Bearing Formations — Basic Terminology

Term Definition Example
Aquifer A geological formation that is sufficiently permeable and porous to store and transmit significant quantities of water under ordinary field conditions. It yields economically useful amounts of water to wells and springs. Sand, gravel, sandstone, fractured limestone, weathered granite
Aquitard A geological formation that is less permeable than an aquifer. It can store water but transmits it only slowly. It retards the flow of water but does not entirely prevent it (allows leakage). Sandy clay, silt, loess
Aquiclude A geological formation that can store water but does not transmit it in significant quantities. It is essentially impermeable for practical purposes. Clay, shale
Aquifuge A geological formation that can neither store nor transmit water. It is absolutely impermeable and non-porous. Massive unfractured granite, quartzite, basalt

Types of Aquifer

Figure 1.4: Types of aquifers — confined, unconfined, leaky, and perched.

1. Unconfined Aquifer (Water-Table Aquifer / Phreatic Aquifer)

An unconfined aquifer has a free water table as its upper boundary. The water table rises and falls freely with recharge and discharge. The water in it is at atmospheric pressure at the water table surface. When a well is drilled into an unconfined aquifer, the water level in the well corresponds to the water table — it does not rise above it.

2. Confined Aquifer (Artesian Aquifer / Pressure Aquifer)

A confined aquifer is bounded both above and below by impervious or nearly impervious layers (aquicludes or aquitards). Water in the aquifer is under pressure greater than atmospheric, characterized by a piezometric surface (or potentiometric surface) that lies above the top of the aquifer. When a well taps a confined aquifer:

  • If the piezometric surface lies above the ground surface, water flows out of the well without pumping — this is called a flowing artesian well.
  • If the piezometric surface is below the ground but above the aquifer top, the water rises in the well above the aquifer top but does not flow; this is a non-flowing artesian well.

3. Leaky Aquifer (Semi-Confined Aquifer)

A leaky aquifer is bounded above or below (or both) by aquitards rather than aquicludes. The semi-pervious confining layers allow slow vertical leakage of water into or out of the aquifer from adjacent formations. When the aquifer is pumped, the pressure difference induces water to leak through the aquitard, supplementing the aquifer's yield. This is the most common type of aquifer in nature.

4. Perched Aquifer

A perched aquifer is a special case of an unconfined aquifer that occurs above the regional water table. It forms when infiltrating water is held up by a small, localized impervious lens (such as a clay layer) within the vadose zone, creating a local body of saturated soil above the main water table. Perched aquifers are typically small and seasonal — they dry up during prolonged dry periods. They may support shallow wells or small springs but are not reliable for large-scale water supply.

1.6 PROPERTIES OF SOIL/ROCK AFFECTING GROUNDWATER FLOW

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS ADDRESSED IN THIS SECTION:

  2. What are water-bearing materials? Explain briefly. [4] (2071 Bhadra, Q5)

1. Porosity (\(n\))

DEFINITION: Porosity is the ratio of the volume of voids (pore spaces) in a soil or rock sample to the total volume of the sample.
$$ n = \frac{V_v}{V_T} \times 100\% $$

where \( V_v \) = volume of voids and \( V_T \) = total volume.

Effective Porosity (\(n_e\)): Not all pores are interconnected. Effective porosity refers to the ratio of interconnected pore volume to total volume — only interconnected pores allow fluid flow. Clay may have very high total porosity (40–70%) but extremely low effective porosity due to its tiny, poorly connected pores.

Material Porosity (%)
Gravel 25 – 40
Sand 25 – 50
Silt 35 – 50
Clay 40 – 70
Sandstone 5 – 30
Limestone 1 – 20
Unfractured Granite < 1

2. Specific Yield (\(S_y\))

DEFINITION: Specific yield is the volume of water that an unconfined aquifer releases from storage per unit surface area per unit decline in the water table. It represents the water that drains freely from the pores under gravity.
$$ S_y = \frac{\text{Volume of water drained by gravity}}{\text{Total volume of aquifer}} $$

Specific Retention (\(S_r\)): The water retained in the pores against gravity (held by capillary and molecular forces). The relationship is:

$$ n = S_y + S_r $$
Figure 1.6: Concept of porosity, specific yield (Sy) and specific retention (Sr).

Fine-grained materials have low specific yield (water is retained) despite having high porosity. Coarse materials like sand and gravel have high specific yield because water drains readily. Typical values: gravel = 15–30%, coarse sand = 15–25%, fine sand = 10–15%, clay = 1–5%.

3. Specific Storage (\(S_s\))

DEFINITION: Specific storage is the volume of water that a unit volume of a confined aquifer releases from or takes into storage per unit change in hydraulic head. It accounts for water released due to the compressibility of the aquifer skeleton and the compressibility of water itself.
$$ S_s = \rho g (\alpha + n \beta) $$

where \( \rho \) = density of water, \( g \) = gravitational acceleration, \( \alpha \) = compressibility of the aquifer skeleton, \( n \) = porosity, and \( \beta \) = compressibility of water.

4. Storage Coefficient / Storativity (\(S\))

DEFINITION: The storage coefficient (storativity) is the volume of water released from or taken into storage per unit surface area of the aquifer per unit change in the component of head perpendicular to that surface.

For a confined aquifer of thickness \( b \):

$$ S = S_s \cdot b $$

Confined aquifers typically have very small storage coefficients (10−5 to 10−3) because water is released mainly through compression of the aquifer and expansion of water.

For an unconfined aquifer:

$$ S \approx S_y $$

Unconfined aquifers have much larger storage coefficients (0.1 to 0.3) because water is released by actual dewatering (draining) of the pore spaces.

5. Transmissivity (\(T\))

DEFINITION: Transmissivity is the rate at which water is transmitted through a unit width of the entire saturated thickness of the aquifer under a unit hydraulic gradient. It combines the hydraulic conductivity and aquifer thickness.
$$ T = K \cdot b $$

where \( K \) = hydraulic conductivity (m/day or m/s) and \( b \) = saturated thickness of the aquifer (m). Units of \( T \) are m²/day or m²/s.

6. Permeability

DEFINITION: Permeability is the ability of a soil or rock formation to transmit fluids through its pore spaces. It defines how easily water can flow through the material.

Permeability depends on the size, shape, and interconnection of pore spaces. It is distinct from porosity — a material can have high porosity but low permeability (e.g., clay has many tiny pores that resist flow). Key comparisons:

  • Clay: High porosity (40–70%) but very low permeability — holds water but does not let it flow readily.
  • Sand/Gravel: Moderate-to-high porosity with high permeability — water flows easily through the interconnected pore spaces.

7. Geological Structures and Rock Joint Fractures

Features such as rock structures, faults, and fractures significantly impact groundwater flow paths. Fractures in rock serve as conduits for water movement, particularly in otherwise impermeable formations like granite. In fractured rock aquifers, the majority of flow occurs through the fracture network rather than through the rock matrix itself.

  • Faults: Can act as barriers (if filled with gouge material) or conduits for groundwater flow.
  • Rock Joints and Fractures: Open fractures increase the effective permeability of rock formations, allowing groundwater to flow through otherwise impermeable rock.
  • Texture & Grain Size: Coarser materials are more permeable. Well-sorted sediments have higher permeability than poorly sorted ones (fine particles fill voids between coarse grains, reducing permeability).

8. Aquifer Heterogeneity and Anisotropy

  • Homogeneous Aquifer: Hydraulic conductivity is the same at every point in the aquifer.
  • Heterogeneous Aquifer: Hydraulic conductivity varies from point to point within the aquifer.
  • Isotropic Aquifer: Hydraulic conductivity is the same in all directions at a given point.
  • Anisotropic Aquifer: Hydraulic conductivity varies with direction at a given point. In sedimentary deposits, horizontal conductivity (\(K_h\)) is typically much greater than vertical conductivity (\(K_v\)) because of the layered nature of deposition. A ratio of \(K_h/K_v\) = 3 to 10 is common; in some layered deposits it can exceed 100.

1.7 CHARACTERISTICS OF GROUNDWATER: COMPARISON WITH SURFACE WATER

General Characteristics of Groundwater

  • Slow Movement: Groundwater moves very slowly (mm/day to m/day) through pore spaces, in contrast to surface water which travels at m/s.
  • Stable Temperature: Groundwater maintains a nearly constant temperature throughout the year, approximately equal to the mean annual air temperature of the region.
  • Natural Filtration: As water percolates through soil and rock, it is naturally filtered, making groundwater generally purer than surface water in terms of suspended solids and pathogens.
  • Quality Variations: Despite natural filtration, groundwater quality varies with local geology and potential contaminants. Dissolved minerals, pH, and hardness vary significantly by location.

1.8 GROUNDWATER BASINS

A groundwater basin is a physiographic unit (a subsurface body of permeable rock or sediment) that contains one or more connected aquifers that share a common source of recharge and a common area of discharge. It is analogous to a surface-water river basin, except its boundaries are defined by geological structures (impervious formations, faults, folds) rather than topographic divides alone.

Key characteristics of a groundwater basin:

  • It includes a recharge area (where precipitation infiltrates and percolates to the water table, e.g., permeable outcrop areas, river beds, irrigated fields).
  • It has a storage body consisting of one or more aquifers.
  • It has a discharge area where groundwater exits naturally (springs, seepage into rivers and wetlands) or artificially (wells).
  • Major groundwater basins in Nepal include the Kathmandu Valley Basin (lacustrine sediments up to 500 m thick), the Inner Terai Basins (Dang, Chitwan, Surkhet), and the Terai Plain (thick alluvial deposits of the Gangetic plain extending into Nepal).
