CHAPTER 9 — NUMERICALS
Groundwater Resources of Nepal

2 Problems — Confined Aquifer Storage & Well-Field Planning

KEY FORMULAE — CHAPTER 9

A. Annual Groundwater Storage Volume:

$$ \boxed{V = S \times A \times \Delta h} $$
  • \(S\) = storage coefficient (dimensionless)
  • \(A\) = aquifer plan area (m²)
  • \(\Delta h\) = annual fluctuation of piezometric surface (m)

B. Number of Wells:

$$ \boxed{n = \frac{V}{q \times t}} $$
  • \(q\) = average yield per well (m³/hr)
  • \(t\) = annual pumping hours = pumping days × 24 hr/day

Physical meaning: The storage coefficient \(S\) for a confined aquifer represents the volume of water released per unit area per unit decline in head. It is very small (10−4 to 10−3) because water is released only by elastic compression of the aquifer skeleton and expansion of water.

SymbolTypical RangeSI Unit
\(S\) (confined)10−4–10−3dimensionless
\(S_y\) (unconfined)0.01–0.30dimensionless
\(A\) (plan area)10–10,000 km²m²
\(\Delta h\) (annual fluctuation)2–15 mm
\(q\) (well yield)10–50 m³/hrm³/hr

WHY IS CONFINED AQUIFER STORAGE SO SMALL?

When piezometric pressure drops in a confined aquifer, water is released by two mechanisms:

  • Compression of the aquifer skeleton: The effective stress increases, compacting the soil matrix slightly. This squeezes out water (~95% of the released volume).
  • Expansion of water: Lower pressure allows water to expand slightly (~5%).

Both mechanisms are elastic and produce very small volumes. In contrast, an unconfined aquifer releases water by actual draining of pore spaces (gravity drainage), which is 100-1000× more efficient. Hence \(S_y \gg S\).

SOLUTION METHODOLOGY — Storage & Well-Field Planning

  1. Convert area to m²: \(A_{m^2} = A_{km^2} \times 10^6\)
  2. Compute storage volume: \(V = S \times A \times \Delta h\)
  3. Compute annual yield per well: \(q_{annual} = q \times 24 \times \text{pumping days}\)
  4. Number of wells: \(n = V / q_{annual}\)
The piezometric surface fluctuates annually between wet and dry seasons. The volume V = S × A × Δh represents the recoverable groundwater storage.
Problem 20: Groundwater Storage — Pokhara 2072 Ashwin Q3 — 8 marks

Question: In Pokhara, a confined aquifer of 30 m average thickness extends over 800 km². The piezometric surface fluctuates annually from 19 m to 9 m above the top of the aquifer. S = 0.0008. What groundwater storage can be expected annually? With average well yield 30 m³/hr and 200 days pumping, how many wells can be drilled?

GIVEN DATA

Aquifer thickness30 m
Plan area, \(A\)800 km² = \(800 \times 10^6\) m²
Piezometric fluctuation19 m to 9 m ⇒ \(\Delta h = 10\) m
Storage coefficient, \(S\)0.0008
Well yield, \(q\)30 m³/hr
Pumping days200 days/year

SOLUTION

Step 1 — Annual groundwater storage. The recoverable volume is the product of storage coefficient, area, and the annual range of piezometric surface fluctuation:

$$ V = S \times A \times \Delta h = 0.0008 \times 800 \times 10^6 \times 10 $$

Breakdown: \(0.0008 \times 8 \times 10^8 = 6.4 \times 10^5\) per m of Δh; multiplied by 10 m:

$$ = 0.0008 \times 8 \times 10^9 = 6.4 \times 10^6 \text{ m}^3 $$
\( V = 6.4 \times 10^6 \text{ m}^3 = 6.4 \text{ million m}^3/\text{year} \)

Step 2 — Annual yield per well. Each well operates continuously (24 hr/day) for 200 days:

$$ q_{annual} = 30 \text{ m}^3/\text{hr} \times 24 \text{ hr/day} \times 200 \text{ days} = 30 \times 4800 = 144{,}000 \text{ m}^3/\text{year per well} $$

Step 3 — Number of wells.

$$ n = \frac{V}{q_{annual}} = \frac{6{,}400{,}000}{144{,}000} $$
\( n = 44.4 \approx \mathbf{44 \text{ wells}} \)
Physical insight: 800 km² is a vast area (roughly 28 km × 28 km), yet only 6.4 million m³ of recoverable storage per year — this is because S = 0.0008 is extremely small (confined artesian storage). In contrast:

WHAT IF THE AQUIFER WERE UNCONFINED?

If the same aquifer were unconfined with specific yield \(S_y = 0.15\):

$$ V_{unconfined} = 0.15 \times 800 \times 10^6 \times 10 = 1.2 \times 10^9 \text{ m}^3 $$

That is 187 times more water! This illustrates why confined aquifers, while capable of artesian flow, are not volumetrically rich. Their advantage lies in pressure-driven flow (artesian wells that flow without pumping) and protection from surface contamination.

Problem 21: Groundwater Storage — Janakpur 2072 Magh Q3 — 8 marks

Question: In Janakpur, a confined aquifer of 20 m average thickness extends over 850 km². Water table fluctuates from 15 m to 10 m above the top. S = 0.0009. What is the annual groundwater storage? With well yield of 30 m³/hr and 210 pumping days, how many wells can be drilled?

GIVEN DATA

Aquifer thickness20 m
Plan area, \(A\)850 km² = \(850 \times 10^6\) m²
Piezometric fluctuation15 m to 10 m ⇒ \(\Delta h = 5\) m
Storage coefficient, \(S\)0.0009
Well yield, \(q\)30 m³/hr
Pumping days210 days/year

SOLUTION

Step 1 — Annual groundwater storage.

$$ V = S \times A \times \Delta h = 0.0009 \times 850 \times 10^6 \times 5 $$

Breakdown: \(A \times \Delta h = 850 \times 10^6 \times 5 = 4.25 \times 10^9\) m³ of aquifer volume affected

$$ V = 0.0009 \times 4.25 \times 10^9 $$
\( V = 3.825 \times 10^6 \text{ m}^3 = 3.825 \text{ million m}^3/\text{year} \)

Step 2 — Annual yield per well. Each well operates 24 hr/day for 210 days:

$$ q_{annual} = 30 \times 24 \times 210 = 30 \times 5040 = 151{,}200 \text{ m}^3/\text{year per well} $$

Step 3 — Number of wells.

$$ n = \frac{3{,}825{,}000}{151{,}200} $$
\( n = 25.3 \approx \mathbf{25 \text{ wells}} \)
Comparison with Problem 20 (Pokhara): Despite Janakpur having a slightly larger area (850 vs 800 km²) and higher S (0.0009 vs 0.0008), its smaller Δh (5 vs 10 m) halves the recoverable storage. The dominant factor is piezometric fluctuation Δh, not area or S.
Pokhara has twice the recoverable storage primarily because Δh is twice as large. Area and S have minor effects.
