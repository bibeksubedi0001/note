Pumping Test & Aquifer Properties

4 Problems — Cooper-Jacob Time-Drawdown & Distance-Drawdown Methods

KEY FORMULAE — CHAPTER 5

A. Theis Equation (general transient well flow):

$$ s = \frac{Q}{4\pi T}\,W(u), \qquad u = \frac{r^2 S}{4Tt} $$

B. Cooper-Jacob Approximation (valid for small u, i.e., large t or small r):

$$ s = \frac{2.303\,Q}{4\pi\,T}\,\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{2.25\,T\,t}{r^2\,S}\right) $$

C. Cooper-Jacob Time-Drawdown Method (plot s vs log10 t):

$$ \boxed{T = \frac{2.303\,Q}{4\pi\,\Delta s}}, \qquad \boxed{S = \frac{2.25\,T\,t_0}{r^2}} $$
  • \(\Delta s\) = drawdown per log cycle of time (slope of the straight line)
  • \(t_0\) = time at which the straight line intersects \(s = 0\)

D. Thiem Distance-Drawdown Method (steady state, plot s vs log10 r):

$$ \boxed{T = \frac{2.303\,Q}{2\pi\,\Delta s}}, \qquad R_0 = r \text{ where } s = 0 $$
  • \(\Delta s\) = drawdown per log cycle of distance, \(R_0\) = radius of influence

DERIVATION HINT — Cooper-Jacob Approximation

The Theis well function \(W(u)\) is an infinite series: \(W(u) = -0.5772 - \ln u + u - u^2/4 + \ldots\). When \(u < 0.01\) (which happens for large \(t\) or small \(r\)), terms beyond \(-\ln u\) become negligible. Substituting \(u = r^2S/(4Tt)\):

$$ s \approx \frac{Q}{4\pi T}\left(-0.5772 - \ln\frac{r^2 S}{4Tt}\right) = \frac{2.303\,Q}{4\pi T}\,\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{2.25Tt}{r^2 S}\right) $$

This is a semi-log straight line when plotting s vs log(t). The slope = \(\Delta s\) per log cycle = \(2.303Q/(4\pi T)\), from which T can be extracted.

COOPER-JACOB VALIDITY CHECK

The approximation requires \(u = r^2S/(4Tt) < 0.01\). Check after finding T and S:

  • If \(u < 0.01\) for all data points → all points should fall on a straight line
  • If early-time points deviate from the line → they have \(u > 0.01\) and should be excluded from fitting
  • In practice, the method is very robust and widely used in hydrogeology

STEP-BY-STEP METHOD — Cooper-Jacob Time-Drawdown

  1. Plot s (y-axis, linear) vs t (x-axis, log scale) on semi-log paper
  2. Fit a best-fit straight line through the late-time data points
  3. Read Δs = drawdown difference over one log cycle of time
  4. Compute \(T = 2.303Q/(4\pi\Delta s)\)
  5. Extend line to s = 0, read the time intercept t0
  6. Compute \(S = 2.25Tt_0/r^2\)
Left: Cooper-Jacob time-drawdown semi-log plot. Right: Thiem distance-drawdown semi-log plot. The slope gives T; the zero intercept gives S or R0.
Problem 13: Cooper-Jacob Time-Drawdown Method 2078 Chaitra Q5(b) — 6 marks

Question: A pumping test in a confined aquifer (Q = 1000 m³/day) has an observation well at r = 100 m. Use the Cooper-Jacob method to find T and S from the following data.

GIVEN DATA

Pumping rate, \(Q\)1000 m³/day
Observation well distance, \(r\)100 m
t (days)0.0010.0050.010.050.10.51510
s (m)0.0830.1960.2490.3760.4310.5590.6140.7420.797
Semi-log plot of drawdown vs time. The data forms a straight line whose slope and intercept yield T and S.

SOLUTION

Step 1 — Plot s vs log10(t) and fit a straight line. The data is remarkably linear from the start, confirming the Cooper-Jacob approximation is valid.

Step 2 — Determine Δs per log cycle. Reading from the data:

  • t = 0.01 → s = 0.249 m
  • t = 0.1 → s = 0.431 m (one log cycle later)
  • \(\Delta s = 0.431 - 0.249 = 0.182\) m

Cross-check: t = 0.1 → 0.431, t = 1 → 0.614: \(\Delta s = 0.183\) m. ✓

Average \(\Delta s \approx 0.183\) m per log cycle.

Step 3 — Compute Transmissivity T.

$$ T = \frac{2.303\,Q}{4\pi\,\Delta s} = \frac{2.303 \times 1000}{4\pi \times 0.183} = \frac{2303}{2.300} $$
\( T \approx 1001 \text{ m}^2/\text{day} \approx 1000 \text{ m}^2/\text{day} \)

Step 4 — Find t0 (where s = 0 on the line).

Using the line equation from point (t = 1, s = 0.614):

$$ s = \Delta s \times [\log_{10}(t) - \log_{10}(t_0)] $$
$$ 0.614 = 0.183 \times [0 - \log_{10}(t_0)] \quad \Rightarrow \quad \log_{10}(t_0) = -\frac{0.614}{0.183} = -3.355 $$
$$ t_0 = 10^{-3.355} = 4.42 \times 10^{-4} \text{ days} $$

Step 5 — Compute Storage Coefficient S.

$$ S = \frac{2.25\,T\,t_0}{r^2} = \frac{2.25 \times 1000 \times 4.42 \times 10^{-4}}{100^2} = \frac{0.9945}{10000} $$
\( S \approx 1.0 \times 10^{-4} \)
Physical insight: S = 10−4 is typical of a confined aquifer (artesian storage). It means that a 1 m drop in piezometric head over 1 m² releases only 0.1 litre of water — all from elastic compression of the aquifer skeleton and water expansion. T = 1000 m²/day indicates a very transmissive aquifer (good for production wells).
Cooper-Jacob validity check: For the earliest data point (t = 0.001 d, r = 100 m): \(u = r^2S/(4Tt) = 100^2 \times 10^{-4}/(4 \times 1000 \times 0.001) = 1/4 = 0.25\). This is > 0.01, so strictly the earliest point should not be fitted. However, the data still forms a good line because the deviation is small for practical purposes.

Problem 16: Distance-Drawdown Method (Multiple Wells) 2071 Bhadra Q8 — 10 marks

Question: Steady-state discharge Q = 0.025 m³/s. Data from multiple observation wells in a confined aquifer. Calculate radius of influence and transmissivity.

GIVEN DATA

Discharge, \(Q\)0.025 m³/s
r (m)73060100160200
s (m)1.300.950.710.5350.360.29
Distance-drawdown semi-log plot. The straight line extends to s = 0 at R0 (radius of influence).

SOLUTION

Step 1 — Plot s vs log10(r). The drawdown decreases with distance (as expected — water further from the well is less affected). On a semi-log plot with linear s-axis and log r-axis, the data forms a straight line.

Step 2 — Determine Δs per log cycle of r.

Reading the best-fit line between r = 10 (interpolated s ≈ 1.21) and r = 100 (s = 0.535):

$$ \Delta s \approx 1.21 - 0.535 = 0.68 \text{ m per log cycle} $$

Cross-check with another log cycle: from r = 100 (s = 0.535) to r = 1000 (extrapolated s ≈ 0, where the line reaches zero). This gives Δs ≈ 0.535/0.787 cycles ≈ 0.68 m/cycle. ✓

Important difference from time-drawdown: In the distance-drawdown method, the formula for T has \(2\pi\) in the denominator (not \(4\pi\)) because we use the Thiem steady-state equation, not the Cooper-Jacob transient approximation.

Step 3 — Compute Transmissivity.

$$ T = \frac{2.303\,Q}{2\pi\,\Delta s} = \frac{2.303 \times 0.025}{2\pi \times 0.68} = \frac{0.05758}{4.273} $$
\( T = 0.01348 \text{ m}^2/\text{s} = 1164 \text{ m}^2/\text{day} \approx 1.35 \times 10^{-2} \text{ m}^2/\text{s} \)

Step 4 — Find Radius of Influence R0.

Extend the straight line to s = 0. Using the line equation from (r = 100, s = 0.535):

$$ s = \Delta s \times [\log_{10}(R_0) - \log_{10}(r)] $$

At \(s = 0\), using the known point (r = 100, s = 0.535):

$$ 0 = 0.535 - 0.68 \times [\log_{10}(R_0) - 2] $$
$$ \log_{10}(R_0) = 2 + \frac{0.535}{0.68} = 2 + 0.787 = 2.787 $$
\( R_0 = 10^{2.787} = 613 \text{ m} \approx 600 \text{ m} \)
Physical insight: The radius of influence (~600 m) tells us that beyond this distance, the aquifer is essentially unaffected by pumping. This is critical for:
  • Well spacing: Another well should be placed >600 m away to avoid interference
  • Contamination assessment: A contaminant source within 600 m could potentially reach the pumping well
  • Aquifer yield: T ≈ 1165 m²/day indicates a very transmissive aquifer (consistent with coarse alluvium or karstified limestone)
