2. Centrifugal Pump Asked ALL 5 exams

2.1 Components

  1. Impeller — rotating vaned disc; heart of pump
  2. Volute casing — spiral-shaped housing; converts KE → pressure energy
  3. Suction pipe + foot valve + strainer — prevents backflow & debris entry
  4. Delivery pipe — carries water to destination
  5. Shaft & bearings — connects impeller to motor
  6. Stuffing box / mechanical seal — prevents leakage along shaft

2.2 Working Principle (6 Steps — write all in exam)

  1. Priming: Pump casing & suction pipe filled with water (to remove air)
  2. Impeller rotation: Motor drives impeller at high speed
  3. Centrifugal action: Water at impeller eye thrown outward by centrifugal force
  4. Low pressure at eye: Partial vacuum created → atmospheric pressure pushes water up suction pipe
  5. Volute action: Expanding volute casing converts kinetic energy to pressure energy
  6. Continuous delivery: Water discharged through delivery pipe at high pressure

2.3 Types of Centrifugal Pumps

TypeFeatureUse
Volute pumpSpiral casing; KE→PE conversionGeneral surface pumping
Turbine (diffuser)Guide vanes instead of volute; vertical shaftDeep-well vertical turbine pump
MultistageMultiple impellers in series; Total H = H/stage × NHigh-head applications

2.4 Advantages & Limitations

Advantages:
  • Simple construction
  • Continuous, uniform flow
  • High efficiency (60–85%)
  • Low maintenance
  • Can handle large discharge
Limitations:
  • Needs priming before start
  • Suction lift limited to 6–7 m
  • Cavitation risk at low NPSH
  • Cannot pump viscous fluids well
  • Efficiency drops at off-design Q
Cavitation: Occurs when pressure drops below vapor pressure → vapor bubbles form and collapse violently → damages impeller. Prevention: Ensure NPSHavailable > NPSHrequired; minimize suction lift; use smooth suction pipe.

3. Submersible Pump 2073 Bhadra 2 marks

  • Motor + pump unit sealed and submerged inside the well below water level
  • Multistage (5–30 stages) centrifugal impellers stacked on common shaft
  • Motor cooled by surrounding groundwater flow
  • No priming needed (always submerged)
  • Can pump from any depth (no suction limit)
Advantages: No priming, no suction limit, compact, quiet, freeze-proof, suitable for deep wells
Limitations: Higher initial cost, difficult to repair (must pull entire unit), dry running burns motor, sand-sensitive

4. Jet Pump (Ejector) 2072 Ashwin 2 marks

  • Combines surface centrifugal pump with down-well nozzle-diffuser assembly
  • Working: Pump sends water down pressure pipe → nozzle creates high-velocity jet → low pressure zone draws GW via Venturi effect → diffuser recovers pressure → mixed water returns up
  • Types: Single-pipe (shallow, ≤25 ft) vs. Double-pipe (deep, 25–110 ft)
  • Efficiency: 25–40% (low); but good for abrasive/corrosive water and small-diameter boreholes
  • No moving parts in well → easy maintenance

5. Airlift Pump

  • Compressed air injected into riser pipe submerged in well
  • Air-water mixture is lighter than surrounding water → buoyancy lifts mixture up the riser
  • Submergence ratio: 50–70%; efficiency: 20–35%
  • Advantages: No moving parts in well, handles sandy/muddy water, good for well development
  • Limitations: Low efficiency, needs compressor, limited lift height

6. Reciprocating Pump (Hand Pump)

  • Suction stroke: Piston moves up → vacuum → suction valve opens → water enters cylinder
  • Delivery stroke: Piston moves down → suction valve closes → delivery valve opens → water pushed up

8. Factors for Pump Selection 2071 Bhadra 4 marks

  1. Well characteristics: Type (shallow/deep), depth, diameter, SWL, drawdown
  2. Discharge & TDH: Required Q (m³/hr); Total Dynamic Head = static lift + friction + velocity head + drawdown
  3. Power source: Electricity, diesel, solar, manual availability
  4. Water quality: Sand content, corrosiveness, temperature
  5. Economics: Initial cost + operating cost + maintenance cost + life-cycle cost
  6. Reliability & serviceability: Local repair capacity, spare parts, VLOM design

9. Key Formulas

FormulaMeaning
$TDH = h_s + h_d + h_f + h_v$Total Dynamic Head (static suction + delivery + friction + velocity head)
$P_{water} = \frac{\gamma Q H}{1000}$ kWWater power (γ = 9.81 kN/m³)
$P_{brake} = \frac{P_{water}}{\eta_{pump}}$Brake/shaft power
$P_{motor} = \frac{P_{brake}}{\eta_{motor}}$Motor input power
$\eta_{overall} = \eta_{pump} \times \eta_{motor}$Overall efficiency
