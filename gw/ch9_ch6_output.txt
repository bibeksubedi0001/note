What is Groundwater & Why it matters (quick background)
●
Groundwater = water stored below ground in aquifers (saturated zone). It 
complements surface water.
●
In Nepal, groundwater supports drinking water, irrigation, and industry,especially 
in Terai and urban centres.
●
In hilly and mountainous areas, springs are often the primary local groundwater 
supply. 
source:USGC.gov

Hydrogeological regions of Nepal
●
Terai and Inner Terai: thick alluvial aquifers (sand, gravel) — high groundwater 
yield; widely used for irrigation and domestic water supply.
●
Hills and Mountains: fractured bedrock and springs dominate; groundwater 
occurrence is more local and patchy.
●
Kathmandu Valley: a bowl-shaped alluvial basin with shallow and deeper aquifers; 
intensive urban extraction has led to declining water tables and pollution risks.

 Uses & socio-economic importance
●
Domestic water supply in rural and urban areas (handpumps, tube wells, dug wells,

●
Recharge is driven by rainfall (highly seasonal) and stream–aquifer 
interactions; monsoon rains are key for recharge.
●
Terai aquifers generally have high storage potential; Kathmandu’s aquifer is 
limited and vulnerable to over-extraction.

industrial effluents that affect shallow wells.

Terai aquifer:
The terai aquifer is the part of  Indo-Gangetic Basin (IGB), one of the world's largest 
groundwater reservoirs, extending across Nepal, India, Bangladesh, and Pakistan. 
     It can be considered as the groundwater power 
house of Nepal which contributes in the agriculture,

Features of Terai aquifer:
●
It is mainly composed unconsolidated alluvial deposits having the alternatives 
layers of sand, silt and clay.
●
The thickness of terai aquifer increases from 50-100m near the foothills to 
500m near some area of indian border.
●
The terai aquifer is classified primarily unconfined to semi-confined as water 
is stored in between thick sand and gravel layer makes unconfined  , while 
clay layer is also present which resist the flow of water makes confined.

Introduction
Groundwater
Groundwater is the water found underground in the cracks and spaces in soil, sand, and rock. It is stored in and moves slowly through geologic formations of soil, sand, and rocks called aquifers.
It represents about 30% of the world's fresh water (with nearly 69% locked in ice caps).
Why to Explore ?
Groundwater is an invisible water resource. Unlike a river or lake, we cannot see it directly. Exploration is the systematic process of investigating the hydrogeological properties of an area to locate water.
Risk Mitigation: Drilling is expensive. Blind drilling has a high failure rate.
Sustainability: Ensuring the withdrawal rate does not exceed the recharge rate.
Health: Avoiding contaminated zones or saline intrusions.

Objectives
Groundwater Exploration is the systematic investigation of subsurface formations to locate, evaluate, and manage groundwater resources sustainably.
Objectives of Groundwater Exploration
The What and Why before the How
To locate potential groundwater-bearing formations (aquifers)
To estimate the quantity of groundwater available
To assess the quality of groundwater
To determine the hydrogeological properties
To select suitable sites & depths for wells, tube wells and boreholes
To evaluate the groundwater fluctuation and behavior
To support planning and management of groundwater resources
To identify the zones suitable for artificial recharge and groundwater conservation measures

Types of groundwater exploration:
6.2.1  Surface exploration of groundwater:
The study of surface features such as geology, landforms, drainage, and vegetation
Indirectly identify potential groundwater zones without digging or drilling.
Easy to operate and implement.
Suitable for preliminary surveys.
6.2.2  Subsurface exploration groundwater:
The investigation below the ground surface using geophysical logging and drilling.
Directly locate, confirm, and evaluate groundwater presence and depth.
Very expensive and complex to implement.
Done for government level projects where large scale investigations are carried out to ascertain the results of surface surveys.

6.2.1 Surface exploration of groundwater
a. Geological methods:
The groundwater occurrence and movement mainly depends on the geology of the area.
Relies on study of rock types, geological structures, and stratigraphy
A geologic investigation begins with the collection, analysis, and hydrogeologic interpretation of existing topographic maps, aerial photographs, geologic maps and logs, and other pertinent records.
Only after the geological investigations, the type of geophysical method to be conducted may be decided.
b. Geophysical exploration
The scientific measurement of physical properties of the earth’s crust for investigation of mineral deposits or geologic structure.
Density, magnetism, elasticity, and electrical resistivity are properties most commonly measured.

Principles of geophysical methods
Electrical resistivity method:
Locate groundwater by measuring variations in subsurface electrical resistivity caused by differences in moisture content and rock type.
Seismic refraction method:
Based on the propagation of elastic (seismic) waves through the subsurface, where wave velocity depends on the density and elastic properties of earth materials.
Gravity method:
Based on variations in the Earth’s gravitational field caused by differences in density of subsurface materials.
Magnetic method:
Based on variations in the Earth’s magnetic field caused by differences in magnetic properties of subsurface rocks.
--- SLIDE 18 ---
6.2.1 Surface exploration of groundwater
c. Geomorphological method:
Involves the study of landforms and surface features to identify areas favorable for groundwater occurrence.
Different geomorphic units such as valleys, flood plains, pediments, terraces, buried channels, and alluvial plains influence infiltration, storage, and movement of groundwater.
Relies on topographic maps, aerial photographs, satellite imagery, and field observations
d. Hydrogeological method:
Based on the study of geological formations, aquifer characteristics, and groundwater conditions.
Includes analysis of existing wells, springs, pumping test data.
Used after geological and geomorphological studies.

Traditional Method
The scientific perspective:
There is no scientific basis for the claims that dowsing can reliably locate underground water and studies show it’s not more effective than random chance.
Why it persists:
Ideomoter effect: It explains how dowsers may unconsciously or due to involuntary  muscle movements influence their tools based on the  subtle environmental clues, such as vegetation, terrain features etc.
High Water Tables: In many areas, groundwater is ubiquitous; drilling anywhere yields water, reinforcing the "success."

Geological Method in Groundwater Exploration
Geological method means studying rocks, soil, land shape and structure to find where groundwater is available.
Advantages of Geological Methods:
Easy, Simple and low cost.
Useful before drilling wells.
Gives overall idea of groundwater.
Disadvantages of Geological Methods.
Cannot give accurate depth of water.
Indirect and Less accurate Method without Geophysical Methods.
--- SLIDE 27 ---
Activities under Geological Method
i.  Lithological Study ( Study of rocks)
Sand, gravel, limestone - - - > Store water well
Clay, hard rock - - - > Store little water
Search for porous and permeable rock.
ii.  Structural Study
Cracks, joints and fault - - - > allow water to move and collect.
More cracks - - - > more groundwater chance.
iii.  Study of Weathering
Weathered zones - - - > contain more groundwater.
Thick weathered layer- - - > better water yield.
--- SLIDE 28 ---
Activities under Geological Method
iv.  Landform Study
River plains & valley - - - > good groundwater.
Hills & mountains - - - > poor groundwater.
v.  Hydrogeological Study
Estimates water table depth
Shows Water movement: Water flows from recharge areas ( where rain enters) to discharge areas ( like rivers/ springs ) .
Marks good spots: Creates maps with zones- high, medium and low chances of finding water.
vi.  Geological Mapping
Prepare maps showing rock types, structure, weathered zone and landforms and water features.
Helps in identifying suitable sites for wells or boreholes and planning groundwater development.
--- SLIDE 29 ---
Geophysical Methods
Geophysical methods are techniques used to explore groundwater by measuring physical properties of subsurface materials.
Advantages of Geophysical methods:
Non-destructive method ( no digging or drilling method)
Large area can be covered in short time.
Can be combined with geological methods for better accuracy.
It is cheaper, simple and environmental friendly method.
Disadvantages of Geophysical Method:
Results are indirect and need interpretation.
Final confirmation still requires drilling.
Site-dependent: Not all methods work everywhere. (eg some methods fail in salty soil, very dry areas or rocky ground).
