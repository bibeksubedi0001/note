1. Explain in brief hydrological cycle focusing on components of subsurface movement of water.
[4]

Hydrological Cycle

The hydrological cycle (water cycle) is the continuous circulation of water within the Earth's hydrosphere through the processes of evaporation, condensation, precipitation, infiltration, runoff, and subsurface flow. It is a closed system driven primarily by solar energy and gravity.

Components of Subsurface Movement of Water

1. Infiltration

Water enters the soil through pores and cracks at the ground surface. Rate depends on soil type, moisture content, and rainfall intensity.

2. Soil Moisture Zone (Vadose Zone)

Contains both air and water in pore spaces. Comprises three sub-zones:

  • Soil Water Zone: Root zone; water held by capillary and adhesive forces
  • Intermediate Vadose Zone: Water percolates downward under gravity
  • Capillary Fringe: Above water table; nearly saturated under negative pressure

3. Percolation

Downward movement of water through vadose zone under gravity to the water table.

4. Groundwater Flow (Saturated Zone)

Below the water table, all pores are saturated. Water flows from higher to lower hydraulic head (Darcy's Law), eventually discharging as baseflow into rivers, springs, or ocean.

5. Baseflow (Groundwater Discharge)

Groundwater seeping into surface water bodies. Sustains rivers during dry seasons.

2. a) Explain Darcy's law for groundwater flow? Derive 3-D form of it and also explain its validity.
[8]

Darcy's Law

Statement: The discharge through a porous medium is proportional to the cross-sectional area and hydraulic gradient.

Mathematical Expression (1-D)

$$Q = -KA\frac{dh}{dl}$$

Where:

  • $Q$ = Discharge (volume flow rate) [m³/s]
  • $K$ = Hydraulic conductivity [m/s]
  • $A$ = Cross-sectional area perpendicular to flow [m²]
  • $\frac{dh}{dl}$ = Hydraulic gradient (change in head per unit length) [dimensionless]
  • The negative sign indicates flow is in the direction of decreasing head

The specific discharge (Darcy velocity) is:

$$q = \frac{Q}{A} = -K\frac{dh}{dl}$$

Derivation of 3-D Form of Darcy's Law (General Groundwater Flow Equation)

Step 1: 3-D Darcy's Law Components

In a three-dimensional porous medium, groundwater flows simultaneously in all three coordinate directions ($x$, $y$, $z$). Applying Darcy's law independently in each direction for a heterogeneous, anisotropic medium:

$$q_x = -K_x \frac{\partial h}{\partial x}, \quad q_y = -K_y \frac{\partial h}{\partial y}, \quad q_z = -K_z \frac{\partial h}{\partial z}$$

where $K_x$, $K_y$, $K_z$ are the hydraulic conductivities in the $x$, $y$, $z$ directions respectively, and $h$ is the hydraulic head at any point $(x, y, z)$ at time $t$.

Step 2: Control Volume and Mass Balance

Consider an elementary control volume (REV — Representative Elementary Volume) of dimensions $dx \cdot dy \cdot dz$ within a saturated porous medium. We apply the principle of conservation of mass:

Mass Balance: [Mass inflow rate] − [Mass outflow rate] = [Rate of change of mass storage within the control volume]

Step 3: Flow Analysis in Each Direction

In the x-direction:

The volume of water entering the left face (area $dy \cdot dz$) per unit time:

$$\text{Inflow}_x = q_x \cdot (dy \cdot dz)$$

The volume of water leaving the right face per unit time (using Taylor series expansion, retaining first-order terms):

$$\text{Outflow}_x = \left(q_x + \frac{\partial q_x}{\partial x} dx\right) \cdot (dy \cdot dz)$$

Net inflow in x-direction:

$$\Delta Q_x = \text{Inflow}_x - \text{Outflow}_x = -\frac{\partial q_x}{\partial x} dx \cdot dy \cdot dz$$

Similarly, in the y-direction:

$$\Delta Q_y = -\frac{\partial q_y}{\partial y} dx \cdot dy \cdot dz$$

And in the z-direction:

$$\Delta Q_z = -\frac{\partial q_z}{\partial z} dx \cdot dy \cdot dz$$

Step 4: Total Net Inflow

The total net volume of water flowing into the control volume per unit time:

$$\Delta Q_{total} = -\left(\frac{\partial q_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial q_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial q_z}{\partial z}\right) dx \cdot dy \cdot dz$$

Step 5: Rate of Change of Storage

The rate of change of water stored within the control volume is expressed using the specific storage $S_s$ (volume of water released from storage per unit volume of aquifer per unit decline in head):

$$\text{Rate of storage change} = S_s \frac{\partial h}{\partial t} \cdot (dx \cdot dy \cdot dz)$$

Step 6: Equation of Continuity

Equating net inflow to rate of storage change:

$$-\left(\frac{\partial q_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial q_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial q_z}{\partial z}\right) dx \cdot dy \cdot dz = S_s \frac{\partial h}{\partial t} \cdot dx \cdot dy \cdot dz$$

Dividing both sides by $dx \cdot dy \cdot dz$:

$$-\left(\frac{\partial q_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial q_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial q_z}{\partial z}\right) = S_s \frac{\partial h}{\partial t}$$

Step 7: Substituting Darcy's Law

Substituting $q_x = -K_x \frac{\partial h}{\partial x}$, $q_y = -K_y \frac{\partial h}{\partial y}$, $q_z = -K_z \frac{\partial h}{\partial z}$:

$$-\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(-K_x \frac{\partial h}{\partial x}\right) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(-K_y \frac{\partial h}{\partial y}\right) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\left(-K_z \frac{\partial h}{\partial z}\right) = S_s \frac{\partial h}{\partial t}$$

The negative signs cancel, giving the General 3-D Groundwater Flow Equation:

$$\boxed{\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(K_x \frac{\partial h}{\partial x}\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(K_y \frac{\partial h}{\partial y}\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\left(K_z \frac{\partial h}{\partial z}\right) = S_s \frac{\partial h}{\partial t}}$$

This is the general 3-D groundwater flow equation for a heterogeneous, anisotropic aquifer under transient conditions. It governs the spatial and temporal distribution of hydraulic head $h(x, y, z, t)$ throughout the aquifer.

Special Cases:

For a homogeneous, isotropic aquifer ($K_x = K_y = K_z = K$):

$$K\left(\frac{\partial^2 h}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 h}{\partial y^2} + \frac{\partial^2 h}{\partial z^2}\right) = S_s \frac{\partial h}{\partial t}$$

For steady-state flow ($\frac{\partial h}{\partial t} = 0$):

$$\frac{\partial^2 h}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 h}{\partial y^2} + \frac{\partial^2 h}{\partial z^2} = 0 \quad \text{(Laplace Equation)}$$

Validity of Darcy's Law

Darcy's law is essentially an application of Newton's law of viscosity to flow through porous media:

$$\tau = \mu \frac{du}{dy}$$

In laminar flow, viscous shear forces dominate. The linear relationship $q \propto \frac{dh}{dl}$ in Darcy's law arises because viscous resistance (governed by Newton's law) is proportional to velocity. Hence, Darcy's law is valid when viscous forces dominate over inertial forces.

1. Laminar Flow Condition

Valid only for laminar flow ($Re < 1$, applicable up to $Re \approx 10$):

$$Re = \frac{q \cdot d}{\nu}$$

where $q$ = specific discharge, $d$ = grain diameter, $\nu$ = kinematic viscosity.

2. Saturated Flow

Applies to fully saturated media. For unsaturated flow, requires modification (Richard's equation).

3. Incompressible, Homogeneous Fluid

Constant density and viscosity assumed. Needs modification for variable-density flow (saltwater intrusion).

4. Not Valid for Very Low Permeability

In clays, a threshold gradient may be required before flow begins.

Summary: Darcy's law (based on Newton's law of viscosity) is valid for laminar flow ($Re < 10$) through saturated, homogeneous porous media. Fails at high velocities (turbulent flow) and very low permeabilities (clays).
2. b) A confined aquifer with a horizontal bed has a varying thickness as shown. K = 12 + 0.006x, where x = 0 at section (1). Piezometric heads at sections (1) and (2) are 14.2 m and 18.8 m respectively. Determine the flow rate per unit width.
[8]

Given Data

  • Confined aquifer with horizontal bed
  • Hydraulic conductivity: $K = 12 + 0.006x$ (inhomogeneous), where $x = 0$ at section (1)
  • Thickness at section 1: $b_1 = 30$ m
  • Thickness at section 2: $b_2 = 75$ m
  • Piezometric head at section 1: $h_1 = 14.2$ m
  • Piezometric head at section 2: $h_2 = 18.8$ m
  • Distance between sections: $L = 3600$ m

Solution

Step 1: Express the varying thickness

The aquifer thickness varies linearly from $b_1 = 30$ m at $x = 0$ to $b_2 = 75$ m at $x = 3600$ m:

$$b(x) = b_1 + \frac{b_2 - b_1}{L} \cdot x = 30 + \frac{75 - 30}{3600} \cdot x = 30 + 0.0125x$$

Step 2: Apply Darcy's Law for the confined aquifer

For steady one-dimensional flow in a confined aquifer per unit width:

$$q = -K(x) \cdot b(x) \cdot \frac{dh}{dx} = -T(x) \cdot \frac{dh}{dx}$$

where $T(x) = K(x) \cdot b(x)$ is the transmissivity at position $x$.

Step 3: Transmissivity as a function of x

$$T(x) = K(x) \cdot b(x) = (12 + 0.006x)(30 + 0.0125x)$$

Expanding:

$$T(x) = 360 + 0.15x + 0.18x + 0.000075x^2 = 360 + 0.33x + 0.000075x^2$$

Step 4: Integrate using continuity

For steady flow, $q$ is constant along the flow path. Rearranging Darcy's law:

$$q = -T(x) \frac{dh}{dx} \implies dh = -\frac{q}{T(x)} dx$$

Integrating from section 1 to section 2:

$$\int_{h_1}^{h_2} dh = -q \int_0^{L} \frac{dx}{T(x)}$$
$$h_2 - h_1 = -q \int_0^{3600} \frac{dx}{360 + 0.33x + 0.000075x^2}$$

Step 5: Evaluate the integral and solve for q

Evaluating the integral $\displaystyle\int_0^{3600} \frac{dx}{360 + 0.33x + 0.000075x^2}$ using calculator:

$$\int_0^{3600} \frac{dx}{T(x)} = 3.7777$$

Substituting:

$$h_2 - h_1 = -q \times 3.7777$$
$$18.8 - 14.2 = -q \times 3.7777$$
$$q = -\frac{4.6}{3.7777} = -1.2176 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}$$

The negative sign indicates flow from section 2 (higher head) towards section 1 (lower head).

Result: Flow rate per unit width = 1.218 m²/s (from section 2 towards section 1).
3. a) Define flow net with neat sketch. What are the application of flow net in geotechnical engineering? Derive the expression for determining seepage discharge from flow net.
[3+3+4]

Definition of Flow Net

A flow net is a graphical representation of 2D groundwater flow consisting of:

  1. Flow lines: Lines along which water particles travel (direction of flow)
  2. Equipotential lines: Lines of equal hydraulic head (perpendicular to flow lines)

These curves intersect at right angles forming curvilinear squares.

Applications of Flow Net in Geotechnical Engineering

  1. Seepage discharge estimation: Calculating the quantity of water seeping through or under earth dams, levees, sheet piles, and retaining walls.
  2. Uplift pressure calculation: Determining the pore water pressure distribution at the base of hydraulic structures (dams, weirs) to check against uplift failure.
  3. Exit gradient determination: Finding the hydraulic gradient at the downstream exit point to check against piping (internal erosion) failure. Critical when exit gradient approaches the critical gradient $i_c = \frac{G_s - 1}{1 + e}$.
  4. Design of dewatering systems: Planning well-point systems and cut-off walls for excavation dewatering.
  5. Seepage force analysis: Evaluating seepage forces acting on soil particles, which affect slope stability and foundation design.
  6. Design of drainage systems: Designing filters, drains, and relief wells to safely control seepage in earth dams.

Derivation: Seepage Discharge from Flow Net

Assumptions

  • Flow is two-dimensional, steady, and laminar
  • The soil is homogeneous, isotropic, and saturated
  • Darcy's law is valid
  • Water is incompressible

Derivation

Consider a flow net with:

  • $N_f$ = Number of flow channels (spaces between adjacent flow lines)
  • $N_d$ = Number of equipotential drops (spaces between adjacent equipotential lines)
  • $H$ = Total head loss = $H_1 - H_2$ (difference between upstream and downstream heads)

Step 1: The total head loss is divided equally among the $N_d$ potential drops:

$$\Delta h = \frac{H}{N_d}$$

Step 2: Consider one elementary square (field) of the flow net with dimensions $a \times b$ (width $\times$ length along flow). For a curvilinear square, $a \approx b$.

Step 3: Apply Darcy's law to this single field. The discharge through one flow channel per unit width perpendicular to the flow plane:

$$\Delta q = K \cdot \frac{\Delta h}{b} \cdot a \cdot 1$$

Since $a \approx b$ (curvilinear square):

$$\Delta q = K \cdot \Delta h = K \cdot \frac{H}{N_d}$$

Step 4: The total seepage discharge through all $N_f$ flow channels:

$$q = N_f \cdot \Delta q = K \cdot H \cdot \frac{N_f}{N_d}$$

For a structure of length $L$ perpendicular to the cross-section:

$$Q = K \cdot H \cdot \frac{N_f}{N_d} \cdot L$$
Final Expression: $Q = K \cdot H \cdot \frac{N_f}{N_d} \cdot L$, where $K$ = hydraulic conductivity, $H$ = total head loss, $N_f$ = number of flow channels, $N_d$ = number of potential drops, and $L$ = length of structure.
3. b) From flownet analysis of a well pumping at 150,000 lpm at Rajbandhu Project: Number of flow channels = 30, Head drop between successive piezometric contours = 6 m, Thickness of aquifer = 30 m. Determine the transmissibility and permeability of the aquifer.
[6]

Given Data

  • Pumping rate: $Q = 150{,}000$ lpm $= \frac{150{,}000}{1000 \times 60} = 2.5$ m³/s
  • Number of flow channels: $N_f = 30$
  • Head drop between successive piezometric contours: $\Delta h = 6$ m
  • Thickness of aquifer: $b = 30$ m

Solution

Step 1: Discharge per flow channel

$$\Delta q = \frac{Q}{N_f} = \frac{2.5}{30} = 0.08333 \text{ m}^3/\text{s per flow channel}$$

Step 2: Determine Transmissivity (T)

$$\Delta q = T \cdot \Delta h$$
$$T = \frac{\Delta q}{\Delta h} = \frac{0.08333}{6} = 0.01389 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}$$
$$\boxed{T = 0.01389 \text{ m}^2/\text{s} = 1200 \text{ m}^2/\text{day}}$$

Step 3: Determine Permeability (K)

$$K = \frac{T}{b} = \frac{0.01389}{30} = 4.63 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m/s}$$
$$\boxed{K = 4.63 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m/s} = 40 \text{ m/day}}$$
Results:
Transmissivity, $T = 0.01389$ m²/s = 1200 m²/day
Permeability, $K = 4.63 \times 10^{-4}$ m/s = 40 m/day
4. a) A 30 cm well penetrates fully a homogeneous aquifer having a permeability of 40 m/day. The saturated thickness of the aquifer is 15 m. What is the drawdown in the well when pumped at the rate of 1,000 lpm? What will be the drawdown if only the bottom half of the aquifer is screened? Assume a radius of influence of 300 m.
[6]

Given Data

  • Well diameter = 30 cm, so radius of well: $r_w = 0.15$ m
  • Permeability: $K = 40$ m/day $= \frac{40}{86400} = 4.63 \times 10^{-4}$ m/s
  • Saturated thickness: $b = H = 15$ m
  • Pumping rate: $Q = 1000$ lpm $= \frac{1000}{1000 \times 60} = 0.01667$ m³/s
  • Radius of influence: $R = 300$ m

Method 1: Confined Aquifer Approach (Thiem's Equation)

Transmissivity:

$$T = K \cdot b = 4.63 \times 10^{-4} \times 15 = 6.944 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^2/\text{s}$$

Part (i): Fully Penetrating Well

$$Q = \frac{2\pi T \cdot s_w}{\ln(R/r_w)} \implies s_w = \frac{Q \cdot \ln(R/r_w)}{2\pi T}$$
$$s_w = \frac{0.01667 \times \ln(300/0.15)}{2\pi \times 6.944 \times 10^{-3}} = \frac{0.01667 \times 7.6009}{0.04363}$$
$$s_w = \boxed{2.904 \text{ m}}$$

Part (ii): Partially Penetrating (Bottom Half Screened)

Screen length: $l_s = b/2 = 7.5$ m, penetration ratio $\alpha = l_s/b = 0.5$

For a partially penetrating well, the drawdown is related to the fully penetrating drawdown by:

$$s_w' = s_w \times \frac{1}{\alpha}\left(1 + 7\sqrt{\frac{r_w}{2b}}\cos\frac{\pi\alpha}{2}\right)$$

Calculating the correction term:

$$7\sqrt{\frac{r_w}{2b}}\cos\frac{\pi\alpha}{2} = 7 \times \sqrt{\frac{0.15}{2 \times 15}} \times \cos\frac{\pi \times 0.5}{2}$$
$$= 7 \times \sqrt{0.005} \times \cos 45° = 7 \times 0.07071 \times 0.7071 = 0.350$$

Drawdown:

$$s_w' = 2.904 \times \frac{1}{0.5}\left(1 + 0.350\right) = 2.904 \times 2 \times 1.35$$
$$s_w' = 2.904 \times 2.70 = \boxed{7.841 \text{ m}}$$
Confined Results:
Fully penetrating: $s_w = $ 2.904 m
Partially penetrating: $s_w' = $ 7.841 m

Method 2: Unconfined Aquifer Approach (Dupuit's Equation)

Here $H = 15$ m is the initial saturated thickness.

Part (i): Fully Penetrating Well

$$H^2 - h_w^2 = \frac{2.3 Q}{\pi K} \log_{10} \frac{R}{r_w}$$
$$15^2 - h_w^2 = \frac{2.3 \times (1/60)}{\pi \times (40/86400)} \log_{10} \frac{300}{0.15}$$
$$225 - h_w^2 = \frac{0.03833}{1.4544 \times 10^{-3}} \times \log_{10}(2000) = 26.356 \times 3.3010 = 86.5$$
$$h_w^2 = 225 - 86.5 = 138.5 \implies h_w = 11.76 \text{ m}$$
$$s_w = H - h_w = 15 - 11.76 = \boxed{3.24 \text{ m}}$$

Part (ii): Partially Penetrating (Bottom Half Screened)

Length of well screen $= \frac{1}{2} \times 15 = 7.5$ m from the bottom of the aquifer.

$$\alpha = \frac{l}{h_w} = \frac{7.5}{11.76} \approx 0.637, \quad 1 - \alpha \approx 0.363$$

Additional drawdown due to partial penetration:

$$\Delta s_w \cdot 2H = \frac{2.3026 \times Q}{\pi K} \cdot \frac{(1 - \alpha)}{\alpha} \cdot \log_{10}\left(\frac{(1 - \alpha) \cdot l}{r_w}\right)$$
$$\Delta s_w \cdot 2H = \frac{2.3026 \times (1/60)}{\pi \times (40/86400)} \cdot \frac{0.363}{0.637} \cdot \log_{10}\left(\frac{0.363 \times 7.5}{0.15}\right)$$
$$= 26.40 \times 0.5699 \times \log_{10}(18.15) = 15.05 \times 1.259 \approx 18.9$$

Total $(H^2 - h_w'^2)$ for partially penetrating well:

$$(H^2 - h_w'^2)_{pp} = 86.5 + 18.9 = 105.4$$
$$15^2 - h_w'^2 = 105.4 \implies h_w'^2 = 119.6 \implies h_w' \approx 10.94 \text{ m}$$
$$s_{wp} = 15 - 10.94 = \boxed{4.06 \text{ m}}$$

Additional drawdown due to partial penetration:

$$\Delta s_w = 4.06 - 3.24 = \boxed{0.82 \text{ m}}$$
Unconfined Results:
Fully penetrating: $s_w = $ 3.24 m
Partially penetrating: $s_{wp} = $ 4.06 m
Additional drawdown: $\Delta s_w = $ 0.82 m

5. a) Write down step by step procedure to determine transmissivity (T) by Thiem's method with necessary mathematical expression.
[3]

Thiem's Method (Equilibrium Method)

A steady-state method to determine T of a confined aquifer using two observation wells.

Step-by-Step Procedure

Step 1: Pump at constant rate $Q$. Measure drawdowns $s_1$, $s_2$ at two observation wells at distances $r_1$, $r_2$ ($r_1 < r_2$) until steady state.

Step 2: Record $Q$, $s_1$, $s_2$, $r_1$, $r_2$.

Step 3: Apply Thiem's Equation

$$T = \frac{Q}{2\pi(s_1 - s_2)} \ln\left(\frac{r_2}{r_1}\right)$$

or equivalently:

$$T = \frac{2.303 \, Q}{2\pi(s_1 - s_2)} \log_{10}\left(\frac{r_2}{r_1}\right)$$

Step 4: Substitute measured values to compute $T$.

Step 5: Determine K

$$K = \frac{T}{b}$$

where $b$ = aquifer thickness.

Note: Thiem's method determines only $T$ (not $S$) — uses steady-state data. For $S$, use Theis or Cooper-Jacob.
5. b) Explain Cooper and Jacob method for the determination of storage coefficient and transmissivity of aquifer using time drawdown data.
[5]

Cooper-Jacob Straight-Line Method (Time-Drawdown)

The Cooper-Jacob method (1946) is a simplification of the Theis equation for determining T and S of a confined aquifer.

Theoretical Background

The Theis equation:

$$s = \frac{Q}{4\pi T} W(u), \quad u = \frac{r^2 S}{4Tt}$$

For small $u$ (i.e., $u < 0.01$), the well function is approximated as:

$$W(u) \approx 2.303 \log\frac{2.25Tt}{r^2 S}$$

Substituting:

$$s = \frac{2.303 \, Q}{4\pi T} \log\left(\frac{2.25Tt}{r^2 S}\right)$$

This shows $s$ vs $\log t$ is a straight line on a semi-log plot.

Determination of T and S

From the straight-line portion of the $s$ vs $\log t$ plot:

  • $\Delta s$ = drawdown per one log cycle of time
  • $t_0$ = time at which the straight line intercepts $s = 0$
$$T = \frac{2.303 \, Q}{4\pi \, \Delta s}$$
$$S = \frac{2.25 \, T \, t_0}{r^2}$$
Summary:
$T = \frac{2.303 \, Q}{4\pi \, \Delta s}$ — from slope of straight line
$S = \frac{2.25 \, T \, t_0}{r^2}$ — from zero-drawdown intercept
Validity: $u = \frac{r^2 S}{4Tt} < 0.01$
6. What are the objectives of groundwater exploration?
[4]

Objectives of Groundwater Exploration

  1. To identify the occurrence and type of groundwater (confined, unconfined, semi-confined aquifers)
  2. To determine the depth, extent, and geometry of aquifers
  3. To estimate the quantity of groundwater available (safe yield and sustainable yield)
  4. To determine hydraulic parameters — transmissivity ($T$), hydraulic conductivity ($K$), and storativity ($S$)
  5. To assess groundwater recharge rate (natural and artificial)
  6. To evaluate the chemical, physical, and biological quality of groundwater for drinking, irrigation, and industrial use
  7. To select the most suitable well sites for maximum yield at minimum cost
  8. To provide data for design of wells — depth, diameter, screen length, pump capacity, and spacing
  9. To plan sustainable groundwater management and prevent over-exploitation
  10. To identify areas vulnerable to contamination (arsenic, fluoride, nitrates)
  11. To establish monitoring networks for water level and quality surveillance
  12. To plan artificial recharge schemes for replenishment of depleted aquifers
7. a) For the irrigation project planned in Kailali by groundwater pumping, a recuperation test was conducted on an open well. The water level in the well was depressed by 4.5 m and it was left to raise by 2.2 m in 90 minutes. Calculate the specific yield of open well in that region. Also determine the yield from a well of 5 m diameter under a depression head of 2.5 m?
[6]

Given Data

  • Initial depression: $s_1 = 4.5$ m
  • Rise in water level in 90 minutes: 2.2 m
  • Residual depression after 90 min: $s_2 = 4.5 - 2.2 = 2.3$ m
  • Time of recuperation: $t = 90$ min = 1.5 hours
  • Well diameter for yield calculation: $D = 5$ m, so $r = 2.5$ m
  • Depression head for yield: $H = 2.5$ m

Solution

Part 1: Specific Yield (Specific Capacity) of the Open Well

The specific yield of an open well from the recuperation test is defined as the rate of flow into the well per unit area of the well under a unit depression head.

The recuperation rate is given by:

$$K' = \frac{1}{t} \ln\left(\frac{s_1}{s_2}\right)$$

where $K'$ is the specific yield (specific capacity) in units of $\text{m}^3/\text{hr}/\text{m}^2$ of well area / m of depression head.

$$K' = \frac{1}{1.5} \ln\left(\frac{4.5}{2.3}\right)$$
$$K' = 0.6667 \times \ln(1.9565)$$
$$K' = 0.6667 \times 0.6710 = 0.4474 \text{ per hour}$$
$$\boxed{K' = 0.4474 \text{ m}^3/\text{hr per m}^2 \text{ of well area per m of head}}$$

Part 2: Yield from the 5 m diameter well

The yield (discharge) from an open well is given by:

$$Q = K' \times A \times H$$

where:

  • $A$ = cross-sectional area of the well $= \frac{\pi D^2}{4} = \frac{\pi \times 5^2}{4} = 19.635$ m²
  • $H$ = depression head $= 2.5$ m
$$Q = 0.4474 \times 19.635 \times 2.5$$
$$Q = 0.4474 \times 49.088 = 21.96 \text{ m}^3/\text{hr}$$

Converting to m³/s:

Types of Tube Wells

1. Strainer Type Tube Well

Most common type, consisting of casing pipe, well screen (strainer), and gravel pack. The screen allows water entry while preventing sand.

  • Suitability: Alluvial formations (sand, gravel) — Terai region
  • Depth: Up to 200–300 m
  • Yield: Moderate to high (20–100 L/s)

2. Cavity Type Tube Well

Casing is driven into the aquifer and a cavity is formed at the bottom by pumping out sand with water. No screen or gravel pack is used.

  • Suitability: Shallow aquifers (10–60 m) in fine to medium sand with clay above
  • Yield: Low to moderate (5–25 L/s)
  • Advantages: Lower cost, quick installation
  • Limitations: Not for deep aquifers; cavity may collapse

3. Slotted Pipe Type Tube Well

Uses slotted PVC or steel pipes instead of manufactured well screens. Slots are cut at regular intervals.

  • Suitability: Coarse-grained formations (coarse sand and gravel)
  • Yield: Moderate
  • Advantages: Lower cost; suitable for rural community supply
  • Limitations: Not for fine sand (sand entry problems); lower efficiency than manufactured screens

Deep Well Vertical Turbine Pump

A centrifugal pump with multi-stage impellers on a vertical shaft, designed for deep wells. Pump bowl assembly is submerged below water level; motor is mounted on the surface.

Major Components

  1. Electric Motor (surface-mounted)
  2. Discharge Head
  3. Column Pipe
  4. Line Shaft
  5. Shaft Bearings
  6. Pump Bowl Assembly
  7. Impellers (Multi-stage)
  8. Suction Strainer

Applications

  • Suitable for high head pumping from deep wells (50–500+ m)
  • Suitable for high lift applications where water must be raised over large vertical distances
  • Provides non-pulsating, continuous flow — smooth and steady discharge
  • Municipal and industrial water supply requiring large, reliable discharge
  • Large-scale irrigation projects from deep aquifers

Disadvantages

  • Performs poorly in sandy wells — sand particles cause abrasion and damage to impellers and bearings
  • Requires skilled workers for installation, alignment, and maintenance
  • High initial cost due to long column pipe, shaft, and multiple bearings
  • Well must be perfectly straight — even slight crookedness causes shaft misalignment and failure
  • Entire column pipe and shaft assembly must be pulled out for any repair

Groundwater Development Possibilities of Terai Belt

1. Introduction

The Terai belt is a flat alluvial plain in southern Nepal (~800 km long, 25–40 km wide, elevation 60–300 m). Covers 17% of Nepal’s area but hosts over 50% of the population. It has the highest groundwater potential in Nepal due to thick alluvial deposits.

2. Hydrogeological Setting

  • Quaternary alluvial deposits: Sand, gravel, silt, and clay from Himalayan rivers
  • Shallow aquifer (0–50 m): Unconfined to semi-confined; fine to medium sand; water table 2–15 m depth
  • Deep aquifer (50–300+ m): Confined; medium to coarse sand/gravel; higher yield, better quality
  • Total alluvial thickness: 200 m (eastern Terai) to 1,500+ m (western Terai)

4. Current Status of Development

  • Irrigation: Over 40,000 STWs and 8,000 DTWs installed by GWRDB
  • Drinking water: Hand pumps and motorized tube wells for rural; deep wells for urban
  • Industrial use: Growing extraction by Terai industries

5. Development Possibilities

  • Large-scale irrigation: Deep tube wells for 50–200 hectares each; solar-powered pumping increasingly viable
  • Municipal water supply: Deep aquifer wells for growing Terai cities (Biratnagar, Birgunj, Nepalgunj, Dhangadhi)
  • Conjunctive use: Integrating surface water irrigation with groundwater during low-flow periods
