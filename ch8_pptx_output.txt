Introduction to Pumps
Definition: A hydraulic machine that converts Mechanical Energy (from a motor) into Hydraulic Energy (Pressure Energy).
Primary Functions:
Transfer liquid from a low-pressure zone to a high-pressure zone.
Lift liquid from a lower level to a higher level.
Circulate liquid against system resistance.

Plunger Pump vs. Piston Pump
While both are reciprocating pumps, the key difference lies in the sealing mechanism.

Working Principle
The operation consists of two strokes: Suction and Discharge.
Suction Stroke (Plunger moves Right): Vacuum created. Inlet valve opens (Green). Outlet valve closes (Red). Fluid enters.
Discharge Stroke (Plunger moves Left): Volume decreases. Pressure rises. Inlet valve closes. Outlet valve opens. Fluid forced out.

Construction Features
1. The Plunger
Unlike a piston, the plunger is a solid cylinder. It is often made of Ceramic or high-grade Stainless Steel to resist wear and corrosion.
2. Stationary Packing
The seal does not move. This allows for tighter sealing arrangements, enabling the pump to reach pressures up to 20,000 PSI.
3. Valves
Spring-loaded check valves (ball or poppet type) ensure uni-directional flow.
4. Drive Mechanism
Powered by an electric motor or diesel engine connected via a crankshaft to convert rotary motion to reciprocating motion.

Suitability: When to use it?
High Pressure, Low Flow
Ideal for applications needing massive pressure where volume is secondary.
Viscous Fluids
Handles heavy oils, sludge, and slurries better than centrifugal pumps.
Metering / Dosing
Exact volume per stroke makes it perfect for chemical dosing (e.g., fertilizer injection).
Self-Priming
Can pump air/gas to clear lines, unlike standard centrifugal pumps.

Advantages

Disadvantages

Introduction to Jet Pump

Venturi Effect:
The Venturi effect is a fluid-flow phenomenon in which the pressure of a fluid decreases when it flows through a constricted section of a pipe while its velocity increases.
The system relies on the interaction between a nozzle, throat, and diffuser to convert velocity energy into pressure energy.
Jet pumps are also referred to as ejector pumps in groundwater engineering literature
--- SLIDE 4 ---
Components of Jet Pump
Presented by: Mission Bhattarai (078BCE088) | Department of Civil Engineering, Pulchowk Campus
The jet pump consists of four critical parts:
Nozzle: Converts the pressure energy of the power fluid into high velocity kinetic energy.
Suction Chamber: The area where the vacuum is formed and the secondary fluid enters.
Mixing Tube (Throat): The cylindrical section where the two fluids exchange momentum and reach a uniform velocity.
Diffuser: A divergent section that recovers pressure before discharge.

Working Principle of Jet Pump
Presented by: Mission Bhattarai (078BCE088) | Department of Civil Engineering, Pulchowk Campus
The jet pump operates primarily on the Venturi Effect and the principle of momentum transfer.
Energy Conversion: A high-pressure "motive" or "power" fluid (liquid or gas) is forced through a constricted nozzle. As the fluid accelerates, its static pressure drops significantly, creating a partial vacuum
Suction and Mixing: This low-pressure zone draws in a secondary "suction" fluid from the reservoir or source. The two fluids enter a mixing tube where the high-velocity motive fluid transfers momentum to the lower velocity suction fluid
Pressure Recovery: The combined mixture then enters a divergent diffuser. Here, the velocity decreases, and the kinetic energy is converted back into static pressure, allowing the fluid to be discharged at a pressure higher than the suction pressure
--- SLIDE 7 ---
The Working Mechanism
Presented by: Mission Bhattarai (078BCE088) | Department of Civil Engineering, Pulchowk Campus
Step 1: High Pressure motive fluid enters the nozzle
Step 2: Velocity increase -> Pressure drops below atmospheric pressure(Bernoulli’s Principle)
Step 3: Secondary fluid is “sucked” into the low pressure zone.
Step 4: Both fluids mix in the throat, equalizing velocity.
Step 5: The diffuser slows the flow, boosting discharge pressure.
--- SLIDE 8 ---
Types of Jet Pump
Presented by: Mission Bhattarai (078BCE088) | Department of Civil Engineering, Pulchowk Campus
SINGLE- PIPE JET PUMP:
Best for shallow wells with water levels up to 25 feet deep.
Uses on suction pipe to draw water up. The jet assembly(venturi and nozzle) is built directly into the pump housing above the ground.
It relies primarily on suction created by the pump to pull water to the surface.
Double – Pipe Jet Pumps:
Designed for deeper wells, typically 25-110 feet.
Uses two pipes running into the well: Pressure pipe pushes water down into the well and the suction (return) pipe pulls water back up to the surface.
The jet assembly is placed down in the well rather than in the pump. Water is forced down the pressure pipe through the submerged jet to create a vacuum, which helps “push” more water back up the large suction pipe.
The choice depends strictly on the vertical distance to the water table and the required discharge head.

Suitability of Jet Pump
Presented by: Mission Bhattarai (078BCE088) | Department of Civil Engineering, Pulchowk Campus
Abrasive Environments: Because there are no submerged moving parts, jet pumps are exceptionally suited for wells containing sand, silt, or muddy water that would otherwise erode mechanical seals in submersible pumps.
Small Diameter Boreholes: Their slim profile allows them to be installed in very narrow casings where other pumps might not fit.
Corrosive Conditions: The jet assembly can be manufactured from non-corrosive materials (like plastic or bronze), making them ideal for pumping contaminated or chemically aggressive groundwater
Ease of Maintenance: The mechanical motor and electrical components remain at the surface, allowing for easy access and repair without pulling the entire pipe string.
Can operate reliably in areas with fluctuating groundwater levels.
Self-Priming Capability: Can clear air from suction lines automatically.

Applications of Jet Pump
Presented by: Mission Bhattarai (078BCE088) | Department of Civil Engineering, Pulchowk Campus
Groundwater extraction from deep or narrow wells.
Aerospace: Fuel pumping and cooling systems in high vibration environments
Oil Wells: Used in "Artificial Lift" for high-viscosity or sandy crude.
Subsea Mining: Annular Jet Pumps (AJPs) transport slurries from the ocean floor.
Water Treatment: Aeration and chemical dosing.
Temporary pumping installations in construction projects
Chemical dosing is the controlled addition of specific chemicals into a system (water, wastewater, industrial process, soil, or fluid stream) in accurately measured amounts to achieve a desired chemical, physical, or biological effect.

Limitations of Jet Pump
Presented by: Mission Bhattarai (078BCE088) | Department of Civil Engineering, Pulchowk Campus
Low Efficiency: Lower than centrifugal pumps due to mixing losses.
High Motive Demand: Requires a continuous supply of high-pressure fluid.
Depth Sensitive: Performance drops as suction lift increases beyond certain limits.
Not suitable for very high‑yield aquifers
Power Requirements: They require a continuous supply of pressurized motive fluid (or compressed air in similar air-lift systems) to function.

Conclusion:
Presented by: Mission Bhattarai (078BCE088) | Department of Civil Engineering, Pulchowk Campus
Jet pumps offer a robust solution for groundwater extraction in narrow or sandy boreholes.
The absence of submerged moving parts ensures long-term durability and lower maintenance costs.
While they have lower hydraulic efficiency, their ability to handle "dirty" water makes them indispensable in specific engineering projects.

Definition
An airlift pump is a device used to raise the liquid from a well or vessels through a vertical pipe, partially submerged in liquid by means of compressed air introduced into the pipe from the lower end(Abou Taleb & Al-jarrah, 2017).
--- SLIDE 36 ---
Overview of Airlift Pumps
Air lift pumps are used in an extensive range of applications because of their simple construction, cost-effective maintenance, easy performance, and high reliability(Mahrous, 2013).
Air-lift pumps are used in specialized applications, where more conventional pumps cannot operate.
It can be used for pumping hydrocarbons, dirty water, dangerous liquids, and viscous liquids(Di Gerlando et al., 2010), biochemical applications in bioreactors (Zimmerman et al., 2009), and for seabed mining (Pougatch & Salcudean, 2008).

Schematic Explanation
Air compressor → supplies compressed air
Injector nozzle → introduces air into riser pipe
Riser pipe → carries air–water mixture upward
Discharge outlet → releases lifted water

--- SLIDE 41 ---
Working principle
Air injection:- Compressed air is injected near the bottom of a vertical riser pipe that is submerged in water.
Formation of air–water mixture:- The injected air forms bubbles and mixes with water inside the pipe, creating a two-phase (air + water) mixture.
Reduction in density:- The average density of the air–water mixture inside the riser pipe becomes lower than the density of the surrounding water outside the pipe.
Buoyant force and upward flow:- Due to this density difference, a buoyant force acts on the lighter mixture, causing it to rise upward through the pipe.
Discharge at the outlet:- As the mixture reaches the top, air escapes to the atmosphere and water is discharged continuously.
(Karassik et al., 2008, Fan et al., 2013)

Air-lift pump works on the density difference principle

Centrifugal Pump
Presented byRoshan Karki 078BCE129
--- SLIDE 45 ---
Definition
A centrifugal pump is a mechanical device used to transport liquids.
It works by converting rotational energy from a motor into kinetic and pressure energy of the                      fluid.
The liquid enters the pump at the center of the impeller and is thrown outward by centrifugal     force, increasing its velocity and pressure.
--- SLIDE 46 ---
Working principle
Works on the principle of centrifugal force
Pump casing and suction pipe are primed with liquid
Impeller rotates when motor starts
Liquid enters at the eye (center) of the impeller
Liquid is thrown outward by centrifugal action
Velocity energy is converted into pressure energy in casing
High-pressure liquid is discharged through delivery pipe
--- SLIDE 48 ---

Applications requiring continuous and uniform flow
Situations where simple design and low maintenance are needed
Low to medium head operations
Irrigation and drainage
Clean or slightly contaminated fluids

--- SLIDE 54 ---
Required Discharge
Discharge depends on:
Water demand (irrigation, domestic, industrial)
Safe yield of aquifer
Pumping test results
The selected pump discharge must not exceed the safe yield of the well, otherwise:
Excessive drawdown occurs
Well efficiency reduces
Aquifer depletion may happen
Therefore, pump capacity should be slightly less than or equal to the well yield.
(Bear, 1979)
--- SLIDE 55 ---
Total Head to be Developed
The pump must be capable of developing sufficient head to lift water from the pumping water level to the delivery point (Raghunath, 2006).
Total head includes:
Static water level: Vertical distance from ground level to static water level
Drawdown in the well: Additional lowering of water level during pumping
Friction losses in pipes: Losses due to pipes, bends, valves, etc.
Delivery head: Height from ground level to the point of water discharge
(Todd & Mays, 2005)
--- SLIDE 56 ---
Aquifer and Well Characteristics
Depth of aquifer: Deep aquifers require deep-well pumps such as submersible or turbine pumps, while shallow aquifers can be served by centrifugal pumps (Raghunath, 2006)
Type of aquifer: Unconfined(Jet/Centrifugal), Confined(Deep-well turbine, Submersible)
Well diameter and depth: Large diameter wells can accommodate larger pumps
Yield of the well: Low-yield wells require low discharge pumps
(Bear, 1979)
--- SLIDE 57 ---
Type of Well
The pump must be physically and hydraulically compatible with the well.
Shallow wells: Centrifugal pumps
Deep tube wells: Submersible pumps, Vertical turbine pumps
Dug wells: Jet pumps
(Todd & Mays, 2005)
