The construction industry uses different types of equipment for different purposes. Equipment relating to earthwork, transportation, aggregate production and handling, concrete production, road pavement, tunnel construction, hydraulic construction, etc. are typical examples.

Equipment classification facilitates identifying equipment, verifying stock, locating the spares, recording repairs, accounting for costs, indexing catalogs, logging performance, monitoring effectiveness, estimating output and planning procurements.

Advantages of Using Equipment

  • Large and complex works can be carried out easily
  • Faster rate of work progress can be achieved
  • High quality of work can be achieved
  • Equipment can work in adverse weather, climatic and topographic conditions
  • Reduces manual labor requirement
  • More uniform output quality

Disadvantages of Using Equipment

  • Consumes a considerable amount of investment, increases project cost
  • Hiring of equipment depends on availability
  • Less environmentally friendly than green technology
  • Requires skilled operators
  • Maintenance and repair costs
  • Fuel and energy costs
  • May not be suitable for small-scale works

Earthwork in excavation is the basic task in any civil engineering project. Excavation may be at the surface of the ground or below the surface. Excavation is done for foundation or for collection and production of construction materials.

a. Excavator

It has a hoe with teeth attached bucket in its back. It is capable of excavating to a depth. Excavation is done by teeth attached to a bucket, which is also capable of loading the excavated material directly to the transporting vehicle. By changing the bucket it can be converted to driller, power shovel, etc.

Excavators are classified on the digging motion: An upward motion unit is known as shovel and downward motion is Hoe.

b. Dozers / Bulldozer

Dozer is a versatile mechanical instrument for excavating in both firm and loose soil conditions. It can work in the worst conditions like waterlogged as well as loose soil where other equipment cannot operate. Uses:

  • Pilot road and site clearance
  • Remove vegetation, bush and shrubs
  • Remove topsoil
  • Move tree stumps
  • Shallow excavation
  • Push material over short distances

c. Power Shovel / Loaders

It has a square tilting bucket on the end of the moveable arm. The loader assembly may be a removable attachment or permanently mounted on the vehicle. They are used to excavate and load into a truck or onto a conveyer belt. They may be crawler mounted or pneumatic wheel mounted.

d. Backhoe Loader

Falls under the group of power shovel. It has a long handle and one digging unit. Known by several names — hoe, backhoe, back shovel, and pull-shovel. Advantageous for digging below ground level as trenches, ditches which requires precise control on depths. A small version with a dipper attached on rear surface is called backhoe loader.

e. Dragline

Works on soft-to-medium soil. Can handle digging wet material or underwater excavation, hauling soil from the pit. Generally the draglines are track-mounted, wheel mounted, or truck-mounted.

f. Clamshells

Named from the shape of its bucket — a hinged double shell. It performs tasks such as digging, excavation and lifting material. Used to handle loose material such as sand, aggregate, gravel, and crushed stones. Can be used for lifting material from deep foundations (cofferdam, pier foundation, manholes). Can be specially used to lift material vertically. Has most of the characteristics of dragline and some of cranes.

g. Trenching Machines

Special machines designed to dig trenches. Used for laying pipelines, sewer ditches, where the job and condition permits. Generally crawler mounted to distribute weight over a greater area.

h. Scrapers

Machines capable of cutting, excavating, loading material in its bowl to be discharged in uniform layers. Can be self-propelled or tractor-pulled type. Self-propelled or rubber tire mounted.

i. Graders

Basically equipment meant to grade surfaces and other large areas. With the help of teeth attached to blade, they scarify earth surface to loosen the material which can be shifted forward or to the sides by grading blades. The blade can rotate up to 270° making it unique. Also known as motor grader.

Classified in four types:

  • a) Light Grader
  • b) Medium Grader — 10-12 tons
  • c) Heavy Grader — 17-24 tons
  • d) Very Heavy Grader

j. Power Jack Hammers and Pneumatic Drills

Equipment used to drill holes in rock. Used to excavate hard rock by drilling and blasting method. The drilling bit is mounted over the Jack hammer. Very useful for:

  • Drilling holes in rock for blasting and clearing
  • Cutting materials with the help of pneumatic circular saws and chains
  • Boreholes in timber for jointing them to make timber structure
  • Running air-operated centrifugal pumps at the construction site

4.2b Equipment for Transportation 2072

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

  1. Explain equipment for transportation. [Part of 10] (2072 Ashwin)

a. Trucks

Available in many types and capacities, and are used for transporting construction materials at the construction site. Includes normal trucks, dumping trucks, etc. Dump trucks come in 2-axle and 3-axle types. Common type "tippers" are readily available and economical.

b. Dumpers

Dumpers have a larger capacity than trucks and can be made to work in adverse conditions. They can be rear tipping or bottom dumping type. Dumpers come with much more capacity than dump trucks.

c. Rail Wagons

Used for transportation of bulk materials along rail lines. Wagons transport large quantities of materials such as sand, aggregate, crushed stones in bulk. They are economical for long distances where rail lines are available.

d. Mini-Dumpers

Small dumpers used for small construction sites. They have lower capacity but can maneuver in tight spaces. Useful inside buildings for small volume work.

e. Loaders

Front-end loaders besides being excavation equipment are also useful in loading material onto trucks and dumpers, i.e., for material handling and transportation.

f. Belt Conveyor

Moving belt running over a series of rollers used to carry material over a given path. The speed and width of belt determine the capacity. Used for bulk material transport in crushing plants, concrete plants, etc.

g. Bucket Conveyor

Uses a series of buckets mounted on chains or belts to transport material vertically or at steep angles. Used for elevating materials from lower to higher levels in aggregate plants, concrete plants, etc.

h. Ropeways

Wires are hung from pylons through which the material is transported in carriers or buckets. Used in hilly terrain where road access is difficult for transporting construction materials.

Compaction is the process of densification of soil by the application of mechanical energy to increase soil density and strength. Different types of compaction equipment are described below:

a. Smooth Wheel Roller (Static Roller)

Also called three-wheel rollers. It consists of a large roller drum in the front and two smaller rollers at the rear. Available in 6 to 16 tons capacity. Used for compaction of granular soils and finishing of road surfaces.

b. Sheep Foot Roller

A steel drum with projecting legs (feet) attached to its surface. The feet knead and compact the soil. Very effective for cohesive soils (clay). Available in different sizes from 5-ton to 20-ton capacity. The projecting feet penetrate into the soil and compact it from bottom up.

c. Grid Roller

Has a cylindrical heavy roller made up of a network of steel bars forming a grid pattern. Used for crushing oversize material and compacting weathered rock, gravel, and coarse materials.

d. Vibrating Roller

Has a mechanism that produces vibration while rolling. The vibrations combined with the static weight provide efficient compaction. Most effective for granular soils (sand and gravel). Available in single drum and tandem (double drum) varieties.

e. Tamping Roller

Similar to sheep foot roller but with larger and shorter feet. The tamping effect compacts the soil by kneading action. Used for compacting silty-clay and clayey soils.

f. Pneumatic Tired Roller

Uses pneumatic rubber tires instead of steel drums. The tires provide a kneading action. Available in different sizes. Can compact both granular and cohesive soils. The tire pressure can be adjusted for different soil types.

g. Frog Rammer / Jumping Hammer

A small mechanical compactor powered by an engine. It operates by jumping up and down on the soil. Used for compaction in confined areas like trenches, around manholes, and near walls where rollers cannot operate.

h. Plate Compactor

A flat vibrating plate mounted over an engine. Compacts soil by vibrating action. Used for small areas and confined spaces. Also used for compacting granular backfill around structures.

4.3b Crushing Plants 2076

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

  1. Describe about aggregate production equipment. [Part of 6] (2076 Magh)

Crushing is the process of reducing the size of large rock pieces to smaller sizes suitable for construction. Types of crushers:

a. Jaw Crusher

Has two jaws, one fixed and one movable. Material is fed from the top and crushed by the compression between the jaws. Used as primary crusher for breaking large rocks.

b. Gyratory Crusher

Has a conical head that gyrates inside a cone-shaped outer shell. Material is crushed by compression between the head and shell. Used as primary crusher for large capacity operations.

c. Cone Crusher

Similar to gyratory crusher but shorter. Used as secondary crusher for producing smaller sized aggregates from primary crushed material.

d. Roll Crusher

Has two cylindrical rollers rotating in opposite directions. Material is fed between the rollers and crushed by compression. Used for soft to medium hard materials.

e. Impact Crusher (Hammer Crusher)

Uses impact force (hammers or blow bars) to break the material. The material is fed into a chamber where rotating hammers break it by impact. Can produce more cubical shaped aggregates.

4.4 Equipment for Concrete Construction 20792076207520732070

Concrete is the most widely used construction material. Different equipment is required for different stages of concrete production and placement.

a. Formwork / Shuttering

Formwork is the temporary structure used to hold fresh concrete in the desired shape until it sets and gains sufficient strength. Formwork can be of timber, steel, aluminum, or plastic. Steel formwork is more durable and reusable.

b. Concrete Batching

Batching is the process of measuring concrete mix ingredients (cement, sand, aggregate, water) by weight or volume. Weight batching is more accurate and preferred for quality work. A concrete batching plant automates this process for large projects.

c. Concrete Mixing

After batching, materials are mixed to produce a homogeneous mix. Types of concrete mixers:

i. Tilting Drum Mixer

A drum-type mixer that tilts for discharging concrete. The drum rotates about an inclined axis. It can be charged and discharged easily. Common for small to medium projects.

ii. Pan Mixer

A circular pan with rotating blades that mix concrete. Provides very uniform mixing. Used for precast concrete and high-quality work.

iii. Non-Tilting Mixer

The drum rotates in a horizontal axis and concrete is discharged by reversing the drum or through a chute. Used in batching plants.

iv. Transit Mixer / Truck Mixer

A truck with a rotating drum that can mix concrete while transporting it from the batching plant to the site. The drum keeps rotating slowly to prevent segregation. Can carry 4–12 cubic meters of concrete.

d. Concrete Transportation

Moving concrete from the mixer to the point of placement:

  • Wheelbarrow: For small quantities over short distances
  • Bucket Conveyor: Buckets on chains for vertical transport
  • Chutes: Inclined troughs for gravity-fed transport
  • Dumpers: Small vehicles for site transport
  • Belt Conveyors: Continuous belt transport
  • Concrete Pumps: Hydraulic pumps that push concrete through pipes to the point of placement. Can pump concrete to great heights and horizontal distances. Two types: line pump and boom pump

e. Concrete Compaction Equipment

i. Internal (Needle) Vibrators

Also called needle vibrator. Consists of three parts: the power unit, flexible tube, and vibratory head. The vibrating head is introduced into the fresh concrete to vibrate and consolidate. Power is given either by engine or electricity. The vibrating needle is a straight iron tube with eccentric weight fitted inside.

ii. Plate Vibrators

Used to compact concrete with lesser thickness. A surface vibrator used extensively in slab construction.

iii. Vibrating Screed

A type of surface vibrator capable of acting over large sections of concrete surface. Used in finishing bridge decks and road pavements.

iv. Form Vibrator

External vibrators attached to concrete formwork. They vibrate the form which in turn vibrates the concrete. Used in precast concrete production.

v. Concrete Rollers

Static rollers used for compacting plum concrete or roller compacted concrete (RCC) with greater thickness. Generally used in compaction of dams.

f. Finishing Equipment

After compaction, the concrete surface is finished by troweling, floating, or by belts. Hand trowels for smaller surfaces, power trowels for larger areas. Power floats are used to level concrete to accurate levels, powered by electric motors.

Hoisting is the operation of lifting the load from one location, transporting it to another location situated at a reasonable distance, and landing it down. Cranes are the versatile and most useful machine. It is the only single machine capable of providing three-dimensional movements to a load virtually in one continuous operation with speed, safety, and precision.

It is necessary to know the lifting capacity and working range of a crane to be selected to perform a given job.

Factors for Selecting Cranes 2072

Past Exam (2072 Magh): What are the factors to be taken into consideration while selecting cranes for lifting of materials? [4]
  1. Maximum load to be lifted: The crane must have sufficient lifting capacity (Safe Working Load, SWL) for the heaviest load.
  2. Working radius required: The distance from the crane center to the farthest point of load placement.
  3. Lifting height: The maximum height to which loads must be raised (e.g., number of stories in building).
  4. Site conditions: Available space for crane erection, ground bearing capacity for foundation, accessibility, and obstructions.
  5. Frequency of lifts: Number of lifts per hour/day — determines whether a high-capacity fixed crane or multiple mobile cranes are needed.
  6. Type of load: Size, shape, weight distribution, and fragility of materials (precast elements, steel sections, formwork, concrete buckets).
  7. Duration of use: Short-term use favors renting mobile cranes; long-term projects justify tower cranes.
  8. Cost: Owning/rental cost, erection/dismantling cost, operating cost (fuel, operator), and transport cost.
  9. Safety considerations: Proximity to power lines, wind conditions, and regulatory requirements.

Classification of Cranes

Broadly cranes are classified into four categories:

a) Stationary / Derrick Crane

Also called derrick crane. These cranes give an exceptionally large working radius and can be designed to give any lifting capacity. They may be steam, electric, or diesel-driven. Derrick cranes may be used on a wide range of applications from building construction and large civil works to industrial erection of plants, handling timber, construction of ports, shipyards, loading and unloading cargos, etc.

Consists of: a boom, a bull wheel, and guy ropes.

b) Overhead / Gantry Crane

These cranes are used for handling materials over a rectangular area such as in factories and workshops. They move in any direction and handle loads. Consist of a hoisting mechanism mounted on a carriage or crab capable of traveling on a girder which spans the area. The girder moves longitudinally on rails on an overhead gantry.

c) Mobile Crane

These cranes are mounted on mobile chassis. Truck cranes have high mobility, whereas crawler cranes move slowly. Classification of mobile cranes:

  • Crawler crane
  • Telescopic boom truck mounted cranes
  • Lattice boom truck mounted cranes
  • Rough terrain cranes
  • All-terrain cranes
  • Heavy lift cranes

d) Tower Crane

Usually used in the construction of industrial and multi-storeyed residential buildings. Tower cranes provide high lifting capacity and good working radius. They take up limited space around the worksite.

These are generally of the fixed base type mounted on a foundation. Structural sections can be added to increase the height of the crane. There is a vertical limit called "maximum free standing" height to which these cranes can safely rise above the base. A self-erecting tower to greater vertical height is fairly easy and economical.

A self-erecting crane has a section of hydraulically operated mechanisms situated below the slewing ring. It is also used in assembling of high industrial plants with elements of structure including loading and unloading works.

Principal components of tower crane:

  • Undercarriage
  • Slewing mechanism
  • Slewing platform
  • Tower with operator's cabin
  • Hoisting and operating mechanism

Lifting System (Luffing System): A system that constantly increases the lifting capacity of a crawler crane. Consists of: trailing counterweight, expendable counterweight, luffing system, and a luffing jib or derrick.

4.6 Equipment for Tunnel Construction 20792070

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

  1. How the tunnel construction process is carried out? Explain with neat sketches. [Part of 10] (2079 Shrawan)
  2. Describe the following equipment with neat sketches: Tunnel excavation by blasting [Part of 10] (2070 Magh)
  3. Describe the following equipment: Tunnel boring machine [Part of 10] (2070 Magh)

There are two methods of tunnel construction: the conventional method (drilling and blasting) and using a Tunnel Boring Machine (TBM).

A. Conventional Method

Excavation of the tunnel by drilling and blasting method is the conventional method of tunnel construction.

  • Excavation by manual equipment is done for soft soil
  • For rocks, drilling and blasting method is adopted
  • The process of taking out excavated/blasted materials from the tunnel is known as mucking
  • Mucking is done manually in wheelbarrows for small sections, whereas for larger diameter tunnels, loaders and railway wagons are used
  • Excavated material is taken out as the tunnel progresses in depth
  • Ventilation is needed when excavation is at sufficient depth. Arrangements are done through exhaust pipes — one pulls foul air from inside and another pushes fresh air inside
  • Power is supplied to run equipment like exhaust fans, drilling machines, etc.

Equipment used in conventional tunnel construction:

  • Excavators for soft ground
  • Drilling machines
  • Compressors to run drilling heads
  • Earth transporting equipment for mucking excavated material
  • Exhaust fans for ventilation
  • Compressors to supply fresh air

B. Tunneling by Tunnel Boring Machine (TBM)

Modern tunnel constructions are done with tunnel boring machines (TBM). These machines have a programmed head (moles) which drill through the mountain to bore the tunnel. The muck is taken out through a pipe attached to the boring machine.

This method of tunneling is very fast and efficient. However, these machines are very costly. The length of the tunnel must justify the use — for longer tunnels TBMs are economical.

TBMs are available in different capacities and diameters. Once programmed before operating, these machines can progress along fixed co-ordinates in all X, Y, and Z directions.

Advantages of TBM 20782076

Past Exam (2078 Chaitra): Introduce TBM and its advantage in tunnel construction. [Part of 7]
Past Exam (2076 Bhadra): Write short notes on: TBM [5]
  1. Speed: Much faster than conventional drill & blast method — can advance 10-20 meters per day vs 2-5 m/day by D&B.
  2. Continuous operation: Excavation, mucking, and lining can proceed simultaneously.
  3. Safety: Reduced exposure of workers to hazards (rock falls, blast fumes). The shield protects workers.
  4. Minimal ground disturbance: Reduces settlement and vibration impacts on surface structures.
  5. Smooth tunnel profile: Produces circular profile requiring less concrete lining material.
  6. Environmental: Less noise, vibration, and dust compared to blasting.
  7. Automation: Can be programmed to follow precise coordinates (X, Y, Z) with laser guidance.

Disadvantages: Very high capital cost, long mobilization time, difficult to transport, limited flexibility for varying cross-sections, not economical for short tunnels.

Steps in Conventional Tunnel Construction (Drill & Blast) 2078

Past Exam (2078 Chaitra): Explain the steps to be followed in sequence while carrying out tunneling work by using conventional method. [Part of 7]
  1. Setting out & survey: Establish tunnel alignment, grade, and cross-section using survey instruments.
  2. Drilling: Drill holes into the rock face using jack hammers, pneumatic drills, or jumbo drills in a predetermined pattern.
  3. Charging: Load explosives (dynamite, ANFO) into drilled holes. Insert detonators and connect wiring.
  4. Blasting: Detonate charges in a controlled sequence. All personnel evacuated to safe distance before firing.
  5. Ventilation: Allow blast fumes to clear. Run exhaust fans — one pulling foul air out, one pushing fresh air in.
  6. Scaling: Remove loose rock from roof and walls to make the work area safe for the next cycle.
  7. Mucking: Remove blasted rock debris using loaders, dumpers, or rail wagons. Muck is transported to disposal area.
  8. Support installation: Install temporary support — rock bolts, shotcrete, steel ribs, or wire mesh (NATM approach).
  9. Lining: Apply permanent concrete lining when sufficient length has been excavated.
  10. Repeat cycle: Advance to next face and repeat the entire process (called the “advance per round”).

4.7 Equipment for Hydraulic Construction

Types of hydraulic construction include the construction of harbors, oil rigging, and deep-sea structures. These projects use special types of earthwork equipment and concreting equipment.

Special requirements include equipment capable of working in water, underwater concreting equipment, pumping systems to handle water, and specialized foundation equipment for deep-water conditions.

4.8 Equipment for Highway Construction 207720722070

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

Highway construction involves earthwork in excavation, embankment construction, and pavement construction.

a. Sub-grade Preparation and Compaction

The subgrade is prepared after earthwork in excavation. Preparation includes site clearance, grading, and compaction. A grader is used to grade the surface to achieve uniform surface both along cross section and longitudinal section. Final compaction is done with rollers — sheep foot roller, tandem rollers, pneumatic tired rollers, etc.

b. Construction of Sub-base and Compaction

After achieving the finished subgrade surface, sub-base is prepared. Sub-base construction involves quarrying of sub-base materials, screening, transporting materials to site, spreading by loader or grader, and compaction in layers. The top surface is graded with grader and final compaction is done.

c. Construction of Base Course

Base course is constructed likewise to sub-base construction. Pavers or laying machines are important equipment for mixing, laying and finishing the base course layer.

d. Equipment for Bituminous Pavement Work

i. Bitumen Boiler

Used to heat bitumen to the required temperature. Modern boilers use diesel fuel burners. These boilers can be stationary or mobile.

ii. Bitumen Distributor

A truck which loads preheated bitumen from boilers. Comes with burners fired by diesel or crude oil to control temperature. Has bitumen spraying bars that spray bitumen in fan shapes through nozzles.

iii. Aggregate Spreader

Used for surface dressing work. Aggregates are spread uniformly in a layer over the bitumen sprayed. A dump truck with spreading attachment on the tail spreads aggregate in a uniform manner.

iv. Asphalt Distributor

Used to apply prime or tack coats on a surface in preparation for paving. Available in truck mounted or trailer models. Considered the most important piece of equipment on any asphalt surface treatment project. Consists of an insulated tank with a heating system, a spray bar, and a control system.

v. Asphalt Concrete Plant

To make asphalt concrete pavements, asphalt concrete (mix of bitumen binder and different types of graded aggregates) is used. For patch work, asphalt concrete is batched and mixed manually. For large jobs, it is done in a batching and mixing plant called asphalt concrete plant.

vi. Concrete Pavers

A paver (paver finisher, asphalt laying machine) is a machine used for laying asphalt or concrete. Normally fed by a dump truck. Also called mat makers — self-propelled machines.

vii. Water Tanker

Used for water supply and dust control during pavement construction.

Equipment selection plays an important role in the execution of any construction project since proper selection contributes to project completion — timely, economically, and of desired quality standards.

There are two principal aspects of equipment selection:

  1. Technical aspects — governed by performance
  2. Economic and Commercial aspects — mainly concerning the contractor from a business sense

After selection from technical point of view, it is necessary to test the viability from economic or business point of view also.

Planning for Equipment Selection

Involves working out answers to the following questions:

  1. What is the equipment needed?
  2. When is the equipment needed and for how long? (Duration of use)
  3. How much equipment is needed? (Capacity assessment)
  4. Which of the equipment is preferred based upon history of performance?

At the onset, it is necessary to take concrete decisions on the method to achieve the ultimate target by utilizing resources (Men, Machinery, Material & Money) in the best possible way. This may be done at the pre-tender stage by studying the contract documents, plans, and local conditions.

Site Survey Factors

  • Geological conditions
  • Terrain conditions
  • Availability of fuel
  • Availability of labor
  • Availability of services (electricity, water)
  • Pollution standards

Main Basis for Selection

Factors to consider when selecting construction plant:

  • Identification of task
  • Quantity of material
  • Unit weight and size
  • Changeable characteristics
  • Haul distance
  • Trafficability

Detailed Selection Factors

A. Task Considerations

  • Nature of task and specifications
  • Daily forecast of planned production
  • Quantity of work and time allowed for completion
  • Distribution of work at the site
  • Interference expected and inter-dependence with other operations

B. Site Conditions

  • Accessibility to location
  • Maneuverability at the site
  • Working space restrictions
  • Altitude and weather conditions
  • Working season and working hours
  • Availability of local resources — manpower, materials, equipment
  • Availability of land, power supply, and water supply for workshop and camp
  • Availability of local equipment hiring, repair and maintenance facilities
  • Availability of fuel, oil, and lubricants

C. Equipment Suitability

  • Type of equipment considered suitable for the task
  • Make, models, and sizes of special-purpose and general-purpose equipment
  • Production capability, service conditions, and delivery time
  • Equipment already owned by the contractor
  • Usefulness for other and future tasks

D. Operating Reliability

  • Manufacturer's reputation
  • Equipment components — engine, transmission, brakes, steering, operator's cabin
  • Use of standard components
  • Warranties and guarantees
  • Vendor after-sales service
  • Operator's requirements, acceptability, adaptability and training
  • Structural design
  • Preventive maintenance program
  • Safety features

E. Availability

  • Availability of fuel, oil, and lubricants
  • Maintenance and repair facilities
  • Vendor after-sales service, repairs, spares and maintenance
  • Availability of spare parts
  • Standardization consideration

F. Economic Considerations

  • Owning costs
  • Operating costs
  • Resale or residual value after use
  • Replacement costs of existing equipment

G. Commercial Considerations

  • Buy second-hand or new equipment
  • Rent equipment
  • Hire-purchase equipment
  • Purchase or lease

Equipment selection analysis is not necessarily limited to these — it leads to alternative choices for acquiring the required equipment, followed up with management deciding on the best option. In most cases, the final equipment selection decision is likely to be a compromise between what is ideally required and what can actually be obtained economically.

Factors for Deciding Extent of Mechanisation 2073

Past Exam (2073 Bhadra): Write down the factors to be considered for deciding the extent of mechanisation of construction project. [4]
  1. Volume and nature of work: Large-volume earthwork, concrete work, and repetitive tasks justify mechanisation. Small, scattered works do not.
  2. Availability of labor: Where manual labor is scarce or expensive, mechanisation is essential. In labor-surplus areas, it may cause unemployment concerns.
  3. Quality requirements: Machine-mixed concrete, machine-compacted earth, and machine-finished surfaces produce more uniform quality than manual work.
  4. Time constraint: If project completion time is tight, mechanisation speeds up work significantly.
  5. Site accessibility: Remote or difficult terrain may necessitate equipment, but narrow/confined spaces may prevent its use.
  6. Cost comparison: Compare equipment cost (owning + operating + transport) vs manual labor cost for the same output.
  7. Future utilization: If the equipment can be reused in future projects, purchase is more justified.
  8. Environmental & safety: Hazardous work (demolition, deep excavation) favors mechanisation for worker safety.

Key Exam Patterns

  • 12 marks = highest chapter weightage — expect 2-3 questions from this chapter in every exam
  • Equipment selection criteria: Asked 8 times — know all 7 factors (nature of work, site conditions, duration, equipment size, availability, cost economics, commercial considerations)
  • Excavation equipment: Differentiate Excavator, Backhoe, Dozer, Scraper, Dragline, Power Shovel — know applications and suitability
  • Crane types: Tower Crane, Mobile Crane, Crawler Crane, Truck Mounted Crane — know components, working principles, and selection criteria
  • Combined questions: "Explain equipment for _____ construction" (tunnels, highways, concrete) — list with brief function of each

Must-Know Points

  1. Advantages of equipment: Increased output, reduced cost, adverse weather capability, quality maintenance, safety, no social/emotional factors
  2. Disadvantages: Breakdown delays, needs skilled operators, heavy investment, spare parts inventory, unemployment concerns
  3. Excavators: Backhoe (below grade), Power Shovel (above grade), Dragline (soft/wet soil, long reach), Clamshell (vertical lifting)
  4. Compaction by soil type: Sheepsfoot → Clay; Vibratory → Sand/gravel; Pneumatic → All types; Smooth wheel → Asphalt finishing; Rammer/Plate → Confined areas
  5. Concrete equipment chain: Crusher → Screening → Batching Plant → Mixer → Transit Mixer → Pump → Vibrator
  6. Crane selection factors: Max load, working radius, lifting height, site conditions, frequency, load type, duration, cost, safety
  7. TBM advantages: Speed (10-20 m/day), continuous operation, safety, minimal ground disturbance, smooth profile, less noise
  8. Tunnel D&B steps: Setting out → Drilling → Charging → Blasting → Ventilation → Scaling → Mucking → Support → Lining → Repeat
  9. Mechanisation factors: Volume, labor availability, quality needs, time, site access, cost comparison, future use, safety
  10. Purchase vs Lease: Purchase for >60-70% utilization; Lease for short-term; Hire-Purchase when capital constrained
  11. Equipment cost: Owning costs (depreciation, insurance, tax) vs Operating costs (fuel, maintenance, operator wages, repair)
  12. Selection criteria: Nature of work, site conditions, project duration, equipment size/capacity, availability, cost economics (own vs rent vs hire-purchase), commercial considerations
