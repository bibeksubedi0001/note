Advantages of Using Equipment in Construction

  1. Higher Productivity: Equipment increases the rate of output manifold compared to manual labor.
  2. Reduced Overall Cost: For large-scale projects, per-unit cost reduces despite high initial investment.
  3. Activities Impossible Manually: Pile driving, tunnel boring, deep excavation, heavy lifting all require equipment.
  4. Adverse Conditions: Equipment works underwater (dredgers), underground (TBM), at heights (cranes), and in harsh weather.
  5. Better Quality: Machine mixing, compaction, and finishing produce uniform quality.
  6. Reduced Fatigue & Hazards: Eliminates health hazards; equipment is free from social and emotional factors.
  7. Labor Shortage: Maintains production rate even when labor is scarce.
  8. Safer & Cleaner Sites: Mechanized operations maintain safer and cleaner construction sites.

Disadvantages of Using Equipment

  1. Heavy Investment: Equipment cost = 25–40% of total project cost.
  2. Breakdown Delays: If a critical machine breaks down, the entire operation halts.
  3. Skilled Operators Required: Only trained labor can operate, commanding higher wages.
  4. Maintenance Cost: Regular maintenance, repairs, and spare parts inventory are expensive.
  5. Environmental Impact: Noise, air pollution, vibration, and high fuel consumption.
  6. Unemployment: Large-scale mechanization displaces unskilled labor.
  7. Disposal Problem: Reselling or disposing old equipment after project completion is difficult.
  8. Fire Risk: Electric tools can cause short circuits which may result in fire.
  • Construction industry uses different types of equipment for different purposes
  • Types: earthwork, transportation, aggregate production & handling, concrete production, road pavement, tunnel construction, hydraulic construction

Equipment classification helps in:

  • Identifying equipment and verifying stock
  • Locating spares and recording repairs
  • Accounting for costs and indexing catalogs
  • Logging performance and monitoring effectiveness
  • Estimating output and planning procurements

  • Earthwork in excavation is the basic task in any civil engineering project
  • Excavation may be at surface or below the surface
  • Done for foundation or for collection and production of construction materials

a. Excavator 2071

  • Has a hoe with teeth-attached bucket at its back
  • Capable of excavating to a depth
  • Teeth-attached bucket also loads excavated material directly to transporting vehicle
  • By changing the bucket, can be converted to driller, power shovel, etc.
  • Classified by digging motion: Upward motion = Shovel; Downward motion = Hoe

b. Dozers / Bulldozer

  • Versatile mechanical instrument for excavating in both firm and loose soil
  • Can work in worst conditions — waterlogged and loose soil where other equipment cannot operate

Uses:

  • Pilot road and site clearance
  • Remove vegetation, bush and shrubs
  • Remove topsoil
  • Move tree stumps
  • Shallow excavation
  • Push material over short distances

c. Power Shovel / Loaders 2079 2076

  • Has a square tilting bucket on the end of a moveable arm
  • Loader assembly may be removable attachment or permanently mounted
  • Used to excavate and load into truck or onto conveyer belt
  • May be crawler mounted or pneumatic wheel mounted

d. Backhoe Loader 2075

  • Falls under the group of power shovel
  • Has a long handle and one digging unit
  • Also known as: hoe, backhoe, back shovel, pull-shovel
  • Advantageous for digging below ground level (trenches, ditches) with precise depth control
  • Small version with dipper attached on rear surface = backhoe loader

e. Dragline 2079 2074

  • Works on soft-to-medium soil
  • Can handle digging wet material or underwater excavation
  • Used for hauling soil from the pit
  • Mounting types: track-mounted, wheel mounted, or truck-mounted

f. Clamshells

  • Named from the shape of its bucket — a hinged double shell
  • Performs digging, excavation, and lifting material
  • Handles loose material: sand, aggregate, gravel, crushed stones
  • Used for lifting material from deep foundations (cofferdam, pier foundation, manholes)
  • Can lift material vertically
  • Has characteristics of both dragline and cranes

g. Trenching Machines

  • Special machines designed to dig trenches
  • Used for laying pipelines, sewer ditches
  • Generally crawler mounted to distribute weight over greater area

h. Scrapers

  • Capable of cutting, excavating, loading material in its bowl and discharging in uniform layers
  • Types: self-propelled or tractor-pulled
  • Mounting: self-propelled or rubber tire mounted

i. Graders 2074 2072

  • Equipment meant to grade surfaces and large areas
  • Teeth attached to blade scarify earth surface to loosen material
  • Material shifted forward or to sides by grading blades
  • Blade can rotate up to 270° — making it unique
  • Also known as motor grader

Classified in four types:

  • a) Light Grader
  • b) Medium Grader — 10-12 tons
  • c) Heavy Grader — 17-24 tons
  • d) Very Heavy Grader

j. Power Jack Hammers and Pneumatic Drills

  • Equipment used to drill holes in rock
  • Used to excavate hard rock by drilling and blasting method
  • Drilling bit is mounted over the Jack hammer

Very useful for:

  • Drilling holes in rock for blasting and clearing
  • Cutting materials with the help of pneumatic circular saws and chains
  • Boreholes in timber for jointing them to make timber structure
  • Running air-operated centrifugal pumps at the construction site

a. Trucks

  • Available in many types and capacities
  • Used for transporting construction materials at site
  • Includes normal trucks and dumping trucks
  • Dump trucks: 2-axle and 3-axle types
  • Common type “tippers” are readily available and economical

b. Dumpers

  • Larger capacity than trucks
  • Can work in adverse conditions
  • Types: rear tipping or bottom dumping

c. Rail Wagons

  • Used for transportation of bulk materials along rail lines
  • Transport large quantities: sand, aggregate, crushed stones
  • Economical for long distances where rail lines are available

d. Mini-Dumpers

  • Small dumpers used for small construction sites
  • Lower capacity but can maneuver in tight spaces
  • Useful inside buildings for small volume work

e. Loaders

  • Front-end loaders are also excavation equipment
  • Useful for loading material onto trucks and dumpers
  • Used for material handling and transportation

f. Belt Conveyor

  • Moving belt running over a series of rollers to carry material over a given path
  • Speed and width of belt determine capacity
  • Used for bulk material transport in crushing plants, concrete plants, etc.

g. Bucket Conveyor

  • Series of buckets mounted on chains or belts
  • Transports material vertically or at steep angles
  • Used for elevating materials from lower to higher levels in aggregate/concrete plants

h. Ropeways

  • Wires hung from pylons through which material transported in carriers or buckets
  • Used in hilly terrain where road access is difficult for transporting construction materials

  • Compaction = process of densification of soil by application of mechanical energy
  • Purpose: increase soil density and strength

a. Smooth Wheel Roller (Static Roller)

  • Also called three-wheel rollers
  • Consists of a large roller drum in front and two smaller rollers at rear
  • Available in 6 to 16 tons capacity
  • Used for compaction of granular soils and finishing of road surfaces

b. Sheep Foot Roller

  • Steel drum with projecting legs (feet) attached to its surface
  • Feet knead and compact the soil
  • Very effective for cohesive soils (clay)
  • Available in 5-ton to 20-ton capacity
  • Projecting feet penetrate into soil and compact from bottom up

c. Grid Roller

  • Cylindrical heavy roller made of a network of steel bars forming a grid pattern
  • Used for crushing oversize material
  • Compacts weathered rock, gravel, and coarse materials

d. Vibrating Roller

  • Has mechanism that produces vibration while rolling
  • Vibrations + static weight = efficient compaction
  • Most effective for granular soils (sand and gravel)
  • Available in single drum and tandem (double drum) varieties

e. Tamping Roller

  • Similar to sheep foot roller but with larger and shorter feet
  • Tamping effect compacts soil by kneading action
  • Used for compacting silty-clay and clayey soils

f. Pneumatic Tired Roller

  • Uses pneumatic rubber tires instead of steel drums
  • Tires provide a kneading action
  • Can compact both granular and cohesive soils
  • Tire pressure adjustable for different soil types

g. Frog Rammer / Jumping Hammer

  • Small mechanical compactor powered by an engine
  • Operates by jumping up and down on soil
  • Used for compaction in confined areas: trenches, around manholes, near walls

h. Plate Compactor

  • Flat vibrating plate mounted over an engine
  • Compacts soil by vibrating action
  • Used for small areas and confined spaces
  • Also used for compacting granular backfill around structures

  • Crushing = process of reducing large rock pieces to smaller sizes suitable for construction

Types of crushers:

a. Jaw Crusher

  • Has two jaws — one fixed and one movable
  • Material fed from top, crushed by compression between jaws
  • Used as primary crusher for breaking large rocks

b. Gyratory Crusher

  • Has a conical head that gyrates inside a cone-shaped outer shell
  • Material crushed by compression between head and shell
  • Used as primary crusher for large capacity operations

c. Cone Crusher

  • Similar to gyratory crusher but shorter
  • Used as secondary crusher for producing smaller sized aggregates

d. Roll Crusher

  • Two cylindrical rollers rotating in opposite directions
  • Material fed between rollers, crushed by compression
  • Used for soft to medium hard materials

e. Impact Crusher (Hammer Crusher)

  • Uses impact force (hammers or blow bars) to break material
  • Material fed into a chamber where rotating hammers break it
  • Can produce more cubical shaped aggregates

  • Concrete = most widely used construction material
  • Different equipment required for different stages of production and placement

a. Formwork / Shuttering

  • Temporary structure to hold fresh concrete in desired shape until it sets
  • Materials: timber, steel, aluminum, or plastic
  • Steel formwork is more durable and reusable

b. Concrete Batching 2079 2071 2070

  • Batching = measuring concrete mix ingredients (cement, sand, aggregate, water) by weight or volume
  • Weight batching is more accurate and preferred for quality work
  • Concrete batching plant automates this process for large projects

c. Concrete Mixing 2079 2075 2073 2071

  • After batching, materials are mixed to produce a homogeneous mix

Types of concrete mixers:

i. Tilting Drum Mixer

  • Drum-type mixer that tilts for discharging concrete
  • Drum rotates about an inclined axis
  • Easy to charge and discharge
  • Common for small to medium projects

ii. Pan Mixer

  • Circular pan with rotating blades that mix concrete
  • Provides very uniform mixing
  • Used for precast concrete and high-quality work

iii. Non-Tilting Mixer

  • Drum rotates in a horizontal axis
  • Concrete discharged by reversing drum or through a chute
  • Used in batching plants

iv. Transit Mixer / Truck Mixer

  • Truck with a rotating drum that mixes concrete while transporting
  • Transports from batching plant to site
  • Drum keeps rotating slowly to prevent segregation
  • Capacity: 4–12 cubic meters of concrete

d. Concrete Transportation

Moving concrete from mixer to point of placement:

  • Wheelbarrow: For small quantities over short distances
  • Bucket Conveyor: Buckets on chains for vertical transport
  • Chutes: Inclined troughs for gravity-fed transport
  • Dumpers: Small vehicles for site transport
  • Belt Conveyors: Continuous belt transport
  • Concrete Pumps: Hydraulic pumps that push concrete through pipes to the point of placement. Can pump concrete to great heights and horizontal distances. Two types: line pump and boom pump

e. Concrete Compaction Equipment

i. Internal (Needle) Vibrators

  • Also called needle vibrator
  • Three parts: power unit, flexible tube, vibratory head
  • Vibrating head introduced into fresh concrete to vibrate and consolidate
  • Power: engine or electricity
  • Vibrating needle = straight iron tube with eccentric weight inside

ii. Plate Vibrators

  • Used to compact concrete with lesser thickness
  • Surface vibrator used extensively in slab construction

iii. Vibrating Screed

  • Surface vibrator capable of acting over large sections of concrete surface
  • Used in finishing bridge decks and road pavements

iv. Form Vibrator

  • External vibrators attached to concrete formwork
  • Vibrate the form, which in turn vibrates the concrete
  • Used in precast concrete production

v. Concrete Rollers

  • Static rollers for compacting plum concrete or roller compacted concrete (RCC)
  • Used for concrete with greater thickness
  • Generally used in compaction of dams

f. Finishing Equipment

  • After compaction, concrete surface finished by troweling, floating, or belts
  • Hand trowels for smaller surfaces
  • Power trowels for larger areas
  • Power floats level concrete to accurate levels, powered by electric motors

Factors for Selecting Cranes 2072

Past Exam (2072 Magh): What are the factors to be taken into consideration while selecting cranes for lifting of materials? [4]
  1. Maximum load to be lifted: The crane must have sufficient lifting capacity (Safe Working Load, SWL) for the heaviest load.
  2. Working radius required: The distance from the crane center to the farthest point of load placement.
  3. Lifting height: The maximum height to which loads must be raised (e.g., number of stories in building).
  4. Site conditions: Available space for crane erection, ground bearing capacity for foundation, accessibility, and obstructions.
  5. Frequency of lifts: Number of lifts per hour/day — determines whether a high-capacity fixed crane or multiple mobile cranes are needed.
  6. Type of load: Size, shape, weight distribution, and fragility of materials (precast elements, steel sections, formwork, concrete buckets).
  7. Duration of use: Short-term use favors renting mobile cranes; long-term projects justify tower cranes.
  8. Cost: Owning/rental cost, erection/dismantling cost, operating cost (fuel, operator), and transport cost.
  9. Safety considerations: Proximity to power lines, wind conditions, and regulatory requirements.

Classification of Cranes 2076

Broadly cranes are classified into four categories:

a) Stationary / Derrick Crane

  • Also called derrick crane
  • Gives exceptionally large working radius
  • Can be designed for any lifting capacity
  • Power: steam, electric, or diesel-driven
  • Applications: building construction, civil works, industrial erection, ports, shipyards, cargo handling
  • Components: boom, bull wheel, and guy ropes

b) Overhead / Gantry Crane

  • Used for handling materials over a rectangular area (factories, workshops)
  • Moves in any direction and handles loads
  • Hoisting mechanism mounted on carriage/crab traveling on a girder
  • Girder moves longitudinally on rails on an overhead gantry

c) Mobile Crane

  • Mounted on mobile chassis
  • Truck cranes: high mobility
  • Crawler cranes: slow but stable

Types of mobile cranes:

  • Crawler crane
  • Telescopic boom truck mounted cranes
  • Lattice boom truck mounted cranes
  • Rough terrain cranes
  • All-terrain cranes
  • Heavy lift cranes

d) Tower Crane

  • Used in construction of industrial and multi-storeyed residential buildings
  • Provides high lifting capacity and good working radius
  • Takes up limited space around the worksite
  • Generally fixed base type, mounted on a foundation
  • Structural sections can be added to increase height
  • Vertical limit called “maximum free standing” height
  • Self-erecting tower to greater height is fairly easy and economical
  • Self-erecting crane has hydraulically operated mechanisms below the slewing ring
  • Also used in assembling high industrial plants and loading/unloading works

Principal components of tower crane:

  • Undercarriage
  • Slewing mechanism
  • Slewing platform
  • Tower with operator's cabin
  • Hoisting and operating mechanism

Lifting System (Luffing System):

  • System that constantly increases lifting capacity of a crawler crane
  • Components: trailing counterweight, expendable counterweight, luffing system, luffing jib/derrick

  • Two methods of tunnel construction: conventional (drilling & blasting) and TBM

A. Conventional Method 2078 2070

  • Excavation of tunnel by drilling and blasting = conventional method of tunnel construction
  • Excavation by manual equipment is done for soft soil
  • For rocks, drilling and blasting method is adopted
  • The process of taking out excavated/blasted materials from the tunnel is known as mucking
  • Mucking is done manually in wheelbarrows for small sections, whereas for larger diameter tunnels, loaders and railway wagons are used
  • Excavated material is taken out as the tunnel progresses in depth
  • Ventilation is needed when excavation is at sufficient depth. Arrangements are done through exhaust pipes — one pulls foul air from inside and another pushes fresh air inside
  • Power is supplied to run equipment like exhaust fans, drilling machines, etc.

Equipment used in conventional tunnel construction:

  • Excavators for soft ground
  • Drilling machines
  • Compressors to run drilling heads
  • Earth transporting equipment for mucking excavated material
  • Exhaust fans for ventilation
  • Compressors to supply fresh air

B. Tunneling by Tunnel Boring Machine (TBM) 2078 2076 2070

  • Modern tunnel constructions use TBMs
  • Programmed head (moles) drills through mountain to bore tunnel
  • Muck taken out through a pipe attached to boring machine
  • Very fast and efficient method
  • However, machines are very costly
  • Length of tunnel must justify use — economical for longer tunnels
  • Available in different capacities and diameters
  • Can progress along fixed co-ordinates in X, Y, and Z directions

Advantages of TBM 20782076

Past Exam (2078 Chaitra): Introduce TBM and its advantage in tunnel construction. [Part of 7]
Past Exam (2076 Bhadra): Write short notes on: TBM [5]
  1. Speed: Much faster than conventional drill & blast method — can advance 10-20 meters per day vs 2-5 m/day by D&B.
  2. Continuous operation: Excavation, mucking, and lining can proceed simultaneously.
  3. Safety: Reduced exposure of workers to hazards (rock falls, blast fumes). The shield protects workers.
  4. Minimal ground disturbance: Reduces settlement and vibration impacts on surface structures.
  5. Smooth tunnel profile: Produces circular profile requiring less concrete lining material.
  6. Environmental: Less noise, vibration, and dust compared to blasting.
  7. Automation: Can be programmed to follow precise coordinates (X, Y, Z) with laser guidance.
  • Disadvantages: Very high capital cost, long mobilization time, difficult to transport, limited flexibility for varying cross-sections, not economical for short tunnels

Steps in Conventional Tunnel Construction (Drill & Blast) 2078

Past Exam (2078 Chaitra): Explain the steps to be followed in sequence while carrying out tunneling work by using conventional method. [Part of 7]
  1. Setting out & survey: Establish tunnel alignment, grade, and cross-section using survey instruments.
  2. Drilling: Drill holes into the rock face using jack hammers, pneumatic drills, or jumbo drills in a predetermined pattern.
  3. Charging: Load explosives (dynamite, ANFO) into drilled holes. Insert detonators and connect wiring.
  4. Blasting: Detonate charges in a controlled sequence. All personnel evacuated to safe distance before firing.
  5. Ventilation: Allow blast fumes to clear. Run exhaust fans — one pulling foul air out, one pushing fresh air in.
  6. Scaling: Remove loose rock from roof and walls to make the work area safe for the next cycle.
  7. Mucking: Remove blasted rock debris using loaders, dumpers, or rail wagons. Muck is transported to disposal area.
  8. Support installation: Install temporary support — rock bolts, shotcrete, steel ribs, or wire mesh (NATM approach).
  9. Lining: Apply permanent concrete lining when sufficient length has been excavated.
  10. Repeat cycle: Advance to next face and repeat the entire process (called the “advance per round”).

Factors for Deciding Extent of Mechanisation 2073

Past Exam (2073 Bhadra): Write down the factors to be considered for deciding the extent of mechanisation of construction project. [4]
  1. Volume and nature of work: Large-volume earthwork, concrete work, and repetitive tasks justify mechanisation. Small, scattered works do not.
  2. Availability of labor: Where manual labor is scarce or expensive, mechanisation is essential. In labor-surplus areas, it may cause unemployment concerns.
  3. Quality requirements: Machine-mixed concrete, machine-compacted earth, and machine-finished surfaces produce more uniform quality than manual work.
  4. Time constraint: If project completion time is tight, mechanisation speeds up work significantly.
  5. Site accessibility: Remote or difficult terrain may necessitate equipment, but narrow/confined spaces may prevent its use.
  6. Cost comparison: Compare equipment cost (owning + operating + transport) vs manual labor cost for the same output.
  7. Future utilization: If the equipment can be reused in future projects, purchase is more justified.
  8. Environmental & safety: Hazardous work (demolition, deep excavation) favors mechanisation for worker safety.

Must-Know Points

  1. Advantages of equipment: Increased output, reduced cost, adverse weather capability, quality maintenance, safety, no social/emotional factors
  2. Disadvantages: Breakdown delays, needs skilled operators, heavy investment, spare parts inventory, unemployment concerns
  3. Excavators: Backhoe (below grade), Power Shovel (above grade), Dragline (soft/wet soil, long reach), Clamshell (vertical lifting)
  4. Compaction by soil type: Sheepsfoot → Clay; Vibratory → Sand/gravel; Pneumatic → All types; Smooth wheel → Asphalt finishing; Rammer/Plate → Confined areas
  5. Concrete equipment chain: Crusher → Screening → Batching Plant → Mixer → Transit Mixer → Pump → Vibrator
  6. Crane selection factors: Max load, working radius, lifting height, site conditions, frequency, load type, duration, cost, safety
  7. TBM advantages: Speed (10-20 m/day), continuous operation, safety, minimal ground disturbance, smooth profile, less noise
  8. Tunnel D&B steps: Setting out → Drilling → Charging → Blasting → Ventilation → Scaling → Mucking → Support → Lining → Repeat
  9. Mechanisation factors: Volume, labor availability, quality needs, time, site access, cost comparison, future use, safety
  10. Purchase vs Lease: Purchase for >60-70% utilization; Lease for short-term; Hire-Purchase when capital constrained
  11. Equipment cost: Owning costs (depreciation, insurance, tax) vs Operating costs (fuel, maintenance, operator wages, repair)
  12. Selection criteria: Nature of work, site conditions, project duration, equipment size/capacity, availability, cost economics (own vs rent vs hire-purchase), commercial considerations
