What is an Accident?

An accident is an unplanned and unexpected occurrence which upsets the planned sequence of events and actions, resulting in the loss of production, injury to the person, and damage to plants and equipment.

Causes of Accidents

In the construction industry, there are many causes of accidents. Broadly, the causes may be classified as follows:

Physical Causes — Relating to Machines

  • Working space for machines may be inadequate
  • Obstruction-free movement of man and material is not possible
  • Machines not being properly placed or adjusted
  • Unsuitable machines not being properly guarded

Physical Causes — Relating to Tools

  • Due to constant use, tools being blunt
  • Tools employed on the job too small
  • Tools used may be of brittle nature (e.g., blades of a circular saw)
  • Handle of tool may be too short or loose
  • Tools used may be unsuitable for the job

Physical Causes — Relating to Materials

  • Materials used may be poisonous and dangerous (acids, salts, etc.)
  • Material may be too hot (tar or bitumen in road construction) with no proper precautions taken
  • Explosives, petroleum products, and chemicals being handled carelessly
  • Not adopting proper precautions while working with materials that emit foul gases

Physical Causes — Relating to Uniform

  • Uniform may be loose
  • Shoes may be slippery and loose
  • No protective device being used (e.g., while welding)
  • Sleeves of a shirt being without buttons

Physical Causes — Relating to Environment

  • Poor lighting arrangement at site of work
  • Poor ventilation and unhygienic conditions
  • Non-insulated wires left carelessly
  • Loose electric cables
  • Obstacles in the working area
  • Floor being slippery
  • Scaffolding used being unsafe
  • Use of unsuitable (weak or short) ladders
  • External disturbances like noise
  • Loose discipline among workers

Objectives of Accident Prevention

  • Saving human life
  • Prevention of temporary and permanent injury
  • Prevention of damages to plants and equipment
  • Saving in the cost of compensation to workers
  • Saving in loss of time due to injury

Classification of Accidents

  1. According to cause of occurrence
  2. According to nature of injury:
    • Temporary disablement
    • Partial disablement
    • Total disablement
    • Death
  3. According to severity of injury:
    • Minor accident
    • Major accident
    • Accident hazard

Safety Requirement

Many safety standards use the concept of a safety requirement to ensure that the system carries out the functions needed to make it acceptably safe. A safety requirement has two components:

  1. Safety functions requirement: Details how the system should avoid the occurrence of a hazard
  2. Safety integrity requirement: Details how often the function can fail and allow the hazard to occur

Why Safety is Important in Construction?

In Nepal, the construction industry is labor-intensive and has the largest labor force after agriculture. The cost of an accident is very expensive.

  1. Humanitarian reasons: The sufferings and distress of the injured worker and family members are difficult to quantify in economic terms
  2. Economic reasons: Accidents have direct and indirect costs
    • Direct costs: Medical expenses, compensation amount, legal charges, cost of replacement of damaged material
    • Indirect costs: Slowdown in progress, productive time lost, decrease in productivity due to moral decrease, need to make up for lost time, loss of confidence, loss of administrative time
  3. Organizational image: Good safety record enhances morals, loyalty, and public image of the organization
  4. Laws and Regulations: The employer must adhere to regulations laid down by the Government for the safety of employees. Violation may lead to punishment

Factors Influencing Safety

During construction, safety is influenced by decisions taken during planning and design. Clear separation of traffic from construction zones can greatly reduce possibility of accidental collisions. Safety also depends on:

  • Education and training of workers
  • Vigilance and cooperation during construction
  • Choice of proper technology and machines
  • Proper choice of materials
  • Educating managers and workers about proper procedures and hazards

Safety Measures in Construction Areas

Safety measures are required in the following areas:

  • Excavation
  • Scaffolding, ladders, and formworks
  • Hot bitumen work
  • Demolition works
  • Drilling and blasting works

Approach to Improve Safety

  1. Organizational approach: Safety committee, development of safety system, incentives
  2. Physical approach
  3. Behavioral approach
  4. Danger signs and signals
  5. Caution signs and signals
  6. Informative signs and signals

Safety-related Acts and Standards

  • The Occupational Safety and Health Act
  • The Health and Safety at Work Act, 1974 (UK)
  • ILO Convention and Recommendation, 1981
  • Labor Act 2074, Nepal

Labor Act 2074 — Safety Provisions

Special Provisions for Construction

  • Contractor is an employer in this case
  • Employer should provide required tools, materials, and safety equipment to workers
  • Employer should provide required measures of safety and security at site
  • Safety and Health Committee: When 20+ workers are engaged, a safety and health committee must be constituted

Employer's Duties

  • Making appropriate safety and health arrangements
  • Ensuring no adverse effect on workers from use of chemicals, physical or biological liquids
  • Disseminating necessary notice, information, and training related to safety and health
  • Putting signs to indicate safety or health hazards
  • Managing gas, chemical waste so as not to cause adverse effect on local animals, people, or environment

Employee's Duties

  • Refraining from doing activities likely to cause adverse effect on safety and health
  • Cooperating with the employer to ensure health and safety
  • Compulsorily using personal safety devices provided by the employer

Stopping Work

In case of immediate threat of injury, adverse health effect, or damage to devices, the worker should inform the employer who should stop work until the threat is prevented or minimized.

11.2 Workman's Compensation Board

Compensation refers to all forms of pay or rewards going to employees arising from their employment. It includes money received in performance of work, plus benefits and services organizations provide.

Two types: Direct compensation and Indirect compensation.

Workers' compensation is a form of insurance providing wage replacement and medical benefits to employees injured in the course of employment, in exchange for the employee's right to sue.

Types of Compensation

  • Wage and Salary
  • Incentives
  • Benefits
  • Performance-based pay

Social Security System in Nepal

Three components of legal environment of pay governance:

  1. The Legal Environment:
    • Labor Act 2074 provisions: Equal pay/no discrimination, minimum wages, provident fund, overtime payment, Dashain allowances, gratuity, pension, leave
    • Bonus Act
    • Public Service Regulations
    • Company Act 1996
    • Constitution of Nepal, 2072
  2. Pay System Governance
  3. Institutional Framework: Department of Labor, Wage Board, Pay Commission/Committee, Tribunals, Labor Court

Contribution Based Social Security Act 2074

Enacted to ensure the rights of employees under Labor Act such as gratuity, golden handshake, etc. Employers are required to deposit contributions monthly, within 15 days from end of month. Interest at 10% charged for late deposits.

Fire Safety

Fire safety refers to precautions taken to prevent or reduce the likelihood of a fire that may result in death, injury, or property damage, alert those in a structure to the presence of fire, enable evacuation, or reduce damage caused by a fire.

Fire Safety Plan Structure

  • Key contact information
  • Utility services (including shut-off valves for water, gas, electric)
  • Access issues
  • Dangerous stored materials
  • Location of people with special needs
  • Connections to the sprinkler system
  • Layout, drawing, and site plan of building
  • Maintenance schedules for life safety systems
  • Personnel training and fire drill procedures

Fire Regulations (Nepal Building Code)

NBC provides fundamental requirements for fire safety in ordinary buildings.

  • Important Buildings: Those housing facilities essential before/after disaster (hospitals, police stations, communication centers), housing several persons (cinema halls, schools, convention centers), national/international importance (palaces), or hazardous facilities (toxic, explosive)
  • Ordinary Buildings: Buildings not in the important category (commercial, office buildings)

Types of Construction and Appliances — Fire Places

  • All buildings with kitchen should have fireplace or chimney
  • Open hearths should be discouraged
  • Timber construction should not be used near fireplace and should remain encased by plaster
  • Portable stoves (kerosene, gas) should be stored in suitable containers
  • Where open fire used for cooking, sufficient water should be ready for use
  • In urban areas, demarcation of fire zones as required by municipal requirements

General Requirements

  • All buildings designed for containment of fire to reduce spread
  • Access: Wide enough for fireman to easily approach building
  • Wide doors: Sufficient for fireman access
  • Fire escape ways: Sufficient ways to allow evacuation of all occupants
  • Open space: Front entrance with enough open space for gathering and firefighting

Exit Requirements

  • Exits must be continuous from street to roof or neighboring building
  • Must allow evacuation in relatively short time
  • Meet minimum size requirements
  • Free of obstructions
  • Clearly visible with proper signs

Number of Exits and Stairs

  • Buildings exceeding 500 sq.m plinth area: minimum one internal and one external fire escape
  • Residential buildings: minimum stair width 90 cm; other buildings: 1.5 m
  • Distance from any point in passageway to staircase: not exceed 20 meters
  • Buildings more than 5 stories: separate fire escape with minimum width 75 cm, tread width 20 cm, riser not more than 19 cm, max 15 risers per flight

Exit Doors

  • Open to passageway or corridor
  • Open outwards without restricting movement
  • Maximum distance from any point: 20 m
  • Minimum 90 cm wide and 180 cm high

Lightning Arresters/Conductors

Many incidents in Nepal where lightning strikes have caused fire. Lightning arresters should be installed at the highest part of building, connected by conductor to an earth rod buried in the earth.

Insurance

Insurance is primarily used to hedge against the risk of uncertain, contingent loss. The insured assumes a known relatively small loss (premium) in exchange for the insurer's promise to compensate in case of financial loss.

Characteristics of Insurable Risks

  • Large number of similar exposure units: Benefits from the law of large numbers
  • Definite loss: Loss at known time, place, and cause
  • Accidental loss: Fortuitous, outside control of beneficiary
  • Large loss: Meaningful from perspective of insured
  • Affordable premium: Likelihood and cost proportionate
  • Calculable loss: Probability estimable and cost assessable
  • Limited catastrophic risk: Losses ideally independent and non-catastrophic

Types of Insurance

  • Health insurance: Covers cost of medical treatment
  • Accident, sickness, and unemployment insurance: Financial support when unable to work
  • Short-term disability: Covers up to six months
  • Long-term disability: Covers until permanently disabled
  • Total permanent disability insurance: Benefits when permanently disabled
  • Workers' compensation insurance: Replaces wages and medical expenses for job-related injury
  • Property insurance: Protection against fire, theft, weather damage

Methods of Insurance

  • Co-insurance: Risks shared between insurer and insured
  • Dual insurance: Same risk covered by two policies
  • Self-insurance: Individuals themselves bear the risk
  • Reinsurance: Insurer passes some/all risks to another insurer (reinsurer)

Construction Works Insurance (Contractor's All Risk — CAR)

CAR Policy provides an "All Risks" cover. Any hazard not specifically excluded is covered. It covers sudden and unforeseen loss or damage from the starting date until the Defects Liability Period.

Contractor's risks covered:

  • Loss of or damage to Works, Plant, and Materials
  • Loss of or damage to Equipment
  • Loss of or damage to property in connection with the Contract
  • Personal injury or death

CAR Policy perils covered:

  • Fire and Lightning
  • Accidental damage during construction (falling, defective workmanship, negligence, malicious act, human error)
  • Water damage, Flood, Storm, Tempest, Earthquake
  • Collapse, Collision, Impact
  • Theft & Burglary, Malicious Damage
  • Subsidence, Landslide, Rockslide

Fire Insurance

Governed by Nepal fire policy. To constitute fire under fire policy, three features required:

  1. There should be actual ignition
  2. It should be fortuitous/accidental in nature
  3. The property destroyed or damaged must be insured

Standard fire insurance policy covers: Fire and Lightning, Explosion, Flood and Inundation, Storm/Cyclone/Hurricane, Strike/Malicious damage, Subsidence and Landslides, Earthquake fire and shock, Aircraft damage, etc.

Environmental Law

Environmental law encompasses those rules, norms, values, and laws that relate primarily to the protection of the whole environment — air, water, and land against pollution, the protection of ecosystems together with public information, remedies, and procedures.

Environmental law, in its broadest sense, is an instrument for achieving sustainability. It requires standards, gives permanence to policies, and is based on scientific understanding and clear analysis. These laws set rules for human conduct and lead to communities living within the limits of the earth.

Major Features of Environmental Law

  • Often framework acts requiring detailed regulations to be made effective
  • Commencement is often delayed or staggered
  • Include unclear definitions to preserve flexibility in application
  • Confer wide discretions in areas like protection, setting standards (e.g., amount of pollutant discharged), and enforcement

International Environmental Law (Selected)

  • Stockholm Declaration 1972
  • World Heritage Convention 1972
  • CITES 1973 (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Wild Flora and Fauna)
  • World Charter for Nature 1982
  • Vienna Convention for Protection of Ozone Layer
  • Montreal Protocol 1987 (Substances depleting Ozone Layer)
  • Basel Convention 1989 (Hazardous Wastes)
  • Rio Declaration 1992 (Convention on Biodiversity)
  • UN Framework Convention on Climate Change 1992
  • Kyoto Protocol 1997

Emerging Principles of International Environmental Law

  • Precautionary Principle
  • Polluter Pays Principle
  • Principle of EIA
  • Principle of Sustainable Development
  • Principle of Inter-Generational Equity
  • Principle of Common but Differentiated Responsibility
  • Principle of Strict Liability
  • The Doctrine of Public Trust
  • Permanent Sovereignty over Natural Resources (PSNR)

Implementation Status in Nepal

Nepal, being a member of UN, SAARC, SACEP, and ICIMOD, has played a significant role in the field of environment. Nepal participated in the UN Conference for Environment (UNCHE) at Stockholm and the Earth Summit in June 1992.

Environment Protection Act 1997 (Nepal)

Enacted to maintain clean and healthy environment, minimize adverse impacts, and achieve sustainable development through proper use and management of natural resources.

Salient Features

  • Defines 'Environment', 'IEE' (Initial Environmental Examination), and 'EIA' (Environmental Impact Assessment) — which must be submitted before implementing any development proposal
  • Section 3, 4, 5, 6: Contain provisions on IEE, EIA, and procedures for getting approval
  • Section 7: Prevention and control of pollution
  • Section 8: Appointment of 'Environment Inspector'
  • Section 9: Protection of national heritage
  • Section 10: Authorizes GoN to declare Environment Protection Areas for areas containing aesthetic/rare wildlife, biological diversity, or places of historical/cultural importance
  • Provision for establishment of 'Environment Protection Fund'
  • Provision of compensation to victims of pollution
  • Empowers Ministry to prohibit any material, fuel, equipment, or plant having adverse effects on environment

Shortcomings of the Act

  • Has not recognized public participation effectively in EIA and IEE processes
  • EIA guideline should be mandatory under Section 23
  • Prevention and control of pollution provisions are almost dead in practice
  • No appointment of Environment Inspectors till date
  • No separate section for biodiversity conservation
  • Laboratories not established
  • No 'Environment Protection Fund' to date
  • Punishment provisions should be reviewed and limits fixed scientifically

National Building Code (NBC)

A building code, or building control, is a set of rules that specify the standards for constructed objects such as buildings and nonbuilding structures. The main purpose is to protect public health, safety, and general welfare as they relate to the construction and occupancy of buildings and structures.

Building codes are generally intended for architects, engineers, constructors, and regulators but used by safety inspectors, environmental scientists, real estate developers, subcontractors, facility managers, tenants, insurance companies, and others.

NBC Background in Nepal

Most of the buildings in Nepal are highly vulnerable to earthquakes. The 1988 earthquake in eastern Nepal resulted in more than 600 deaths and 20,000 buildings collapsed or damaged, including school buildings.

As a result, the government ultimately developed the National Building Code (NBC) which provides both regulations and guidelines for design of buildings in all areas of Nepal. Nepal lies in a high seismic zone, and NBC plays a significant role in earthquake-resistant design of buildings.

Although NBC came into existence in 1994, it was approved by the government only in 2003.

Four Levels of NBC

Part I: International State of the Art

NBC 000 — Applicable to modern and sophisticated buildings such as BICC, constructed considering building codes of developed countries.

Part II: Professionally Engineered Buildings

Standard code requirements for professionally qualified engineers. Covers structures such as hospitals, meeting halls, factories, warehouses, multistoried buildings, and residential buildings where:

  • Plinth area > 1000 sq. feet
  • Story > 3 numbers
  • Structural span > 4.5 meters

Related codes: NBC 101 to 114 and NBC 206 to 208.

Part III: Mandatory Rules of Thumb

For buildings not requiring professional engineer design (plinth area ≤ 1000 sq. ft, stories ≤ 3, span ≤ 4.5 m). Can be designed by trained level technicians.

  • NBC 201: RC Buildings with Masonry Infill
  • NBC 205: RC Buildings without Masonry Infill
  • NBC 202: Load Bearing Masonry

Part IV: Guidelines for Remote Rural Buildings

Address typical building styles identified from approximately 40 intensive surveys. Emphasize changes to improve seismic resistance. Normally earthen construction (unfired masonry, mud mortar, rubble, dry stone).

  • NBC 203: Earthquake Resistance Construction — LSM
  • NBC 204: Earthquake Resistance Construction — Earthen Buildings

Building Permit Application Forms

  • NBC 206: Architectural Design Requirement
  • NBC 000:1994 to NBC 114:1994 — Structural Design Requirement
  • NBC 207:2003 — Electrical Design Requirement
  • NBC 208:1994 — Sanitary and Plumbing Design Requirement

NBC Significance

  • Maintaining uniformity for materials and techniques
  • Focusing on earthquake-resistant design
  • Emphasizing use of spaces with proper guidelines
  • Guiding engineers, sub-engineers
  • Flowing information and standards for electrical and sanitary designs
  • Serving as basic document to verify designs submitted for approval

Challenge: Construction as per the spirit of the code is not happening due to resource lack, lack of effective coordination and evaluation mechanisms.

Building Bye-Laws

It is mandatory to have building permission from Town Development Office, Metro/sub-Metro, Municipalities, and VDCs. Building bye-laws are prepared to ensure public health and safety, planned development, and planned built environment.

Building Bye-Laws cover:

  • Right of Way (ROW)
  • Set back
  • Floor Area Ratio (FAR)
  • Ground Coverage Ratio (GCR)
  • Height of the Building
  • Guidelines for Cultural Heritage Zone
  • Clearance required from important sites

Planning Bylaws: Deal with elements like land use, size of plot, area and its depth-width ratio, length of cul-de-sac, area of open space, public facility requirements.

Quality Control (QC)

Quality Control is a process by which entities review the quality of all factors involved in production. This approach places emphasis on three aspects:

  1. Elements: Controls, job management, defined and well-managed processes, performance and integrity criteria, identification of records
  2. Competence: Knowledge, skills, experience, qualifications
  3. Soft Elements: Personnel integrity, confidence, motivation, team spirit, and quality relationships

Quality involves single product inspection, where every product is examined before delivery to the external market, using inspection for fine details to detect surface blemishes and other defects.
