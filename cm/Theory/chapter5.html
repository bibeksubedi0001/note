5.1 Methods of Work Execution 2076

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

  1. Define the method of work execution in reference to the PPA 2063 and PPR 2064. [Part of exam] (2076)

Work execution is the procurement of any goods, consultancy services, and construction works. Prior to procurement, the organization shall have specifications, plans, drawings, designs, special requirements, or other descriptions.

Broadly any construction works can be executed through two methods:

  1. Through Amanat (Force Account)
  2. Through Contract

Amanat (Force Account)

Amanat can be defined as force account. Public Procurement Act (PPA) 2063 states that any work such as repair and maintenance of ordinary nature, regular petty work, or sanitation may be done by force account.

  • Execution of public works by using the resources of a public agency of the government, without recourse to competitive bidding or negotiated contract
  • May apply in works costing up to NRs. 100,000 as per PPA

Situations for Force Account:

  • The size, nature, and location of the works being unsuitable for competitive bidding
  • Works must be carried out without disrupting existing operations, by crews familiar with those operations
  • When the implementing agency can bear the associated risks better than the contractor
  • No contractor is interested in carrying out the works
  • Emergencies
  • Institutional development

Through Contract

Works can be executed through sealed bidding, sealed quotation, or any other method except force account. PPA, PPR along with Guidelines of Public Procurement Monitoring Office (PPMO) describes types of work execution as per the total cost.

1. National Competitive Bidding (NCB)

Also called local competitive bidding. All eligible domestic bidders are invited to participate. Notice is published in national newspaper with a period of at least 30 days. NCB is necessary for the public entity if estimated amount of work is greater than 20 lakhs or estimated goods are greater than 10 lakhs.

2. International Competitive Bidding (ICB)

When the amount of work is big and national/domestic contractors cannot perform the job, eligible bidders are invited from all over the world. Tender notice shall be published in international/national newspaper in English language providing a period of at least 45 days.

Conditions for ICB:

  • Goods or construction works not available under competitive price from more than one entrepreneur within Nepal
  • No bid was submitted in response to NCB
  • Goods/works must be procured from abroad under a foreign assistance agreement
  • Public Entity certifies goods/works are of complex and special nature requiring international level bidding

3. Sealed Quotation

For goods, services valuing up to one million Rupees and construction works valuing up to two million Rupees. Notice shall be published in national or local newspaper with a period of at least fifteen days.

  • A form of specifications, quality, conditions of supply and time shall be prepared before inviting
  • The sealed quotation, once submitted, cannot be withdrawn or amended
  • The lowest evaluated sealed quotation within the estimate shall be approved

4. Direct Procurement

Expendable or capital goods or consultancy services valuing up to three lakhs and construction work valuing up to five lakh Rupees may be directly procured. A construction work cannot be directly procured from the same individual/company more than one time in a fiscal year.

5. Through Users Committee

Construction works may be carried out by the beneficiary community if economy, quality, or sustainability is increased.

6. Procurement under Special Circumstances

Emergency/special circumstances or when only one supplier has exclusive right or technical efficiency.

5.2.1 Definition of Contract

According to Contract Act 2056: "Contract is an agreement between two or more persons to do or not to do something, which can be enforced by law."

An Engineering Contract is a mutual undertaking negotiated between two parties for the purpose of carrying out, on a commercial basis, certain clearly specified work. The main objective of entering into a contract is to seek legal action/remedies if any party breaches the contract.

"All contracts are agreements but all agreements are not contracts."

Agreement + Legality = Contract

An agreement is the acceptance of the offer (proposal) with or without any condition. It may not have a legal obligation. But a contract is an agreement concluded between two or more parties for performing or not performing any work with legal enforceability.

Elements of a Valid Contract

  1. Offer and Acceptance: If a person advances a proposal and another makes acceptance, a contract exists. If no time limit is specified, it must be accepted within reasonable time. If the offerer dies or becomes insane before acceptance, no contract exists.
  2. Consideration: The cause, motive, price or impelling influence that induces a contracting party to enter into a contract. Something of value exchanged by contracting parties.
  3. Capacity to contract: All parties must be capable. Idiots, drunkards, insane, and children below 16 years are not capable of signing contracts.
  4. Lawful purpose: An illegal contract is invalid.
  5. Possibility of performance: An impossible contract is invalid.
  6. Free consent: The contract should not involve coercion, fraud, undue influence, or deceit.
  7. Certainty: Vague/ambiguous contracts are not valid.
  8. Legal relationship: Clear intention of parties to enter into contract with necessary documents.
  9. Written form: Verbal agreement cannot be a contract.
  10. Two or more competent parties

Contract as per Enforceability

  1. Valid Contract: If all elements of the contract are present.
  2. Voidable Contract: Contracts that a party may make void:
    • Forceful contract or contract against free consent
    • Entered due to undue influence
    • Contract involving fraud or misstatement
  3. Void Contract: Null and void:
    • Contrary to statutory law
    • Ambiguous, vague, and unlimited contract
    • Not the possibility of performance
    • Contrary to public policy and welfare
    • Signed by incompetent parties

Purpose of Engineering Contract

  • Define the quantity and quality of work to be done
  • Define the time frame for completion and payment
  • Set out in advance courses of action if either party fails to perform
  • Define words and establish common meanings
  • Specify what is and is not included in the contract
  • Specify how the contract will be terminated
  • Specify responsibilities to parties and third parties

5.2.2 Types of Contract

Contracts are classified by three characteristics:

  1. Method of payment for the work
  2. Method by which the contractor is selected
  3. Method of allocating technical and administrative responsibility

A. Classification by Method of Payment

(a) Lump-Sum / Stipulated Sum Contracts

The contractor based on available complete set of plans and specifications quotes one single price which covers all direct costs (field staff, machines, materials) and indirect costs (office supervision, secretarial support, equipment maintenance) and includes the profit of the contractor.

(b) Unit-Price / Schedule of Rates Contracts

The project is broken down into work items with measurable units (Cum, Sq.m, R.m, Nos). An estimated quantity is given for each work item. The contractor quotes a unit price for each item. Total price = unit price × estimated quantity for all items. The lowest reasonable bid is selected. Progress payments are based on precise field measurement.

(c) Cost-Plus Contracts

The contractor is reimbursed for all costs incurred plus a variable fee to cover overhead costs and profit. Four types of fee structures:

i. Cost + Percent of Cost: Oldest form. Lucrative for contractor but subject to abuse — little incentive for efficiency since higher costs = higher fee.

ii. Cost + Fixed Fee: A fixed amount of fee regardless of cost fluctuation. Gives contractor incentive to finish quickly. However, contractor may use expensive methods to expedite completion.

iii. Cost + Fixed Fee + Profit Sharing: A target price is specified. If contractor completes under target, savings are shared (typically contractor gets 25% of savings). Example: Target Rs.10M, actual Rs.9.5M → bonus = 25% of Rs.0.5M = Rs.125,000.

iv. Cost + Sliding Fee: Provides bonus for underrun AND penalizes for overrun:

Fee = R (2T - A)

Where: T = Target price, R = Base percent value, A = Actual cost of construction

Advantages of Cost-Plus (Owner's Position)

  • Permits reduction of design-construct time by utilizing phased construction
  • Quick reaction to major design changes, minimizes adversary position
  • Opportunity to utilize contractor expertise during design phase
  • Owner may participate fully in management and control
  • May eliminate marginal contractors by letting work to prequalified subcontractors

Advantages of Cost-Plus (Contractor's Position)

  • Avoidance of risk inherent in fixed-price contracting
  • Work plan items not needed as in competitively bid contract
  • Opportunity to obtain future work from harmonious relationship
  • Job site can be staffed and managed efficiently

Disadvantages of Cost-Plus (Owner's Position)

  • May not be most competitive in market
  • Disreputable contractor can abuse the arrangement
  • Owner's involvement is increased
  • Definition of reimbursable items can be source of disputes

Disadvantages of Cost-Plus (Contractor's Position)

  • Fees may be minimal compared to profit potential
  • Planning and control functions made difficult by simultaneous construction and design
  • Contractor may resent owner participation in tasks they'd normally control
  • Contractor's reputation may suffer from delays or cost overruns

B. Classification by Method of Selecting the Contractor

(a) Competitively Tendered Contracts

The owner invites bids for the works following a formal competitive bidding procedure. A number of tenderers submit bids based on complete plans and specifications. The contract is awarded to the lowest responsible bidder. This is the most widely used form of engineering contract.

(b) Negotiated Contracts

The principal negotiates directly with a contractor to arrive at a mutually satisfactory agreement. Major items of negotiation are:

  • Level and amount of fee in addition to the charge schedule
  • Quality of the works to be performed
  • Projected time for completion

Although all types of contracts can be adopted, the most common form concluded is COST+FEE, since design documentation is often not complete at the time of negotiation.

C. Classification by Technical & Administrative Responsibility

(a) Traditional Approach

A design team prepares estimates and specifications before contractor selection. Design and construction are separated by the tendering period. The owner employs a designer for plans, then exercises monitoring during construction. Construction itself is the responsibility of a single general contractor.

Advantages: Ensures competition. Works well provided:

  • Design is complete before tender stage (certainty)
  • Designer understands how construction will be undertaken (buildability)
  • Design does not change substantially during construction

Types under traditional: Lump-sum, Unit-price, Negotiated cost-plus fee contracts.

(b) Owner-Builder Approach

Owners perform both their own design work and some or all of the actual construction with their own forces (force account). Owner may retain management and conceptual design responsibilities while using consultants for detailed design and construction contractors for actual work.

(c) Design and Build (D&B) Contract

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

  1. Define design and build contract and explain with its advantages and disadvantages. [5] (2075 Bhadra)

The main contractor is responsible for both the design and construction work for an agreed lump-sum price. The employer has control over design elements in their requirements, but once the contract is let, responsibility for design passes to the contractor.

(d) EPC Contract (Engineering, Procurement and Construction)

Under an EPC contract, the contractor designs, procures the necessary materials, and builds the project, either directly or by subcontracting part of the work. The contractor carries project risk for schedule and budget in return for a fixed price.

(e) Turn-Key Contract

A project constructed so it could be sold as a completed product. "Just turn a key in the door." The contractor is entrusted to design, construct, commission, and handover the project. The employer makes lump-sum payment at different stages.

Advantages:

  • Responsibility lies with a single firm, relieving the promoter
  • Project is put into operation more rapidly than other contracts
  • Minimal owner coordination required between construction, design, and other elements
  • Opportunity for construction economies to be incorporated during design phase

Disadvantages:

  • No firm project cost until design is well underway
  • Quality may be subordinated to ensure profitability under lump sum
  • Very few checks and balances; owner may not be fully aware of design/construction problems
  • Completed project may not fully comply with owner expectations

(f) BOOT Contract (Build-Own-Operate-Transfer)

A form of project financing where a private entity receives a concession from public or private sector to finance, design, construct, and operate a facility. This enables the proponent to recover investment, operating, and maintenance expenses through fees over the concession period.

Major components of BOOT:

  • Build: Design, manage, implement, procure, construct and finance
  • Own: Own the asset for the concession period
  • Operate: Manage and operate the plant, carry out maintenance, deliver product/service and receive payment
  • Transfer: Handover plant in operating condition at end of concession period

Advantages of BOOT:

  • Promotion of private investment
  • Completion of projects on time without cost overruns
  • Good management and efficient operation
  • Transfer of new and advanced technology
  • Utilization of foreign companies' resources
  • Additional financial sources for priority projects
  • No burden on public budget for infrastructure development

Comparison of Turn-Key and BOOT

  • BOOT projects are contractor-financed turnkey contracts
  • In turnkey contracts, governments bear risk of financing and operating; in BOOT, promoter bears these risks
  • In turnkey, feasibility studies are by the principal; in BOOT, by the promoter
  • In turnkey, operation is by principal after commissioning; in BOOT, promoter operates over concession period

(g) Construction Management Contracts 2079

Past Exam (2079 Shrawan): Define construction management contracts. What are the advantages? [Part of 5]

Construction management is defined as that group of management activities related to a construction program, carried out during pre-design, design, and construction phases. Particularly suitable for entities that periodically build complex structures but do not desire to maintain construction staff to supervise projects regularly.

Advantages:

  • Professional management expertise throughout all project phases
  • Better coordination between design and construction
  • Early involvement of construction knowledge during design reduces rework
  • Faster project delivery through phased construction (fast-tracking)
  • Owner retains control over design and contractor selection
  • Transparent cost management — owner sees all subcontract bids and costs
  • Reduced adversarial relationships compared to traditional contracting

(h) BOO Contract (Build-Own-Operate) 2079

Past Exam (2079 Jestha): Explain with suitable examples: EPC contract, Turn key contract, BOO contract and BTO contract. [4]

In a BOO contract, the private entity builds, owns, and operates the facility permanently — there is no transfer back to the government. The operator maintains ownership indefinitely and earns revenue through user fees or service charges.

Example: A private company builds and permanently operates a waste treatment plant, charging municipalities for waste processing services.

(i) BTO Contract (Build-Transfer-Operate)

In a BTO contract, the private entity builds the facility and transfers ownership to the government immediately upon completion, but then operates and maintains it under a management/lease agreement for an agreed period.

Example: A private company builds a hospital, transfers ownership to the government, but operates it under a 20-year management contract.

Definition

A tender is an offer in writing by the tenderer to execute some specified work or to supply specified goods at a certain rate/amount within a fixed time frame under certain conditions of agreement. It is the first step in the formulation of the contract.

Why Tendering?

  • To select the best contractor
  • To get quality work
  • To get work at a competitive price
  • To ensure transparency
  • Public-private partnership (encourage contracting procedure)

Bidding Stages / Procedures

i. Single-Stage One-Envelope (1S1E)

Bidders submit a single envelope containing both the Financial Proposal and Technical Proposal. Usually for procurement of construction work having estimated amount less than Rs. 2 Crore.

ii. Single-Stage Two-Envelope (1S2E)

Bidders submit two sealed envelopes simultaneously — one containing Technical Proposal and other the Price Proposal — enclosed together in an outer single envelope. This is the most common and popular method in Nepal.

iii. Two-Stage Bidding Procedure

Used when it is not feasible to define fully the technical aspects or terms and conditions at the time of invitation.

1st Stage: Bidders submit only technical proposal (no price). Discussions are held, specifications modified, evaluation system determined.

2nd Stage: Bidders whose bids were not cancelled from 1st stage are invited to submit bids along with price in accordance with revised documents.

Tender Notice

Information inviting bids from contractors. Should be widely published in newspaper.

Details in Tender Notice:

  • Name and address of Public Entity
  • Nature of work and its location
  • Place of delivery / construction work to be performed
  • Amount of bid security and validity period
  • Date, time, place for tender document availability
  • Cost of tender document
  • Place, manner, deadline for submission
  • Provision of e-bidding and its process
  • Place, date, time for opening of bids
  • Expected date of acceptance

For NCB: at least 30 days. For ICB: at least 45 days. Domestic preference may be given to Nepalese entrepreneurs.

5.4 Bid Evaluation & Award 2079207720762072

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

  1. What do you mean by procurement and contract? What is the process of bidding, bid evaluation and contract award in public procurement? [10] (2072 Ashwin)
  2. Write short note on: Substantially responsive bid. [Part of exam] (Various years)
  3. What is the purpose of bid evaluation? Describe the process. [Part of 10] (2070 Magh)

Opening of Bids

The Public Entity shall open bids in the presence of the bidders or their representative. There shall be no constraint for opening bids due to absence of a bidder.

Preliminary Examination of Bids

Purpose: To identify and reject bids that are incomplete as required by bidding documents before detailed evaluation.

Principal areas covered: Verification of signature, registration, agreement, eligibility, bid security, completeness.

Detailed Bid Evaluation

  • Evaluation shall be on the basis of information in the bids alone
  • Comparison made by fixing quoted price excluding VAT
  • Technical, commercial, and financial aspects shall be evaluated
  • Arithmetic errors: If discrepancy between unit rate and total amount → unit rate shall prevail
  • Figures vs Words: If discrepancy → amount in words shall prevail

Substantially Responsive Bid

A bid is substantially responsive if it does not contain any major deviations from the bidding documents. The purpose is to reject bids not responsive to major commercial and technical requirements.

Commercial reasons for rejection: Inadequate bid security, inability to meet critical work periods, failure to comply with minimum capability, conditional bids.

Technical reasons for rejection: Failure to bid for required scope of work, failure to quote for each item in BOQ.

LESRB (Lowest Evaluated Substantially Responsive Bid)

The bid having the lowest bid price whose qualification is in conformity with evaluation criteria shall be the LESRB. The Public Entity shall select for acceptance only the LESRB.

Letter of Intent & Award

Within 7 days of bid selection, the Public Entity shall serve a notice of intent of acceptance. The contract is awarded to the selected bidder, who must provide performance bond within 15 days.

Performance Security

Amount of money deposited by successful bidder as security against performance. General amount = 5% of bid price.

If LESRB's bid is within 15% below estimated price (BP ≥ 0.85×EP): PS = 5% of Bid Price.

If LESRB's bid is more than 15% below estimated price: PS = (0.85×EP - BP)×0.5 + 5% of Bid Price.

If bids are excessively low or front-loaded: Extra 8% of bid price demanded.

Refunded after completion of Defect Liability Period. If work is unsatisfactory, the fund is forfeited.

Performance Guarantee Calculation Example 2081

Past Exam (2081 Shrawan): Calculate performance guarantee amount required for a contractor whose bidding amount is Rs 1,00,50,000. The estimated cost of the project is Rs 2,00,00,000. As per PPR 2064. [2]

Given: BP (Bid Price) = Rs 1,00,50,000; EP (Estimated Price) = Rs 2,00,00,000

Step 1: Check if BP ≥ 0.85 × EP

0.85 × EP = 0.85 × 2,00,00,000 = Rs 1,70,00,000

Since BP (1,00,50,000) < 0.85 × EP (1,70,00,000), the bid is >15% below estimate.

Step 2: Apply additional PS formula:

Additional PS = (0.85 × EP − BP) × 0.5 = (1,70,00,000 − 1,00,50,000) × 0.5 = 69,50,000 × 0.5 = Rs 34,75,000

Step 3: Basic PS = 5% of BP = 0.05 × 1,00,50,000 = Rs 5,02,500

Total Performance Guarantee = Rs 34,75,000 + Rs 5,02,500 = Rs 39,77,500

5.5 Prequalification 207920772074207320722070

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

  1. Define pre-qualification of a contractor. [Part of 10] (2070 Magh)
  2. What is prequalification? Describe its advantages and disadvantages. [5] (2073 Magh)
  3. What is pre-qualification of contractors? [Part of 10] (2071 Magh)
  4. What is the importance of prequalification of contractors in tendering? [5] (2074 Bhadra)
  5. Define the importance of pre-qualification in bidding process. [5] (2077 Chaitra)
  6. Define the importance of pre-qualification of contractors in tendering. [3] (2079 Jestha)

Pre-Qualification is the assessment by an implementing agency of the suitability of firms to carry out a particular contract prior to being invited to submit a bid. It is a kind of shortlisting of the eligible bidder to avoid crowding.

It ensures invitation is extended only to prospective bidders who have adequate capacity and resources, considering:

  • Experience and past performance on similar contracts
  • Capabilities w.r.t. personnel, equipment, and construction facilities
  • Financial position
  • Litigation history

Matters in Pre-qualification Documents

  • Qualification criteria for the proposed work
  • Documents to be submitted by bidders to prove qualifications
  • Procurement to be made by separate lots or packages
  • Method of preparing the proposal
  • Procedure for evaluating pre-qualification proposal
  • Major terms and conditions
  • Method, place, deadline for submitting proposals

Benefits of Prequalification

  • Enables prospective bidders to form joint ventures for better chances
  • Qualified firms price competitively knowing they compete against qualified bidders only
  • Employer can assess interest and adjust procurement conditions
  • Identifies conflicts of interest
  • Saves time evaluating unqualified bids
  • Encourages local firms to form JVs with international firms
  • Reduces problems from low-priced bids by unqualified bidders
  • Gives Financing Agencies indication of Employer's procurement ability

Disadvantages of Prequalification

  • May increase procurement lead time
  • Requires review of all applications (vs post-qualification reviewing only one)
  • Collusion easier among limited identified bidders
  • Subjective judgment in applying criteria may lead to bias

Post-Qualification

The pre-qualification process is not adopted. All eligible bidders participate in the bidding process.

Pre-bid Conference

For big projects, a pre-bid conference is conducted to appraise tenderers of the project. Questions and replies should form part of the contract. Employers should not hesitate to relax rigid conditions for better offers.

5.6 Consultant Selection Methods 207920762072

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

  1. Explain different methods of consultant selection. [Part of 10] (2072 Ashwin)
  2. How are consultants selected? Describe the quality and cost based selection process. [5] (2076 Bhadra)
  3. Define the methods of consultant selection in brief. [5] (2079 Shrawan)

a) Quality and Cost Based Selection (QCBS)

Both quality (Technical Proposal) and costs of consulting services are considered. Technical and Financial Proposals are submitted separately in sealed envelopes, then put in an outer sealed envelope.

b) Quality Based Selection (QBS)

Appropriate for complex or highly specialized assignments for which it is difficult to define precise TOR. Similar to QCBS except that Financial Proposal of the highest-ranked firm in Technical Proposal only is opened. Negotiations on Financial Proposal and Contract follow.

c) Least Cost-Based Selection

More appropriate for assignments of standard or routine nature. Financial Proposals of all consultants passing minimum Technical score are opened.

d) Fixed Budget Selection (FBS)

Used when the budget is fixed and the best technical proposal within the budget is selected.

e) Consultants' Qualifications Selection (CQS)

Based on qualifications and past experience of the consultant.

f) Single-Source Selection (SSS)

Directly negotiated with a specific consultant without competitive process.

Expression of Interest (EOI)

EOI is the process of shortlisting consulting firms. The EOI notice shall indicate: name and address of public entity, description of proposed assignment, source of funding, qualifications required, profile of firm, details of experience, etc.

After shortlist, Request for Proposals (RFP) is issued to shortlisted firms only.

Types of Contract for Consultancy Service

  1. Time-based contract: Fee = rate × time consumed. For work where period cannot be predicted (e.g., supervision of construction, complex designs).
  2. Performance-based contract: Fee based on work completed or as and when required.
  3. Lump-sum contract: For clearly identified work with low risk (feasibility study, project design, preparation of bidding documents).
  4. Percentage-based contract: Payment as percentage of estimated or actual cost of the concerned work. For inspection, architect's services, supervision, or monitoring.
  5. Indefinite service period contract: For consultancy required from time to time — arbitrators, dispute resolution, procurement consultants, technical troubleshooters.

Sub-contracting

The practice of assigning part of the obligations and tasks under a contract to another party known as a subcontractor. As per PPA/PPR of Nepal, only 25% of the contract value can be sub-contracted.

Conditions of Contract

Terms and references that bind all parties of the agreement. Purpose: easy and smooth running of work and minimizing disputes. Contains two parts:

General Conditions of Contract (GCC)

  1. Definition & interpretations
  2. Time for completion and delays
  3. Payment to contractor
  4. Execution of work and measurement of completed work
  5. Breach of contract
  6. Arbitration — settlement of dispute
  7. Suspension of work
  8. General obligation of the contractor
  9. Labor and labor welfare

Special Conditions of Contract (SCC)

Some conditions may be changed by mutual agreement between parties based on the law of the country, nature of work, etc.

Contract Agreement

A contract may consist of multiple documents which collectively contain all essentials and are usually linked by cross-reference. Engineering contract documents usually contain:

  • Contract agreement
  • Letter of acceptance
  • Contractor's bid
  • Special conditions of contract (SCC)
  • General conditions of contract (GCC)
  • Specifications
  • Drawings
  • Bill of quantities (priced BOQ)
  • Addenda
  • Any other relevant documents

Priority of Document

The documents forming the contract shall be interpreted in the following order of priority:

  1. Contract Agreement
  2. Letter of Acceptance
  3. Contractor's Bid
  4. Special Conditions of Contract
  5. General Conditions of Contract
  6. Specifications
  7. Drawings
  8. Bill of Quantities
  9. Addenda

Factors in Preparing Contract Documents

  • The contract must be fair
  • The contract must be clear
  • Contract language must be consistent
  • No repetition (say it once, say it in proper place)
  • Use each part of the contract for its proper purpose
  • Contract information must be retrievable
  • Use foresight (foresee possible problems and address them)
  • If you want it, get it in the contract

Interpretation of Contract

Contra Proferentem Rule: If a contract is capable of two different interpretations, the interpretation favoring the party that did not draft the document shall be adopted. Ambiguity is resolved in favor of the party that did not prepare the contract document.

5.8 Important Contract Provisions

Liquidated Damages (LD) 20792076207520742072

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

  1. Define: Liquidity damage. [Part of exam] (Various years)
  2. Explain liquidated damages and its application. [Part of 5] (2076)

Liquidated (delay) damages are remedies available to any contracting party to compensate for financial loss suffered from a proven breach of contract.

  • In Common Law Countries: Liquidated Damages
  • In Civil Law Countries: Contractual Penalties

Rate: Generally 0.05% of the contract price per day, not exceeding 10% of total contract price.

LD is NOT applicable if delay is due to:

  • Delay in giving possession of the site
  • Carrying out extra works
  • Interfering extra items / increase in scope of works
  • Price adjustment

Price Adjustment

For contracts with duration exceeding 15 months, the price may be adjusted. Maximum adjustment shall not exceed 25% of the contract amount.

Price Adjustment Formula:

Pn = A + b(Ln/L0) + c(Mn/M0) + d(En/E0) + etc.

Where: Pn = price adjustment factor, A = constant factor, b, c, d = coefficients of each cost element (labor, materials, equipment), Ln, Mn, En = current cost indices, L0, M0, E0 = base cost indices (30 days prior to bid submission).

Variation Order (VO)

After starting work, if addition/reduction in quantity or addition of new items is required due to technical reasons, the competent authority may issue a variation order for a variation of up to 15%.

Advance Payment / Mobilization Amount

May be made to supplier/construction entrepreneur up to 20% of the procurement contract price. The advance shall be deducted from every running bill.

Provisional Sum

An amount allocated for specialized work by a specialized firm, where details are not available at time of tender. It represents the best guess by the employer or consultant. Items that can be included: lab test, insurance, OSH, site office construction, environmental safeguards, purchasing survey instruments, etc.

Contingency

Estimated costs of "known-unknowns" based on past experience. In public projects, contingencies are limited to 4% of estimated cost (2% each for work charge establishment and minor physical contingencies).

For projects with construction period greater than 12 months: additional 20% (10% each for variations and price adjustment) must be provisioned in the budget.

Interim Payment Certificates (IPC) / Running Bill

Payment certificates issued periodically for work carried out. Based on cumulative valuation of work done. Payments on account are neither binding nor conclusive of acceptance of the work.

Taking Over Certificate (TOC)

The most important milestone — handing over the works to the client. The Site Manager must carefully study the Conditions of Contract to ensure notices are given in writing. Defect Liability Period (DLP) starts from the date of issuing TOC and is typically one year.

Defect Liability Period (DLP)

Period after TOC where the contractor is responsible for rectifying defects. Starts from the date of issuing TOC. Typically one year for permanent works.

5.9 Joint Venture (JV)

A business arrangement where two or more parties agree to pool resources for a specific task. Each participant is responsible for profits, losses, and costs. The venture is its own entity, separate from participants' other business interests. Allows both parties to share burden and resulting profits.

5.11 Other Important Terms

Line of Credit (LC)

A flexible loan from a bank/financial institution. A limited amount of money that can be accessed as needed and repaid over a pre-specified period. Interest paid only on money borrowed.

Breach of Contract

  • Violation of an obligation, engagement, duty or agreement
  • Non-performance of a promise
  • Refusal or neglect to execute as per contract
  • Disrespect of contractual obligations

Effects: Termination, claims, disputes, and arbitration situations. A serious breach may be a "contract killer."

Termination of Contract

  • When no further action is required
  • By breach
  • By mutual agreement
  • By frustration (unforeseeable event destroys the basis of the contract)

Incoterms

International Commercial Terms published by International Chamber of Commerce (ICC). A series of three-letter trade terms used in international transactions. Incoterms 2010 defines 11 rules (reduced from 13 in 2000). Seven rules apply regardless of transport method, four apply only to water transport.

5.12 FIDIC & FCAN

FCAN (Federation of Contractors' Associations of Nepal)

Umbrella organization of all Nepalese contractors, formed in 1997. Objectives:

  • Unite all construction companies to protect, promote, and develop professional capabilities
  • Promote unity, co-operation, and mutual help among contractors
  • Advise and assist members in technical, non-technical and legal issues
  • Render suggestions to government regarding policies, rules, and principles
  • Mitigate dependence on foreign contractors
  • Improve technical knowledge through lectures, seminars, etc.
  • Acquire membership of international associations
  • Involve in social service activities

FIDIC (Fédération Internationale Des Ingénieurs Conseils)

International Federation of Consulting Engineers, formed in 1913. Promotes interests of consulting engineering firms globally. Best known for range of standard conditions of contract. FIDIC forms are the most widely used standard contracts in construction industry.

  4. Green Book: Conditions of Short Form of Contract
  5. Blue Book: Contract for Dredging and Reclamation Works
  6. White Book: Client/Consultant Model Services Agreement
  7. Gold Book: FIDIC Design, Build and Operate Projects

  • Prequalification: Definition, purpose, advantages/disadvantages — asked 6 times; know criteria (experience, financial capability, equipment, personnel)
  • 8-mark long answer: Expect comprehensive question on contract types or tendering process

Must-Know Points

  1. Valid contract essentials: Offer & acceptance, competent parties, lawful object, consideration, free consent, certainty
  2. Work execution methods: Amanat (departmental), Negotiation, Contract (competitive bidding)
  3. ICB vs NCB: ICB for international/donor projects (45-day notice, English); NCB for national (>20 lakh, 30-day notice)
  4. Bid security: 2-3% of contract value; Performance security: 5-13% of contract value
  5. PS formula (if BP < 0.85×EP): PS = 5% of BP + 0.5 × (0.85×EP − BP)
  6. LOA (Letter of Acceptance): Issued to selected bidder requesting performance security & contract signing
  7. Consultant selection: QCBS (Quality & Cost Based), QBS, FBS, LCS, SSS, CQS
  8. Contract documents priority: Agreement → LOA → Bid → SCC → GCC → Specifications → Drawings → BOQ
  9. FIDIC Rainbow Suite: Red (Construction), Yellow (Plant/D&B), Silver (EPC/Turnkey), Green (Short Form), Blue (Dredging), White (Consultant), Gold (DBO)
  10. Liquidated damages: 0.05% per day, max 10% of contract price; NOT applicable for employer-caused delays
  11. BOOT vs BOO vs BTO: BOOT = transfer after concession; BOO = permanent ownership; BTO = immediate transfer, operate under lease
  12. CM contract advantages: Professional management, design-construction coordination, fast-tracking, transparent costs, reduced adversarial relations
  13. Sample tender notice must include: Entity name, work description, estimated amount, bid security, submission deadline, opening date, bidding method, validity period
