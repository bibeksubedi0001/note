PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

  • Work execution = procurement of goods, consultancy services, and construction works
  • Prior to procurement → organization must have specifications, plans, drawings, designs, special requirements, or other descriptions

Two broad methods of executing construction works:

  1. Through Amanat (Force Account)
  2. Through Contract

Amanat (Force Account)

  • Definition: Force account — execution of public works using resources of a public agency, without competitive bidding or negotiated contract
  • PPA 2063 states: repair/maintenance of ordinary nature, regular petty work, or sanitation may be done by force account
  • May apply in works costing up to NRs. 100,000 as per PPA

Situations for Force Account:

  • The size, nature, and location of the works being unsuitable for competitive bidding
  • Works must be carried out without disrupting existing operations, by crews familiar with those operations
  • When the implementing agency can bear the associated risks better than the contractor
  • No contractor is interested in carrying out the works
  • Emergencies
  • Institutional development

Through Contract

  • Works executed through sealed bidding, sealed quotation, or any other method except force account
  • PPA, PPR along with Guidelines of PPMO describes methods of work execution as per total cost

As per Public Procurement Regulation (PPR), the methods of execution of work are:

  1. International Competitive Bidding (ICB) — for works > threshold amount (5 Arab NPR)
  2. National Competitive Bidding (NCB) — for works > 20 Lakh NPR
  3. Sealed Quotation — for works up to 20 Lakh NPR
  4. Direct Procurement — for works up to 5 Lakh NPR or emergency
  5. Through Users Committee — community works up to 1 Crore (simple nature)
  6. Force Account (Amanat) — government departments, up to NRs. 100,000
  7. Procurement under Special Circumstances — emergency/single source

1. National Competitive Bidding (NCB)

  • Also called local competitive bidding — all eligible domestic bidders invited to participate
  • Notice published in national newspaper with period of at least 30 days
  • NCB necessary if estimated work > 20 lakhs or estimated goods > 10 lakhs

2. International Competitive Bidding (ICB)

  • When work is big and domestic contractors cannot perform → eligible bidders invited from all over the world
  • Tender notice published in international/national newspaper in English, period of at least 45 days

Conditions for ICB:

  • Goods or construction works not available under competitive price from more than one entrepreneur within Nepal
  • No bid was submitted in response to NCB
  • Goods/works must be procured from abroad under a foreign assistance agreement
  • Public Entity certifies goods/works are of complex and special nature requiring international level bidding

3. Sealed Quotation

  • For goods/services up to Rs. 10 Lakh and construction works up to Rs. 20 Lakh
  • Notice published in national/local newspaper, period of at least 15 days
  • A form of specifications, quality, conditions of supply and time shall be prepared before inviting
  • The sealed quotation, once submitted, cannot be withdrawn or amended
  • The lowest evaluated sealed quotation within the estimate shall be approved

4. Direct Procurement

  • Goods/consultancy services up to Rs. 3 Lakh and construction work up to Rs. 5 Lakh
  • Cannot be directly procured from same individual/company more than once in a fiscal year

5. Through Users Committee

  • Construction works carried out by beneficiary community
  • Used when economy, quality, or sustainability is increased
  • Up to Rs. 1 Crore for works of simple nature

6. Procurement under Special Circumstances

  • Emergency/special circumstances
  • When only one supplier has exclusive right or technical efficiency

5.2.1 Definition of Contract 2078 2076

  • As per Contract Act 2056: "Contract is an agreement between two or more persons to do or not to do something, which can be enforced by law."
  • Engineering Contract: A mutual undertaking negotiated between two parties for carrying out clearly specified work on a commercial basis
  • Main objective: To seek legal action/remedies if any party breaches the contract

"All contracts are agreements but all agreements are not contracts."

Agreement + Legality = Contract

An agreement is the acceptance of the offer (proposal) with or without any condition. It may not have a legal obligation. But a contract is an agreement concluded between two or more parties for performing or not performing any work with legal enforceability.

ANSWER: What is contract and what are its essential elements? Explain how it is important in any large scale construction project? [4+2] (2078 Kartik)

Definition: As per Contract Act 2056, "Contract is an agreement between two or more persons to do or not to do something, which can be enforced by law."

Essential Elements of a Valid Contract:

  1. Offer and Acceptance: A valid proposal by one party and acceptance by another
  2. Consideration: Something of value exchanged between contracting parties
  3. Capacity to contract: All parties must be competent (not minors, insane, etc.)
  4. Lawful purpose: Contract must not be illegal
  5. Possibility of performance: Must be physically possible to perform
  6. Free consent: No coercion, fraud, undue influence, or deceit
  7. Certainty: Terms must be clear, not vague or ambiguous
  8. Legal relationship: Clear intention of parties with necessary documents
  9. Written form: Verbal agreement cannot be a contract
  10. Two or more competent parties

Importance in large scale construction projects:

  • Defines quantity and quality of work to be done
  • Defines time frame for completion and payment
  • Sets courses of action if either party fails to perform
  • Specifies responsibilities to parties and third parties
  • Provides legal protection and remedies for breach
  • Reduces disputes by establishing clear terms
ANSWER: "All contracts are agreements but all agreements are not contracts" — Justify [3] (2079 Jestha)
  • Agreement = acceptance of an offer/proposal, with or without conditions; may not have legal obligation
  • Contract = agreement concluded between two or more parties for performing/not performing work, enforceable by law
  • Agreement + Legality = Contract
  • Every contract is an agreement because it involves offer and acceptance
  • But NOT every agreement is a contract — only those with legal enforceability qualify
  • Example: Agreement to have dinner is not a contract (no legal binding); agreement to build a house for payment IS a contract

Elements of a Valid Contract 2078 2076

  1. Offer and Acceptance: If a person advances a proposal and another makes acceptance, a contract exists. If no time limit is specified, it must be accepted within reasonable time. If the offerer dies or becomes insane before acceptance, no contract exists.
  2. Consideration: The cause, motive, price or impelling influence that induces a contracting party to enter into a contract. Something of value exchanged by contracting parties.
  3. Capacity to contract: All parties must be capable. Idiots, drunkards, insane, and children below 16 years are not capable of signing contracts.
  4. Lawful purpose: An illegal contract is invalid.
  5. Possibility of performance: An impossible contract is invalid.
  6. Free consent: The contract should not involve coercion, fraud, undue influence, or deceit.
  7. Certainty: Vague/ambiguous contracts are not valid.
  8. Legal relationship: Clear intention of parties to enter into contract with necessary documents.
  9. Written form: Verbal agreement cannot be a contract.
  10. Two or more competent parties

Contract as per Enforceability

  1. Valid Contract: If all elements of the contract are present.
  2. Voidable Contract: Contracts that a party may make void:
    • Forceful contract or contract against free consent
    • Entered due to undue influence
    • Contract involving fraud or misstatement
  3. Void Contract: Null and void:
    • Contrary to statutory law
    • Ambiguous, vague, and unlimited contract
    • Not the possibility of performance
    • Contrary to public policy and welfare
    • Signed by incompetent parties

Purpose of Engineering Contract

  • Define the quantity and quality of work to be done
  • Define the time frame for completion and payment
  • Set out in advance courses of action if either party fails to perform
  • Define words and establish common meanings
  • Specify what is and is not included in the contract
  • Specify how the contract will be terminated
  • Specify responsibilities to parties and third parties

A. Classification by Method of Payment 2076 2075 2071

(a) Lump-Sum / Stipulated Sum Contracts

  • Contractor quotes one single price based on complete plans and specifications
  • Covers all direct costs (field staff, machines, materials) and indirect costs (office supervision, secretarial support, equipment maintenance)
  • Includes contractor's profit
  • Risk is on the contractor — if costs exceed the lump sum, contractor absorbs the loss

(b) Unit-Price / Schedule of Rates Contracts

  • Project broken down into work items with measurable units (Cum, Sq.m, R.m, Nos)
  • An estimated quantity is given for each item
  • Contractor quotes a unit price for each item
  • Total price = unit price × estimated quantity for all items
  • Lowest reasonable bid selected; progress payments based on precise field measurement
  • Most common type in Nepal for government projects
ANSWER: Write short notes on Unit price contract and its disadvantages [3] (2077 Chaitra)

Unit Price Contract: Project is broken into measurable work items. Contractor quotes unit price for each item. Payment based on actual quantities measured in field multiplied by quoted unit rates.

Disadvantages:

  • Final cost is uncertain until all work is measured — overruns possible
  • Requires detailed measurement of every item — time-consuming
  • Disputes may arise over measurement methods and quantities
  • Contractor may "unbalance" bid by inflating rates for items likely to increase
  • Requires complete BOQ preparation before tendering

(c) Cost-Plus Contracts

  • Contractor reimbursed for all costs incurred plus a variable fee to cover overhead and profit
  • Four types of fee structures:

i. Cost + Percent of Cost:

  • Oldest form — very lucrative for contractor but subject to abuse
  • Little incentive for efficiency since higher costs = higher fee

ii. Cost + Fixed Fee:

  • Fixed amount of fee regardless of cost fluctuation
  • Gives contractor incentive to finish quickly
  • Drawback: contractor may use expensive methods to expedite completion

iii. Cost + Fixed Fee + Profit Sharing:

  • A target price is specified; if completed under target, savings are shared
  • Typical formula: contractor gets 25% of savings below target
  • Example: Target Rs.10M, actual Rs.9.5M → bonus = 25% of Rs.0.5M = Rs.125,000

iv. Cost + Sliding Fee:

  • Provides bonus for underrun AND penalizes for overrun

Fee = R (2T - A)

Where: T = Target price, R = Base percent value, A = Actual cost of construction

Advantages of Cost-Plus (Owner's Position):

  • Permits reduction of design-construct time by utilizing phased construction
  • Quick reaction to major design changes, minimizes adversary position
  • Opportunity to utilize contractor expertise during design phase
  • Owner may participate fully in management and control
  • May eliminate marginal contractors by letting work to prequalified subcontractors

Advantages of Cost-Plus (Contractor's Position):

  • Avoidance of risk inherent in fixed-price contracting
  • Work plan items not needed as in competitively bid contract
  • Opportunity to obtain future work from harmonious relationship
  • Job site can be staffed and managed efficiently

Disadvantages of Cost-Plus (Owner's Position):

  • May not be most competitive in market
  • Disreputable contractor can abuse the arrangement
  • Owner's involvement is increased
  • Definition of reimbursable items can be source of disputes

Disadvantages of Cost-Plus (Contractor's Position):

  • Fees may be minimal compared to profit potential
  • Planning and control functions made difficult by simultaneous construction and design
  • Contractor may resent owner participation in tasks they'd normally control
  • Contractor's reputation may suffer from delays or cost overruns

Classification by Method of Payment:

  1. Lump-Sum Contract — single fixed price for entire work
  2. Unit-Price Contract — rate per measured work item
  3. Cost-Plus Contract — actual cost + fee/percentage

Lump Sum Contract:

  • Contractor quotes one single price based on complete plans and specifications
  • Covers all direct costs (staff, machines, materials) and indirect costs (overhead, maintenance) + profit
  • Risk lies with contractor — cost overruns absorbed by contractor
  • Used when scope is well-defined and design is complete

Cost Plus Contract:

  • Contractor reimbursed for all actual costs incurred + a fee for overhead and profit
  • Four types: Cost + Percent of Cost, Cost + Fixed Fee, Cost + Fixed Fee + Profit Sharing, Cost + Sliding Fee
  • Advantages: Permits fast-tracking, quick reaction to design changes, contractor expertise during design
  • Disadvantages: Owner bears cost risk, potential for abuse, increased owner involvement needed
  • Formula for sliding fee: Fee = R(2T − A) where T=target price, R=base percent, A=actual cost

B. Classification by Method of Selecting the Contractor

(a) Competitively Tendered Contracts

  • Owner invites bids following a formal competitive bidding procedure
  • Multiple tenderers submit bids based on complete plans and specifications
  • Contract awarded to the lowest responsible bidder
  • Most widely used form of engineering contract

(b) Negotiated Contracts

  • Principal negotiates directly with a contractor for mutually satisfactory agreement
  • Major items of negotiation: fee level/amount, quality of work, projected completion time
  • Most common form concluded is COST+FEE (since design documentation often not complete at negotiation time)

C. Classification by Technical & Administrative Responsibility

(a) Traditional Approach

  • Design team prepares estimates and specifications before contractor selection
  • Design and construction are separated by the tendering period
  • Owner employs designer for plans, then monitors during construction
  • Construction is responsibility of a single general contractor

Works well provided:

  • Design is complete before tender stage (certainty)
  • Designer understands how construction will be undertaken (buildability)
  • Design does not change substantially during construction

Types under traditional: Lump-sum, Unit-price, Negotiated cost-plus fee contracts.

(b) Owner-Builder Approach

  • Owners perform both design work and some/all construction with their own forces (force account)
  • Owner retains management and conceptual design responsibilities
  • May use consultants for detailed design and contractors for actual work

(c) Design and Build (D&B) Contract

  • Main contractor responsible for both design and construction for an agreed lump-sum price
  • Employer has control over design elements in their requirements
  • Once contract is let, responsibility for design passes to contractor
  • Contractor often appoints own consultants or uses in-house design

  1. Traditional Approach: Design team prepares plans/specs before contractor selection. Design and construction separated by tendering period. Construction by single general contractor.
  2. Owner-Builder: Owner performs own design and some/all construction (force account).
  3. Design & Build (D&B): Single contractor handles both design and construction for lump-sum price.
  4. EPC (Engineering, Procurement, Construction): Contractor designs, procures materials, and builds. Carries risk for schedule and budget for fixed price.
  5. Turn-Key: Contractor entrusted to design, construct, commission, and handover. "Just turn a key in the door."

  7. Construction Management: Professional management firm coordinates all project activities. Owner retains control over design and contractor selection.

(d) EPC Contract (Engineering, Procurement and Construction) 2080 2079

  • Contractor designs, procures materials, and builds the project (directly or by subcontracting)
  • Contractor carries project risk for schedule and budget in return for a fixed price
  • Price is negotiated, finalized, and paid in mutually agreed installments
ANSWER: Explain EPC contract in brief [2] (2080 Chaitra)
  • EPC = Engineering, Procurement, and Construction
  • Single contractor responsible for complete project delivery — design (engineering), material procurement, and construction
  • Contractor carries project risk for both schedule and budget for a fixed price
  • May subcontract parts of the work
  • Paid in agreed installments as per contract milestones

(e) Turn-Key Contract 2079 2074

  • Project constructed so it could be sold as a completed product — "Just turn a key in the door"
  • Contractor entrusted to design, construct, commission, and handover the project
  • Employer makes lump-sum payment at different stages
  • Entire risk borne by the contractor

Advantages:

  • Responsibility lies with a single firm, relieving the promoter
  • Project is put into operation more rapidly than other contracts
  • Minimal owner coordination required between construction, design, and other elements
  • Opportunity for construction economies to be incorporated during design phase

Disadvantages:

  • No firm project cost until design is well underway
  • Quality may be subordinated to ensure profitability under lump sum
  • Very few checks and balances; owner may not be fully aware of design/construction problems
  • Completed project may not fully comply with owner expectations

(f) BOOT Contract (Build-Own-Operate-Transfer) 2079 2074 2073 2070

  • A form of project financing where a private entity receives a concession from public/private sector
  • Private entity finances, designs, constructs, and operates the facility
  • Recovers investment, operating, and maintenance expenses through fees over the concession period
  • Fee increases often tied to combination of internal and external variables

Major components of BOOT:

  • Build: Design, manage, implement, procure, construct and finance
  • Own: Own the asset for the concession period
  • Operate: Manage and operate the plant, carry out maintenance, deliver product/service and receive payment
  • Transfer: Handover plant in operating condition at end of concession period

Advantages of BOOT:

  • Promotion of private investment
  • Completion of projects on time without cost overruns
  • Good management and efficient operation
  • Transfer of new and advanced technology
  • Utilization of foreign companies' resources
  • Additional financial sources for priority projects
  • No burden on public budget for infrastructure development

Comparison of Turn-Key and BOOT

  • BOOT projects are contractor-financed turnkey contracts
  • In turnkey contracts, governments bear risk of financing and operating; in BOOT, promoter bears these risks
  • In turnkey, feasibility studies are by the principal; in BOOT, by the promoter
  • In turnkey, operation is by principal after commissioning; in BOOT, promoter operates over concession period

BOOT Contract (Build-Own-Operate-Transfer):

BOOT: Build-Own-Operate-Transfer — a form of project financing where private entity receives concession to finance, design, construct, and operate a facility, then transfers it to government after concession period.

Importance in Developing Countries like Nepal:

(g) Construction Management Contracts 2079

  • Group of management activities related to a construction program, carried out during pre-design, design, and construction phases
  • Particularly suitable for entities that periodically build complex structures but do not desire to maintain construction staff

Advantages:

  • Professional management expertise throughout all project phases
  • Better coordination between design and construction
  • Early involvement of construction knowledge during design reduces rework
  • Faster project delivery through phased construction (fast-tracking)
  • Owner retains control over design and contractor selection
  • Transparent cost management — owner sees all subcontract bids and costs
  • Reduced adversarial relationships compared to traditional contracting

(h) BOO Contract (Build-Own-Operate) 2079

  • Private entity builds, owns, and operates the facility permanently — no transfer back to government
  • Operator maintains ownership indefinitely and earns revenue through user fees or service charges
  • Example: Private company builds and permanently operates a waste treatment plant

(i) BTO Contract (Build-Transfer-Operate) 2079

  • Private entity builds the facility and transfers ownership to government immediately upon completion
  • Then operates and maintains it under a management/lease agreement for an agreed period
  • Example: Private company builds a hospital, transfers ownership, then operates under 20-year management contract

Definition

  • Tender = an offer in writing by the tenderer to execute specified work or supply specified goods at a certain rate/amount within a fixed time frame
  • It is the first step in the formulation of the contract
  • Tenders are invited through open or limited bidding process
ANSWER: Write short notes on Tendering Process [5] (2079 Shrawan)

Tendering Process is the formal procedure of inviting and evaluating bids for construction works:

  1. Preparation: Feasibility study, quantity estimation, cost estimate, approval, resource planning, tender document preparation
  2. Tender Notice: Published in newspaper (NCB: 30 days, ICB: 45 days) with work details, bid security, deadlines
  3. Document Sale: Bidding documents made available to interested contractors
  4. Pre-bid Conference: Clarifications and Q&A session for bidders (for complex projects)
  5. Bid Submission: Sealed bids submitted by deadline (1S1E, 1S2E, or Two-stage)
  6. Bid Opening: Opened publicly in presence of bidders or their representatives
  7. Evaluation: Preliminary examination → Detailed evaluation → LESRB determination
  8. Award: Letter of Intent issued within 7 days → Performance security submitted within 15 days → Contract signing

Why Tendering?

  • To select the best contractor
  • To get quality work
  • To get work at a competitive price
  • To ensure transparency
  • Public-private partnership (encourage contracting procedure)

Bidding Stages / Procedures

i. Single-Stage One-Envelope (1S1E)

  • Bidders submit single envelope containing both Financial and Technical Proposals
  • Usually for construction work with estimated amount less than Rs. 2 Crore

ii. Single-Stage Two-Envelope (1S2E)

  • Bidders submit two sealed envelopes simultaneously — Technical Proposal and Price Proposal — in an outer envelope
  • Most common and popular method in Nepal

iii. Two-Stage Bidding Procedure

  • Used when it is not feasible to define fully the technical aspects at time of invitation
  • 1st Stage: Bidders submit only technical proposal (no price); discussions held, specifications modified, evaluation system determined
  • 2nd Stage: Non-cancelled bidders invited to submit bids with price per revised documents

Tender Notice 2079 2075 2074 2072

  • Tender Notice = information inviting bids from contractors, published widely in newspaper

ANSWER: Explain Performance Security in brief [2] (2080 Chaitra)
  • Performance Security is a financial guarantee submitted by the successful bidder after contract award to ensure satisfactory completion of work
  • Normally 5% of bid price in cash deposit or bank guarantee
  • If BP < 0.85 × EP: PS = 5% of BP + 0.5 × (0.85×EP − BP)
  • If bids are excessively low or front-loaded: additional 8% may be demanded
  • Refunded after completion of Defect Liability Period
  • Forfeited if work is unsatisfactory or contractor defaults
  • If BP ≥ 0.85×EP (within 15% below): PS = 5% of Bid Price
  • If BP < 0.85×EP (more than 15% below): PS = 5% of BP + 0.5×(0.85×EP − BP)
  • If bids are excessively low or front-loaded: extra 8% of bid price demanded
  • Refunded after completion of Defect Liability Period
  • If work is unsatisfactory, fund is forfeited

Performance Guarantee Calculation Example 2081

Past Exam (2081 Shrawan): Calculate performance guarantee amount required for a contractor whose bidding amount is Rs 1,00,50,000. The estimated cost of the project is Rs 2,00,00,000. As per PPR 2064. [2]

Given: BP (Bid Price) = Rs 1,00,50,000; EP (Estimated Price) = Rs 2,00,00,000

Step 1: Check if BP ≥ 0.85 × EP

0.85 × EP = 0.85 × 2,00,00,000 = Rs 1,70,00,000

Since BP (1,00,50,000) < 0.85 × EP (1,70,00,000), the bid is >15% below estimate.

Step 2: Apply additional PS formula:

Additional PS = (0.85 × EP − BP) × 0.5 = (1,70,00,000 − 1,00,50,000) × 0.5 = 69,50,000 × 0.5 = Rs 34,75,000

Step 3: Basic PS = 5% of BP = 0.05 × 1,00,50,000 = Rs 5,02,500

Total Performance Guarantee = Rs 34,75,000 + Rs 5,02,500 = Rs 39,77,500

ANSWER: Explain about Pre-qualification of contractor / Why it is necessary [4/5] (2079 Shrawan, 2074 Bhadra, 2075 Bhadra, 2073 Magh, 2070 Bhadra)

Pre-Qualification: The process of evaluating and shortlisting contractors based on their capability, experience, and resources before they are invited to submit bids. It is a preliminary screening to ensure only competent firms participate in bidding.

Why Pre-Qualification is Necessary:

  1. Quality assurance: Only capable and experienced contractors compete, ensuring better quality of work
  2. Risk reduction: Reduces risk of awarding contract to unqualified firms that may default
  3. Time saving: Fewer bids to evaluate in detail, saving evaluation time
  4. Competitive pricing: Qualified firms price competitively knowing they compete against equally qualified bidders
  5. Avoid crowding: Filters out unqualified bidders, making bidding process manageable

Pre-qualification Criteria:

  1. Experience: Past performance on similar projects (size, complexity, type)
  2. Financial capacity: Bank statements, annual turnover, available credit lines
  3. Equipment: Owned or available equipment relevant to the work
  4. Personnel: Qualified key staff with relevant experience (CVs)
  5. Litigation history: Past and pending legal disputes
  6. Current commitments: Ongoing work and available capacity

Advantages: JV formation, competitive pricing, conflict identification, saves evaluation time, reduces problems from unqualified low bids.

Disadvantages: Increases procurement lead time, requires reviewing all applications, easier collusion among identified bidders, potential bias in subjective criteria.

Matters in Pre-qualification Documents

  • Qualification criteria for the proposed work
  • Documents to be submitted by bidders to prove qualifications
  • Procurement to be made by separate lots or packages
  • Method of preparing the proposal
  • Procedure for evaluating pre-qualification proposal
  • Major terms and conditions
  • Method, place, deadline for submitting proposals

Benefits of Prequalification

  • Enables prospective bidders to form joint ventures for better chances
  • Qualified firms price competitively knowing they compete against qualified bidders only
  • Employer can assess interest and adjust procurement conditions
  • Identifies conflicts of interest
  • Saves time evaluating unqualified bids
  • Encourages local firms to form JVs with international firms
  • Reduces problems from low-priced bids by unqualified bidders
  • Gives Financing Agencies indication of Employer's procurement ability

Disadvantages of Prequalification

  • May increase procurement lead time
  • Requires review of all applications (vs post-qualification reviewing only one)
  • Collusion easier among limited identified bidders
  • Subjective judgment in applying criteria may lead to bias

Post-Qualification

  • Pre-qualification process is not adopted
  • All eligible bidders participate in the bidding process
  • Qualification of the lowest evaluated bidder is checked after bid evaluation

5.6 Consultant Selection Methods 208120732069

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

  1. Explain methods of selection of consultants as per PPA and PPR in Nepal. [6] (2081 Shrawan)

a) Quality and Cost Based Selection (QCBS)

  • Both quality (Technical Proposal) and cost of consulting services are considered
  • Technical and Financial Proposals submitted separately in sealed envelopes, put in outer sealed envelope
  • Most commonly used method for consultant selection

b) Quality Based Selection (QBS)

  • Appropriate for complex or highly specialized assignments with difficult TOR definition
  • Only highest-ranked firm in Technical Proposal has Financial Proposal opened
  • Negotiations on Financial Proposal and Contract follow

c) Least Cost-Based Selection

  • Appropriate for assignments of standard or routine nature
  • Financial Proposals of all consultants passing minimum Technical score are opened
  • Lowest cost among technically qualified firms is selected

d) Fixed Budget Selection (FBS)

  • Budget is fixed and best technical proposal within budget is selected

e) Consultants' Qualifications Selection (CQS)

  • Based on qualifications and past experience of the consultant
  • Used for small assignments

f) Single-Source Selection (SSS)

  • Directly negotiated with a specific consultant without competitive process
  • Used in emergency or when only one qualified firm exists
ANSWER: Explain methods of selection of consultants as per PPA and PPR in Nepal [6] (2081 Shrawan)
  1. QCBS (Quality and Cost Based Selection): Both technical quality and cost evaluated. Most common method. Technical weightage typically 80%, Financial 20%.
  2. QBS (Quality Based Selection): Only quality evaluated; financial proposal of top-ranked firm opened and negotiated. For complex/specialized assignments.
  3. LCS (Least Cost Selection): Among firms meeting minimum technical score, lowest cost selected. For standard/routine tasks.
  4. FBS (Fixed Budget Selection): Budget is fixed; highest-quality proposal within budget wins.
  5. CQS (Consultant Qualification Selection): Based on qualifications and experience. For small, simple assignments.
  6. SSS (Single Source Selection): Direct negotiation without competition. For emergencies, continuity, or unique expertise.

Process: EOI notice → Shortlisting → RFP to shortlisted firms → Proposal submission → Technical evaluation → Financial evaluation → Negotiation → Contract award.

Expression of Interest (EOI)

  • EOI = process of shortlisting consulting firms
  • EOI notice indicates: name and address of public entity, description of assignment, source of funding, qualifications required, profile of firm, experience details
  • After shortlist, Request for Proposals (RFP) is issued to shortlisted firms only
ANSWER: Write short notes on Pre-bid meeting [4] (2073 Bhadra)
  • Pre-bid meeting (also called pre-bid conference) is a meeting organized by the procuring entity for prospective bidders before bid submission deadline
  • Purpose: To clarify the bidding documents, specifications, scope of work, and answer bidders' questions
  • All questions raised and answers given are recorded in minutes
  • Minutes are circulated to all prospective bidders as an addendum to the bidding documents
  • Responses become part of the contract documents
  • Conducted for large, complex projects where tender documents may need clarification
  • Employers may relax rigid conditions based on feedback for better offers
  • Attendance is usually optional but highly recommended

Types of Contract for Consultancy Service

  1. Time-based contract: Fee = rate × time consumed. For work where period cannot be predicted (e.g., supervision of construction, complex designs).
  2. Performance-based contract: Fee based on work completed or as and when required.
  3. Lump-sum contract: For clearly identified work with low risk (feasibility study, project design, preparation of bidding documents).
  4. Percentage-based contract: Payment as percentage of estimated or actual cost of the concerned work. For inspection, architect's services, supervision, or monitoring.
  5. Indefinite service period contract: For consultancy required from time to time — arbitrators, dispute resolution, procurement consultants, technical troubleshooters.

Liquidated Damages (LD)

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

No direct questions asked in the past.

  • Liquidated (delay) damages = remedies to compensate for financial loss from proven breach of contract
  • In Common Law Countries: Liquidated Damages
  • In Civil Law Countries: Contractual Penalties
  • Rate: 0.05% of contract price per day

LD is NOT applicable if delay is due to:

  • Carrying out extra works

Price Adjustment

  • For contracts with duration exceeding 15 months, price may be adjusted
  • Maximum adjustment: 25% of contract amount

Price Adjustment Formula:

Pn = A + b(Ln/L0) + c(Mn/M0) + d(En/E0) + etc.

Where: Pn = price adjustment factor, A = constant factor, b, c, d = coefficients of each cost element (labor, materials, equipment), Ln, Mn, En = current cost indices, L0, M0, E0 = base cost indices (30 days prior to bid submission).

Variation Order (VO)

  • After starting work, if addition/reduction in quantity or addition of new items is required due to technical reasons
  • Competent authority may issue a variation order for up to 15%

Advance Payment / Mobilization Amount

  • May be made up to 20% of procurement contract price
  • Advance shall be deducted from every running bill

Provisional Sum

  • Amount allocated for specialized work by specialized firm, where details not available at tender time
  • Represents best guess by employer/consultant
  • Items: lab test, insurance, OSH, site office, environmental safeguards, survey instruments, etc.

Contingency

  • Estimated costs of "known-unknowns" based on past experience
  • Public projects: limited to 4% of estimated cost (2% work charge establishment + 2% minor physical contingencies)
  • For construction >12 months: additional 20% (10% variations + 10% price adjustment) in budget

Interim Payment Certificates (IPC) / Running Bill

  • Payment certificates issued periodically for work carried out
  • Based on cumulative valuation of work done
  • Payments on account are neither binding nor conclusive of acceptance of work

Taking Over Certificate (TOC)

  • Site Manager must ensure notices are given in writing per Conditions of Contract
  • Defect Liability Period (DLP) starts from date of issuing TOC

Defect Liability Period (DLP)

  • Period after TOC where contractor is responsible for rectifying defects
  • Starts from date of issuing TOC
  • Typically one year for permanent works

Key Exam Patterns

  • Contract types: The most asked topic (16 times) — know Lump Sum, Unit Price, Cost Plus, Percentage, BOOT/BOT, Turnkey, D&B, EPC, CM types with pros/cons
  • Tendering process: Know complete flow: Notice → Document Sale → Bid Submission → Opening → Evaluation → Award → Contract Signing
  • As per PPA Nepal: ICB (>threshold, 45 days notice), NCB (>20 lakh, 30 days), Sealed Quotation (≤20 lakh), Direct Procurement (emergency)
  • Prequalification: Definition, purpose, advantages/disadvantages — asked 6 times; know criteria (experience, financial capability, equipment, personnel)
  • 8-mark long answer: Expect comprehensive question on contract types or tendering process

Must-Know Points

  1. Valid contract essentials: Offer & acceptance, competent parties, lawful object, consideration, free consent, certainty
  2. Work execution methods: Amanat (departmental), Negotiation, Contract (competitive bidding)
  3. ICB vs NCB: ICB for international/donor projects (45-day notice, English); NCB for national (>20 lakh, 30-day notice)
  4. Bid security: 2-3% of contract value; Performance security: 5-13% of contract value
  5. PS formula (if BP < 0.85×EP): PS = 5% of BP + 0.5 × (0.85×EP − BP)
  6. LOA (Letter of Acceptance): Issued to selected bidder requesting performance security & contract signing
  7. Consultant selection: QCBS (Quality & Cost Based), QBS, FBS, LCS, SSS, CQS
  8. Contract documents priority: Agreement → LOA → Bid → SCC → GCC → Specifications → Drawings → BOQ
  9. FIDIC Rainbow Suite: Red (Construction), Yellow (Plant/D&B), Silver (EPC/Turnkey), Green (Short Form), Blue (Dredging), White (Consultant), Gold (DBO)
  10. Liquidated damages: 0.05% per day, max 10% of contract price; NOT applicable for employer-caused delays
  11. BOOT vs BOO vs BTO: BOOT = transfer after concession; BOO = permanent ownership; BTO = immediate transfer, operate under lease
  12. CM contract advantages: Professional management, design-construction coordination, fast-tracking, transparent costs, reduced adversarial relations
  13. Sample tender notice must include: Entity name, work description, estimated amount, bid security, submission deadline, opening date, bidding method, validity period
