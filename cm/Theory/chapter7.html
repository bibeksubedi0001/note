  • Construction project defines the works undertaken to provide a facility, a product, or a service with specified features
  • Project work scope management aims at ensuring that planned works are done to achieve the stated purpose of the project
  • It focuses on the project scope of work and its components

Typical Project Scope Definition Covers:

  • Project purpose and objectives
  • Project Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)
  • Outline execution methodology
  • Milestone and one-time plan
  • Cash flow pattern
  • Project organization
  • Potential risks and problem areas

Scope Control Processes 2081 2077 2073 2071

  • Control processes at the site include: work quantity, work quality, construction resources productivity, schedule, and performance
  • Typical scope-related processes and the phases of a construction project are shown below:
PhasePlanningExecution
DesignProject Charter, Policy DocumentsConcept & Shop Drawings
CostInitial EstimateCost Control Estimate and Contract Administration
ContractsContract DocumentsAs-built (production) records

Scope Control Includes:

  • Timely approval of designs and drawings
  • Work quality control
  • Time and cost control
  • Resources productivity control
  • Scope verification of contracts

Scope management = Application of knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques to meet or exceed project work scope objectives within quality constraints.

Project Scope Control System

  • Project control follows a systems approach
  • Each organizational unit (a responsibility center) can be viewed as a sub-system
  • The control process involves the following steps:
  1. Step 1: Define the parameters to be controlled
  2. Step 2: Establish baselines for measuring performance
  3. Step 3: Account for performance by measuring performance and reporting performance
  4. Step 4: Analyze performance variances and identify trends
  5. Step 5: Communicate information and record-keeping
  • Project scope control covers the processes required to ensure the project includes all the work required, and only the work required, to complete the project successfully
  • Typical scope-related parameters controlled: design, contracts, quality, time, and cost

  2. What are the major causes of Low labor productivity? [3] (2078 Chaitra)

  6. Write short notes on: Labor productivity control. [5] (2074 Bhadra)
  7. Write short notes on: Labor productivity control. [4] (2070 Bhadra)
  • Productivity = Ratio of output to input (the ability to produce)
  • Input can be measured in physical quantities, monetary value, or a combination of both
  • Labor productivity at a construction site provides a measure of the labor’s efficiency
  • It evaluates how labor was employed — between productive work and unproductive time
  • Studies reveal productive time varies from 50–70% of total employment time
  • Remaining time wasted due to:
    • Unscheduled resting
    • Late starting and early leaving
    • Finding and receiving work instructions
    • Delays in receipt of tools, materials, and instructions

Labor Productivity Control Process

  1. Accounting for the unproductive time of laborers
  2. Comparing actual with the standard
  3. Analyzing variations between actual and standard
  4. Formulating measures to improve productivity

Labor Productivity Accounting

The project labor accounting system serves multiple purposes:

  • Enables computation of accurate pay for laborers
  • Provides data for evaluation of labor productivity in various tasks
  • Facilitates estimation of labor costs executed
  • Provides information needs of all project departments (accounting, costing, planning)
  • Input necessary: Time-keeper’s daily time records for labor, the foreman’s task reports

Labor Productivity Analysis

Labor Productivity Variance = Actual Labor Productivity − Standard Labor Productivity

Labor Productivity Index = Actual Labor Productivity / Standard Labor Productivity

Factors Affecting Labor Productivity 2080 2079 2078

  1. Physical-organic factors: Location, and technological factors
  2. Cultural belief-value: Individual attitudinal, motivational factors
  3. International influences: Levels of innovativeness and efficiency as part of the owners and managers of investing companies
  4. Managerial-organizational: Wider economic and political-legal environments
  5. Level in internal labor markets: Presence or absence of demarcation lines and barriers to occupational flexibility
  6. Performance of personnel managers: Communicating with and performance-managing on the basis of pay and other incentives

Typical Causes of Low Labor Productivity 2078

  1. Un-productive time:
    • Rework of defective work done
    • Waiting for material, equipment and/or instructions
    • Absence from work by workers
    • Frequent changes in scope of work methodology
    • Conflicts between supervisors and workers
  2. Worker's low morale:
    • Non-fulfillment of employment terms and conditions by management
    • Insecurity of employment
    • Sub-standard working conditions
    • Frequent transfers
    • Frequent changes in scope of work methodology
  3. Poor pre-work preparation by supervisors:
    • Excess workers employed for the task
    • Insufficient instructions for execution of work
    • Incorrect sequencing of work activities
  4. Directional failures of project management:
    • Failure to set performance targets
    • Failure to make provision for timely resource supply
    • Failure to provide feedback
    • Failure to motivate workers

Labor Productivity Improvement Measures 2079 2071

  1. Reduce unproductive time by constantly reviewing and minimizing the causes contributing to unproductive time
  2. Replace labor by appropriate equipment, where economically feasible
  3. Substitute inefficient working tools by appropriate efficient tools
  4. Improve the method of executing work — remember that there is always a better way of doing a task
  5. Improve working conditions
  6. Employ competent supervisors

Numerical Example: Masonry Wall Construction

ParameterPlanned/BudgetedActual
Scope of masonry work1200 m²1200 m²
Crew size8 men8 men
Construction time15 days18 days
  • Planned labor effort = 8 × 15 = 120 man-days
  • Actual labor effort = 8 × 18 = 144 man-days
  • Labor planned productivity = 1200/120 = 10 m²/man-day
  • Labor actual productivity = 1200/144 = 8.33 m²/man-day
  • Productivity variance = 8.33 − 10 = −1.67 m²/man-day (Unfavourable)
  • Performance index = 8.33/10 = 0.833

7.5 Material Productivity Control 2078207520732072

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

  1. Explain about the material productivity control. [3] (2078 Chaitra)
  2. Why it is necessary to do the material productivity control in the construction project? [3] (2075 Bhadra)
  3. Define material productivity and write down the basic reasons for material productivity control in construction project. [4] (2073 Bhadra)
  4. Explain the importance of material productivity control in construction project. [Part of 6] (2073 Magh)
  5. Write short notes on: Material productivity control. [5] (2072 Ashwin, Q8)
  • Materials productivity control aims at minimizing wastage
  • Control at the construction responsibility level is broadly divided into:
  1. Accounting for materials
  2. Analyzing variances to determine causes of wastage
  3. Implementing measures to minimize wastage

Material Accounting System

  • The system documents materials procurement data, materials inventory for incoming materials, and the issue of materials to the site
  • Project planners aim at ensuring timely procurement and availability of materials at the right quantity and quality, at the required time and place
  • Also aim at minimizing wastage during usage and updating cost estimates

The accounting process from demand initiation to consumption stage involves:

  • Maintenance of up-to-date records of receipts and issues at the project central store
  • Monthly stock checking of materials in the store and at the worksite
  • Return of unused materials after completion of the intended job

Materials Stock Accounting (Bin Card)

  • The stock record card (bin card) provides an up-to-date record of the stock of materials
  • It shows the following information:
  • Stock code, description, unit of specification, stock location
  • Stock levels (minimum, maximum, and re-order levels)
  • Source of supply, lead time for procurement
  • Sequential record of receipt and issue quantities
  • Balances at any point of time

An effective stock accounting system assists in controlling stocks by ensuring:

  • Materials of the right quantity and quality are available at the right time
  • Inventories are kept as low as feasible to minimize investment and holding costs
  • Minimize losses resulting from theft, obsolescence, and wastage
  • Avoid occupation of excessive storage space

Materials Issues and Returns Accounting

  • Construction materials are obtained either from the project central stores or delivered directly to the place of work
  • The materials requisition note is sent to the project central store in-charge, who issues materials based on this note
  • If materials are unavailable at the central store, the note is passed to the materials controller for procurement
  • The goods received note (GRN) is signed by the site supervisor upon receipt of materials

Monthly Stock-Taking

  • Stock-taking = Process of physically counting the stock in hand and verifying with balances shown in accounting documents at pre-determined intervals
  • Stock-taking enables:
  • Cost evaluation of materials for billing
  • Costing materials inventory and financial advance
  • Mobilization status and reconciling stock
  • Controlling materials discrepancies

Causes of discrepancies between physical stock and storekeeper's records:

  1. Materials received were of different types/qualities than shown on GRN
  2. Quantity of stock issued differs from quantity on requisition note
  3. Surplus materials transferred or returned without documentation
  4. Breakages, damages, and losses at construction centers not accounted for
  5. Clerical errors in accounting

Materials Wastage Analysis

Materials usage variance = Actual materials consumed − Standard acceptable material wastage

Causes and Remedies to Minimize Wastage 2078 2075 2073

Wastage during procurement:

  1. Buying materials of wrong specifications
  2. Buying more than actual requirements
  3. Unnecessary buying for unforeseen eventualities
  4. Untimely buying of short-lived materials
  5. Improper and unnecessary handling of materials
  6. Wastage in transportation

Wastage during storage:

  1. Damages and breakages during handling
  2. Deterioration due to incorrect storage conditions and short-shelf life
  3. Losses due to fire, thefts, and exposure to weather conditions

Main reasons for excessive material wastage during use:

  1. Excess quantity estimation
  2. Shortage in delivery
  3. Theft and pilferage
  4. Breakage and damage during handling
  5. Lack of pre-work preparation and coordination
  6. Inferior quality of materials
  7. Improper accounting and poor store-keeping
  8. Negligent and careless attitude of the supervisor
  9. Unforeseen circumstances (accidents, fire, etc.)
  10. High rate of deterioration due to long storage at the place of work
  11. Over-issues from central stores and failure to return unused surplus materials

Preventive Measures:

  • Proper handling and transportation of equipment and materials
  • Proper and secure storage
  • Correct accounting
  • Fire precautions
  • Education and training of staff and workers to ensure productivity improvement

What is EVA? 2079 2076 2075 2074 2072 2071 2070

  • EVA is a tool that compares the value of work done with the value of work that should have been done
  • Often presented in the form of progress or S-curve diagrams
  • EVA measures the amount of work actually completed in a project (i.e., to measure its progress)
  • It forecasts a project’s cost and date of completion

Three Key Budget Parameters

ParameterFull FormMeaning
BCWSBudgeted Cost for Work ScheduledCumulative time-phased cost budget for activities scheduled to be performed. Answers: "How much work should have been completed by now?"
BCWPBudgeted Cost for Work PerformedValue earned from progress. The cumulative cost budgeted for work that has been completed. Also known as Earned Value. Answers: "How much work has actually been done?"
ACWPActual Cost of Work PerformedCumulative actual cost incurred in accomplishing work. Answers: "How much have we actually spent?"

BCWS = SQ × SR (Standard Quantity × Standard Rate)

BCWP = AQ × SR (Actual Quantity × Standard Rate)

ACWP = AQ × AR (Actual Quantity × Actual Rate)

Variance Formulae

MetricFormulaInterpretation
Cost Variance (CV)CV = BCWP − ACWP+ve → cost underrun (under budget)
−ve → cost overrun (over budget)
0 → on budget
Schedule Variance (SV)SV = BCWP − BCWS+ve → ahead of schedule
−ve → behind schedule
0 → on schedule
CV %(BCWP − ACWP) / BCWP × 100Percentage cost variance
SV %(BCWP − BCWS) / BCWS × 100Percentage schedule variance

Performance Indices

IndexFormulaInterpretation
CPI (Cost Performance Index)BCWP / ACWP> 1 → under budget
< 1 → over budget
SPI (Schedule Performance Index)BCWP / BCWS> 1 → ahead of schedule
< 1 → behind schedule

Status in Percentage

  • % Completed on data date = (BCWP / BAC) × 100
  • % Spent on data date = (ACWP / BAC) × 100
  • % Scheduled to be achieved = (BCWS / BAC) × 100

Where BAC = Budgeted Cost at Completion (total scheduled cost)

Trends and Forecasts Formulae

MetricFormula
Estimate at Completion (EAC)BAC / CPI
Schedule at Completion (SAC)Original Duration / SPI
VAC (cost)BAC − EAC
VAC (schedule)Scheduled Duration − SAC

Numerical Examples

Example 1: Concreting Work

Given: Er. Gautam responsible for 125 m³ concreting in 10 days, budget Rs. 12,50,000. At end of day 3: 40 m³ completed, Rs. 3,75,000 spent.

  • Cost per day = Rs 12,50,000 / 10 = Rs 1,25,000
  • Cost per m³ = Rs 12,50,000 / 125 = Rs 10,000
ParameterCalculationValue
BCWSRs 1,25,000 × 3 daysRs 3,75,000
BCWPRs 10,000 × 40 m³Rs 4,00,000
ACWPActual expensesRs 3,75,000

Variances:

  • CV = 4,00,000 − 3,75,000 = +25,000 (under budget)
  • SV = 4,00,000 − 3,75,000 = +25,000 (ahead of schedule)
  • CPI = 4,00,000 / 3,75,000 = 1.06 > 1 (under budget)
  • SPI = 4,00,000 / 3,75,000 = 1.06 > 1 (ahead of schedule)
  • CV% = 25,000/4,00,000 = 6.25%
  • SV% = 25,000/3,75,000 = 6.67%

Forecast:

  • EAC = 12,50,000 / 1.06 = Rs 11,79,245.28
  • SAC = 10 / 1.06 = 9.43 days
  • VAC (cost) = 12,50,000 − 11,79,245 = +70,755
  • VAC (schedule) = 10 − 9.43 = +0.57 day (~14 hrs)

Example 3: Chino Company — Koteshwor to Kalanki Road

Given: 8.7 km road, Rs 1.5 million/km, duration 1.5 years (18 months). After 6 months: 2.3 km completed, Rs 4.6 million spent.

  • Total project cost = 1.5 × 8.7 = Rs 13.05 million
  • BCWS = (1.5 × 8.7/18) × 6 = Rs 4.35 million
  • BCWP = 1.5 × 2.3 = Rs 3.45 million
  • ACWP = Rs 4.6 million

Variances:

  • CV = 3.45 − 4.6 = −1.15 million (cost overrun)
  • SV = 3.45 − 4.35 = −0.9 million (behind schedule)
  • CPI = 3.45/4.6 = 0.75 < 1 (over budget)
  • SPI = 3.45/4.35 = 0.79 < 1 (behind schedule)
  • CV% = −1.15/3.45 × 100 = −33.33% (over spending by 33%)
  • SV% = −0.9/4.35 × 100 = −20.68% (behind by ~21%)

Forecast:

  • EAC = 13.05/0.75 = Rs 17.4 million
  • SAC = 18/0.79 = 22.78 months
  • VAC (cost) = 13.05 − 17.4 = −4.35 million
  • VAC (schedule) = 18 − 22.78 = −4.78 months

Conclusion: The project is not executing well. If the same pattern continues, duration will be prolonged to 22.78 months costing 17.4 million.

Key Exam Patterns

  • EVA is the most critical topic — asked 12 times across theory and numericals; expect both definition-type and calculation-type questions
  • EVA numericals: Given BCWS, BCWP, ACWP — calculate CV, SV, CPI, SPI, EAC, SAC, VAC. Practice at least 3 different numerical problems
  • Labor productivity: Know factors affecting labor productivity, methods to improve it, and productivity measurement techniques
  • Combined questions: "Explain controlling project work" may combine scope, quality, labor, equipment, and material control

Must-Know Points

  1. EVA key terms: BCWS (Planned Value), BCWP (Earned Value), ACWP (Actual Cost)
  2. EVA formulas: CV = BCWP − ACWP; SV = BCWP − BCWS; CPI = BCWP/ACWP; SPI = BCWP/BCWS
  3. Forecasting: EAC = BAC/CPI; SAC = OD/SPI; VAC(cost) = BAC − EAC; VAC(schedule) = OD − SAC
  4. Interpretation: CPI < 1 = over budget; SPI < 1 = behind schedule; both > 1 = project performing well
  5. Labor productivity factors: Weather, skill level, motivation, supervision, site conditions, tools availability, design complexity
  6. Equipment productivity: Output = Volume per cycle × Cycles per hour × Efficiency factor
  7. Material control: Procurement → Storage → Issue → Consumption tracking → Wastage monitoring
