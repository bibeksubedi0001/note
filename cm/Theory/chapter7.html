The construction project defines the works undertaken to provide a facility, a product, or a service with specified features. Project work scope management aims at ensuring that planned works are done to ensure the achievement of the stated purpose of the project. It focuses on the project scope of work and its components.

Typical Project Scope Definition Covers:

  • Project purpose and objectives
  • Project Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)
  • Outline execution methodology
  • Milestone and one-time plan
  • Cash flow pattern
  • Project organization
  • Potential risks and problem areas

Scope Control Processes

Control processes at the site include work quantity, work quality, construction resources productivity, schedule, and performance. Typical scope-related processes and the phases of a construction project are:

PhasePlanningExecution
DesignProject Charter, Policy DocumentsConcept & Shop Drawings
CostInitial EstimateCost Control Estimate and Contract Administration
ContractsContract DocumentsAs-built (production) records

Scope control includes timely approval of designs and drawings, work quality control, time and cost control, resources productivity control, and scope verification of contracts.

Scope management is the application of knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques to meet or exceed project work scope objectives within quality constraints.

Project Scope Control System

Project control follows a systems approach. Each organizational unit (a responsibility center) can be viewed as a sub-system. The control process involves:

  1. Step 1: Define the parameters to be controlled
  2. Step 2: Establish baselines for measuring performance
  3. Step 3: Account for performance by measuring performance and reporting performance
  4. Step 4: Analyze performance variances and identify trends
  5. Step 5: Communicate information and record-keeping

Project scope control covers the processes required to ensure the project includes all the work required, and only the work required, to complete the project successfully. In a construction project, typical scope-related parameters controlled are: design, contracts, quality, time, and cost.

7.2 Product Quality Control 2072

PAST EXAM QUESTIONS:

In a construction project, the quality management involves processes required to ensure that the project satisfies the needs for which it was undertaken. Quality control in a large-sized project is met by an equipped and staffed quality control department as an organizational unit. It involves monitoring the output at various stages in the production of the facility.

Quality-Related Processes

ProcessDescription
Quality PlanningIdentifying quality standards relevant to the project and determining how to ensure conformance to these standards
Quality AssuranceAll planned and systematic activities implemented within the quality system to provide adequate confidence that an entity will fulfill requirements for quality
Quality ControlOperational techniques and activities which together sustain the product, service, or quality to specific requirements
Quality InspectionDetermines acceptability of a product or service or stages of construction. Includes measuring, examining, and testing to determine whether results conform to requirements
Quality AuditA systematic and independent examination to determine whether quality activities and related results comply with planned arrangements, are implemented effectively, and are suitable to achieve objectives
Quality ImprovementActions throughout the organization to increase effectiveness and efficiency of activities and processes to provide added benefits to both organizers and customers

Quality Control Measures in a Project

  • Creating quality consciousness
  • Mobilizing resources for assuring and controlling product quality
  • Setting up site material testing laboratory and establishing other sources for materials testing
  • Training the concerned staff
  • Implementing a quality plan
  • Testing incoming and locally produced materials
  • Monitoring quality performance
  • Investigating cases relating to quality failure to prevent recurrence
  • Formulating a plan for re-work when needed
  • Feedback quality performance for effecting improvement

Quality Audit

A structured review of quality activities. Its objective is to identify areas of non-conformance and to stimulate and maintain project quality performance and product quality. Audits can be planned or random, and may be carried out by internal or registered third parties.

Six Sigma Model of Quality Control

Six Sigma is a business management strategy that uses statistical and management methods including benefit analysis, thought-process mapping, differential control charts, and regression analysis. The emphasis is on measurements as the starting point of culture change and decision-making.

The Six Sigma methodology (DMAIC) for a project:

  1. Define: Determination of quality characteristics of the output from the project that are critical to customer satisfaction
  2. Measure: Determine what to measure, sources of information and data, and data collection plan
  3. Analyze: Use appropriate quality planning or quality control tools and techniques to analyze collected data
  4. Improve: Based on performance, comparing with standards, modify or redesign existing methods and implement change
  5. Control: Monitoring the change to assure the required performance level is satisfied

Six Sigma methodology helps greatly to reduce errors and defects in products, processes, and services. It originated as a quality improvement initiative at Motorola Company and is becoming popular worldwide. It is a management philosophy aiming at improvement through rigorous application of both statistical and non-statistical tools to reduce the number of variations, implying increased efficiency and lower costs.

Certifications: Green Belt and Black Belt certifications are available.

Productivity implies the ratio of output to input. Productivity means the ability to produce. The input can be measured in physical quantities, monetary value, or a combination of both.

Labor productivity achieved at a construction site provides a measure of the labor's efficiency. It evaluates how the labor was employed at work — between productive work and the time remaining unproductive.

Studies at construction sites reveal that the productive time varies from 50–70% of total employment time. The remaining time is wasted for various reasons: unscheduled resting, late starting, early leaving, finding and receiving work instructions, and delays in receipt of tools, materials, and instructions.

Labor Productivity Control Process

  1. Accounting for the unproductive time of laborers
  2. Comparing actual with the standard
  3. Analyzing variations between actual and standard
  4. Formulating measures to improve productivity

Labor Productivity Accounting

The project labor accounting system is designed to serve multiple purposes:

  • Enables computation of accurate pay for laborers
  • Provides data for evaluation of labor productivity in various tasks
  • Facilitates estimation of labor costs executed
  • Provides information needs of all project departments (accounting, costing, planning)

The input necessary for labor productivity accounting: time-keeper's daily time records for labor, the foreman's task reports.

Labor Productivity Analysis

Labor Productivity Variance = Actual Labor Productivity − Standard Labor Productivity

Labor Productivity Index = Actual Labor Productivity / Standard Labor Productivity

Factors Affecting Labor Productivity

  1. Physical-organic factors: Location, and technological factors
  2. Cultural belief-value: Individual attitudinal, motivational factors
  3. International influences: Levels of innovativeness and efficiency as part of the owners and managers of investing companies
  4. Managerial-organizational: Wider economic and political-legal environments
  5. Level in internal labor markets: Presence or absence of demarcation lines and barriers to occupational flexibility
  6. Performance of personnel managers: Communicating with and performance-managing on the basis of pay and other incentives

Typical Causes of Low Labor Productivity

  1. Un-productive time:
    • Rework of defective work done
    • Waiting for material, equipment and/or instructions
    • Absence from work by workers
    • Frequent changes in scope of work methodology
    • Conflicts between supervisors and workers
  2. Worker's low morale:
    • Non-fulfillment of employment terms and conditions by management
    • Insecurity of employment
    • Sub-standard working conditions
    • Frequent transfers
    • Frequent changes in scope of work methodology
  3. Poor pre-work preparation by supervisors:
    • Excess workers employed for the task
    • Insufficient instructions for execution of work
    • Incorrect sequencing of work activities
  4. Directional failures of project management:
    • Failure to set performance targets
    • Failure to make provision for timely resource supply
    • Failure to provide feedback
    • Failure to motivate workers

Labor Productivity Improvement Measures

  1. Reduce unproductive time by constantly reviewing and minimizing the causes contributing to unproductive time
  2. Replace labor by appropriate equipment, where economically feasible
  3. Substitute inefficient working tools by appropriate efficient tools
  4. Improve the method of executing work — remember that there is always a better way of doing a task
  5. Improve working conditions
  6. Employ competent supervisors

Numerical Example: Masonry Wall Construction

ParameterPlanned/BudgetedActual
Scope of masonry work1200 m²1200 m²
Crew size8 men8 men
Construction time15 days18 days
  • Planned labor effort = 8 × 15 = 120 man-days
  • Actual labor effort = 8 × 18 = 144 man-days
  • Labor planned productivity = 1200/120 = 10 m²/man-day
  • Labor actual productivity = 1200/144 = 8.33 m²/man-day
  • Productivity variance = 8.33 − 10 = −1.67 m²/man-day (Unfavourable)
  • Performance index = 8.33/10 = 0.833

In construction, some tasks are labor-intensive, some employ equipment, and some use a combination of both. Equipment productivity control is based on: employment time, output achieved, and expenditure. The main purpose is to minimize wastage in utilization.

Equipment Productivity Accounting

Stress is laid on tracing the employment time, expenditure, and corresponding quantity of work achieved for each major item of equipment.

Daily Equipment Employment Accounting

The equipment time-card is the basic document for accounting the equipment's employment time and corresponding performance. For each equipment, it follows the layout of the labor timecard (suitably modified). Information recorded:

  1. Engine operating hours
  2. Received fuel data
  3. Repairs carried out

At the end of the work shift, the daily equipment time-card is handed over by the equipment operator to the equipment cost center.

Equipment Productivity Analysis

To make the construction site cost-conscious, items of work are charged to the equipment cost center at an hourly rate. This accounting takes into consideration the costs of owning and operating an equipment, which can be determined by maintaining records including:

  • The equipment identification (log numbers, make and model, purchase particulars)
  • Purchase cost and replacement costs of major components
  • Equipment's major repair history
  • Employment history (operating hours and fuel consumption)
  • Operator's record (do's and don'ts of equipment operation)

Causes and Remedies for Low Equipment Productivity

In initial stages, actual productivity is less than the planned productivity. It gradually improves provided the equipment is kept in a serviceable condition. Controllable factors:

  1. Insufficient preparation
  2. Lack of continuity of task
  3. Inadequate operator skill
  4. Lack of effective supervision
  5. Non-availability of maintenance measures
  6. Lack of proper equipment management, especially preventive maintenance
  7. Accidents

Materials productivity control aims at minimizing wastage. Control at the construction responsibility level can be broadly divided into:

  1. Accounting for materials
  2. Analyzing variances to determine causes of wastage
  3. Implementing measures to minimize wastage

Material Accounting System

The system documents materials procurement data, materials inventory for incoming materials, and the issue of materials to the site. Project planners aim at ensuring timely procurement and availability of materials at the right quantity and quality, at the required time and place. They also aim at minimizing wastage during usage and updating cost estimates.

The accounting process from demand initiation to consumption stage involves:

  • Maintenance of up-to-date records of receipts and issues at the project central store
  • Monthly stock checking of materials in the store and at the worksite
  • Return of unused materials after completion of the intended job

Materials Stock Accounting (Bin Card)

The stock record card (bin card) provides an up-to-date record of the stock of materials. It shows:

  • Stock code, description, unit of specification, stock location
  • Stock levels (minimum, maximum, and re-order levels)
  • Source of supply, lead time for procurement
  • Sequential record of receipt and issue quantities
  • Balances at any point of time

An effective stock accounting system assists in controlling stocks by ensuring:

  • Materials of the right quantity and quality are available at the right time
  • Inventories are kept as low as feasible to minimize investment and holding costs
  • Minimize losses resulting from theft, obsolescence, and wastage
  • Avoid occupation of excessive storage space

Materials Issues and Returns Accounting

Construction materials are obtained either from the project central stores or delivered directly to the place of work. The materials requisition note is sent to the project central store in-charge, who issues materials based on this note.

If materials are unavailable at the central store, the note is passed to the materials controller for procurement.

The goods received note (GRN) is signed by the site supervisor upon receipt of materials.

Monthly Stock-Taking

The process of physically counting the stock in hand and verifying with balances shown in accounting documents at pre-determined intervals. Stock-taking enables:

  • Cost evaluation of materials for billing
  • Costing materials inventory and financial advance
  • Mobilization status and reconciling stock
  • Controlling materials discrepancies

Causes of discrepancies between physical stock and storekeeper's records:

  1. Materials received were of different types/qualities than shown on GRN
  2. Quantity of stock issued differs from quantity on requisition note
  3. Surplus materials transferred or returned without documentation
  4. Breakages, damages, and losses at construction centers not accounted for
  5. Clerical errors in accounting

Materials Wastage Analysis

Materials usage variance = Actual materials consumed − Standard acceptable material wastage

Causes and Remedies to Minimize Wastage

Wastage during procurement:

  1. Buying materials of wrong specifications
  2. Buying more than actual requirements
  3. Unnecessary buying for unforeseen eventualities
  4. Untimely buying of short-lived materials
  5. Improper and unnecessary handling of materials
  6. Wastage in transportation

Wastage during storage:

  1. Damages and breakages during handling
  2. Deterioration due to incorrect storage conditions and short-shelf life
  3. Losses due to fire, thefts, and exposure to weather conditions

Main reasons for excessive material wastage during use:

  1. Excess quantity estimation
  2. Shortage in delivery
  3. Theft and pilferage
  4. Breakage and damage during handling
  5. Lack of pre-work preparation and coordination
  6. Inferior quality of materials
  7. Improper accounting and poor store-keeping
  8. Negligent and careless attitude of the supervisor
  9. Unforeseen circumstances (accidents, fire, etc.)
  10. High rate of deterioration due to long storage at the place of work
  11. Over-issues from central stores and failure to return unused surplus materials

Preventive measures: Proper handling and transportation of equipment and materials, proper and secure storage, correct accounting, fire precautions, and education and training of staff and workers to ensure productivity improvement.

7.6 Work Schedule Control

A schedule is necessary for the day-to-day operation of any construction project. The process of creating a schedule is called scheduling. The construction schedule — including monthly status reports, schedule updates, and revisions — is integral to the project's successful completion.

What is EVA?

Earned Value Analysis (EVA) is a tool that compares the value of work done with the value of work that should have been done. It is often presented in the form of progress or S-curve diagrams. EVA is a way to measure the amount of work actually completed in a project (i.e., to measure its progress) and to forecast a project's cost and date of completion.

Three Key Budget Parameters

ParameterFull FormMeaning
BCWSBudgeted Cost for Work ScheduledCumulative time-phased cost budget for activities scheduled to be performed. Answers: "How much work should have been completed by now?"
BCWPBudgeted Cost for Work PerformedValue earned from progress. The cumulative cost budgeted for work that has been completed. Also known as Earned Value. Answers: "How much work has actually been done?"
ACWPActual Cost of Work PerformedCumulative actual cost incurred in accomplishing work. Answers: "How much have we actually spent?"

BCWS = SQ × SR (Standard Quantity × Standard Rate)

BCWP = AQ × SR (Actual Quantity × Standard Rate)

ACWP = AQ × AR (Actual Quantity × Actual Rate)

Variance Formulae

MetricFormulaInterpretation
Cost Variance (CV)CV = BCWP − ACWP+ve → cost underrun (under budget)
−ve → cost overrun (over budget)
0 → on budget
Schedule Variance (SV)SV = BCWP − BCWS+ve → ahead of schedule
−ve → behind schedule
0 → on schedule
CV %(BCWP − ACWP) / BCWP × 100Percentage cost variance
SV %(BCWP − BCWS) / BCWS × 100Percentage schedule variance

Performance Indices

IndexFormulaInterpretation
CPI (Cost Performance Index)BCWP / ACWP> 1 → under budget
< 1 → over budget
SPI (Schedule Performance Index)BCWP / BCWS> 1 → ahead of schedule
< 1 → behind schedule

Status in Percentage

  • % Completed on data date = (BCWP / BAC) × 100
  • % Spent on data date = (ACWP / BAC) × 100
  • % Scheduled to be achieved = (BCWS / BAC) × 100

Where BAC = Budgeted Cost at Completion (total scheduled cost)

Trends and Forecasts Formulae

MetricFormula
Estimate at Completion (EAC)BAC / CPI
Schedule at Completion (SAC)Original Duration / SPI
VAC (cost)BAC − EAC
VAC (schedule)Scheduled Duration − SAC

Numerical Examples

Example 1: Concreting Work

Given: Er. Gautam responsible for 125 m³ concreting in 10 days, budget Rs. 12,50,000. At end of day 3: 40 m³ completed, Rs. 3,75,000 spent.

  • Cost per day = Rs 12,50,000 / 10 = Rs 1,25,000
  • Cost per m³ = Rs 12,50,000 / 125 = Rs 10,000
ParameterCalculationValue
BCWSRs 1,25,000 × 3 daysRs 3,75,000
BCWPRs 10,000 × 40 m³Rs 4,00,000
ACWPActual expensesRs 3,75,000

Variances:

  • CV = 4,00,000 − 3,75,000 = +25,000 (under budget)
  • SV = 4,00,000 − 3,75,000 = +25,000 (ahead of schedule)
  • CPI = 4,00,000 / 3,75,000 = 1.06 > 1 (under budget)
  • SPI = 4,00,000 / 3,75,000 = 1.06 > 1 (ahead of schedule)
  • CV% = 25,000/4,00,000 = 6.25%
  • SV% = 25,000/3,75,000 = 6.67%

Forecast:

  • SAC = 10 / 1.06 = 9.43 days
  • VAC (cost) = 12,50,000 − 11,79,245 = +70,755
  • VAC (schedule) = 10 − 9.43 = +0.57 day (~14 hrs)

Example 3: Chino Company — Koteshwor to Kalanki Road

Given: 8.7 km road, Rs 1.5 million/km, duration 1.5 years (18 months). After 6 months: 2.3 km completed, Rs 4.6 million spent.

  • Total project cost = 1.5 × 8.7 = Rs 13.05 million
  • BCWS = (1.5 × 8.7/18) × 6 = Rs 4.35 million
  • BCWP = 1.5 × 2.3 = Rs 3.45 million
  • ACWP = Rs 4.6 million

Variances:

  • CV = 3.45 − 4.6 = −1.15 million (cost overrun)
  • SV = 3.45 − 4.35 = −0.9 million (behind schedule)
  • CPI = 3.45/4.6 = 0.75 < 1 (over budget)
  • SPI = 3.45/4.35 = 0.79 < 1 (behind schedule)
  • CV% = −1.15/3.45 × 100 = −33.33% (over spending by 33%)
  • SV% = −0.9/4.35 × 100 = −20.68% (behind by ~21%)

Forecast:

  • EAC = 13.05/0.75 = Rs 17.4 million
  • SAC = 18/0.79 = 22.78 months
  • VAC (cost) = 13.05 − 17.4 = −4.35 million
  • VAC (schedule) = 18 − 22.78 = −4.78 months

Conclusion: The project is not executing well. If the same pattern continues, duration will be prolonged to 22.78 months costing 17.4 million.
