Overview

The owner (client/employer) has several choices for executing construction work. The choice depends on project size, urgency, complexity, and available resources.

A. Amanat (Force Account / Departmental Execution)

  • Works executed by the owner itself by hiring workers and supplying materials & equipment
  • Records of labor, materials, and equipment maintained in book-keeping
  • Useful for: small works, emergency repairs, immediate execution needed, expertise available within organization
  • Saves contractor’s profit margin, keeps staff busy
  • Disadvantage: owner bears all risks, may lack construction expertise

B. Negotiation

  • Contract awarded through direct negotiation with a selected contractor
  • Used when competitive bidding is not practical or when specialized skills are needed

C. Contract

  • Legal agreement between two or more parties to do or not to do any work
  • Time-bound, requires offer and acceptance
  • Most common method for public construction projects

Methods Under Public Procurement Act (Nepal)

1. Sealed Competitive Bidding

Contractor selected based on competition among bidders.

FeatureICB (International)NCB/LCB (National/Local)
When usedPer donor agency agreement; when Nepalese contractors lack experience/capability; no bid received from local contractorsConstruction > Rs. 20 Lakhs; Goods/services > Rs. 10 Lakhs
LanguageEnglishNepali (may also publish internationally)
Notice periodMinimum 45 daysMinimum 30 days
PublicationNational & international media + PPMO websiteNational newspaper + PPMO website

2. Sealed Quotation (SQ)

  • Shorter process than NCB
  • Construction work ≤ Rs. 20 Lakhs; Goods/services ≤ Rs. 10 Lakhs

3. Direct Procurement (DP)

  • Construction work up to Rs. 5 Lakhs; Goods/services up to Rs. 3 Lakhs
  • Simplest and fastest method

4. User’s Committee (UC)

  • Works up to Rs. 60 Lakhs can be done by User’s Committee
  • Used for small labor-intensive works where objective is to:
    • Provide employment and income to persons in project area
    • Increase utilization of local know-how, appropriate technologies & materials
    • Handover completed works for O&M directly to local persons

5. Procurement Under Special Circumstances

  • Emergency/special circumstances
  • Amount depends on contract value
Nepal Context: Public Procurement Act provisions — ICB for large donor-funded projects, NCB for domestic projects above threshold, SQ for medium works, DP for small works, UC for community-level labor-intensive works. Always published on PPMO website.

Two Broad Categories

  1. Price-Based: Lump sum or unit rate contracts — price/rates submitted by contractor
  2. Cost-Based: Cost-reimbursable and target cost — actual cost reimbursed + fee for overheads and profit

A. Lump Sum Contract (Drawings & Specifications Contract)

Contractor agrees to complete entire work for a fixed total price. Used when quantities are difficult to measure (e.g., underground pipeline installation).

AdvantagesDisadvantages
Final price known before work startsChanges in drawings/specs are very expensive
Contractor incentivized to reduce costHigh risk contingency included in tender price
Quick completion motivationCompetent contractors may avoid bidding
Owner has cost certaintyLimited flexibility for design changes

B. Unit Price (BOQ) Contract

Payment based on unit price set forth in Bill of Quantity. Bidder includes materials, labor, and other costs in unit price. Payment made on basis of work actually done and measured in field.

AdvantagesDisadvantages
Fair basis for competitionFinal price unknown until completion
Changes can be made more easilyUp-to-date record keeping necessary
Lower risk for contractorPossibility of unbalanced bid
Payment for actual work doneMore administrative burden

C. Cost Reimbursable Contract (Cost Plus Contract)

Used for high-risk, unpredictable conditions when contractors refuse or are unable to perform under unit price contract. Payment includes actual cost incurred + overheads + profit as per approved estimate.

AdvantagesDisadvantages
Start construction without complete drawingsDifficult to predict final cost
More flexibility for owner changesFinancial planning problems for owner
Draw contractor expertise during designContractor pays less attention to cost control
Low risk for contractorHigh risk for owner

D. Design and Build Contract (Turnkey Contract)

Single agreement where contractor performs both design and construction. Contractor is responsible for both faulty workmanship and design deficiencies. Preferred under tight schedule circumstances.

AdvantagesDisadvantages
Reduced disputes (single responsibility)Difficult for owner to compare design proposals
Shorter project duration (phased design+construction)Owner's design input is limited
Efficient construction using familiar methodsMay not get lowest cost
Early material ordering at reduced costLess control over design quality

E. Target Contract

  • A target cost is fixed before execution
  • If work completed per target → target amount + agreed percentage paid
  • Savings/overruns shared between owner and contractor per agreed formula

F. BOOT (Build Own Operate Transfer)

  • Private organization builds and owns the facility
  • Operates for specified concession period to recover investment + profit
  • Transfers to government free of cost after concession period
  • Examples: toll roads, hydropower, bridges

Key Definitions

  • Tender: An invitation to bid for a project or accept a formal offer
  • Bid: A formal proposal submitted by a contractor in response to a tender
  • Bid Price: The highest price a buyer is willing to pay (contractor’s offered price)
  • Ask Price: The price at which the seller is willing to sell
  • Tendering: The process of making an offer, bid, or proposal in response to an invitation
  • Tender Document: Written request sent to potential suppliers/contractors containing all project information

Process Before Inviting Tender

  1. Desk study & field survey
  2. Design, drawing, specifications
  3. Rate analysis & cost estimation
  4. Approval of budget
  5. Tender/bid document preparation
  6. Tender notice publication

Essentials of Tender Notice

  • Implementing agency (client’s name & address)
  • Notice number and first date of publication
  • Contract number, description of work, and location
  • Bid security / earnest money deposit amount
  • Bidding document fee and where to obtain
  • Office to buy documents and submit tender
  • Last date of submission
  • Cost estimate of the project
  • Name of authority to accept or reject tender
  • Date, place, and time for opening tender

Contents of Tender Document

  • Bid form
  • Letter of Acceptance (LOA) sample
  • General Conditions of Contract (GCOC) & Special Conditions of Contract (SCOC)
  • Specifications
  • Bill of Quantities (BOQ)
  • Tender drawings
  • Sample forms: bid security, performance security, advance guarantee
  • Qualification certificates

Standard Bidding Documents (SBD) Used

  • FIDIC: Fédération Internationale Des Ingénieurs Conseils (International Federation of Consulting Engineers)
  • World Bank (WB) standard documents
  • Asian Development Bank (ADB) standard documents

Definition

Conditions of Contract (COC) are legally binding terms that clearly spell out rights, obligations, and procedures for both owner and contractor. They minimize disputes.

A. General Conditions of Contract (GCOC)

Almost same for all types of contracts. Copied from FIDIC/WB/ADB standard bidding documents.

GCOC Covers:

  • Definitions and interpretations
  • Time for completion and delays
  • Payment to contractors
  • Execution of work and measurement of completed work
  • Breach of contract
  • Arbitration — settlement of disputes
  • Suspension of work
  • General obligation of contractors
  • Labor and labor welfare provisions
  • Insurance requirements
  • Variation and extra work procedures
  • Defects liability period

B. Special Conditions of Contract (SCOC)

Special for specific project/contract. Some conditions may be changed by mutual agreement based on law of country, nature of work, etc.

SCOC provides:

  • Detailing of provisions treated generally in GCOC
  • Deletion of inapplicable GCOC provisions
  • Amendment of GCOC provisions for project-specific requirements
  • Addition of further provisions for satisfactory administration and execution
  • Country-specific legal requirements
  • Location-specific or project-specific additional clauses

Difference between GCOC and SCOC

GCOCSCOC
Standard for all contractsSpecific to each project
Based on FIDIC/WB/ADBBased on local laws, project needs
General provisionsDetailed, modified, or added provisions
Cannot be changed unilaterallyCan be modified by mutual agreement
Covers all basic contract aspectsSupplements/overrides GCOC where needed

A. Prequalification (PQ)

Assessment of suitability of firms to carry out a particular contract prior to being invited to submit a bid. A process of short-listing eligible bidders.

Purpose of Prequalification

  • Ensure invitation extends only to firms with adequate capacity and resources
  • Required for large, complex, or high-cost works
  • Reduces crowding of unqualified bidders

PQ Application Requirements

  1. Organizational Structure: List of owners, officers, partners, authorized persons
  2. Classification: Type of work firm normally performs, licensure
  3. Experience: History of projects of similar size, scope, and complexity
  4. Financial: Minimum one year audited financial statements
  5. Litigation/Claims: History including disputes with owners and subcontractors
  6. Timeliness: Track record of completing projects on time; liquidated damages history
  7. Capacity: Bonding capacity, insurance, resources, key personnel
  8. Safety: Acceptable safety history; complaints, violations, reports
  9. Legal Authorization: Valid license or statement of ability to acquire one

Considerations for PQ of Contractor

  • Turnover: Average Annual Turnover (AAT) = (2 × V) / T. If AAT is insufficient, Joint Venture (JV) may be solution
  • Similar Work Experience: Taken as 0.8V (V = contract value)
  • Equipment: Types and numbers available
  • Financial Status: Net worth must be positive; sufficient working capital (4–6 months minimum)
  • Manpower: Key personnel qualifications and availability

Advantages and Disadvantages of PQ

AdvantagesDisadvantages
Avoids expense of bidding for unqualified firmsIncreases procurement lead time
Qualified firms compete against other qualified biddersEmployer must review all PQ applications
Exposes potential conflicts of interestSubjective judgment in evaluation
Reduces bid evaluation time—
Encourages local firms to build capacity—
Reduces problems of low-priced bid rejection—

Selection Systems

  • QBS (Quality Based Selection): Best qualified consultant ranked first; negotiate financial terms; if unsuccessful, call second-ranked
  • QCBS (Quality and Cost Based): Evaluation on both technical capacity and financial terms per weightage; highest combined score wins
  • CBS (Cost Based): Among technically qualified, lowest financial proposal selected

B. Post-Qualification (Post-Q)

Qualification done after bidding. Contractor submits both technical and financial proposals together.

Option 1: Single Stage One Envelope

  • Both financial and technical proposals in one envelope
  • Opened publicly at specified date/time
  • Financial lowest bidder identified first → technical evaluation done
  • If lowest bidder not technically qualified, second lowest evaluated

Option 2: Single Stage Two Envelope

  • Two sealed envelopes (financial + technical) in outer envelope
  • Technical proposals opened and evaluated first; financial proposals remain sealed
  • Financial proposals of technically qualified bidders opened publicly
  • Lowest price among qualified bidders wins

Tender Evaluation Process

  1. Bid Opening: Public opening at specified date, time, and place
  2. Preliminary Check: Verify signature, authorization, completeness
  3. Arithmetic Correction: Check calculations; correct errors per standard rules
  4. Bid Security Check: Valid bid security (2–3% of contract value) & validity period
  5. Responsiveness Check: Compliance with all tender requirements
  6. Technical Evaluation: Assess technical compliance and capacity
  7. Financial Comparison: Compare evaluated prices of responsive bids
  8. Identify Lowest Responsive Bid: Recommend for award

Key Securities in Contract

Security TypePercentagePurpose
Bid Security (Earnest Money)2–3% of contract valueEnsures bidder doesn’t withdraw; forfeited if winner fails to sign
Performance Security5–13% of contract valueEnsures contractor performs as per contract
Advance GuaranteePer contract termsSecures advance payment given to contractor
Retention Money5–10% of each billHeld until defects liability period ends

Selection and Award

  1. Bid Evaluation Committee identifies lowest responsive bid and recommends award
  2. Decision taken for award of contract
  3. Letter of Acceptance (LOA) issued to selected bidder
  4. Selected bidder submits performance security and signs contract
  5. Bid security of other bidders returned
  6. If successful bidder fails to carry out contract, bid security is forfeited
