Advantages

  • Higher productivity: Equipment performs work faster than manual labor
  • Better quality: Uniform and consistent output (e.g., compaction, mixing)
  • Economy in large projects: Reduces cost per unit for bulk work
  • Handling heavy loads: Impossible to lift/move manually (heavy steel, concrete elements)
  • Reduced labor dependency: Overcomes labor shortage problems
  • Safety improvement: Reduces human exposure to hazardous tasks
  • Time saving: Completes work in less time, meeting tight schedules
  • Working in difficult conditions: Underwater, underground, high altitude

Disadvantages

  • High initial investment: Purchase cost is substantial
  • Maintenance cost: Regular maintenance, repairs, spare parts are expensive
  • Skilled operators needed: Requires trained and experienced operators
  • Idle time cost: Equipment earns nothing when idle but depreciates
  • Transportation cost: Moving heavy equipment to/from site is expensive
  • Environmental impact: Noise, air pollution, vibration, fuel consumption
  • Unemployment: Reduces employment opportunities for unskilled labor
  • Not suitable for small projects: Uneconomical for small-scale works
  • Breakdown risk: Equipment failure can halt entire project

a) Excavator (Hydraulic Excavator / Face Shovel)

  • Most versatile excavation equipment with 360° rotation capability
  • Used for: digging trenches, foundations, loading trucks, demolition
  • Components: boom, stick (dipper arm), bucket, cab, tracks/wheels
  • Available in various sizes: mini (1–5 ton) to large (40+ ton)
  • Bucket capacity ranges from 0.1 m³ to 5+ m³

b) Backhoe (Backhoe Loader)

  • Combined machine with a loader bucket in front and a backhoe (excavator arm) at rear
  • Versatile for small to medium projects
  • Used for: digging below ground level, trenching, loading, backfilling
  • Mounted on rubber tires — good mobility on roads
  • Suitable for: foundation trenches, pipe laying, drainage work

c) Dragline

  • Uses a crane-like boom with a bucket suspended by cables (hoist rope and drag rope)
  • Bucket is cast/thrown out and dragged toward the machine to fill
  • Used for: deep excavation below machine level, dredging, canal excavation, underwater excavation
  • Can excavate at greater depth and reach than hydraulic excavators
  • Cannot load trucks directly — material dumped nearby

d) Bulldozer

  • Track-mounted machine with a large metal blade at the front
  • Used for: pushing earth/material, clearing land, rough grading, spreading fill
  • Can work in rough terrain with track-mounted stability
  • Blade types: straight, U-blade, angle blade
  • Not suitable for loading trucks or precision excavation

e) Grader (Motor Grader)

  • Self-propelled machine with a long adjustable blade between front and rear axles
  • Used for: fine grading, leveling, shaping road surfaces, creating ditches and slopes
  • Blade can be rotated, tilted, and shifted for various angles
  • Essential equipment for road construction
  • Cannot excavate deep or load trucks

f) Scraper

  • Self-loading, hauling, and spreading machine
  • Cuts thin layers of soil, loads into its bowl, transports, and spreads at disposal site
  • Used for: large earthwork operations, stripping topsoil, cut-fill operations
  • Types: single engine, twin engine, push-pull
  • Economical for haul distances of 100m to 1500m

g) Front-End Loader

  • Wheel or track-mounted with a wide bucket at the front
  • Used for: loading trucks, moving loose material (sand, gravel, earth), stockpiling
  • Bucket capacity: 0.5 to 10+ m³
  • Fast cycle time, good for high-volume loading operations

h) Trencher

  • Specialized machine for digging narrow trenches
  • Used for: pipe laying, cable installation, drainage
  • Types: chain trencher, wheel trencher
  • Produces uniform trench width and depth efficiently

Transportation Equipment

  • Dump Trucks / Tippers: For transporting excavated earth, aggregate, sand. Various capacities (5–40 tonnes)
  • Concrete Transit Mixers: Transport ready-mix concrete while continuously mixing to prevent setting
  • Trailers / Low-bed: Transport heavy equipment between sites
  • Conveyors: Belt-type for continuous material transport (aggregate, concrete) over short distances
  • Concrete Pumps: Pump concrete through pipes to placement location; essential for high-rise construction

Compaction Equipment

Aggregate Production Equipment

  • Primary Crushers: Jaw crusher, gyratory crusher — reduce large boulders to smaller pieces
  • Secondary Crushers: Cone crusher, impact crusher — further size reduction
  • Tertiary Crushers: VSI (Vertical Shaft Impact) — produce fine aggregates and manufactured sand
  • Screening Equipment: Vibrating screens separate aggregates by size
    • Screening formula: Q = A × C × E × D × G (where A = screen area, C = capacity factor, E = efficiency, D = deck factor, G = gradation factor)
  • Washing Equipment: Log washer, sand screw — remove fines and impurities

Concrete Construction Equipment

1. Batching Equipment

  • Volume Batching: Using gauge boxes (farma) — less accurate, used for small works
  • Weight Batching: Using weighing scales — more accurate, preferred for important works
  • Batching Plant: Automated weighing and feeding system — high accuracy, high output

2. Mixing Equipment

  • Hand Mixing: On watertight platform for small quantities; not recommended for structural work
  • Machine Mixing:
    • Tilting Drum Mixer: Small capacity (0.1–0.5 m³), tilts to discharge
    • Non-tilting Mixer: Discharges through chute without tilting
    • Pan Mixer: For stiff mixes, precast elements
    • Transit Mixer (Truck Mixer): Mixes during transport, 4–10 m³ capacity

3. Concrete Transport Equipment

  • Wheelbarrow, chute, dumper for short distances
  • Transit mixer for medium distances
  • Concrete Pump: For high-rise buildings, long-distance horizontal/vertical transport
  • Concrete bucket with crane for vertical transport

4. Compaction Equipment for Concrete

  • Internal (Needle) Vibrator: Immersed in concrete; most common for columns, beams, walls
  • External (Form) Vibrator: Attached to formwork; for thin sections, precast elements
  • Surface Vibrator: For slabs and pavement; screed vibrator
  • Vibrating Table: For precast elements in factory

5. Concrete Paver

  • Used for road/pavement construction
  • Spreads, levels, and compacts concrete in a single pass
  • Types: slip-form paver (no side forms), fixed-form paver

Types of Cranes

  • Tower Crane: Fixed at site, used for high-rise construction. Components: base, mast, jib (horizontal boom), counter-jib, trolley, hook. Can lift 5–20+ tonnes to heights of 80m+
  • Mobile Crane: Truck-mounted or crawler-mounted; versatile, can be moved between locations. Various capacities (10–500 tonnes)
  • Crawler Crane: Track-mounted for stability on rough terrain and soft ground
  • Overhead Crane (Gantry): For factories, warehouses; runs on rails
  • Telehandler (Telescopic Handler): Versatile lifting machine with telescopic boom; used for material handling at medium heights

Factors for Selecting Cranes

  • Required lifting capacity (load weight)
  • Required lifting height and radius
  • Site conditions (space, ground bearing capacity)
  • Duration of need (hire vs purchase)
  • Frequency of lifts and cycle time
  • Type of materials to be lifted
  • Availability and cost

Purpose of Tunnels

Transportation (road, rail), water conveyance (hydropower, irrigation), utility conduits (sewerage, cables), mining.

Classification of Tunnels

  • By purpose: transportation, hydraulic, utility, mining
  • By shape: circular, horseshoe, D-shaped, rectangular
  • By method: conventional (drill & blast), mechanized (TBM)

Conventional Method (Drill & Blast)

  1. Surveying & Setting Out: Mark tunnel alignment and grade
  2. Drilling: Drill holes in rock face using pneumatic/hydraulic drills (jumbo drill)
  3. Charging: Insert explosives in drill holes
  4. Blasting: Controlled detonation to break rock
  5. Ventilation: Remove gases and dust after blast
  6. Mucking: Remove blasted rock (muck) using loaders and trucks
  7. Scaling: Remove loose rock from roof and sides
  8. Support: Install primary support (rock bolts, shotcrete, steel ribs)
  9. Lining: Final concrete lining for permanent stability

TBM (Tunnel Boring Machine)

  • Mechanized full-face tunnel excavation machine
  • Components: cutter head (rotating disc cutters), thrust system, gripper, conveyor system, segment erector
  • Produces smooth circular tunnel profile
  • Types: Hard rock TBM, Earth Pressure Balance (EPB), Slurry TBM
  • Advantages: Faster than conventional, less ground disturbance, safer, consistent profile, minimal overbreak
  • Disadvantages: Very high initial cost, long manufacturing time, limited to specific geology, less flexible for varying ground conditions

Factors Governing Equipment Selection

  1. Nature and type of work: Excavation, compaction, lifting, concreting — determines equipment type
  2. Volume of work: Large volume justifies heavy equipment; small volume prefers manual/small equipment
  3. Site conditions: Topography, soil type, access roads, space availability, ground bearing capacity
  4. Project duration: Long duration favors purchase; short duration favors rental/hire
  5. Technology available: Modern vs conventional equipment based on availability
  6. Fuel efficiency: Operating cost depends significantly on fuel consumption
  7. After-sales support: Availability of spare parts, service centers, technical support
  8. Reliability and durability: Equipment downtime affects project schedule
  9. Price (capital cost): Purchase price, financing options, depreciation
  10. Operating cost: Fuel, operator wages, maintenance, repair
  11. Safety features: Operator protection, stability, emergency systems
  12. Environmental considerations: Emission standards, noise levels
  13. Versatility: Multi-purpose equipment provides better utilization
  14. Availability: Local market availability for purchase or hire

Economic Considerations

  • Purchase vs Hire vs Lease: Decision based on utilization rate, project duration, capital availability
  • Owning Cost: Depreciation + interest on investment + insurance + storage
  • Operating Cost: Fuel + operator wages + maintenance + repair + tire replacement
  • Purchase justified when utilization is >60–70% of available time and across multiple projects
  • Hire/lease preferred for short-term or specialized equipment needs

Extent of Mechanization

  • Depends on: labor availability, labor cost, project size, schedule urgency, site accessibility
  • In Nepal: semi-mechanized approach often optimal due to labor availability and terrain constraints
