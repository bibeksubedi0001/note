Definition

A Specification is a detailed written description of materials, workmanship, and methods to be used in a construction project. It supplements the drawings by providing information that cannot be shown graphically.

Purpose of Specification

  • Define quality of materials and workmanship expected
  • Provide basis for fair competition among bidders
  • Serve as a contract document — legal basis for quality enforcement
  • Guide the contractor in executing work
  • Provide standards for inspection and acceptance of work
  • Supplement drawings with details that cannot be shown graphically
  • Reduce disputes by clearly stating requirements
  • Enable accurate cost estimation

Importance

  • For Owner: Ensures quality meets expectations, basis for acceptance/rejection
  • For Contractor: Clear understanding of requirements, avoids rework
  • For Consultant: Basis for supervision and quality control
  • For Bidders: Uniform understanding for fair pricing
  • For Dispute Resolution: Reference document for settling disagreements

A. General Specification

  • Broad outline of work to be done, without detailed descriptions
  • Covers general conditions, materials, and workmanship applicable to entire project
  • Used for rough cost estimation and preliminary planning
  • Does not include individual item details

B. Detailed Specification

  • Complete and specific description of each work item
  • Includes: materials (type, grade, source), proportions (mix ratios), methods of execution, workmanship quality, testing requirements, acceptance criteria
  • Forms part of contract document
  • Used for: detailed cost estimation, bidding, construction, quality control
  • Example: “1:2:4 cement concrete using OPC 53 grade cement, 20mm graded aggregate, clean river sand, water-cement ratio 0.45, minimum cube strength 20 MPa at 28 days”

C. Standard Specification

  • Published by standardization bodies (BIS, NBS, ASTM)
  • Reference specifications that can be directly cited
  • Examples: IS 456 for concrete, IS 800 for steel structures, NS for Nepal Standards
  • Advantages: widely accepted, tested, saves time in writing specifications

D. Performance Specification

  • Specifies required results/performance without prescribing how to achieve them
  • Gives contractor flexibility to choose materials and methods
  • Example: “Waterproofing system shall prevent water ingress for minimum 15 years”
  • Advantage: encourages innovation; Disadvantage: difficult to verify compliance

E. Manufacturer’s / Proprietary Specification

  • Specifies a particular brand, make, or product by name
  • Used when specific product performance is proven and required
  • Usually includes “or equivalent” to allow competition
  • Example: “Shivam Cement OPC 53 Grade or equivalent approved by Engineer”

Principles of Good Specification Writing

  • Clarity: Use simple, unambiguous language
  • Completeness: Cover all aspects of material, workmanship, and testing
  • Conciseness: Avoid unnecessary repetition and verbosity
  • Correctness: Technically accurate and up-to-date
  • Consistency: No contradictions within document or with drawings
  • Enforceability: Requirements must be measurable and verifiable

Structure of a Detailed Specification Item

  1. Scope: What work is covered
  2. Materials: Type, grade, quality, source, testing requirements
  3. Execution: Method of work, proportions, procedures
  4. Workmanship: Quality standards, tolerances, finish
  5. Testing & Acceptance: Tests to be performed, acceptance criteria
  6. Measurement & Payment: How quantity will be measured and unit of payment

Common Mistakes in Specification Writing

  • Using vague terms like “good quality” or “as directed” without standards
  • Contradicting the drawings
  • Specifying unavailable materials or outdated standards
  • Over-specifying (leading to unnecessarily high cost)
  • Under-specifying (leading to poor quality)

Highest Frequency Topic: Writing detailed specifications for specific items is the most frequently asked question in this chapter. Prepare at least 5–6 items.

1. Brickwork in Cement Mortar

  • Bricks: First class bricks, well-burnt, uniform color, regular shape, free from cracks, efflorescence and flaws. Crushing strength ≥ 10.5 MPa. Size: 230 × 115 × 75 mm (standard). Water absorption ≤ 20%.
  • Mortar: 1:4 or 1:6 cement-sand mortar (as specified). OPC 43/53 grade cement. Clean, well-graded river sand.
  • Execution: Bricks soaked in water for minimum 2 hours before laying. Mortar bed thickness 10–12 mm. Courses laid in English/Flemish bond (as specified). Plumb and level checked every course. Proper bonding at corners and junctions. Curing for minimum 7 days.
  • Measurement: Per cubic meter of brickwork in position.

2. RCC (Reinforced Cement Concrete) Work

  • Cement: OPC 53 grade, fresh stock, tested for consistency and setting time
  • Sand: Clean river sand, conforming to Zone II grading, free from organic matter
  • Aggregate: 20 mm nominal size, clean, well-graded, crushed stone
  • Water: Clean, potable, free from oil, acid, alkali, organic matter
  • Steel: Fe500 TMT bars, IS 1786 compliant, free from rust, certified mill test report
  • Mix: M20 grade (1:1.5:3) or as per design mix. W/C ratio ≤ 0.50. Minimum cube strength 20 MPa at 28 days.
  • Execution: Machine mixing only. Slump 50–100 mm. Placed within 30 minutes of mixing. Compacted by needle vibrator. Curing for minimum 14 days. Minimum cover as per IS 456.
  • Testing: 6 cubes per 50 m³ or per day’s work. 3 cubes tested at 7 days, 3 at 28 days.
  • Measurement: Per cubic meter of finished concrete in position.

3. Stone Masonry (Random Rubble / Coursed Rubble)

  • Stone: Hard, durable, sound quarry stone. Free from cracks, weathering, lamination. Minimum crushing strength 40 MPa.
  • Mortar: 1:4 or 1:6 cement-sand mortar
  • Execution: Through stones at regular intervals. Proper bond (staggered joints). No continuous vertical joints. Maximum lift per day: 1.0–1.5 m. Joints fully filled with mortar. Face stones with hammer-dressed edges.
  • Measurement: Per cubic meter.

4. Cement Plaster

  • Materials: OPC cement, clean river sand. Mix ratio: 1:3 for ceiling, 1:4 for internal walls, 1:4 or 1:6 for external walls.
  • Surface preparation: Clean surface, rake joints to 10mm depth, wet surface before plastering
  • Execution: Two-coat plaster — rough coat 12 mm, finishing coat 6 mm. Total thickness 12–20 mm as specified. Level and plumb checked. Finished surface true to line and level. Curing for minimum 7 days.
  • Measurement: Per square meter.

5. Gabion Work

  • Gabion box: GI wire mesh, mesh size 80 × 100 mm, wire diameter 2.7 mm (mesh) and 3.4 mm (selvedge). PVC coated if specified.
  • Stone fill: Hard, durable stone. Size: 100–300 mm (minimum 1.5 × mesh size). No flat or elongated stones.
  • Execution: Boxes assembled and placed per drawing. Filled in layers of 300 mm. Bracing wires at every 300 mm lift. Lids closed and tied with binding wire. Adjacent boxes laced together.
  • Measurement: Per cubic meter of finished gabion in position.

6. Painting

  • Surface preparation: Clean, dry, smooth surface. Cracks filled with putty. Old loose paint removed. Primer coat applied.
  • Material: Approved brand acrylic emulsion / oil-based paint as specified
  • Execution: One coat primer + two coats finish (minimum). Each coat allowed to dry before next. Uniform color and finish. No brush marks, sags, or runs.
  • Measurement: Per square meter of painted surface.
