Definition

Maintenance is the process of preserving a facility (building, road, bridge, dam, etc.) in a condition where it can be used for its intended purpose throughout its design life. It involves all activities required to retain or restore a facility to a satisfactory condition.

Need for Maintenance

  • All structures deteriorate over time due to weather, use, and aging
  • Prevents minor defects from becoming major structural problems
  • Extends the useful life of the facility
  • Maintains safety for users and occupants
  • Preserves property value and functionality
  • Reduces long-term repair and replacement costs
  • Ensures compliance with building codes and safety regulations

Objectives of Maintenance

  • Keep the facility in good working condition
  • Maximize the service life of the structure
  • Minimize disruption to users during maintenance
  • Optimize maintenance costs (balance between maintenance spending and facility condition)
  • Ensure safety and functionality at all times

Causes of Deterioration

  • Natural: Weathering, erosion, seismic activity, settlement
  • Chemical: Corrosion of steel, alkali-silica reaction, carbonation of concrete
  • Physical: Wear and tear, abrasion, thermal expansion/contraction
  • Biological: Termite attack, fungal growth, vegetation growth
  • Human: Overloading, misuse, poor maintenance, unauthorized modifications
  • Design/Construction defects: Poor drainage, inadequate cover, bad materials

A. Planned Maintenance

Maintenance organized and carried out with forethought, control, and records according to a predetermined plan.

1. Preventive Maintenance (PM)

  • Carried out at predetermined intervals or per prescribed criteria
  • Aimed at reducing probability of failure or deterioration
  • Time-based: Regular schedule (e.g., painting every 5 years, roof waterproofing every 10 years)
  • Condition-based: Based on actual condition assessment (inspection, testing)
  • Examples: regular painting, waterproofing, termite treatment, cleaning drains, joint sealing
  • Philosophy: “Prevention is better than cure”
  • Advantages: Reduces major repairs, extends life, minimizes disruption, cost-effective long-term

2. Corrective Maintenance

  • Carried out after a defect or failure is detected
  • Aimed at restoring facility to a condition where it can perform its required function
  • Examples: crack repair, leakage repair, structural strengthening, replacing damaged components
  • More expensive than preventive maintenance
  • May cause disruption to facility users

B. Unplanned Maintenance

3. Emergency / Breakdown Maintenance

  • Carried out after an unexpected failure that requires immediate action
  • Cannot be planned in advance
  • Examples: structural failure after earthquake, roof collapse, water pipe burst, electrical failure
  • Most expensive type of maintenance
  • Disrupts normal operations significantly

Other Classifications

  • Routine Maintenance: Day-to-day minor works (cleaning, oiling, adjusting)
  • Periodic Maintenance: At regular intervals (annual painting, 5-yearly waterproofing)
  • Special Maintenance: Major renovation, rehabilitation, retrofitting
  • Predictive Maintenance: Based on monitoring data predicting when failure will occur (NDT, structural health monitoring)

Maintenance Planning

Systematic approach to organizing maintenance activities for a facility throughout its service life.

Steps in Maintenance Planning

  1. Inventory of facilities: List all components requiring maintenance (structure, finishes, services, landscape)
  2. Condition survey: Assess current condition of each component
  3. Maintenance standards: Define acceptable condition levels
  4. Work identification: Determine what maintenance work is needed
  5. Priority setting: Rank maintenance tasks by urgency and importance
  6. Resource allocation: Assign budget, manpower, materials
  7. Scheduling: Create timeline for maintenance activities
  8. Execution: Carry out maintenance work
  9. Feedback: Record results and update maintenance plan

Maintenance Cost

Annual maintenance cost typically ranges from 1–3% of the original construction cost for buildings, and higher for roads and infrastructure.

Components of Maintenance Cost

  • Direct costs: Labor, materials, equipment for maintenance work
  • Indirect costs: Supervision, administration, planning
  • Disruption costs: Loss of use during maintenance (revenue loss, alternate arrangements)

Factors Affecting Maintenance Cost

  • Quality of original construction
  • Age of facility
  • Intensity of use
  • Environmental conditions (climate, pollution)
  • Type of materials used
  • Quality and frequency of maintenance performed

Maintenance Management

Systematic approach to directing and controlling maintenance operations.

Key Elements

  • Maintenance policy: Organization’s approach to maintenance (preventive vs reactive)
  • Maintenance manual: Document containing maintenance procedures, schedules, standards
  • Record system: Database of maintenance history, costs, work orders
  • Budget allocation: Annual maintenance budget provision
  • Performance monitoring: Track condition, compare actual vs planned maintenance
  • Feedback and improvement: Use records to improve future maintenance planning
Exam Tip: The relationship between construction quality and maintenance cost is important — higher initial quality leads to lower maintenance cost. Life cycle cost = Construction cost + Maintenance cost + Operating cost over design life.
