Henri Fayol’s 14 Principles

  1. Division of Work: Specialization increases efficiency and productivity
  2. Authority & Responsibility: Right to give orders comes with responsibility for outcomes
  3. Discipline: Obedience, proper conduct, respect for agreements
  4. Unity of Command: Each employee should receive orders from one superior only
  5. Unity of Direction: One head, one plan for each group of activities with same objective
  6. Subordination of Individual Interest: Organization interest above individual interest
  7. Remuneration: Fair wages/salary for services rendered
  8. Centralization: Degree to which authority is concentrated at top level
  9. Scalar Chain: Chain of authority from top to bottom (line of authority)
  10. Order: A place for everything and everything in its place (material and social order)
  11. Equity: Combination of kindness and justice in dealing with employees
  12. Stability of Tenure: Employees need time to settle into jobs; high turnover is costly
  13. Initiative: Encourage employees to take initiative within limits of authority
  14. Esprit de Corps: Team spirit — unity is strength; promote harmony among staff

Centralization vs Decentralization

CentralizationDecentralization
Decision-making at top levelDecision-making delegated to lower levels
Better for small organizationsBetter for large/complex organizations
Uniform policies and controlFaster local decisions
Less employee initiativeMore employee motivation
Slower response to local issuesMay lead to inconsistent policies

Definition

Motivation is the force that drives people to act in a certain way or achieve certain goals. In construction, motivated workers are more productive, safer, and produce higher quality work.

A. Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs (1943)

Five levels of human needs arranged in a hierarchy — lower needs must be satisfied before higher needs become motivating.

  1. Physiological Needs: Food, water, shelter, clothing, sleep — basic survival needs
  2. Safety Needs: Job security, safe working conditions, health insurance, financial security
  3. Social Needs (Love/Belonging): Friendship, teamwork, sense of belonging, acceptance
  4. Esteem Needs: Recognition, respect, status, achievement, self-confidence
  5. Self-Actualization: Reaching full potential, creativity, personal growth, challenging work

B. Herzberg’s Two-Factor Theory (1959)

Hygiene Factors (Dissatisfiers)Motivating Factors (Satisfiers)
Salary and wagesAchievement
Working conditionsRecognition
Company policyResponsibility
Supervision qualityAdvancement/promotion
Job securityThe work itself
Interpersonal relationsPersonal growth

Hygiene factors prevent dissatisfaction but don’t motivate. Motivating factors create job satisfaction and drive performance.

C. ERG Theory (Alderfer, 1969)

Simplified Maslow’s hierarchy into three categories:

  • Existence: Physiological + safety needs
  • Relatedness: Social needs + external esteem
  • Growth: Internal esteem + self-actualization

Unlike Maslow, ERG allows multiple needs simultaneously and regression to lower needs if higher ones are frustrated.

D. McClelland’s Need Theory (1961)

  • Need for Achievement (nAch): Desire to excel, accomplish challenging goals
  • Need for Power (nPow): Desire to influence and control others
  • Need for Affiliation (nAff): Desire for friendly relationships and belonging

E. Equity Theory (Adams, 1963)

People compare their input/output ratio with others. If perceived as inequitable, they adjust behavior (reduce effort, demand more pay, or leave).

F. Expectancy Theory (Vroom, 1964)

Motivation = Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence

  • Expectancy: Belief that effort will lead to performance
  • Instrumentality: Belief that performance will lead to reward
  • Valence: Value placed on the reward

Personnel Selection Process

  1. Job Analysis: Define job requirements, duties, responsibilities, qualifications
  2. Recruitment: Attract candidates through advertisement, referrals, agencies
  3. Screening: Review applications, shortlist based on qualifications and experience
  4. Testing: Aptitude tests, skill tests, psychometric tests
  5. Interview: Assess suitability through structured/unstructured interviews
  6. Reference Check: Verify background and previous employment
  7. Selection and Appointment: Offer letter, terms and conditions
  8. Induction: Orientation to organization, project, safety procedures

Types of Tests

  • Aptitude Test: Assess potential ability for the job
  • Achievement/Trade Test: Test current skill level (welding test, masonry test)
  • Intelligence Test: General mental ability
  • Personality Test: Behavioral traits and characteristics
  • Physical Test: Physical fitness for construction work

Training & Development

  • On-the-job Training: Learning while working under supervision (apprenticeship, mentoring)
  • Off-the-job Training: Classroom, workshop, simulation (technical institutes, CTEVT)
  • Safety Training: Mandatory for all construction workers (PPE use, hazard awareness)
  • Skill Upgrading: New technology, modern methods, equipment operation
  • Management Training: Leadership, planning, communication for supervisors

Trade Unions

Organizations of workers formed to protect and advance the interests and rights of their members through collective action.

Functions of Trade Unions

  • Negotiate wages and working conditions (collective bargaining)
  • Protect workers’ rights and welfare
  • Settle disputes between workers and management
  • Provide social security and welfare benefits
  • Represent workers in legal matters

Collective Bargaining

Process of negotiation between employer and trade union representatives to establish terms and conditions of employment including wages, hours, benefits, and working conditions.

Types of Labor Disputes

  • Strike: Workers collectively stop working to pressure employer
  • Lockout: Employer closes workplace to pressure workers
  • Go-slow: Workers deliberately reduce work speed
  • Gherao: Workers surround management to force decisions
  • Picketing: Workers stand outside workplace to discourage others from entering

Dispute Resolution Methods

  1. Negotiation: Direct discussion between parties
  2. Conciliation: Third party facilitates agreement
  3. Mediation: Third party suggests solutions (non-binding)
  4. Arbitration: Third party makes binding decision
  5. Adjudication: Labor court decides the matter

Communication in Construction

  • Types: Verbal (meetings, phone), written (letters, reports, email), visual (drawings, photos)
  • Direction: Downward (instructions), upward (reports), lateral (coordination)
  • Importance: Ensures everyone has correct information; reduces errors, conflicts, and delays
  • Barriers: Language differences, noise at site, lack of documentation, information overload

Information Systems in Construction

  • Management Information System (MIS) provides timely and accurate information for decision-making
  • Types of information: financial, progress, quality, safety, personnel
  • Modern tools: project management software, BIM, cloud-based collaboration
