Definition

Site surveying is the inspection of an area where work is proposed, to gather information for design or estimate to complete initial tasks. It determines precise location, access, best orientation, and location of obstacles.

Purpose of Site Survey

  • Take all information from existing/new site to provide better solution
  • Determine feasibility of desired coverage
  • Assess topography, soil conditions, access roads, utilities
  • Identify obstacles and constraints
  • Plan construction sequence and logistics

Site Survey Procedure

  1. Pre-survey Preparation: Gather tools (camera, GPS, compass, safety gear), study existing documents
  2. Survey Coordination Meeting:
    • Arranging survey and coordination personnel
    • Preparing vehicle and equipment
    • Making survey plan and confirming survey route
    • Offering primary schemes of transmission and power
  3. Survey Activity: Field data collection
  4. Survey Analysis: Processing and interpreting collected data
  5. Design and Solution: Using survey data for project planning
  6. Implementation: Executing the design

Steps of Site Survey

  1. Gather tools and configuration
  2. Research and investigate industry-specific concerns
  3. Gather recommended equipment list / site survey kit
  4. Implement the site survey
  5. Document the site survey

Setting Out

Locating points on the ground as per drawing/location map with help of reference points. Involves assigning locations for:

  • Major construction materials storage (cement, sand, gravel, brick, rebar)
  • Equipment (mixer, tower crane, other equipment)
  • Facilities (officer housing, labor housing, office, guard house, toilet, gate)

Site Preparation Activities

  • Land clearing and grubbing (removing vegetation, debris)
  • Demolition of existing structures (if applicable)
  • Establishing benchmarks and reference points
  • Temporary fencing and site security
  • Temporary roads and access paths
  • Drainage arrangements
  • Utility connections (water, electricity, telephone)
  • Setting up site office, stores, labor camp

Definition

Job/Site Layout is a scaled drawing of the proposed construction site showing site space allocation for material storage, working areas, units of accommodation, plant positions, general circulation areas, and access/egress for deliveries and emergency services.

Purpose

Every construction project should be organized and executed in the most economical and safest manner. A job layout is prepared to aid smooth execution.

Elements of Job Layout

  • Entry and exit points to the site
  • Storage areas for materials (cement godown, sand/aggregate stockpile, brick stack, rebar yard)
  • Contractor’s office and site engineer’s office
  • Equipment positions (mixer, crane, pump)
  • Bar bending area
  • Labor housing and toilets
  • First aid kit location (in site engineer’s office)
  • Temporary roads and circulation paths
  • Disposal area for waste
  • Guard house

Factors for Preparing Job Layout

  1. Nature, Scope & Type of Work: Site layout for a dam is much more complex than for a residential building
  2. Topography of Site: Location, size, terrain — rocky mountainous terrain layout differs from plain terrain
  3. Methods of Work Execution: Mechanized work needs more facilities for machinery; labor-intensive needs worker facilities
  4. Availability of Space: Constrained urban sites require more careful layout planning
  5. Duration of Project: Longer projects justify permanent facilities
  6. Season/Climate: Monsoon considerations for material storage

Advantages of Good Job Layout

  • Smooth and economical working of the project
  • Reduces completion time of the project
  • Provides more safety in working
  • Material wastage and deterioration are reduced
  • Material transactions become easy, speedy, and economical
  • Increases output from labor and machinery
  • Better supervision and monitoring
  • Reduces material handling costs

Definition

Material Handling is the movement, protection, storage, and control of materials and products throughout manufacturing, warehousing, distribution, consumption, and disposal.

Key Principles

  • Establishes a plan including basic requirements, desirable alternatives, and contingencies
  • Integrates handling and storage activities into cost-effective system design
  • “Least handling is the best handling” — minimize number of times material is moved
  • Best handling of construction materials can reduce cost by 4–5% of total cost

Advantages of Effective Material Handling

  • Improve customer/client service
  • Reduce inventory excesses
  • Shorten delivery times
  • Lower overall handling costs in manufacturing, distribution, and transportation
  • Enhance handling through streamlining and better timing
  • Facilitate product control through better monitoring and management
  • Improve inventory management and accuracy through real-time monitoring
  • Reduce material damage, wastage, and deterioration

Material Handling Methods in Construction

  • Manual: Labor carrying materials — for small quantities, confined spaces
  • Mechanical: Cranes, hoists, forklifts, conveyors — for heavy/bulk materials
  • Automated: Concrete pumps, pneumatic systems — for specific materials

Material Handling Considerations

  • Type and nature of material (fragile, heavy, perishable)
  • Distance of movement (horizontal and vertical)
  • Frequency of handling
  • Available equipment
  • Site layout and accessibility
  • Safety requirements

Construction Financial Management

A construction financial manager concentrates on proper recording of job costs. Financial management includes planning, organizing, and controlling financial activities.

Contractor’s Financial Requirements

  • Up to tender submission: Buying tender documents (Rs. 1,000–10,000), field visit, preparing bid (~1% of bid amount), bid security (2.5% of bid amount)
  • After selection: Signing contract and performance security deposit
  • Mobilization fund: About 20% of contract amount (if available)
  • Working capital: For materials, labor, equipment until first payment received

Financial Issues in Construction Industry

  • Lack of financial facilities restricts industry growth
  • Lengthy and cumbersome disbursement/payment procedures cause project delays
  • Some contractors/consultants lack professional management
  • Inadequate research and development
  • Lack of capacity in Project Managers

Cash Flow Management

Cash flow forecast (cash flow budget/projection) helps identify whether a firm needs to borrow, how much, when, and how to repay.

Project Cash Flow = Sources of Cash − Uses of Cash

Cash Flow Diagram

  • X-axis = Time (months/years); Y-axis = Money (income/expenditure)
  • Money invested/paid out: drawn pointing down from time axis
  • Money returned/received: drawn pointing up from time axis
  • Without mobilization: initial period shows negative cash flow (S-curve expenditure precedes income)
  • With mobilization: initial negative period is reduced or eliminated

Tips for Managing Cash Flow on Construction Projects

  1. Know your client: Assess client’s payment reliability
  2. Start with realistic estimate: Ensure profitable bid
  3. Perform cash flow forecast: Project income vs expenditure timeline
  4. Negotiate favorable contract terms: Advance payment, frequent billing
  5. Be aggressive in collecting payments: Timely bill submission, follow up
  6. Stay on top of change orders: Bill extra work promptly
  7. Close out project promptly: Final billing, retention release

Best Practices for Healthy Cash Flow

  • Identify business risks & prepare in advance
  • Create separate bank account for business
  • Monitor inventory efficiently
  • Always keep buffer money
  • Implement better system to manage cash flow
  • Control cash outflows
  • Keep cash growing
  • “Focus on cash flow, not profit” — without cash, profits are meaningless
Key Exam Point: Cash flow diagram with and without mobilization fund is frequently asked. Draw S-curve showing cumulative expenditure preceding cumulative income, with the gap representing the financing need.
