Cost vs Value vs Price

TermMeaning
CostActual expenditure incurred in construction/production of the property
ValuePresent worth of future benefits arising from ownership of the property
PriceAmount paid in a particular transaction (may differ from both cost and value)

Key Distinction: Cost is a fact (historical), value is an opinion/estimate (current/future), price is a transaction outcome.

Types of Value

TypeDefinition
Market ValuePrice the property would fetch in an open market between willing buyer and willing seller, both with reasonable knowledge
Book ValueOriginal cost minus accumulated depreciation (as shown in books of accounts)
Salvage ValueValue of property at the end of its useful life (residual value)
Scrap ValueValue of materials when the structure is dismantled and sold as scrap
Distress ValueValue obtained when property is sold under compulsion/urgency (below market value)
Replacement ValueCost of replacing the property with a new one of similar utility at current prices
Assessed ValueValue determined by government for taxation purposes
Potential ValueValue based on future development possibilities of the property

Purpose of Valuation

  • Buying & selling: Determining fair market price
  • Taxation: Assessment of property tax, capital gains tax
  • Insurance: Determining insured amount and premium
  • Mortgage/loan: Collateral value for banks
  • Acquisition/compensation: Government land acquisition under eminent domain
  • Rent fixation: Determining fair rent for tenants
  • Investment decisions: Evaluating real estate investment
  • Accounting: Balance sheet reporting, asset management
  • Legal disputes: Partition suits, inheritance, divorce settlements

Principles of Valuation

  • Principle of Substitution: A buyer will pay no more than the cost of acquiring an equally desirable substitute property
  • Principle of Supply and Demand: Value increases with demand and decreases with supply
  • Principle of Highest and Best Use: Value based on the most profitable legal use of the property
  • Principle of Anticipation: Value is influenced by expected future benefits
  • Principle of Change: Property values are affected by economic, political, and social changes
  • Principle of Conformity: Maximum value when property conforms to neighborhood standards

Factors Affecting Value

  • Location (most important factor)
  • Size and shape of plot
  • Type and quality of construction
  • Age and condition of building
  • Accessibility (road, transportation)
  • Infrastructure (water, electricity, drainage)
  • Legal aspects (title, encumbrances, zoning)
  • Neighborhood and environment
  • Market conditions (economic situation)

Sinking Fund

A fund created by setting aside a fixed annual amount so that at the end of the useful life of the property, the accumulated amount equals the cost of replacement.

Annual Sinking Fund (A):

$$A = \frac{(C - S) \times r}{(1+r)^n - 1}$$

Where C = original cost, S = salvage value, r = rate of interest, n = useful life in years

  • Used for: replacement of machinery, buildings, equipment
  • Accumulated in a separate account earning interest
  • Sinking Fund Factor = r / [(1+r)n − 1]

Capitalized Value

The sum of money whose annual interest at the given rate equals the net annual income from the property.

$$\text{Capitalized Value} = \frac{\text{Net Annual Income}}{r}$$

Where r = rate of interest (as decimal)

  • Used for permanent income-producing properties
  • Assumes income continues perpetually

Years Purchase (YP)

The multiplier used to convert annual income into capital value. It represents the number of years’ income that equals the present capital value.

$$\text{Years Purchase} = \frac{1}{r} \quad \text{(for perpetuity)}$$

$$\text{Years Purchase} = \frac{(1+r)^n - 1}{r(1+r)^n} \quad \text{(for n years)}$$

  • Higher YP = Higher capitalized value
  • YP decreases with increasing interest rate
  • YP for perpetuity = 1/r (e.g., at 10%, YP = 10)

High-Frequency Topic: Depreciation methods are one of the most asked topics from this chapter (both theory and numericals). Understand all three methods thoroughly.

Obsolescence

Loss of value due to the property becoming outdated or functionally inadequate, even though it may still be structurally sound.

  • Functional obsolescence: Poor layout, old design, inadequate facilities
  • Economic obsolescence: External factors — change in neighborhood, market decline, zoning changes
  • Technological obsolescence: New technology makes old systems inefficient

Depreciation

Decrease in the value of a property over time due to wear and tear, age, decay, and obsolescence.

  • Physical depreciation: Due to use, weather, age (wear and tear)
  • Functional depreciation: Due to poor design, obsolescence
  • Economic depreciation: Due to external market factors

Methods of Calculating Depreciation

1. Straight-Line Method (SLM)

$$\text{Annual Depreciation} = \frac{C - S}{n}$$

Where C = original cost, S = salvage value, n = useful life

  • Simplest method, equal depreciation every year
  • Book value decreases linearly
  • Rate of depreciation = (1/n) × 100%

2. Declining Balance Method (Reducing Balance / Written Down Value)

$$D_n = C \times (1 - r)^{n-1} \times r$$

Book value after n years: $V_n = C(1-r)^n$

$$r = 1 - \left(\frac{S}{C}\right)^{1/n}$$

  • Fixed percentage applied to remaining book value each year
  • Higher depreciation in early years, lower in later years
  • More realistic for assets that lose value rapidly early on

3. Sinking Fund Method

$$\text{Annual Depreciation Charge} = \frac{(C - S) \times r}{(1+r)^n - 1}$$

  • Annual amount set aside earns interest
  • Total accumulated at end = C − S
  • Lower charges in early years, higher effective charge later
  • Most accurate but complex to calculate

Comparison of Methods

Later yearsModerateLow depreciationHigh total charge
ComplexitySimpleModerateComplex

Most Asked Topic: Valuation methods appear in almost every exam. Prepare all 6 methods with applicability.

1. Cost / Replacement Method

  • Estimate cost of constructing a new building of similar type at current rates
  • Deduct depreciation for age and condition
  • Add land value
  • Formula: Value = (Replacement cost − Depreciation) + Land value
  • Suitable for: Buildings with no rental income, special-purpose buildings (hospitals, schools)

2. Plinth Area Method

  • Value based on plinth area (covered area) at specified rate per sq. ft. / sq. m.
  • Rate obtained from current construction market rates or DSR
  • Formula: Value = Plinth area × Rate per unit area × Number of floors
  • Suitable for: Quick/rough estimation, insurance purposes

3. Depreciated Value Method

  • Calculate original cost → deduct depreciation based on age and useful life
  • Uses SLM, declining balance, or sinking fund method for depreciation
  • Formula: Depreciated value = Original cost − Total depreciation
  • Suitable for: Insurance claims, book value, accounting

4. Rental / Income Capitalization Method

  • Value based on net annual income the property can generate
  • Net income = Gross rent − (Maintenance + Taxes + Insurance + Vacancy)
  • Formula: Value = Net annual income × Years Purchase
  • Suitable for: Income-producing properties (commercial, rental residential)

5. Profit / Accounts Method

  • Used for properties where profitability determines value
  • Estimate average annual profit, capitalize to find value
  • Formula: Value = Net annual profit × Years Purchase
  • Suitable for: Hotels, cinemas, petrol pumps, hospitals

6. Development Method (Residual Method)

  • Used for properties with development potential (vacant land, redevelopment sites)
  • Estimate completed value of development → deduct all costs → residue is land value
  • Formula: Land value = Gross Development Value − (Construction cost + Professional fees + Finance cost + Developer’s profit)
  • Suitable for: Undeveloped land, redevelopment projects

Method Selection Guide

Property TypeRecommended Method
Residential (owner-occupied)Cost / Plinth Area
Rental propertyIncome / Rental
Commercial / HotelProfit / Income
Special purpose (school, hospital)Cost / Replacement
Vacant landDevelopment / Comparable Sales
InsuranceDepreciated / Cost

Qualification of a Valuer

  • Degree in civil engineering or related field
  • Registration with relevant professional body
  • Practical experience in construction and valuation
  • Knowledge of local market conditions, legal provisions
  • Familiarity with building standards, construction costs, and land laws
  • Impartiality and integrity

Valuation Report Contents

  1. Date and purpose of valuation
  2. Description and location of property (with map/plan)
  3. Land area, building area, construction type
  4. Age, condition, and remaining life of building
  5. Method of valuation adopted and justification
  6. Detailed calculations
  7. Market comparison data (if applicable)
  8. Assumptions and limiting conditions
  9. Final assessed value
  10. Signature, stamp, and registration number of valuer
