2.1 Construction Planning – Introduction 2071 2072 2080

Definition

Planning is the process of deciding in advance what to do, how to do it, when to do it, and who should do it. It is the first and most fundamental function of management.

Construction planning involves defining the activities, their sequence, resource requirements, duration estimates, and the interrelationships between activities needed to complete a project.

Objectives of Planning

  • To complete the project within the specified time, cost, and quality
  • To provide a roadmap for all stakeholders — client, consultant, contractor
  • To optimize resource utilization — labor, material, equipment
  • To identify potential problems and risks in advance and develop mitigation strategies
  • To establish a basis for monitoring and control during execution
  • To coordinate activities among different trades and subcontractors

Principles of Planning

  • Principle of Primacy: Planning precedes all other management functions (organizing, staffing, directing, controlling)
  • Principle of Flexibility: Plans should be adaptable to changing conditions
  • Principle of Commitment: Plans cover a time period necessary to fulfill commitments
  • Principle of Limiting Factor: Identify and address constraints (critical resources, bottlenecks)
  • Principle of Navigational Change: Periodically check and modify plans as needed

Necessity of Construction Planning

  • Construction projects are complex with many interdependent activities
  • Resources are limited and must be allocated efficiently
  • Weather, site conditions, and external factors create uncertainty
  • Multiple parties (client, consultant, contractor, subcontractors, suppliers) must coordinate
  • Without planning: delays, cost overruns, quality failures, disputes are inevitable
  • Nepalese context: Poor planning is the primary cause of construction delays — inadequate site investigation, unrealistic schedules, poor resource planning

Types of Planning

  • Strategic Planning: Long-range, 3–5 years, organizational level
  • Tactical Planning: Medium-range, project-specific
  • Operational Planning: Short-range, day-to-day activities

2.2 Steps and Stages of Planning 2070 2072

Past Questions:
  • Explain the importances and steps of construction planning and scheduling. [Part of 10] (2072 Ashwin)
  • Discuss on Time cost Trade-off with examples and steps in planning. [5] (2070 Bhadra)

Steps of Construction Planning

  1. Define project objectives: Scope, quality, time, cost targets
  2. Identify activities: Break down the project into manageable work packages (WBS)
  3. Determine activity sequence: Logical relationships — predecessors, successors
  4. Estimate duration: Time required for each activity based on resources and productivity
  5. Estimate resources: Labor, material, equipment needed for each activity
  6. Develop schedule: Using bar chart, CPM, or PERT
  7. Allocate resources: Assign and level resources across activities
  8. Prepare budget: Cost estimate for each activity and overall project
  9. Risk assessment: Identify risks and prepare contingency plans
  10. Documentation: Prepare project plan document for approval and reference

Stages of Planning

Stage 1: Pre-Planning (Conceptual Stage)

  • Client decides on the need for the project
  • Feasibility study conducted
  • Preliminary estimates prepared
  • Project approval and funding arrangement

Stage 2: Detailed Planning

  • Detailed design and working drawings
  • Preparation of specifications, BOQ, tender documents
  • Detailed schedule using CPM/PERT/Bar Chart
  • Resource allocation and procurement planning
  • Cash flow projection

Stage 3: Construction Planning / Monitoring

  • Execution of construction activities per plan
  • Progress monitoring against baseline schedule
  • Schedule updating and revision as needed
  • Resource re-allocation and corrective actions

A) Client’s Planning

Pre-Tender Stage (Client)

  • Need assessment: Identify project need and define objectives
  • Feasibility study: Technical, economic, financial, environmental, social analysis
  • Desk study: Review existing data, maps, reports, surveys
  • Field survey: Topographical, geotechnical, hydrological investigations
  • Design: Conceptual → Preliminary → Detailed design, drawings, specifications
  • Rate analysis & cost estimation: Prepare detailed cost estimates
  • Budget approval: Get necessary financial and administrative approvals
  • Tender document preparation: Prepare bid documents (BOQ, drawings, specifications, conditions of contract)
  • Tender notice publication: Invite bids through media

Contract Stage (Client)

  • Bid evaluation and contractor selection
  • Contract negotiation and signing
  • Mobilization advance arrangement

Implementation Stage (Client)

  • Monitoring progress through consultant
  • Approving variation orders and extension of time
  • Making timely payments (IPC processing)
  • Resolving disputes and claims
  • Quality assurance through consultant supervision
  • Review meetings and site visits

B) Contractor’s Planning

Pre-Tender Stage (Contractor)

  • Decision to bid: Evaluate project suitability, resource availability, competition
  • Buy tender documents: Purchase and study bid documents thoroughly
  • Site visit: Inspect site conditions, access, topography, local materials, labor availability
  • Quantity take-off: Verify BOQ quantities from drawings
  • Method statement: Plan construction methodology
  • Rate analysis: Prepare rates for each BOQ item considering local conditions
  • Resource planning: Identify required labor, material, equipment
  • Preliminary schedule: Prepare tentative construction schedule
  • Bid preparation: Fill BOQ with rates, prepare bid documents
  • Bid submission: Submit before deadline with bid security
  • Time frame: Typically 30–45 days from tender notice to submission

Contract Stage (Contractor)

  • Submit performance security (5–13% of contract value)
  • Sign contract and obtain mobilization advance (if applicable)
  • Prepare detailed construction schedule (CPM/Bar Chart)
  • Mobilize resources to site
  • Establish site office, storage, temporary facilities
  • Procurement of key materials and equipment
  • Sub-contracting arrangements

Implementation Stage (Contractor)

  • Execute work as per approved schedule and method statement
  • Monitor daily progress and compare with plan
  • Update schedule periodically (weekly/monthly)
  • Manage cash flow — submit running bills timely
  • Quality control on materials and workmanship
  • Safety management
  • Record keeping — MB, site diary, muster roll, stock register
Key Exam Point: This is heavily asked (8 questions). Know both client and contractor planning at each stage. Pre-tender stage by client includes: desk study → field survey → design → rate analysis → cost estimation → budget approval → tender documents → tender notice. Pre-tender by contractor: decision to bid → site visit → rate analysis → preliminary schedule → bid preparation.

2.4 Preparing Schedule 2069 2070

Past Questions:
  • Why do you prefer Bar Chart as planning tool? [2] (2070 Magh)
  • Among CPM, PERT and bar chart which planning tool do you prefer for building construction? Explain. [8] (2069 Bhadra)

Definition of Scheduling

Scheduling is the process of converting a plan into a time-based chart showing the start and finish of each activity, their sequence, and the overall project duration. It is the backbone of project control.

Types of Schedule

  • Master Schedule: Overall project schedule showing major milestones
  • Detailed Schedule: Activity-level schedule with dates and dependencies
  • Short-term Schedule: Weekly/bi-weekly look-ahead schedule for immediate execution

Scheduling Methods / Tools

1. Bar Chart (Gantt Chart)

  • Simplest and most widely used scheduling tool
  • Shows activities as horizontal bars on a time scale
  • Easy to understand, prepare, and update
  • Advantages: Simple, visual, easy communication, shows progress graphically
  • Limitations: Does not show activity dependencies, cannot identify critical path, not suitable for complex projects
  • Best for: Small to medium projects, progress reporting, presentation to non-technical stakeholders

2. CPM (Critical Path Method)

  • Network-based scheduling technique developed in 1957 by DuPont/Remington Rand
  • Uses deterministic activity durations (single time estimate)
  • Identifies the Critical Path — longest path through the network; determines minimum project duration
  • Activities on critical path have zero float — any delay delays the entire project
  • Calculates: EST, EFT, LST, LFT, Total Float, Free Float
  • Advantages: Shows logical relationships, identifies critical activities, enables resource optimization
  • Best for: Construction projects with well-defined activities and deterministic durations

3. PERT (Program Evaluation and Review Technique)

  • Developed in 1958 for US Navy Polaris missile project
  • Uses probabilistic duration estimates: Optimistic (a), Most Likely (m), Pessimistic (b)
  • Expected Time: te = (a + 4m + b) / 6
  • Variance: σ² = [(b − a) / 6]²
  • Useful when activity durations are uncertain (R&D, new technology)
  • Best for: Projects with high uncertainty in duration estimates

Comparison: Bar Chart vs CPM vs PERT

FeatureBar ChartCPMPERT
ComplexitySimpleModerateComplex
DependenciesNot shownClearly shownClearly shown
Critical PathNot identifiedIdentifiedIdentified
Time EstimateSingleSingle (deterministic)Three (probabilistic)

Key Exam Point (2069): “Among CPM, PERT and bar chart which planning tool do you prefer for building construction?” — CPM is preferred because building construction has well-defined activities with deterministic durations, many interdependent activities, and need for critical path identification. Bar chart is supplementary for progress reporting.

Definition

Time-Cost Trade Off (TCTO) is the process of reducing (crashing) the project duration by allocating additional resources to critical activities, thereby increasing the direct cost while reducing the indirect cost, to find the optimum project duration at minimum total cost.

Key Concepts

  • Normal Duration (Dn): Duration with normal resources and effort
  • Crash Duration (Dc): Minimum possible duration with maximum resources
  • Normal Cost (Cn): Cost at normal duration
  • Crash Cost (Cc): Cost at crash duration (always higher than normal)
  • Direct Cost: Cost directly related to activity execution (labor, material, equipment) — increases with crashing
  • Indirect Cost: Overhead costs (office rent, staff salary, utilities) — proportional to project duration, decreases with crashing
  • Total Cost = Direct Cost + Indirect Cost
  • Cost Slope: Rate of increase in direct cost per unit time reduction

Cost Slope Formula

Cost Slope = (Crash Cost − Normal Cost) / (Normal Duration − Crash Duration)

Lower cost slope means the activity is cheaper to crash first.

Steps of TCTO Analysis

  1. Draw the CPM network with normal durations and find the critical path
  2. Calculate cost slope for each activity
  3. Compute total project cost at normal duration (Direct Cost + Indirect Cost)
  4. Select the critical activity with the lowest cost slope
  5. Crash this activity by one time unit (or maximum possible)
  6. Recalculate total cost: add increase in direct cost, subtract decrease in indirect cost
  7. Check if new critical paths have formed
  8. Repeat until:
    • All critical activities are fully crashed, OR
    • Total cost starts increasing (optimum point reached)
  9. Plot the time-cost trade-off chart showing direct cost, indirect cost, and total cost curves

Importance of TCTO

  • Helps determine the optimum project duration with minimum total cost
  • Useful when project must be completed early (liquidated damages, bonus clauses)
  • Helps in resource allocation decisions
  • Enables comparison of cost of acceleration vs benefits of early completion
  • Essential for contractual decisions — extension of time vs crashing
Key Exam Point: TCTO theory is asked 5 times. Know: definition, cost slope formula, steps, direct vs indirect cost behavior. Numerical TCTO is asked 13 times (excluded here as numerical). The concept: crash the critical activity with lowest cost slope first, continue until total cost starts increasing.
