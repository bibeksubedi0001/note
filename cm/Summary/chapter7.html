Definition

Work scope defines the total work to be performed under a contract including design, specifications, drawings, BOQ, and all deliverables. Scope control ensures the project stays within the defined scope.

Why Scope Control is Needed

  • Prevent scope creep (uncontrolled expansion of work)
  • Ensure project stays within budget and schedule
  • Maintain quality standards
  • Avoid disputes between owner and contractor

Scope Control Process

  1. Scope Definition: Clearly define all deliverables, specifications, and exclusions
  2. Work Breakdown Structure (WBS): Divide project into manageable packages
  3. Scope Verification: Formal acceptance of completed deliverables
  4. Change Control: Formal procedure for handling scope changes
    • Change request submitted by either party
    • Impact analysis (cost, time, quality)
    • Approval or rejection by authorized person
    • Implementation and documentation of approved changes
  5. Variation Order: Formal instruction to contractor for changes in scope, with cost and time implications assessed

Causes of Scope Changes

  • Design errors or omissions
  • Changed site conditions (unforeseen ground conditions)
  • Owner’s additional requirements
  • Regulatory changes
  • Value engineering proposals
  • Force majeure events

Definition

Quality is the degree to which the project fulfills requirements and fitness for purpose. Quality Control (QC) is the process of monitoring and recording results of executing quality activities to assess performance.

Quality Management Components

  • Quality Assurance (QA): Planned and systematic activities to ensure quality requirements are fulfilled (prevention-focused)
  • Quality Control (QC): Inspection and testing to verify quality meets standards (detection-focused)
  • Quality Improvement: Continuous enhancement of processes and products

Quality Control Methods in Construction

  1. Material Testing: Cement (consistency, setting time, compressive strength), steel (tensile test), aggregate (sieve analysis, abrasion), concrete (slump test, cube test)
  2. Inspection: Visual inspection of workmanship, dimensions, alignment, finish
  3. Field Testing: Soil compaction (sand replacement, core cutter), concrete cover check, NDT (rebound hammer, UPV)
  4. Laboratory Testing: As per IS/NS standards for all materials
  5. Process Control: Monitoring batching, mixing, placing, curing of concrete; welding quality for steel

Quality Control Responsibilities

  • Contractor: Self-inspection, maintaining quality as per specifications, providing test reports
  • Consultant/Engineer: Supervision, independent testing, approval of materials and workmanship
  • Owner: Third-party quality audit, defining quality requirements in contract

Quality Standards & Documentation

  • ISO 9001: Quality Management System standard
  • Nepal Standards (NS): For construction materials
  • IS Codes: Indian Standards for design, testing, construction
  • Quality documentation: inspection reports, test certificates, non-conformance reports, method statements

  • Write short notes on: Productivity in construction. [4] (2070 Magh)

Definition

Productivity = Output / Input. In construction, it measures how efficiently resources (labor, equipment, materials) are converted into completed work.

A. Labor Productivity

Labor Productivity = Work Output / Labor Hours (or Labor Cost)

Factors Affecting Labor Productivity

  • Working conditions: Weather, site access, safety, facilities
  • Skill level: Training, experience, specialization
  • Motivation: Wages, incentives, job satisfaction, leadership
  • Supervision: Quality of supervision and instruction
  • Tool & equipment: Availability of proper tools
  • Material availability: Waiting time for materials reduces productivity
  • Crew composition: Proper mix of skilled and unskilled workers
  • Work complexity: Repetitive work is more productive than unique tasks
  • Overtime: Extended overtime reduces productivity due to fatigue
  • Absenteeism: Disrupts planned work crews

Improving Labor Productivity

  • Proper planning and scheduling to minimize idle time
  • Training and skill development programs
  • Incentive schemes (bonus for exceeding targets)
  • Good working conditions and safety measures
  • Effective supervision and clear instructions
  • Pre-fabrication and standardization to reduce field work

B. Equipment Productivity

Equipment Productivity = Work Output / Operating Hours

Factors Affecting Equipment Productivity

  • Machine capacity and condition
  • Operator skill and experience
  • Site conditions (terrain, soil type, access)
  • Maintenance schedule and breakdown frequency
  • Job planning and equipment matching
  • Weather and working conditions
  • Fuel quality and availability

C. Material Productivity

Material Productivity = Work Output / Material Input

  • Affected by: wastage rate, quality of material, proper storage, handling, mix design accuracy
  • Improved by: better inventory management, proper storage, trained workers, quality control

  • Write short notes on: Schedule control. [4] (2073 Bhadra)
  • Write short notes on: Time control in construction. [4] (2071 Bhadra)

Definition

Schedule control is the process of monitoring project progress against the baseline schedule and taking corrective actions when deviations are detected.

Steps of Schedule Control

  1. Baseline Schedule: Approved original schedule (CPM/PERT/Bar Chart)
  2. Progress Monitoring: Regular tracking of actual vs planned progress
  3. Deviation Analysis: Identify activities behind/ahead of schedule
  4. Impact Assessment: Determine effect on project completion date
  5. Corrective Actions:
    • Crashing (adding resources to critical activities)
    • Fast-tracking (overlapping sequential activities)
    • Resource reallocation
    • Overtime working
    • Scope reduction (if approved)
  6. Schedule Update: Revise schedule based on actual progress
  7. Reporting: Regular progress reports to stakeholders

Causes of Schedule Delay

  • Late material delivery
  • Equipment breakdown
  • Labor shortages
  • Weather conditions
  • Design changes
  • Financial problems (delayed payments)
  • Poor planning and coordination
  • Disputes and claims

Tools for Schedule Control

  • Bar Chart (Gantt Chart) — simple visual comparison
  • CPM network — critical path identification
  • S-Curve — cumulative progress comparison
  • Earned Value Analysis (EVA) — integrated cost-schedule performance

Definition

Earned Value Analysis (EVA) is a project management technique that integrates scope, schedule, and cost to measure project performance and progress objectively. It compares what was planned, what was accomplished, and what was spent.

Three Key Parameters

ParameterFull FormDefinition
BCWSBudgeted Cost of Work ScheduledPlanned Value (PV) — budgeted cost for work that should have been completed by now (as per schedule)
BCWPBudgeted Cost of Work PerformedEarned Value (EV) — budgeted cost for work actually completed to date
ACWPActual Cost of Work PerformedActual Cost (AC) — actual money spent for work completed to date

Performance Indices

MetricFormulaInterpretation
Cost Variance (CV)BCWP − ACWP+ve = under budget; −ve = over budget
Schedule Variance (SV)BCWP − BCWS+ve = ahead of schedule; −ve = behind schedule
Cost Performance Index (CPI)BCWP / ACWP>1 = under budget; <1 = over budget
Schedule Performance Index (SPI)BCWP / BCWS>1 = ahead; <1 = behind schedule
Estimate at Completion (EAC)BAC / CPIProjected total cost at completion
Estimate to Complete (ETC)EAC − ACWPProjected cost to finish remaining work
Variance at Completion (VAC)BAC − EAC+ve = projected under budget

EVA Interpretation Table

ConditionCVSVStatus
BCWP > ACWP, BCWP > BCWS+ve+veUnder budget & ahead of schedule (BEST)
BCWP > ACWP, BCWP < BCWS+ve−veUnder budget but behind schedule
BCWP < ACWP, BCWP > BCWS−ve+veOver budget but ahead of schedule
BCWP < ACWP, BCWP < BCWS−ve−veOver budget & behind schedule (WORST)
Most Asked Topic: EVA is the single most frequently asked topic in CM exams. Know: BCWS/BCWP/ACWP definitions, CV/SV/CPI/SPI formulae, interpretation, and how to read EVA graphs.
