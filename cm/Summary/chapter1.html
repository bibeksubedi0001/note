History of Construction in Nepal (Timeline)

  • 1769 — ‘Banaune Adda’ (construction bureau) established by Prithvi Narayan Shah.
  • 1846–1951 — Rana Regime: Iron bridge over Bagmati (1850), first water supply system Bir Dhara (1888–95), Pharphing Hydropower (1911, 500 kW), Ropeway Hetauda–Kathmandu.
  • 1951 — Democratic government formed; Ministry of Transport established.
  • 1956 — First Five Year Plan started.
  • 1957 — Industrial Resolution declared construction as a priority sector.
  • 1974 — Industrial Enterprise Act formally defined ‘Contractor’ with A, B, C classification.
  • Current Status: 325 Class A, 374 Class B, 1347 Class C, ~15,000 Class D contractors (FCAN 2013 data).

Role of Construction in National Economy

  • Construction industry contributes more than 10% of GDP in Nepal.
  • Creates employment for 1+ million people directly and indirectly.
  • In developing countries, approximately 60% of national budget is allocated on development/infrastructure works.
  • Construction is the largest industry in many economies (10–15% in developed, 3–8% in developing).

Scope of CM — Five M’s

Construction Management deals with the management of five key resources:

  • Men — Human resources (labor, supervisors, engineers, managers)
  • Materials — All construction materials (cement, steel, aggregates, timber, etc.)
  • Machines — Equipment and plants used in construction
  • Money — Financial resources, budgets, cash flows
  • Methods — Techniques, processes, and approaches to construction

Two Areas of Scope

A) International Export Market:

  • 220+ top international companies compete globally.
  • Major scopes: building construction, transportation, petroleum infrastructure.
  • Revenue exceeds $120 billion annually for top 250 international contractors.

B) Infrastructure Domestic Market (Nepal Prospects):

  • Road network expansion: Nepal can become “Land Linked” between India and China. Fast track, tunnels, express highways are priority.
  • Irrigation & Hydropower: Nepal is 2nd richest in water resources after Brazil. Only ~829 MW of 83,000 MW theoretical potential utilized (about 1%).
  • Building/Housing/Real Estate: Rapid urbanization driving residential and commercial projects.
  • PPP (Public-Private Partnership): BOOT Act 2063 enables partnerships. Types: BT, BOT, BOOT, BTO, LOT, DOT.
  • Airport, Railways, Tunnels: Emerging construction sectors in Nepal.

1.3 Construction Project Characteristics 2071 2079

Past Questions:
  • What are the characteristics of Construction Project? Explain. [4] (2071 Bhadra)
  • The construction projects face delay and cost over-run majorly due to management failures. Do you agree? Discuss on different construction environment. [6] (2079 Shrawan)

A construction project is a unique, temporary endeavor to create a specific product or facility within defined scope, cost, and time constraints. Key characteristics:

  1. Unique & Non-repetitive: Each project is one-of-a-kind; no two projects are exactly the same even if designs are similar. Different site conditions, labor, and management.
  2. Temporary Nature: Every project has a definite start and end date. The project organization is dissolved after completion.
  3. Progressive Elaboration: Project details are developed step-by-step as more information becomes available. Design progresses from concept → preliminary → detailed.
  4. Long Gestation Period: Construction projects have relatively long duration from conception to completion compared to manufacturing.
  5. Subject to Environmental Influences: Weather, geology, social conditions, political environment — all affect construction.
  6. Multi-disciplinary: Involves various disciplines — structural, architectural, electrical, mechanical, environmental engineering.
  7. Labor Intensive: Despite mechanization, construction remains highly dependent on human labor, especially in developing countries like Nepal.
  8. Site-Specific Production: Unlike factory production, construction happens at the project site itself. The factory (site) changes with each project.
  9. High Risk & Uncertainty: Projects face risk from weather, geology, market conditions, political changes, labor disputes, and accidents.
  10. Interdependent Activities: Many activities are sequential and dependent on preceding activities. Delay in one affects the whole chain.

Construction Environment

The environment that affects construction projects includes:

  • Physical Environment: Climate, topography, geology, soil conditions, water table, seismicity.
  • Economic Environment: Inflation, interest rates, availability of finance, material prices, labor wages.
  • Political Environment: Government stability, policies, regulations, labor laws, political interference.
  • Social Environment: Community attitudes, labor availability, cultural factors, population density.
  • Legal Environment: Building codes, environmental laws, labor laws, contract laws, PPR, PPA.
  • Technological Environment: Available technology, equipment, innovation, ICT usage.

Every construction project passes through a series of phases from conception to completion and beyond. The life cycle provides a framework for managing the project.

Phase 1: Conception / Initiation Phase

  • Need identification and feasibility study
  • Project objective definition
  • Pre-feasibility and feasibility analysis (technical, economic, financial, environmental, social)
  • Decision: Go / No-Go
  • Characteristics: Lowest cost, highest uncertainty, highest influence on final outcome

Phase 2: Planning & Design Phase

  • Detailed project planning, scheduling, budgeting
  • Preliminary design → Detailed design → Working drawings
  • Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA/IEE)
  • Tender document preparation
  • Selection of contractor through bidding process
  • Characteristics: Most critical for quality, scope definition; highest manpower involvement of design team

Phase 3: Implementation / Construction Phase

  • Actual physical construction work on site
  • Resource mobilization — men, material, machines, money
  • Quality control, schedule monitoring, cost control
  • Progress reporting and documentation
  • Change order management
  • Characteristics: Highest cost, highest resource utilization, maximum risk of delays

Phase 4: Commissioning / Handover Phase

  • Final inspection and testing
  • Defect liability period (DLP) — typically 6–12 months
  • Handover to owner/client
  • As-built drawings and O&M manuals submission
  • Final account settlement, performance security release

Phase 5: Operation & Maintenance Phase

  • Regular preventive and corrective maintenance
  • Monitoring structural health
  • Revenue generation and asset management
  • Eventual renovation, rehabilitation, or demolition
Key Exam Point (2080 Chaitra): In mega projects in Nepal, the conception/initiation and planning phases are most problematic due to: political instability, delayed approvals, frequent government changes, weak feasibility studies, and prolonged EIA/IEE processes.

Definition

Construction Project Management is the application of knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques to project activities to meet the project objectives of scope, quality, time, and cost. It involves planning, organizing, staffing, directing, controlling, and coordinating construction activities.

Functions of Management (POSDCORB)

Luther Gulick’s POSDCORB framework describes seven management functions:

LetterFunctionDescription
PPlanningDeciding what to do, how, when, and by whom
OOrganizingEstablishing organizational structure and allocating resources
SStaffingRecruiting, selecting, training, and developing personnel
DDirectingLeading, motivating, and guiding subordinates
COCoordinatingHarmonizing activities of different departments
RReportingKeeping stakeholders informed of progress
BBudgetingFinancial planning, accounting, and control

Scientific Management (F.W. Taylor)

Frederick W. Taylor (1856–1915) is called the Father of Scientific Management. His principles:

  1. Science not Rule of Thumb: Replace traditional methods with scientifically designed work methods.
  2. Harmony not Discord: Foster cooperation between management and workers instead of conflict.
  3. Cooperation not Individualism: Work together as a team for common goals.
  4. Maximum Output: Train workers for maximum productivity benefiting both employer and employee.
  5. Development of Workers: Select, train, and develop each worker to their fullest potential.

Why CM Knowledge is Needed

  • To minimize delays through proper scheduling and monitoring
  • To control cost overruns through budgeting, earned value analysis, and financial management
  • To ensure quality through specifications, quality control, and inspection systems
  • To manage risks — safety, environmental, contractual
  • To improve coordination among client, consultant, contractor, and subcontractors
  • To optimize resources — labor, material, equipment utilization
  • Nepal context: Poor construction management leads to delays (e.g., Melamchi Water Supply delayed 15+ years), cost overruns, and poor quality
Key Exam Point (2079 Jestha): “How could CM course help in addressing delay, poor quality, and exceeding budgeted cost?” — Answer: Planning (scheduling, WBS), Quality control (specifications, inspection), Cost control (EVA, cash flow), Resource management, Safety management, Proper documentation & contract management.

The three primary stakeholders in any construction project form a triangular relationship:

A) Client (Owner / Employer)

  • Initiates the project and provides funding
  • Defines project objectives, scope, and requirements
  • Appoints consultant for design and supervision
  • Selects contractor through tendering process
  • Makes payments to contractor based on IPC (Interim Payment Certificates)
  • Accepts/rejects completed work
  • Key Roles: Decision maker, fund provider, project initiator

B) Consultant (Engineer / Designer)

  • Appointed by client for design and supervision services
  • Prepares detailed design, drawings, specifications, cost estimates
  • Prepares tender documents and assists in contractor selection
  • Supervises construction work for quality and compliance
  • Certifies measurements and recommends payments (IPC)
  • Acts as impartial mediator between client and contractor
  • Issues variation orders and handles claims
  • Key Role in Quality: Site inspection, material testing, specification compliance, defect identification

C) Contractor (Builder / Constructor)

  • Executes the physical construction work as per contract
  • Mobilizes resources: labor, material, equipment
  • Prepares construction schedule and work plan
  • Responsible for safety of workers and site
  • Submits progress reports and claims for payment
  • Bears risk of cost overrun within contracted scope
  • Key Responsibilities: Quality workmanship, timely completion, safety, proper record-keeping

Relationship Dynamics

  • Client ↔ Consultant: Contractual relationship for design and supervision services. Consultant works in the interest of the client.
  • Client ↔ Contractor: Contractual relationship for construction work. Payment is based on completed work.
  • Consultant ↔ Contractor: No direct contractual relationship. Consultant supervises contractor’s work on behalf of the client.
  • For effective project delivery: All three must cooperate, communicate, and coordinate despite separate contractual relationships.

Activities from Employer’s Perspective in Construction Phase

  • Monitoring progress against schedule
  • Ensuring quality compliance through consultant
  • Approving variation orders and change requests
  • Making timely payments (within 28 days of IPC certification)
  • Resolving disputes and claims
  • Site visits and review meetings
Key Exam Point: This is the MOST ASKED topic in Chapter 1 (12 questions). Focus on: roles of each stakeholder, their interrelation, why cooperation is essential for timely and quality project delivery.
