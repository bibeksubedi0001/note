Who is a Site Engineer?

A site engineer is the technical professional responsible for the day-to-day management and supervision of construction work at the project site. Acts as the representative of the contractor or client on site.

Key Responsibilities

  1. Setting out: Transferring design dimensions to actual ground positions
  2. Quality control: Ensuring work is done as per drawings, specifications, and standards
  3. Supervision: Monitoring labor, equipment, and material usage
  4. Measurement: Taking measurements of completed work for billing
  5. Safety management: Ensuring safety of workers and public at site
  6. Material management: Receiving, storing, issuing materials; maintaining records
  7. Record keeping: Maintaining site diary, measurement book, muster roll, stock register
  8. Coordination: Liaising between design team, contractor, sub-contractors, and client
  9. Progress reporting: Preparing daily/weekly/monthly progress reports
  10. Problem solving: Handling site difficulties, unforeseen conditions, technical problems
  11. Testing: Arranging and supervising field and laboratory tests
  12. Change management: Documenting variations and extra work
  13. Financial: Preparing running bills, checking interim payment certificates
  14. Environmental compliance: Ensuring adherence to environmental regulations

Qualities of a Good Site Engineer

  • Technical knowledge and practical skills
  • Leadership and communication ability
  • Decision-making capability under pressure
  • Attention to detail and accuracy
  • Integrity and honesty
  • Good documentation habits

Definition

Supervision is the process of overseeing construction work to ensure it is carried out according to the approved plans, specifications, and quality standards.

Supervisory Activities

  1. Before Work Starts:
    • Study drawings, specifications, and contract documents
    • Check setting out and alignment
    • Verify material quality (testing certificates)
    • Ensure equipment is in working condition
    • Brief workers on the task
  2. During Work:
    • Monitor work progress and quality continuously
    • Check mix proportions (concrete), compaction levels (earthwork)
    • Ensure proper construction sequence
    • Enforce safety measures (PPE, scaffolding, barriers)
    • Coordinate between different trades (plumber, electrician, mason)
    • Address technical queries and problems
  3. After Work Completion:
    • Inspect finished work for defects
    • Take measurements for billing
    • Record in measurement book and site diary
    • Approve or reject work

Levels of Supervision

  • Project Manager: Overall project supervision, reporting to management
  • Site Engineer: Day-to-day technical supervision at site
  • Foreman / Overseer: Direct supervision of labor and specific work activities
  • Consultant’s Representative: Independent supervision on behalf of client

Definition

A Site Order Book is a bound book used for written instructions from the Engineer/Consultant to the Contractor at site. It serves as an official record of all instructions given during construction.

Purpose

  • Record instructions that may have cost or time implications
  • Provide written evidence of instructions for dispute resolution
  • Ensure contractor acknowledges receipt of instructions
  • Track compliance with instructions

Contents of Site Order Book

  • Date and serial number of order
  • Detailed description of instruction
  • Signature of engineer giving the order
  • Signature of contractor acknowledging receipt
  • Reference to contract clause (if applicable)
  • Any cost/time implications noted

Measurement Book (MB)

The Measurement Book is the most important site record. It is a bound, serially numbered book where all measurements of completed work are recorded. It forms the basis of payment to the contractor.

Rules for Measurement Book

  • Entries must be in ink, never pencil
  • No erasing or overwriting — corrections by striking and initialing
  • Pages serially numbered and bound
  • Measurements recorded in presence of both parties (engineer and contractor)
  • Each entry signed by recording officer and checked by supervisor
  • Abstract of measurements calculated and carried to bill

Contents of MB Entry

  • Name and location of work
  • Contract number and item number
  • Date of measurement
  • Description of work item
  • Detailed dimensions (length, breadth, height/depth)
  • Calculations and totals
  • Deductions for voids
  • Signatures of parties

Bills Procedure

Types of Bills

  1. Running Bill (Interim Bill / RA Bill):
    • Periodic payment for work completed to date
    • Usually monthly or at specified intervals
    • Based on measurements recorded in MB
    • Subject to retention money deduction (5–10%)
    • “On account” payment — subject to final adjustment
  2. Final Bill:
    • Prepared after completion of entire work
    • Includes all items, variations, extra work, claims
    • Retention money released after defects liability period
    • Final settlement of all accounts
  3. Secured Advance Bill:
    • Advance payment against materials brought to site but not yet used
    • Usually 75% of material value
    • Recovered from subsequent running bills
  4. Mobilization Advance Bill:
    • Advance for contractor’s mobilization (typically 10–20% of contract)
    • Against bank guarantee
    • Recovered proportionally from running bills

Bill Processing Steps

  1. Contractor submits bill with supporting measurements
  2. Site engineer checks measurements against MB
  3. Quantity surveyor verifies calculations
  4. Deductions applied (retention, advance recovery, penalties)
  5. Bill certified by project manager/engineer
  6. Payment processed by accounts department

Muster Roll

Daily record of laborers employed at site showing: name, trade/skill, daily attendance, overtime hours, wage rate, and payment. Essential for: (a) labor cost tracking, (b) force account (amanat) work payment, (c) insurance and compensation claims.
