Definition

ABC Classification (also called Always Better Control or Selective Inventory Control) is a method of classifying construction materials based on their annual consumption value (quantity × unit cost). It follows the Pareto Principle (80-20 rule).

Classification Groups

Category% of Total Items% of Total ValueControl LevelExamples
A (High Value)10–20%70–80%Tight control, frequent review, exact recordsCement, steel, timber
B (Moderate Value)20–30%15–20%Moderate control, periodic reviewBricks, sand, pipes
C (Low Value)50–70%5–10%Loose control, bulk orderingNails, wire, binding wire, paint

Basis of ABC Classification

  • Annual consumption value = Annual quantity consumed × Unit price
  • Items arranged in descending order of annual consumption value
  • Cumulative percentage calculated for both items and value
  • Graphically represented as ABC curve (cumulative value % vs cumulative item %)

Steps for ABC Classification

  1. List all material items with their annual consumption quantity and unit price
  2. Calculate annual consumption value for each item
  3. Arrange items in descending order of consumption value
  4. Calculate cumulative consumption value and cumulative percentage
  5. Classify: Top 70–80% value = A, next 15–20% = B, remaining = C
  6. Draw the ABC curve

Key Benefits / Significance

  • Selective control: Focus management attention on high-value items (A category)
  • Cost reduction: Better procurement strategies reduce inventory costs
  • Prevents stock-out: Close monitoring of A-items prevents production delays
  • Better ordering: A-items: frequent small orders; C-items: bulk orders at intervals
  • Working capital optimization: Reduces money locked in excess inventory
  • Storage management: Prioritize secure storage for A-items
  • Management effort distribution: Maximum effort on A (few items, high value)

Definition

Material Wastage Standard is the acceptable percentage of material that can be lost, damaged, or wasted during procurement, storage, handling, and use in construction. It serves as a benchmark for controlling material waste.

Wastage Formula

Wastage % = [(Material Issued − Material Used in Work) / Material Issued] × 100

Types of Wastage

  • Controllable Wastage: Can be reduced through better management — theft, improper handling, careless mixing, over-ordering
  • Uncontrollable Wastage: Inherent in process — cutting waste, mixing residues, natural deterioration, breakage during transit

Standard Wastage Percentages

MaterialStandard Wastage %
Cement3–5%
Steel reinforcement3–5% (cutting waste)
Bricks5–10% (breakage)
Sand5–10%
Aggregate5–8%
Timber10–15%
Paint5%
Tiles3–5%

Causes of Excessive Material Wastage During Usage

  1. Excess quantity estimation in mix design
  2. Shortage in delivery (supplier cheating)
  3. Theft and pilferage at site
  4. Breakage and damage during handling/transport
  5. Lack of pre-work preparation and planning
  6. Use of inferior quality materials leading to rejection
  7. Poor accounting and inventory records
  8. Negligent supervision and monitoring
  9. Unforeseen circumstances (rain, flooding)
  10. Deterioration from long storage (cement absorbs moisture)
  11. Over-issues and failure to return unused materials

Preventive Measures

  • Proper inventory management using ABC classification and EOQ
  • Adequate storage facilities — covered, dry, organized
  • Strict material issue procedures with documentation
  • Regular stock checking and physical verification
  • Training workers on proper material handling
  • Security measures to prevent theft
  • Use of standard mix designs to minimize over-mixing
  • Proper transportation methods to minimize breakage

Definition

Material provisioning (procurement) is the systematic process of identifying, ordering, receiving, storing, and issuing materials for construction work. It ensures the right material, in the right quantity, at the right time, at the right place, and at the right price.

Steps of Material Provisioning Process

  1. Identification of materials needed: From drawings, BOQ, specifications
  2. Quantity estimation: Calculate required quantities with wastage allowance
  3. Scheduling delivery: Align material delivery with construction schedule
  4. Identification of sources/suppliers: Local markets, factories, importers
  5. Inquiry and quotation: Obtain competitive quotes from multiple suppliers
  6. Comparison and selection: Evaluate on price, quality, delivery reliability, credit terms
  7. Purchase order: Formal order with specifications, quantity, price, delivery date
  8. Inspection and receipt: Check quality and quantity against order; reject substandard
  9. Storage: Proper storage per material requirements (covered for cement, open for aggregates)
  10. Issue and use: Issue materials against requisitions; track consumption vs plan

Considerations for Rural Area Procurement (Exam Context)

  • Limited supplier options — may need to arrange transport from nearest urban center
  • Storage challenges — may need temporary shelters for cement, steel
  • Lead time is longer — order earlier with higher safety stock
  • Local materials (sand, aggregate, stone) may be sourced locally — quality testing essential
  • Seasonal access restrictions — monsoon affects road access in hills

What is Inventory?

Inventory refers to the stock of materials, goods, and supplies held by an organization for use in construction. Proper inventory management ensures materials are available when needed without excessive stock.

Key Inventory Terms

TermDefinition
InventoryStock of materials held for use in construction (includes raw materials, work-in-progress, finished goods)
Inventory CostTotal cost of holding inventory = Ordering Cost + Carrying Cost + Shortage Cost
Ordering Cost (Co)Cost per order placed (transportation, paperwork, inspection) — decreases with larger order quantity
Carrying Cost (Ci)Cost of holding inventory (storage, insurance, deterioration, interest on locked capital) — increases with larger order quantity
Lead TimeTime between placing an order and receiving the material
Safety StockExtra stock maintained as buffer against demand variations and supply delays
Re-Order Level (ROL)Inventory level at which a new order must be placed = (Lead time × Daily consumption) + Safety stock
Cycle TimeTime between two successive orders
Goods in Transit (GiT)Materials ordered but not yet received; physically in transport

Objectives of Inventory Management

  • Ensure uninterrupted supply of materials for construction
  • Minimize total inventory cost (ordering + carrying)
  • Prevent stock-outs that cause work stoppages
  • Reduce wastage and deterioration of stored materials
  • Optimize working capital locked in inventory

Economic Order Quantity (EOQ)

EOQ is the optimal order quantity that minimizes total inventory cost (ordering cost + carrying cost). At EOQ, ordering cost equals carrying cost.

EOQ Formula:

EOQ = √(2DCo / CiP)

Where:

  • D = Annual demand (total quantity required per year)
  • Co = Ordering cost per order (Rs)
  • Ci = Carrying cost rate (% of unit price per year)
  • P = Unit price of material (Rs)

Inventory Level Formulae

  • Re-Order Level (ROL) = (Lead Time × Daily Consumption) + Safety Stock
  • Maximum Stock Level = ROL + EOQ − (Minimum Consumption × Minimum Lead Time)
  • Minimum Stock Level = Safety Stock
  • Average Stock Level = Minimum Stock + EOQ/2
  • Total Inventory Cost = (D/EOQ) × Co + (EOQ/2) × Ci × P
  • Number of Orders = D / EOQ
  • Cycle Time = Working Days / Number of Orders
Key Exam Point: EOQ derivation may be asked (2081 Shrawan). EOQ is derived by setting d(Total Cost)/d(Q) = 0. Total Cost = (D/Q)Co + (Q/2)CiP. Differentiating and solving gives Q* = √(2DCo/CiP).

3.5 Inventory Planning Process for Repetitive Materials 2073 2077 2079

Past Questions:

Definition

Inventory planning for repetitive (regularly consumed) materials involves determining when to order, how much to order, and how to maintain optimal stock levels throughout the project duration.

Key Components

  • Working Stock: Inventory consumed during normal operations between orders
  • Safety Stock: Buffer stock to cover variations in demand and supply
  • Lead Time Stock: Stock needed to sustain operations during the procurement lead time
  • Re-Order Point (ROP): Stock level that triggers a new order
  • Maximum Stock Level: Upper limit of inventory to prevent overstocking
  • Minimum Stock Level: Lower limit to prevent stock-out

Planning Steps for Repetitive Materials

  1. Determine daily/weekly consumption rate from construction schedule
  2. Calculate total quantity required for the project
  3. Determine EOQ for optimal order quantity
  4. Establish lead time with suppliers
  5. Set safety stock level based on consumption variability and supply reliability
  6. Calculate ROL = (Lead time × Daily consumption) + Safety stock
  7. Set maximum and minimum stock levels
  8. Prepare ordering schedule aligned with construction schedule

Inventory Diagram

The inventory level follows a saw-tooth pattern: stock decreases linearly as materials are consumed, and jumps up when a new order is received. Order is placed when stock reaches ROL, and material arrives after lead time.

Definition

Value Engineering (VE) is a systematic method to improve the “value” of goods or services by examining function, cost, and performance. It seeks to achieve the required function at the lowest overall cost without sacrificing quality or performance.

Value = Function (Worth) / Cost

Key Concept

  • Value can be improved by: (a) increasing function at same cost, (b) maintaining function at lower cost, (c) increasing function at lower cost
  • Value Engineering ≠ Cost cutting. VE maintains or improves required functionality.
  • Originated from General Electric during WWII when material shortages forced engineers to find alternatives

Application in Material Procurement

  • Material substitution: Replace expensive materials with equally functional but cheaper alternatives (e.g., hollow blocks instead of solid bricks for non-load-bearing walls)
  • Standardization: Use standard sizes and specifications to reduce waste and cost
  • Design simplification: Simplify construction details to reduce material consumption
  • Alternative construction methods: Precast elements vs cast-in-situ, prefabricated vs conventional
  • Bulk purchasing: Negotiate better prices through volume orders
  • Local material use: Utilize locally available materials to reduce transportation costs
  • Waste reduction: Optimize cutting patterns for steel, timber; use by-products

Value Engineering Selection Criteria / Awareness Checklist

  • Can the material be eliminated without affecting function?
  • Can a cheaper material serve the same purpose?
  • Can standard commercially available products be used?
  • Can the tolerance/specification be relaxed without affecting performance?
  • Can a different construction method reduce material need?
  • Is the material being used for more than its required function?
  • Can the material be recycled or reused?

Importance of Value Engineering

  • Reduces project cost without compromising quality
  • Promotes innovation and creative thinking
  • Improves constructability and efficiency
  • Results in better value for money for the client
  • Encourages sustainable construction practices
