Question 2

What are the prominent advantages and disadvantages of using construction equipments? Explain in short. [3]

2075 Bhadra

Advantages:

  • Higher Productivity: Equipment performs work much faster than manual labor, increasing the rate of output significantly.
  • Better Quality: Uniform and consistent output is achieved (e.g., uniform compaction by rollers, proper mixing of concrete).
  • Economy in Large Projects: Reduces cost per unit for bulk work, making large projects economically feasible.
  • Handles Heavy Loads: Tasks impossible to perform manually like lifting heavy steel, concrete elements, deep excavation, etc.
  • Reduced Labor Dependency: Maintains planned rate of production even during labor shortages.
  • Safety: Reduces human exposure to hazardous tasks; equipment is free from social and emotional factors.
  • Works in Difficult Conditions: Equipment can work in adverse weather, climatic conditions, and difficult topography.

Disadvantages:

  • High Initial Investment: Purchase cost of construction equipment is substantial.
  • Maintenance Cost: Regular maintenance, repairs, and large inventory of spare parts are expensive.
  • Skilled Operators Needed: Requires trained and experienced operators to operate efficiently.
  • Breakdown Risk: Equipment failure can halt the entire project, causing significant delays.
  • Environmental Impact: Noise, air pollution, vibration, and high fuel consumption.
  • Unemployment: May create social problem due to replacement of manual labor by machines.
  • Disposal Problem: Reselling or disposing of old construction equipment is difficult.

4.2 Equipment for Excavation

Question 7

What are various equipment used for excavation works? List them and explain any two with proper sketches. [6]

2079 Shrawan

List of Excavation Equipment:

  1. Excavator (Hydraulic Excavator)
  2. Backhoe Loader
  3. Bulldozer
  4. Scraper (Wheel Tractor Scraper)
  5. Dragline Excavator
  6. Power Shovel / Front-end Loader
  7. Grader (Motor Grader)
  8. Trencher (Trenching Machine)
  9. Clamshell

1. Excavator (Hydraulic Excavator):

  • Excavators are the most important and widely used equipment in the construction industry.
  • They are used for excavation, heavy lifting, demolition, river dredging, cutting of trees, etc.
  • An excavator consists of a long boom arm with a digging bucket at its end, mounted on a rotating cabin (capable of 360° rotation).
  • The cabin is the operator’s place from where the machine is controlled.
  • Available in both wheeled and tracked forms — tracked type provides better stability on rough terrain.
  • It has a hoe (teeth-attached bucket) capable of excavating to depth and loading excavated material directly into transporting vehicles.
  • By changing the bucket, it can be converted to a driller, power shovel, etc.
  • Classified based on digging motion: upward motion = shovel, downward motion = hoe.

2. Bulldozer:

  • Bulldozer is soil-excavating equipment used for removing topsoil, rock strata, and lifting soil.
  • It is a crawler-type vehicle equipped with a substantial wide metal blade (plate) at the front that can be lowered and raised using hydraulic pistons.
  • At the rear, it has a claw-like device (ripper) to loosen densely compacted materials.
  • It is a versatile mechanical instrument that can work in the worst site conditions like waterlogged as well as loose soil where other equipment cannot operate.
  • Uses: Pushing large quantities of soil/sand/rubble, clearing vegetation, bush and shrubs, removing tree stumps, shallow excavation, making pilot roads, moving material over long distances.
  • It is used for excavating in both firm and soft ground conditions.

The commonly used excavation equipment in construction works are:

a) Excavator:

  • Most widely used equipment; used for excavation, heavy lifting, demolition, dredging.
  • Has a long arm with digging bucket, cabin rotatable 360°, available in tracked and wheeled forms.
  • Classified as Shovel (upward digging) and Hoe (downward digging).

b) Bulldozer:

  • Crawler equipped with a wide metal blade at front and ripper at rear.
  • Used for pushing soil, clearing vegetation, shallow excavation, making pilot roads.
  • Can work in worst site conditions including waterlogged areas.

c) Power Shovel / Front-end Loader:

  • Has a square tilting bucket on the end of a moveable arm.
  • Used to excavate soil and load into trucks or conveyor belts.
  • Available in crawler-mounted or pneumatic wheel-mounted types.

d) Backhoe Loader:

  • Combined machine: loader bucket in front, backhoe (excavator arm) at rear.
  • Suitable for digging below ground level — trenches, pipe laying, drainage work.
  • Mounted on rubber tires giving good road mobility.

e) Dragline Excavator:

  • Heavy equipment for larger depth excavations.
  • Has a long boom with a digging bucket suspended from the top using cables.
  • Used for port construction, underwater excavation, sediment removal.

f) Scraper (Wheel Tractor Scraper):

  • Earth-moving equipment that scrapes, loads, hauls, and dumps material.
  • Front blade digs soil, which is collected in hopper via conveyor belt.
  • Suitable for large-area excavation and leveling.

g) Grader (Motor Grader):

  • Used mainly to level soil surfaces in road construction.
  • Has a horizontal blade between front and rear wheels.
  • Used to flatten surface before laying asphalt, remove snow/dirt from roads.

h) Trencher:

  • Specialized machine for excavating trenches for pipeline/cable laying and drainage.
  • Available as chain trencher and wheeled trencher.

i) Clamshell:

  • Bucket shaped like a hinged double shell; used for digging, excavation, and lifting loose material.
  • Used for handling sand, aggregate, gravel; lifting from deep foundations like cofferdams, manholes.

Question 16

Explain equipment for compaction. [Part of 10]

2072 Ashwin

Compaction is done to remove air entrapped in the soil and increase its strength and density. Various types of compacting equipment are:

a) Rammer:

  • Simplest type of compacting equipment.
  • Heavy iron weight provided with a wooden handle.
  • Soil surface is compacted by lifting and releasing in a cycle.
  • Suitable for very small areas.

b) Frog Rammer (Monkey Jumper):

  • Rammer with a small ramming plate that jumps with the help of an engine mounted on top.
  • Suitable for very small trenches, backfill behind culvert approaches, and corners of structures.

c) Plate Compactor (Hand Compactor):

  • Hand-held vibrating plate with a diesel/petrol engine mounted on springs.
  • The engine provides vibration to the plate, compacting the soil beneath.
  • Suitable for small to medium areas, pavement edges, trench fills.

d) Hand Roller / Tamping Roller:

  • Suitable for compacting small trenches (90 cm to 1.2 m wide).
  • Available in both vibrating and static load types.
  • Single steel drum is the common type.

e) Smooth Wheel Roller:

  • Used for compaction of cohesive soils and shallow layers.
  • Consists of three steel wheels — one at the front and two at the back.
  • Suitable for compacting asphalt layers and finishing surfaces.

f) Sheep Foot Roller:

  • Consists of a hollow steel drum with pads (shaped like sheep’s feet) attached on the outer surface.
  • The hollow drum is filled with moist sand or stones for desired weight.
  • Suitable for deep compaction of clayey and cohesive soils.

g) Pneumatic Tired Roller:

  • Uses rubber tires (typically 3 in front and 4 in rear) to produce compaction.
  • Non-vibrating type, suitable for clayey soils and bituminous pavement.
  • Most suitable for asphalt concrete paving, especially final rolling of finished surface.

h) Vibrating Roller:

  • Single drum type steel wheel with vibrating mechanism.
  • Suitable for granular soil compaction.
  • A vibrating mechanism is fitted to the steel frame which makes the drum vibrate.

i) Tandem Roller:

  • Self-propelled steel wheel type rollers having 3 axles with 3 steel drums.
  • Can be ballasted to increase dead load.
  • May be vibrating or non-vibrating type.

4.2 Concrete Construction Equipment

Question 22

List the different type of equipments for concrete construction with their function. Explain briefly. [6]

2073 Magh

Concrete construction involves a series of operations from material production to curing. The equipment used at each stage are:

1. Batching Equipment:

  • Gauge Box: Used for volumetric batching in small-scale works.
  • Ordinary Batching Scale: Used for weight batching in small/medium works; generally used in labs.
  • Batching Plant (Automatic): For large-scale jobs; receives ingredients, weighs them to required proportions, and transfers to mixing plant.

2. Mixing Equipment:

  • Hand-Fed Drum Mixer: Small-scale mixing; manually loaded drum that rotates to mix.
  • Self-Loading Concrete Mixer: Automatically loads and mixes materials.
  • Concrete Mixing Pan: Fixed stationary type; used at central mixing plants.
  • Transit Mixer: Truck-mounted rotating drum; mixes concrete during transportation from batching plant to site.
  • Mixing Plant: Large-scale continuous mixing facility.

3. Transportation Equipment:

  • Metal Pan & Wheel Barrow: Manual transport for small quantities.
  • Mini Dumper: Small front-end dump trucks for short-distance transport.
  • Conveyor Belt: For continuous transport; suitable for fixed locations.
  • Concrete Pump: Pumps concrete through pipelines to hard-to-reach locations, especially in high-rise buildings and tunnel construction.
  • Chutes: Transfer concrete from higher level to lower level using gravity.
  • Transit Mixer (Truck): For long-distance transport without segregation.

4. Compaction Equipment (for Concrete):

Question 31

Discuss the methods of tunnel excavation. [6]

2071 Bhadra

There are two main methods of tunnel excavation:

1. Conventional Method (Drill and Blast Method):

This is the traditional method of tunnel construction. The process involves:

  1. Setting up & Drilling: Drilling machines (jack hammers, drifters) are used to drill holes in the rock face.
  2. Loading Holes & Blasting: Holes are loaded with explosives and detonated in a controlled manner using electric detonating arrangements.
  3. Ventilating & Dust Removal: After the blast, exhaust fans ventilate the tunnel and remove dust.
  4. Mucking: Loading and hauling the blasted material (muck) out of the tunnel using wheelbarrows, trucks, loaders, or rail buggies.
  5. Ground Water Removal: Pumps are used if necessary to remove ground water.
  6. Supporting: Erecting temporary supports for roof and sides (steel arches, rock bolts, shotcrete) if necessary.
  7. Reinforcement: Placing reinforcing steel as required.
  8. Lining: Placing the concrete lining for permanent support.

This cycle is repeated progressively. For soft soil, excavation is done with excavators and manual equipment. For hard rock, drilling and blasting is adopted.

2. Tunnel Boring Machine (TBM) Method:

  • TBM is a machine that excavates tunnels with a circular cross section.
  • The machine has a programmed cutting head (mole) that drills through the mountain.
  • Can bore tunnels of diameter ranging from 1 m to 20 m.
  • Can be used on any geology (soft ground and hard rock).
  • Once programmed, these machines can progress along fixed coordinates in X, Y, and Z directions.
  • Muck is taken out through a pipe/conveyor belt attached to the machine.
  • This method is very fast and efficient but machines are very costly.
  • Economical for longer tunnels where the high cost of TBM is justified.
  • Precast tunnel lining segments are placed by segment erectors immediately behind the TBM.

3. New Austrian Tunneling Method (NATM):

  • Modern method that utilizes the inherent strength of surrounding rock mass.
  • Involves controlled excavation followed by immediate application of shotcrete and rock bolts.
  • Monitoring of convergence and deformation is done to ensure stability.

Explain the steps to be followed in sequence while carrying out tunneling work by using conventional method. Introduce TBM and its advantage in tunnel construction. [7]

Steps in Conventional Method of Tunnel Construction (in sequence):

  1. Survey and Alignment: Fix the tunnel alignment, portal locations, gradient, and cross-section dimensions.
  2. Portal Construction: Construct the tunnel portal (entrance) where open cut meets the tunnel.
  3. Setting up & Drilling: Set up drilling equipment (jack hammers, pneumatic drills) and drill holes in the rock face as per the designed pattern.
  4. Loading Holes with Explosives: Load the drilled holes with carefully measured explosives.
  5. Blasting: Detonate the explosives using electric detonating arrangement in a controlled sequence.
  6. Ventilating and Dust Removal: After blasting, ventilate the tunnel using exhaust fans to remove dust and foul air. Fresh air is pumped in from the other end.
  7. Mucking (Loading and Hauling): Remove the blasted material (muck) using wheelbarrows, loaders, trucks, or rail buggies.
  8. Ground Water Removal: Pump out ground water if encountered.
  9. Temporary Support: Erect supports for roof and sides using steel arches, rock bolts, and shotcrete as necessary.
  10. Reinforcement Placement: Place reinforcing steel as per design.
  11. Concrete Lining: Place concrete lining for permanent structural support using formwork, batching plant, and concrete pumps.

This cycle is repeated progressively as the tunnel advances.

Introduction to TBM:

  • Tunnel Boring Machine (TBM) is a modern machine used to excavate tunnels with a circular cross section.
  • It has a programmed cutting head (mole) that bores through rock or soil.
  • Can bore tunnels of diameter ranging from 1 m to 20 m.
  • Can work on any geology (soft clay to hard rock).
  • Once programmed, TBMs can progress along fixed coordinates in all three directions (X, Y, Z).
  • Muck is removed through conveyor belts or pipes attached to the machine.

Advantages of TBM:

  1. Very fast rate of tunnel boring compared to conventional method.
  2. Makes tunnel boring process much safer (less risk of rock falls, no blasting hazards).

Drawbacks of TBM:

  • Very high initial cost of the machine and supporting equipment (conveyor belts, rails, slurry plant, pipelines).
  • High cost of consumables (drill bits, TBM cutters, blasting agents).
  • Economical only for longer tunnels where cost is justified by the length.

Question 46

Discuss why economic and commercial considerations are vital factors affecting in selection of proper construction equipment with examples. [6]

2078 Kartik

Economic and commercial considerations are vital because the cost of construction equipment ranges from 25% to 40% of the total project cost. Proper economic evaluation ensures that the investment in equipment is recovered during its useful life and contributes to project profitability.

Key Economic & Commercial Considerations:

  1. Purchase Price vs. Output: The machine must be appropriately priced for its output capacity. Considering 10% per year depreciation, a machine should last at least 10 years, pay for itself, and earn profits.
    Example: A ₹1 crore excavator depreciating at 10%/year should generate sufficient revenue over 10 years to recover the investment and profit.
  2. Fuel Cost: Fuel constitutes up to 60% of operating costs. A fuel-efficient machine significantly reduces the overall cost.
    Example: A more fuel-efficient excavator consuming 15 liters/hour vs. 20 liters/hour saves ₹500+/hour in fuel costs, translating to lakhs of savings over the project.
  3. Depreciation: Yearly depreciation of the machine’s value must be accounted for. Equipment loses value over time whether or not it is used.
    Example: A machine bought for ₹50 lakhs has only ₹25 lakhs book value after 5 years (assuming straight-line depreciation).
  4. Maintenance & Spare Parts Cost: Regular maintenance, repairs, and spare parts add significantly to lifecycle cost. Equipment with easily available and affordable parts is preferred.
    Example: A machine with locally available spare parts costs much less to maintain than one requiring imported parts.
  5. Idle Time Cost: Equipment earns nothing when idle but continues to depreciate. Minimizing idle time is crucial.
    Example: A tower crane costing ₹5 lakhs/month in rental that sits idle for 2 months wastes ₹10 lakhs.
  6. Resale Value: The make of engine, hydraulics, and transmission affects the resale value. Machines from reputed manufacturers with good after-sales support have higher resale value.
    Example: A CAT excavator typically retains 40–50% value after 5 years, while an unknown brand may retain only 20%.
  7. Purchase vs. Lease vs. Hire Decision:
    • Purchase: Economical for long-duration, multiple projects
    • Lease: Suitable for medium-term use; avoids large capital outlay
    • Hire: Best for short-term or specialized equipment needs
    Example: For a 6-month project, hiring a crane is more economical than purchasing one.
  8. Insurance, Storage, Logistics & Operator Costs: These overhead costs must be considered in the total cost of ownership.

Therefore, after technical selection, the economic viability must be thoroughly evaluated considering all direct and indirect costs to ensure the equipment contributes to project profitability rather than becoming a financial burden.

Question 47

Write down the factors to be considered for deciding the extent of mechanisation of construction project. [4]

2073 Bhadra

The extent of mechanization depends on balancing the benefits of equipment use against manual labor. The following factors should be considered:

  1. Scale of Project: Large-scale projects (dams, highways, airports) require high mechanization; small projects may not justify equipment costs.
  2. Time Constraints: Tight schedules demand higher mechanization for faster work output.
  3. Availability of Labor: In areas with labor shortage, mechanization is essential to maintain planned production rates.
  4. Cost of Labor vs. Equipment: Where labor is expensive, mechanization reduces per-unit cost. Where labor is cheap and abundant, manual work may be more economical.
  5. Nature of Work: Some tasks like pile driving, TBM tunneling, and heavy lifting cannot be done manually and require full mechanization.
  6. Quality Requirements: When high precision and uniform quality are needed (e.g., batching, compaction), equipment ensures better control.
  7. Site Conditions: Difficult terrain, adverse climate, underwater work, or underground work demands mechanized operations.
  8. Availability of Equipment: Whether required equipment is available for purchase, lease, or hire in the local market.
  9. Support Infrastructure: Availability of fuel, electricity, spare parts, skilled operators, and workshops at or near the site.
  10. Social Considerations: In developing countries, providing employment to unskilled labor may be a social priority, limiting the extent of mechanization.
  11. Pollution Standards: Environmental regulations may limit use of certain equipment.
Question 48

Write short notes on: Purchase or lease of equipment. [4]

2069 Bhadra (Old Syllabus)

Purchase of Equipment:

  • Advantages:
    • Ownership gives full control over equipment availability and scheduling.
    • Economical for long-duration projects or when equipment is needed across multiple projects.
    • Can be modified or customized as per project needs.
    • Generates revenue even when rented out to other contractors during idle periods.
    • Tax benefits from depreciation can be claimed.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Requires heavy initial capital investment.
    • Owner bears all maintenance, repair, and insurance costs.
    • Risk of obsolescence — technology may advance, making the machine outdated.
    • Equipment depreciates whether it is used or idle.
    • Disposal/resale is difficult and may result in loss.

Lease of Equipment:

  • Advantages:
    • Avoids large capital outlay; conserves working capital for other uses.
    • Lease payments are operational expenses, often tax-deductible.
    • Access to latest technology without bearing obsolescence risk.
    • Maintenance may be included in the lease (depending on contract).
    • Flexible — can upgrade or return equipment at end of lease.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Total cost over a long period may exceed the purchase price.
    • No ownership — equipment must be returned after lease period.
    • May have restrictions on usage, modifications, or working hours.
    • Availability depends on the lessor; may not always be available when needed.
